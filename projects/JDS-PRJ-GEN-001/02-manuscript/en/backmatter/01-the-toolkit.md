# The Toolkit: Lists, Logs & Your Maintenance Program

A good engineer doesn't trust memory — he keeps records. The difference between a garage that
*was* organized and one that *stays* organized is a handful of simple sheets you fill in and a
rhythm you follow. This is that system, and every sheet is free to download and print.

> phone, or type the address under it, and print the sheet at home on ordinary A4.

## Keep the record

Three lists turn a tidy garage into a managed one:

- **The inventory** — what you own and which zone it lives in. It doubles as your **insurance and
  theft record**: add serial numbers and photograph anything valuable, and a break-in or a fire
  claim becomes a five-minute job instead of a guessing game.
- **The lifetime journal** — one sheet per machine or major tool: when you bought it, what it
  cost, and every service and repair since. A tool with a history lasts longer, is safer, and is
  worth more the day you sell it.
- **The hazardous-materials log** — what fuel, paint, and chemicals you keep, how much, and when
  each was bought or thrown out. It keeps the dangerous pile small, legal, and known (chapter 5).

## The continuous maintenance program

A garage doesn't stay in order by luck or by rescue missions — it stays in order on a rhythm. Run
these four and it never slides back:

| Cadence | Time | What you do |
|---------|------|-------------|
| **Weekly** | 10 min | Put things back · recycling out · wipe the bench |
| **Monthly** | 15 min | A quick **fire round** · scan for new clutter · note low consumables |
| **Spring & Fall** | ½ day | Seasonal swap + a Sort pass + hazard check + a **safety round** |
| **Yearly** | 1–2 h | Walk the inventory · update the journals · photograph valuables · test alarms & GFCI |

The weekly round is the engine; the yearly review is what catches everything the small rounds
miss. Print the **maintenance program & log**, hang it inside the door, and sign each pass — a
system the whole house can read and share outlives one person's good intentions.

> **Often missed — fold these into the yearly review**
> - A **fire extinguisher** by the door, gauge in the green, and **smoke / CO alarms** that work.
> - **GFCI / RCD** protection on garage outlets — tested, not assumed.
> - **Insurance photos + serial numbers** of valuable tools (your inventory already holds them).
> - **Manuals, receipts, and warranties** in one labeled folder or pocket.
> - A **consumables reorder list** so you never run out mid-job.
> - **Damp and security**: condensation control, a sound door, a decent lock.

## Fire rounds & safety rounds

On a ship you don't wait for a fire to find you — someone walks a **fire round**, on a schedule,
looking for the start of trouble. A workshop runs a **safety round** (the Swedish *skyddsrond*)
the same way: a planned look for what could hurt someone. Your garage holds fuel, blades,
electricity, and heavy weight, so it has earned the same habit.

- **Fire round — monthly, and after any hot or oily work.** A two-minute walk: extinguisher
  charged and reachable, escape route clear, fuel and gas stored right, no pile of oily rags, no
  overloaded sockets, the fire door shut. Catching one of these is the cheapest insurance you'll
  ever buy.
- **Safety round — spring and fall.** A slower look for hazards: trip points, anchored shelving,
  guarded tools, PPE and first-aid to hand, GFCI tested, ladders sound, chemicals out of reach,
  jack stands under any raised car. Write down what you find *and a date to fix it* — that's what
  makes it a round and not a glance.
