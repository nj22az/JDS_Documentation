# The Dangerous and the Toxic

In every garage there's a pile that can't be treated like ordinary trash. Paint cans with a
skin of dried sludge. A jug of old petrol. The car battery you swapped out. Spray cans, glue,
solvent, the leaking can of oil. This category gets its own chapter for a reason: it's the most
common way a garage becomes genuinely dangerous, and it follows completely different rules.

This still belongs to the **Sort** step, but the dangerous things must not go into the same
piles as everything else. They need handling on their own, and they need handling correctly.

## The problem: the dangerous stuff hides and gets forgotten

Chemicals in a garage have a nasty habit: they accumulate slowly and they never announce
themselves. Nobody buys ten liters of old petrol at once. It's half a liter here, a splash there,
a jug forgotten after a move — until one day there are flammable liquids in several spots that
nobody ever planned.

Three things make this pile dangerous:

1. **Fire.** Petrol, solvents, and spray cans give off vapor. It's the vapor, not the liquid, that
   ignites — and it's heavier than air, so it creeps along the floor toward ignition sources you'd
   never connect to the jug.
2. **Poison and leaks.** Old oil, antifreeze, and chemicals that leak contaminate both the air
   indoors and the ground if you pour them the wrong way.
3. **It must never go in the trash.** Most of this pile is hazardous waste by law and belongs at a
   household hazardous-waste drop-off — never in the household garbage, never down the drain.

Pay special attention to the first point, because it's widely misunderstood. Many people think
petrol is only dangerous near an open flame. But it's the vapor that burns, not the liquid, and the
vapor is heavier than air. It pools along the floor and can travel several meters to an ignition
source you never linked to the jug: a water heater with a pilot light, a motor starting, a switch
that sparks. So "putting the petrol a bit out of the way" isn't enough. It has to leave — in the
right amount and the right container, and away from anything that can make a spark.

## The method here: gather, judge, dispose right

Handle the dangerous pile in three steps, and never let it mix with the ordinary sorting piles.

**Gather it all in one place.** Go through the garage and bring everything flammable, toxic, or
corrosive to a single spot, on a tray that catches leaks. Now you see the whole quantity — and, as
with the tools, most people are startled by how much has piled up.

**Judge: keep or lose.** Ask of each item: do I use it, and is it intact? A nearly empty paint can
you used last year can stay. A dried-up can, an unlabeled jug, or a rusty spray bottle goes. If
you're unsure what something is — treat it as hazardous waste and take it away.

**Dispose of it right.** Whatever goes leaves for the **recycling center** or a **hazardous-waste
drop-off**, sorted by type: paint and solvent together, waste oil together, batteries together.
Never pour chemicals down the drain, onto the ground, or into the household trash. Call your
municipality if you're unsure — they're glad to answer, and it's free.

The little that stays, you then store correctly — and here the step points ahead to **Set in
Order**: flammables low and cool, in their original packaging with a legible label, in a metal
cabinet if you have one, and never near the heater, the water boiler, or a spot where sparks can
happen.

> **Workshop Rule**
> If you're not sure what it is, treat it as hazardous waste and take it to the drop-off. Never
> guess with chemicals.

## What belongs in this pile

It's easy to miss how many everyday garage items are actually hazardous waste. Here are the most
common, so you recognize them as you gather:

- **Flammable liquids:** petrol, diesel, lamp oil, lighter fluid, mineral spirits, turpentine.
- **Paint and chemicals:** oil and enamel paint, solvent, glue, caulk, cleaners with a warning
  label.
- **Oils and fluids:** waste oil, motor oil, gear oil, glycol, antifreeze.
- **Spray cans:** anything pressurized, even "empty" ones — they hold propellant and residue.
- **Batteries:** the car battery, but also button cells, tool batteries, and rechargeable packs.
- **Fluorescent tubes and low-energy bulbs:** they contain small amounts of toxins and don't belong
  in the trash.

If you're unsure whether something belongs here, let the packaging decide: a flame, a skull, or a
corrosion symbol, or the words "danger," "flammable," or "harmful" mean hazardous waste. And one
simple rule of thumb goes a long way — if you wouldn't want it running into a stream, it doesn't
belong in the trash either.

Note also what does *not* belong here, to save yourself trouble: clean wood, clean metal, cardboard,
and whole tools are ordinary materials, sorted like everything else. The hazardous pile should be
small and specific. If it's large, that's because it accumulated over years — all the more reason to
deal with it now.

## An example: five kinds of petrol

A homeowner named Erik thought he had "a little petrol somewhere." When he gathered everything
flammable onto one board, he found five different containers of petrol — two unlabeled, one in an
old soda bottle, and one so old it smelled of varnish. Scattered through the garage, the quantity
had been invisible. Gathered, it was an obvious fire risk in the middle of his house.

He took everything but one labeled, approved can to the recycling center that same weekend. The
soda bottle was what frightened him most afterward — because it could just as easily have been
within reach of a child.

> **Safety**
> - Work with the garage door open so vapors clear.
> - Don't smoke, and avoid anything that sparks while you move flammables.
> - Never decant petrol or chemicals into soda or juice bottles — someone may think it's a drink.
>   Original container, legible label, always.
> - Never mix different chemicals in one container to "save space."
> - Store flammables low, cool, and away from heat sources and the electrical panel.

> **Weekend Project: clear the hazard corner**
> 1. Set out a tray or pan that catches leaks.
> 2. Go through the garage and gather everything flammable, toxic, and corrosive onto the tray.
> 3. Split into two: intact and in use (keep) — dried, unlabeled, or unknown (lose).
> 4. Take the "lose" half to the hazardous-waste drop-off, sorted by type. Call the municipality if
>    you're unsure.
> 5. Put back the little that stays — low and cool, original packaging, legible label.

## Takeaways

- Dangerous and toxic is its own category — it must never mix with the ordinary piles.
- The danger is fire (the vapor ignites), poison and leaks, and that it's illegal in the trash.
- Gather everything flammable in one place; only then do you see how much has quietly accumulated.
- Keep only what's intact and used; everything else goes to the hazardous-waste drop-off, sorted.
- Never store chemicals in drink bottles, and keep flammables low, cool, and safe.
