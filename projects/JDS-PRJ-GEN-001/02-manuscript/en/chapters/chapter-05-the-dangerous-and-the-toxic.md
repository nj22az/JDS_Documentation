# The Dangerous and the Toxic

[[CARD step=Step 4 of 10 | time=One morning | task=Make the hazard corner small, safe, and gone | need=Gloves, a sturdy box, your tip's opening hours]]

Every garage hides a pile that can't go in the bin: paint cans skinned over with sludge, a
jug of old petrol, the battery you swapped out, spray cans, solvent, a leaking can of oil. This
gets its own chapter because it's the most common way a garage turns genuinely dangerous — and
because it follows different rules from everything else.

It's still the **Sort** step. But the dangerous things never touch the ordinary piles. They get
handled on their own, and they get handled right.

## The problem: it accumulates invisibly, and the vapour is the killer

Chemicals creep in. Nobody buys ten litres of stale petrol at once — it's half a litre here, a
splash there, a can forgotten after a move, until flammable liquid sits in five places nobody
planned.

And here's the part most people get wrong: **with petrol, it's the vapour that burns, not the
liquid — and the vapour is heavier than air.** It pools along the slab and rolls, several metres,
to an ignition source you never connected to the can: a boiler pilot, a furnace, a fridge
compressor kicking on, a switch that sparks. "Putting the petrol a bit out of the way" does
nothing. It has to leave the space, or live in the right can — low, ventilated, and far from
anything that sparks.

That's also why the wall between an attached garage and the house is a **fire barrier.** Keep it
intact — don't cut holes in it for shelving — and keep its door shut. Building codes set how it's
built; your only job is not to breach it.

## The method here: gather, judge, dispose

Handle the hazard pile in three moves, and never let it mix with the sorting piles.

**Gather it on one tray.** Bring everything flammable, toxic, or corrosive to a single drip tray.
Now you see the real quantity — and, like the tools, the total usually startles people.

**Judge.** Of each item: do I use it, and is it intact? A near-empty can of paint you used last
season stays. A dried can, an unlabelled jug, a rusted spray bottle goes. Unsure what it is? It
goes — as hazardous waste.

**Dispose right.** Take it to a household hazardous-waste drop-off or recycling centre, sorted:
paint and solvent, waste oil, batteries. Never down the drain, onto the ground, or in the bin.
A call to your municipality settles any doubt, and it's free.

> **Workshop Rule**
> If you can't say what it is, it's hazardous waste. Never guess with chemicals.

## What counts as hazardous

- **Flammable liquids:** petrol, diesel, lamp oil, lighter fluid, mineral spirits, turpentine.
- **Paint and chemicals:** oil/enamel paint, solvent, glue, adhesives, anything with a warning label.
- **Fluids:** waste motor oil, gear oil, antifreeze/glycol, brake fluid.
- **Pressurised cans:** every spray can, even "empty" — propellant and residue remain.
- **Batteries:** car (lead-acid), plus tool packs, button cells, anything rechargeable.
- **Bulbs:** fluorescent tubes and CFLs contain mercury.

Rule of thumb: a flame, skull, or corrosion symbol — or the words *danger*, *flammable*,
*harmful* — means it goes to the drop-off. If you wouldn't want it running into a creek, it
doesn't belong in the bin.

> **Specs: safe-storage numbers worth knowing**
>
> | What | The number / rule |
> |---|---|
> | Petrol | Approved cans only, 20 litres (5 gal) or less each; Swedish cap ~25 litres without a permit — keep a season's worth |
> | Propane / LPG (gasol) | Never indoors or in an attached garage — store outdoors, upright, shaded |
> | Flammables | Keep low (vapour sinks), cool, away from any pilot, boiler, or fuse box; a cabinet past a can or two |
> | Extinguisher | Powder ABC, ~6 kg, by the exit door, ~1.3 m (4–5 ft) up — where you reach it on the way out |

## An example: five kinds of petrol

Erik thought he had "a little petrol somewhere." Gathered onto one board, it was five containers —
two unlabelled, one in an old soda bottle, one so old it smelled of varnish. Scattered, the
quantity had been invisible. Together, it was an open fire risk in the middle of his house.

Everything but one labelled safety can went to the recycling centre that weekend. The soda bottle
scared him most afterwards — because it could just as easily have been within a child's reach.

> **Safety**
> - Work with the door open so vapour clears; no smoking, no sparks, no grinding nearby.
> - Never decant fuel or chemicals into drink bottles — someone will think it's a drink.
> - Never combine chemicals "to save space"; some mixtures make heat or toxic gas.
> - Wear gloves for old fluids, and don't let a leaking battery's acid touch skin or concrete.

> **Weekend Project: clear the hazard corner**
> 1. Set out a drip tray.
> 2. Gather everything flammable, toxic, or corrosive onto it.
> 3. Split it: intact and in use (keep) — dried, unlabelled, or unknown (lose).
> 4. Run the "lose" half to the hazardous-waste drop-off, sorted by type.
> 5. Put the survivors back low, cool, labelled, in their original cans — propane outside.
