# The Flat and the Storage Locker

[[CARD step=Special case | time=One morning | task=Empty and rebuild the locker properly | need=A few stackable boxes]]

Not everyone has a garage. Many have a small storage unit instead — a cage in the basement, an
attic locker, a wardrobe off the stairwell. The space is smaller, often shared, and rarely visited.
But the method shrinks beautifully: the five steps work just as well on four square metres as on
forty. This chapter shows how to make a little space honest.

A small locker has one advantage and one drawback. The advantage is that it's quick to work
through. The drawback is that every misplaced thing shows immediately — there's no slack to hide
the mess in.

## The problem: a small space forgives nothing

In a big garage, disorder can hide for a while. In a basement locker of a few square metres, it
shows at once: a single unlabelled heap fills half the space, and then the door barely shuts. The
small locker punishes every "maybe" harder, because there simply isn't room for things you're
unsure about.

At the same time, the small locker invites exactly that overfilling. Because it's out of sight and
seldom visited, it becomes a place to shove whatever you don't want to deal with — moving boxes, old
electronics, "keep just in case." And because you rarely open the door, you never see how bad it's
got until you need something in there.

There are special considerations, too: if the locker is the mesh kind, everything shows from outside,
and in a shared basement there are rules about what may be stored and fire codes about clear
passageways. A small space, then, demands both more discipline and a little more consideration for
the neighbours.

## The method here: shrink the method, raise the bar

You run the five steps exactly as before, but adapted to the small format.

**Sort harder.** With so little room, the bar for "keep" has to sit higher. What fits is what you
genuinely use and can't store in the flat. Every "maybe" is a luxury the locker can't afford —
here the maybe-box from chapter four is especially valuable.

**Build upward, just as in the garage.** The same principle holds: the floor is most precious.
Stackable totes and a slim shelf frame along one wall often triple a small locker. Heavy and often
low, light and rarely high.

**Label everything — here it shows at once.** In a small, often mesh-fronted locker, labelling matters
even more. You should be able to open the door and see at a glance where everything is, without moving
a single tote. Clear bins plus labels make the whole locker readable from the doorway.

**Respect the shared rules.** In a shared basement: store nothing flammable or moisture-sensitive,
put nothing in the common passage, and stay inside your own cage. It's not just courtesy — clear
passageways in a basement are a fire requirement.

> **Workshop Rule**
> A small space has no slack for "maybe." Raise the bar for keeping — floor space is most precious
> where there's least of it.

## An example: the locker that held double

A tenant named Sofia was sure her basement cage was too small. Everything lay in one heap on the
floor, and she couldn't reach the back without climbing. She emptied the locker into the laundry room
one afternoon, sorted hard, and threw out two bags she'd forgotten she owned.

Then she put in a slim shelf and four stackable, labelled totes. The same cage now held twice as much,
visibly, and she could reach everything from the door. The space had never been too small — it had
just been used as a floor-heap instead of as a room with walls.

## What actually belongs in a basement locker

Part of keeping a small locker in order is knowing what shouldn't be there at all. A basement locker
suits rarely-used but robust things: seasonal gear, suitcases, tyres if the building allows them,
tools, whatever tolerates a cool and sometimes slightly damp environment. It suits poorly anything
that takes damage from moisture — important papers, photos, electronics — and it's outright wrong for
the moisture-sensitive if the basement is raw.

Think of the locker as a complement to the flat, not a second flat. What you use often
belongs upstairs, near where it's used; the locker is for what you need but seldom. Sort with that
split in mind and many decisions get easy: does this belong to my daily life, or is it something I
reach for a couple of times a year? The first lives upstairs, the second down below.

And be honest about the "keep just in case" boxes. A small locker is precisely the place where old
electronics and half-broken gadgets gather, waiting for a "maybe" that never comes. The same rule as
in the garage applies: if a year passes without your opening the box, the box has given its answer. A
small space simply can't afford to house futures that never arrive.

The small locker has a hidden upside, too: it's quick to keep in order precisely because it's small. A
full cleanup is an afternoon, not a weekend, and the weekly round here is a two-minute affair — a glance
from the doorway is often enough to see if anything's drifted. Where the garage needs a method so it
won't overwhelm you, the locker mostly just needs you to actually go down there now and then. Set a
reminder to look in at every seasonal swap, the same rhythm as the tyres, and it never has time to
decay. A small space that's seen regularly is one of the easiest rooms in your whole life to keep in
order — it's only the out-of-sight, unseen locker that becomes a heap. Visit it, and it stays a room;
ignore it, and it reverts to a pile. That single habit of looking in matters more than any shelf you buy.

> **Weekend Project: empty and rebuild the locker**
> 1. Empty the whole locker onto a clear surface — an afternoon is enough for most basement units.
> 2. Sort hard: keep only what you use and can't store in the flat.
> 3. Put in a slim shelf and stackable totes so you build upward instead of outward.
> 4. Label every tote, ideally clear, so the whole locker is readable from the door.
> 5. Check that nothing stands in a common passage and nothing flammable is inside.

> **Safety**
> Never store petrol, propane, or other flammables in a basement locker — it doesn't belong in a
> residential building, and it's often against the building's rules. Keep shared passages completely
> clear; they're an escape route in a fire. Check what your building allows before storing anything
> unusual.
