# The Rules That Hold the Line

The garage is cleared, the things have their places, and everything is labeled. And yet — if
nothing more is done — the mess comes back. Not in a day, but in half a year. This chapter is
about the **Standardize** step: the few simple rules that make order hold instead of slowly
crumbling.

A rule here isn't some dreary edict. A good rule is a decision you make once so you don't have to
make it a hundred more times.

## The problem: order is a habit, not an event

The most common mistake after a successful cleanup is believing you're done. You're not done — you've
created a good starting point. A garage is a room in use, and everything in use drifts toward disorder
unless something holds the line. Tools come out, boxes come in, things get bought. Each of those small
events nudges the garage a little toward chaos.

Without rules, every nudge gets handled with a fresh decision: "where do I put this, I'm in a hurry?"
And the in-a-hurry decision is almost always "somewhere around here," which is exactly how the mess was
built the first time. You can't stop life from nudging the garage. But you can decide in advance how the
nudges get handled, so they don't demand a decision every time.

## The method here: four rules that make the right thing easiest

You don't need many rules. Four go a long way, and they work because they make the right move the easy
move — not because they demand discipline.

**Back right away.** A tool that came out goes back when you're done, not "later." "Later" is where order
goes to die. It takes three seconds now and ten minutes later.

**One in, one out.** When a new thing comes into the garage, an old one leaves. Buy a new drill driver,
and the old one goes. That keeps the quantity constant instead of letting it creep upward year after
year.

**Empty packaging out the same day.** The cardboard, plastic, and foam a new thing arrived in go straight
out to the recycling zone — not onto the floor "until I make a trip." Empty packaging is the single most
common seed of a new pile.

**Everything new earns a place before it's allowed in.** This is the most important rule. A thing without
a fixed place isn't allowed to live in the garage. If you can't find a place for it, that's a sign it
should either leave or that something else should leave to make room.

Notice that none of these rules asks you to become a different person. They simply move the easy move to
the right side: with the rules, the easiest thing is the correct thing, and so you do the correct thing.

> **Workshop Rule**
> "Later" is where order goes to die. Back right away; empty packaging out the same day.

## An example: the rule that cleared the bench

A man named Paul had a recurring problem: the workbench turned into a pile within a week, no matter how
often he cleared it. He introduced a single rule — the bench is empty when I leave the garage — and a
single habit: the last two minutes, I put back whatever I took out.

The bench stopped becoming a pile. Not because he cleaned more, but because he never again let the pile
begin. An empty bench in the evening meant an empty bench in the morning, and an empty bench is so much
easier to keep empty than a half-full one. The rule did the hard work by never letting the hard
work arise in the first place.

## Rules the whole house shares

A rule only you know about isn't a rule — it's a wish. If the garage is used by several people in the
household, the rules have to be known and shared, or the others will cheerfully undo the order you keep.
That doesn't mean nagging. It means the rules should be visible and simple enough that everyone can follow
them without thinking.

That's why you hang the rules where they can be seen, and why they're phrased as actions rather than
scoldings. "Empty packaging out the same day" is something anyone can do; "keep things tidy" is just a
sigh. The more a rule points to a concrete action, the easier it is to share — and a garage the whole
house can run holds far better than one that hangs on a single person.

Be generous about deleting, too. If you have a rule nobody follows, ask whether it's the people or the
rule that's wrong. Often the rule is too fiddly, or a better storage place solves the problem with no
rule at all. The best rule is the one you can remove because the system already does the right thing by
itself.

There's also a reason the rules work that's worth understanding, because then you trust them. A rule
doesn't save you effort in the moment — hanging the tool back takes the same time either way. What it
saves is the *decision.* Without a rule you have to weigh, every time, whether to put the thing back now
or later, and that little weighing, a hundred times over, is what wears you down and eventually tips the
wrong way. With a rule the question is answered once and for all, and you stop negotiating with yourself.

Don't expect the rules to stick instantly, either. A new habit takes a few weeks to set, and at first
you'll have to remind yourself. The card by the door is there for exactly that — it does the work until
your hand has learned the move on its own. One day you'll notice you put the tool back without deciding
to, the way you lock the front door without thinking, and then the rule has become a habit, and the
habit is what truly holds.

> **Workshop Rule**
> A good rule is a decision you make once, so you don't have to make it a hundred times.

> **Weekend Project: set the house's four rules**
> 1. Write the four rules on a card: back right away, one in one out, empty packaging out, place first.
> 2. Post the card visibly — ideally by the door, beside the card with the five steps.
> 3. Talk them through once with everyone who uses the garage, so they apply to the whole house.
> 4. Add one rule of your own if you have a recurring problem (e.g. "bench empty by evening").
> 5. Live with them for a week and delete any that isn't followed — fewer rules kept beats more forgotten.

## Takeaways

- **Standardize** is the few rules that keep order after the big cleanup.
- Order is a habit, not an event — every use of the garage nudges it toward disorder.
- Four rules suffice: back right away, one in one out, empty packaging out, place first.
- A good rule needs no discipline; it just makes the right thing the easiest move.
- "Later" is where order dies. Never let the pile begin, and you spare yourself the work of tearing it down.
