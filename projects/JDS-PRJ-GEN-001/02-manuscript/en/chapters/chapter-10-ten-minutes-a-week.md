# Ten Minutes a Week

This is the last step, and the easiest to write but the hardest to keep: **Sustain**. It isn't
about doing something big. It's about doing something small, often. Ten minutes a week is the
difference between a garage that was in order in March and one that's still in order in November.

All the work in the earlier chapters built the order once. This chapter keeps it alive for good —
with an effort so small you'll barely notice it.

## The problem: order leaks slowly

Even with good zones, wall storage, and rules, small deviations will creep in. A thing that didn't
make it back to its place. A box that got left standing. A new gadget with no home yet. Each one is
trivial. But they accumulate, quietly, week by week, exactly as they did the first time.

The danger is that you don't notice the leak until it's big. A single misplaced thing is invisible,
but twenty make the garage messy again — and then it feels as if the whole job was wasted. It wasn't.
All that was missing was a small, regular reset before the deviations had time to become a pile.

The fix isn't more discipline or another big cleanup. The fix is to make the reset so small and so
regular that it never grows large.

## The method here: a short round, the same time each week

**Sustain** is a routine, not an effort. It has three parts.

**Ten minutes, a fixed time.** Pick a moment you're at the garage anyway — Sunday evening, maybe, or
after the weekly shop — and give ten minutes to a reset round. A fixed time makes it a habit instead of
a decision you have to summon the energy for.

**Just three things.** The round has only three jobs: put back what's landed in the wrong place, take
out what's bound for recycling, and wipe the bench. Nothing more. You don't hunt for problems and you
don't deep-clean — you only reset to the state you already built.

**Note, don't fix.** If you spot a bigger problem — a shelf starting to overflow, a thing with no place
— write it down, but don't solve it now. Big things belong in the twice-a-year pass that the next
chapter covers. The weekly round must stay ten minutes, or you'll stop doing it.

That's the whole secret to keeping a garage in order: not a heroic effort twice a year, but a trivial
effort every week. The person who cleans big and rarely always has a messy garage between cleanups. The
person who resets small and often never does.

> **Workshop Rule**
> A little, often, beats a lot, rarely. Ten minutes a week holds what would otherwise take a whole weekend.

## An example: the Sunday round that disappeared

A woman named Anna decided to test the weekly round for a month, mostly to prove it wouldn't last.
Sunday evening, ten minutes, three things. The first few times it felt like a chore. After a month she
noticed something unexpected: the round no longer took ten minutes, but four. There was almost nothing to
reset, because nothing had time to go wrong between rounds.

That's how a routine matures. At first it repairs damage; then it prevents it, and so it shrinks on its
own. The garage stayed in order not through a big effort, but through one so small she eventually barely
thought about it.

## Hook the round onto something you already do

The hardest part of a new habit is remembering it. The trick is not to rely on memory, but to hook the
round onto something you already do without thinking. If you take out the trash on Sundays, do the round
right after. If you wash the car once a month, finish with a round. When the new habit rides along with an
old one, you never have to remember it separately — it comes along for the ride.

It also helps to set the bar absurdly low. Ten minutes sounds like nothing, but a tired Sunday can make
even ten minutes feel like too much. So tell yourself: I'll just do one thing — put back what's out of
place. Almost always you'll get going and do the other two as well, because you're already moving. And if
you only do the one thing, that's still infinitely better than nothing. A small round that happens beats a
big one that's postponed.

Don't be hard on yourself if a week gets skipped, either. A missed round isn't a failure — it's a missed
round. The difference from before is that a single week no longer builds a pile, because the system carries
you. Take the next round as planned, and everything's in order again.

And make it pleasant: music or a podcast, a coffee, the door open. Ten minutes in a room you're proud
of is no hardship — you're tending something that works, not fighting a pile, which is exactly why this
habit holds where big cleanups fail. Think of it like brushing your teeth: trivial and daily, and it
spares you the equivalent of the dentist's chair. Keep it small enough that it never feels worth
skipping — the round you actually do is the only one that counts.

> **Workshop Rule**
> The weekly round resets, it doesn't renovate. Big problems are noted now and solved in spring and fall.

> **Weekend Project: schedule the round**
> 1. Pick a fixed time each week when you're home and near the garage.
> 2. Write "reset, 10 min" wherever you keep your other reminders.
> 3. Do the first round now: back in place, out to recycling, wipe the bench.
> 4. Hang a small note in the garage for "to deal with later" — the big things you spot.
> 5. Do the round three weeks running. By then it's set, and you can trust it to carry.
