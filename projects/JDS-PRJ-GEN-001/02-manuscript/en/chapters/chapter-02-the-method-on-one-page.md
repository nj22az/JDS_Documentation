# The Method on One Page

[[CARD step=The method (5S) | time=20 minutes | task=Learn the five steps and pin them up where you'll see them | need=The book and a bit of wall]]

There are entire books about tidying up. This method fits on one page, and that's on purpose. A
garage doesn't get cleared with a philosophy — it gets cleared with five steps you do in order,
one weekend at a time, until it's done.

Read this chapter slowly, once. Then come back to it whenever you like, because it's the map of
everything that follows.

## The problem: good advice you can't actually follow

Most tidying advice doesn't fail because it's wrong. It fails because it's too big. "Organise
the garage" isn't a task — it's a whole project disguised as a sentence. Your brain sees the
size of it, gets tired in advance, and pulls the door back down.

The fix is to break the big thing into steps that each have a clear beginning and a clear end.
You should never stand in the garage wondering "what now." You should always know which of five
steps you're on, and what the next physical move is.

There's a second trap, too: jumping straight to the fun part. It's tempting to start by buying
a sleek pegboard and nice bins, because it feels like progress. But storage you buy before you've
sorted is just expensive packaging around things you should have thrown out. If your first
instinct is to order a slick storage system, pause — that's the most common, and most expensive,
mistake there is. The method keeps you out of both traps: the too-big task, and doing things in
the wrong order.

## The method here: five steps, in this order

The method has five steps, and the order isn't optional. You can't give a thing a place before
you've decided it stays. You can't keep a system alive before the system exists. The steps build
on one another. Engineers call this method **5S**, after five words that — handily — all start
with the same letter.

### 1. Sort

First you decide what stays. Every item lands in one of three piles: keep, lose, or relocate to
another room. Nothing else happens in this step — you build no shelves and buy nothing. You make
decisions. This is **Sort**, and it's the heaviest step emotionally, because this is where
"maybe" meets reality.

### 2. Set in Order

Once you know what stays, you give each thing a fixed place. Like lives with like, in **zones** —
the car in one, tools in another, the garden in a third. Most of it goes up off the floor, onto
shelves and a pegboard. The floor is for working on, not storing on. This is **Set in Order**.

### 3. Shine

Now, and only now, you clean. You sweep the floor, wipe the bench, and see what the room actually
looks like empty. A clean floor and a clean bench should become the normal state, not something
you achieve once a year. This is **Shine**, and it's quick — if the first two steps are done, it
goes fast.

### 4. Standardise

A system only you understand collapses the moment you're in a hurry. So you set a few simple
rules that make order the default: the tool goes back right away, the empty box goes out the same
evening, every new thing earns a place before it's allowed in. This is **Standardise** — you make
it easy to do the right thing.

### 5. Sustain

Finally, you keep the system alive. Not with big efforts, but with a short routine: ten minutes a
week and two longer passes a year, spring and autumn. This is **Sustain**, and it's the difference
between a garage that was clean in March and a garage that's still clean in November.

## How long does it take, and where do you start?

You always start with **Sort**, no matter how the garage looks. It can feel wrong — you'd love to
hang a shelf first, because that's visible and satisfying. But a shelf you put up before sorting
just fills with the same things you should have got rid of. First you decide what stays. Then,
and only then, you decide where it lives.

How long? Figure roughly one weekend per step — about five weekends for an ordinary one-car
garage. If yours is bigger or worse, **Sort** might take two; it's the heavy step and deserves
the time. The last three steps go faster than you'd think, because once the sorting is done, most
of the work is already behind you.

And you don't have to do the whole garage at once. It's perfectly fine to take one wall or one
zone through all five steps, then the next. What matters isn't the size of the bite — it's that
you take each bite through the steps in order.

One more thing: the steps are a sequence, but they're also a loop. When you reach **Sustain** and
a season has passed, you start again in miniature — a quick **Sort** each spring and autumn keeps
the garage from refilling. You don't go through the five steps once in a lifetime. You go through
them in earnest once, and then in miniature twice a year.

## An example: one step a weekend beats a hero weekend

Two neighbours tackled their garages the same month. The first took a whole weekend off, dragged
everything out onto the driveway, and tried to do it all at once. By Sunday night he was wrecked,
half the pile was still there, and the rain came. The second did one step per weekend: a weekend
to **Sort**, a weekend to **Set in Order**, and so on. After five weekends his garage was done —
and it's still done, because he had a system, not just a clean surface.

The slow one won. He almost always does, because a garage isn't a race — it's a build. The man
who emptied everything at once created a bigger problem inside the old one: a mountain on the
driveway, no system to put things back into, and a deadline when the clouds opened. He had to
carry it all back in before he'd decided anything — so it landed at random, exactly as before.

> **Workshop Rule**
> One step a weekend beats a hero weekend. The garage is a build, not a race.

> **Weekend Project: put the five steps where you'll see them**
> 1. Write the five words on a card: Sort, Set in Order, Shine, Standardise, Sustain.
> 2. Tape the card inside the garage door, at eye level.
> 3. Mark the step you're on right now. For most people, that's Sort.
> 4. Every time you walk in, you'll know in a second where you are in the build.

> **Workshop Rule**
> Buy nothing until after step one. You don't know what you need to store until you know what
> gets to stay.

## What you need to begin

The method needs no expensive purchases — that's the whole point. Before your first weekend you
need only a few simple things, and you probably have most of them already: bin bags and boxes
to sort into (label them with a marker right away), a wide marker for writing dates and contents,
a couple of tarps for laying things out on a wet driveway, work gloves and good light, and a
clear surface to sort on — half the garage floor, the driveway, or the carport.

Notice what's *not* on the list: shelves, hooks, bin systems, pegboards. All of that comes in
step two, **Set in Order**, and by then you'll know exactly how much you need — not one shelf too
many, not one too few. Buying storage last instead of first is one of the biggest differences
between this method and an ordinary cleaning weekend, and it's often the one that saves the most
money.

With that, you're ready. The next chapter is your first real weekend: you empty it out, and you
look.
