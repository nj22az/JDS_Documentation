# Spring and Fall

The weekly round keeps the garage on its feet, but it doesn't do everything. Twice a year the
garage needs a longer pass — and the seasons invite you to it themselves. Spring and fall are the
natural turning points, and they double neatly as decluttering checkpoints. This is still
**Sustain**, just in its larger form.

The nice part is that you don't have to invent these occasions. They're already in your calendar,
built into the shape of the year.

## The problem: some things only surface twice a year

Some disorder is the season's fault, not yours. In spring the winter tires come off and the garden
gear comes out. In fall it's the reverse. Cushions, the grill, the hose, the patio furniture go in;
scrapers, salt, the snow shovel come out. At each of these swaps you stir the garage anyway — and
right then, things surface that you haven't seen since the last swap.

The problem is that if you just swap without paying attention, you shuffle junk around instead of
getting rid of it. The broken patio chair goes in for the winter and out in spring, year after year,
without anyone ever deciding it should go. The seasonal swap becomes a carousel that keeps alive the
very things that should have left the garage long ago.

The fix is to couple the swap you do anyway with a short round of the method's first step, **Sort**.

## The method here: swap, and clear while you're at it

Twice a year, alongside the tire and garden swap, you do an extended pass. It has three parts.

**Swap the seasonal zone.** Do the usual: bring forward what the season needs and stow what it
doesn't. The seasonal zone — high and far back, from chapter six — is the one you turn over now.

**Sort while the things are already in your hands.** This is the whole point. For every item you're
lifting anyway, ask one quick question: did I use this at all last season? The grill you never lit,
the furniture you never sat in, the tool you never touched — those are candidates for "lose." You've
already got the thing in your hand; the decision costs nothing extra.

**Go through the dangerous stuff.** Take the chance to check the hazard corner from chapter five.
Paint that's dried, petrol that's gone stale, spray cans that are spent — gather them up and run them
to the hazardous-waste drop-off in the same trip as everything else that's leaving. The twice-a-year
rhythm is exactly right for keeping that pile small.

Two passes a year, each maybe half a day, are enough to keep the garage from sliding back. Together
with the weekly round they make a complete maintenance plan: a little every week, a little more every
half-year, never a whole lost weekend again.

> **Workshop Rule**
> If you're carrying a thing out at the seasonal swap anyway — ask whether it deserves to be carried
> back in.

## An example: the patio chair on a six-year carousel

A man named Greg realized, during a fall pass, that he'd carried the same broken patio chair in and
out of the garage every spring and fall for six years. Twelve times he'd lifted it, and not once had
anyone sat in it. It had been broken from the start.

Once he saw the pattern, the decision took two seconds: the chair went to recycling that same day. It
wasn't the chair that was the problem, but that the seasonal swap had happened on autopilot, without a
single question. A short sort while he was already at it would have spared him eleven pointless lifts.

## Make the pass a fixed point

What separates a twice-a-year pass that actually happens from one that gets forgotten is tying it to
something unavoidable. The tire swap is perfect, because it has to be done anyway and it falls
naturally in spring and fall. Hook the pass to the tires, and you never have to decide when it's time —
the calendar and the weather decide for you.

Take the chance to do other maintenance in the same session, too, so the garage gets all its yearly
care at two moments instead of scattered and forgotten. In fall: check that the winter gear works
before you need it, and that the lighting holds up for the dark months ahead. In spring: look over the
garden machines and air the place out after winter. When the pass becomes the whole garage's recurring
checkup, and not just a declutter, it gains a clear purpose that makes it easy to stick to.

And always finish by booking the next one. With the next pass already in the calendar when you're done,
there's no occasion to forget it. Two fixed points a year, each tied to the tire swap, are enough that
the garage never again has time to decay between them.

One more habit makes the pass easier: start it by reading the "to deal with later" note from the
weekly rounds. The big things you postponed collect there — the overflowing shelf, the zone that
doesn't work, the thing with no home — and the pass is where they finally get solved, while you have
the time and the things in hand. Small every week, big twice a year, and nothing falls through the
cracks.

> **Specs: storing tires right**
> - Keep them **cool, dry, and out of direct sun** — UV and ozone crack rubber.
> - **Off the bare slab** — set them on a shelf, a strip of cardboard, or hang them; concrete wicks
>   moisture into the tire.
> - **Unmounted tires stand upright; mounted tires (on rims) lie flat or hang.** Keep flat stacks to
>   four or fewer so the bottom ones don't deform.
> - Bag each in a contractor bag to slow drying out.

> **Safety**
> Heavy seasonal things live high and far back. Use a sturdy ladder, not a chair, when you bring tires
> and boxes down from height, and lift with your legs. Never carry a heavy load down stairs or over
> obstacles without a clear view ahead. And take stale petrol and dried paint to the hazardous-waste
> drop-off — never the household trash.

> **Weekend Project: tie clearing to the seasonal swap**
> 1. Next time you change tires or reset the garden, spend an extra half hour on it.
> 2. Ask of every item you lift: did I use this last season?
> 3. Put the "no" things in a lose pile and haul them out the same day.
> 4. Glance through the hazard corner and gather what's dried out or gone stale.
> 5. Write the next pass into the calendar right away — half a year out, at the next seasonal swap.
