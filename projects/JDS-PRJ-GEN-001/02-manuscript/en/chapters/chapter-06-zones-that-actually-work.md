# Zones That Actually Work

[[CARD step=Step 5 of 10 | time=One evening | task=Draw your five-zone map | need=Paper, pencil, a tape measure]]

Now the book turns. The first chapters cleared things out. From here you build — you give
everything that survived a fixed place. This is **Set in Order**, and it starts not with shelves
but with a map.

Decide *where* things belong before you drill. Hang the pegboard on the first free wall and
you'll find out a month later it's over the spot where the car door opens.

## The problem: things land wherever there's a gap

With no plan, chance rules. The garden gear ends up not where it's handy but wherever there was a
gap the day you carried it in. Tools spread across three walls because no wall ever became *the
tools' wall*. Nothing belongs anywhere, so everything wanders everywhere — and you cross the
whole garage for one small job, fetching the rake from one corner, gloves from another, the bag
by the door. You pay those extra steps every day.

The fix is to stop thinking in shelves and think in zones: an area that owns one kind of task,
with everything for that task living together.

## The method here: five zones, placed around the car

Most garages split into five zones. Merge them if space is tight, but these five cover almost
everything.

**Car zone.** Where the car parks, plus what serves it — washer fluid, scraper, jumper cables.
Keep it lean; everything that isn't the car comes off the floor so the car fits.

**Tool zone.** Hand tools, power tools, the bench. Put it under the best light, because this is
where you actually work.

**Garden zone.** Rakes, spades, hose, pots. Park it by the door to the garden so you don't drag a
muddy spade through the whole space.

**Seasonal zone.** Off-season tyres, holiday boxes, cushions. Send it to the hard-to-reach
spots — high and far back — because you touch it twice a year.

**Recycling zone.** A small station by the overhead door for what's leaving: cardboard, returns,
scrap, hazardous waste awaiting a drop-off run. Without it, "to discard" becomes a pile anywhere.

The rule that ties it together: **put each zone where your body uses it.** Often-used, easy to
reach; rarely-used, out of the way. A zone in the wrong place nags you daily; a zone in the right
place you stop noticing.

> **Workshop Rule**
> Plan the car in first. Everything else is built around the clearances it needs.

> **Specs: clearances that keep the car usable**
> - A single bay is about **3.5 m (12 ft)** wide; a two-car garage runs roughly **6 × 6 m
>   (20 × 20 ft)**. A car is about **1.8–2 m** wide — less spare room than it feels like.
> - Leave **75 cm (30 in) on the driver's side** to open the door and walk through; ~30 cm is
>   enough on the far side.
> - Keep a **1 m (3 ft) clear aisle** to the exit door — it's your walkway and your fire escape.
> - Pull forward enough to leave a **1–1.2 m (3–4 ft) work strip** behind the car (a tennis ball on
>   a string from the ceiling marks the stopping point).
> - Wall storage over the bonnet eats depth: keep shelves there **shallow (≤ 30 cm)** or the bumper
>   meets them.

## An example: three steps for a broom

Lena understood zones the day she thought about her broom. It hung at the back, behind the bikes.
Every time she swept the driveway she moved two bikes, grabbed the broom, and moved the bikes
back — three moves to sweep. She put it on a hook just inside the door, where she used it. Next
time: two seconds. Small, and exactly the point. A zone in the right place erases a friction you'd
stopped noticing. Twenty of those are the difference between a garage that fights you and one that
helps.

## Fit the zones to your garage

The five are a template, not a law. No car? Drop the car zone. Too small for five? Merge — car and
tools share a wall, seasonal and recycling share a corner. What matters is that every item belongs
to a zone, and the zone sits where the task happens. Big garage or real workshop? Split a zone:
hand tools at the bench, machines a few steps away.

Then test the map before you commit. Walk a normal job in your head: washing the car — where's the
bucket, the sponge, the soap? Three corners means a broken zone. Run it for mowing, for a bike
repair, for the recycling run. The chores that feel awkward tell you which zones to pull together.
Let your own habits draw the map.

> **Weekend Project: draw the zone map**
> 1. Sketch the garage from above — overhead door, side door, windows, and the parked car's footprint.
> 2. Mark the five zones, keeping the clearances above around the car.
> 3. Often-used near the doors and the light; rarely-used high and far back.
> 4. Walk it with the sketch and sanity-check each zone where it sits.
> 5. Keep the map — next chapter you put up the walls, following this plan.

> **Safety**
> Never place the tool zone so you reach over the bonnet or a stack to get to it. Keep the 1 m exit
> aisle clear at all times — zones never block the path to the door.
