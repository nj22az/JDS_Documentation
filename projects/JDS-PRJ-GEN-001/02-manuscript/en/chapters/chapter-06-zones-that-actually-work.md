# Zones That Actually Work

Now the book turns. The first chapters were about clearing out — deciding what stays. From here
on you build: you give everything that survived the sort a fixed place. This is the **Set in
Order** step, and it doesn't begin with shelves or hooks. It begins with a map.

Before you drill a single screw into the wall, you decide *where* things belong. Otherwise you
hang the pegboard on the first free wall and discover a month later that it's in the wrong spot.

## The problem: things land wherever there's room

In a garage without a plan, everything is ruled by chance. The garden gear doesn't end up where
it's handiest — it ends up wherever there happened to be a gap the day you carried it in. The
tools spread across three walls because no place ever became *the tools' place.* And because
nothing belongs anywhere, everything is free to wander everywhere.

The result is familiar: you walk back and forth across the whole garage for one simple task,
because what you need is scattered. You fetch the rake from one corner, the gloves from another,
and the sack by the door. Each step costs you a few extra paces, and you take those paces again
and again, every day.

The fix is to stop thinking in shelves and start thinking in zones. A zone is an area that belongs
to one kind of task, where everything for that task lives together.

## The method here: divide the garage into five zones

Most garages divide naturally into five zones. You don't need all of them, and you can merge them
when space is tight, but the five cover almost everything that lives in a garage.

**The car zone.** The spot where the car parks, plus what belongs to the car: washer fluid, an ice
scraper, jumper cables, a bucket. Keep this zone lean — most of what isn't the car should be off the
floor so the car fits.

**The tool zone.** Hand tools, power tools, and a workbench if you have one. Put it near a window or
good light, because this is where you actually work and need to see.

**The garden zone.** Rakes, spades, hose, pots, the mower's accessories. It belongs near the door
you use to get out to the garden — you don't want to carry a muddy spade through the whole garage.

**The seasonal zone.** Summer tires in winter and winter tires in summer, holiday boxes, patio
cushions — the things used only half the year. Let it take the spots that are hardest to reach, up
high or far back, since you touch it rarely.

**The recycling zone.** A small station near the door for what's heading out: cardboard, returns,
scrap metal, hazardous waste waiting for a trip to the drop-off. Without such a place, "to be
discarded" becomes a pile anywhere.

The rule that ties it together: **put each zone where your body actually uses it.** What you do
often should be easiest to reach. What you do rarely can take the awkward spots. A zone in the
wrong place feels wrong every day; a zone in the right place is one you stop thinking about.

> **Workshop Rule**
> What you use often should be easiest to reach. What you use rarely can take the hard spots.

## An example: three steps for a broom

A reader named Lena finally understood zones when she thought about her broom. It hung at the very
back of the garage, behind the bikes. Every time she wanted to sweep the driveway she had to move
two bikes, fetch the broom, and move the bikes back. Three extra moves to sweep.

She moved the broom to a hook just inside the door — in the exit-and-recycling zone, where it was
actually used. Next time it took two seconds. It was no big deal, and that was exactly the point: a
zone in the right place removes small frictions you'd barely noticed you were living with. Twenty of
those small frictions are the difference between a garage that fights you and one that helps.

## Fit the zones to your garage

The five zones are a template, not a law. Your garage might not have a car at all, or it might be so
small that five separate zones won't fit. Then you merge. The car and tool zones can share a wall,
and the seasonal and recycling zones can share a corner. What matters isn't that you have exactly
five boxes, but that every item belongs to a zone and that the zone sits where the task is done.

If instead you have a large garage or a combined workshop, a zone may need splitting. Many people
find the tool zone wants to become two: one for hand tools at the bench, and one for the big power
tools and machines a little further off. Let need decide. A zone should be as big as its task
demands — no bigger, no smaller.

And think about the flow between zones. Things you often use together should sit near each other.
Car care wants to be near the car; cords and lights near the bench. When zones that belong together
sit side by side, you stop walking needlessly between the ends of the garage — which is the whole
reason zones exist.

One simple way to test your map is to walk through a normal task in your head before you commit. Say
you're going to wash the car: where's the bucket, where's the sponge, where's the soap? If those
three things live in three different corners, you've found a zone that doesn't work. Run the same
thought for mowing the lawn, for fixing a bike, for taking out the recycling. The chores that feel
awkward tell you exactly which zones need to move closer together. You don't have to guess — let your
own habits draw the map for you.

> **Weekend Project: draw the zone map**
> 1. Take a sheet of paper and sketch the garage from above, with the door and any windows and side
>    doors.
> 2. Mark out the five zones: car, tools, garden, seasonal, recycling.
> 3. Put often-used zones near entrances and good light; rarely-used zones up high and far back.
> 4. Walk the garage with the map and check: does each zone make sense where it sits?
> 5. Keep the map — next chapter you hang things up, and you'll follow this plan.

> **Safety**
> Never lay out the car zone so you have to climb or stretch over something heavy to reach the
> tools. And keep a clear path through the garage — at least wide enough to carry something large,
> or to get out fast in a fire. Zones must never block the way to the door.

## Takeaways

- **Set in Order** begins with a map, not with shelves — decide where things belong before you drill.
- Think in zones, not loose shelves: one area per kind of task, everything for that task together.
- Most garages divide into five zones: car, tools, garden, seasonal, and recycling.
- Put each zone where your body uses it — often-used near and lit, rarely-used high and far.
- A zone in the right place removes small daily frictions you'd barely noticed living with.
