# When You Inherit a Workshop

[[CARD step=Special case | time=Take your time | task=Make a safe first pass through what you inherited | need=Gloves, patience, no rush]]

Some garages you don't clear for yourself, but for someone who's gone. When a parent or relative
dies, it often falls to you to deal with a whole workshop — a lifetime of tools, parts, and things,
gathered by someone else, with memories in every drawer. This is the method's hardest case, and it's
as much an emotional task as a practical one.

It's the workshop's version of a death cleanout: you go through what someone spent a life gathering.
You should do it with respect, but also with a plan — otherwise the workshop stands untouched for
years because starting feels too heavy.

## The problem: memories make every decision heavy

In your own garage, "did I use it last year" is an easy question. In an inherited workshop, nothing is
easy, because the things carry someone else's life. Your grandfather's hand plane isn't just a plane —
it's his hands, his smell, his Saturday mornings in the shop. Putting it in a "lose" pile feels like
throwing away a piece of him.

So many people freeze. Either nothing gets touched, and the workshop stands like a museum no one has the
heart to visit, or it all goes too fast in a rush of grief and practical pressure, and you regret later
what you let go. Both extremes come from the same thing: trying to make the emotional decisions and the
practical ones at the same time.

The fix is to separate them. You don't sort everything by feeling, and you don't sort everything by use.
You first split into piles where only one of the piles asks anything of your heart.

## The method here: separate the memory from the mass

You still run the five steps, but **Sort** takes a special form here, shaped for the fact that another
person's life lies in front of you.

**Start with the easy, not the hard.** Consumables, duplicates, broken things, and obvious junk carry no
memories — they're screws, old paint, rusty scraps. Clear those first. It removes a large mass at no
emotional cost, and it makes the rest manageable to see.

**Put the charged things in a pile of their own — "to choose from."** Anything that carries a memory goes
to one side, gathered, with no decision attached. You shouldn't have to rule on your grandfather's plane
the same day you carry out old waste oil. The memory things get their own pile and their own calmer hour.

**Choose a few to keep — with meaning.** From the charged pile, pick a small number of things to truly
treasure, ideally ones you can use or display. A plane on a shelf in your own garage honours him more than
twenty tools buried in a box you never open. Choosing isn't betraying the memory — it's giving the memory
a place where it shows.

**Let the good things go on.** Good tools want to work. What you don't keep can be sold or given away, and
a craftsman who receives his tools and uses them carries them forward. Tools alive in someone else's hands
are often a more beautiful continuation than a box in an attic.

> **Workshop Rule**
> One thing on a shelf, seen and used, honours the memory more than twenty buried in a box.

## An example: three tools and a photograph

A woman named Helen inherited her father's crammed workshop and put it off for two years — starting felt
impossible. When she finally began, she started with the easy: screws, scraps, broken things. That emptied
half the shop without a single hard feeling.

The charged things she set in a pile of their own. From it she chose three tools she remembered him using,
and hung them on her own wall. The rest of the good tools she gave to a local woodworking guild, and took a
photograph of the whole set before they went on. She didn't keep everything, but she didn't lose him. She
kept what meant something and let the rest do good — and the photo carried the memory of the mass without
the mass having to stay.

## Give yourself time, but set a frame

The inherited workshop is the one case in this book where I'll say: don't hurry. Grief has its own pace,
and decisions made in the first heavy weeks are often regretted. It's entirely fine to let the memory pile
sit a while, as long as you've handled the practical and the dangerous first.

But time without a frame slips into years of putting off, and then the workshop stands as a museum that
does no one any good. So set a soft boundary: pick a day, a few months out, when you'll come back to the
memory pile in calm. Bring someone who also knew the person, if you can — remembering together is both
easier and more beautiful, and another person helps you see what truly matters and what's merely hard to
release.

And remember you don't have to carry the whole collection's weight. That someone gathered things over a
lifetime doesn't mean you must steward all of it. You honour her by keeping what holds her memory — not by
letting her entire workshop weigh on you. Choose with the heart, release with a clear conscience, and let
the tools live on with someone who'll use them.

> **Inherited & Hard**
> If the task feels too big to carry alone, ask for help — it's not weakness, it's wisdom. A friend or
> relative who stands a little further back can lift the heavy parts, literally and emotionally, and keep
> you moving without forcing decisions. The hardest memory things can wait; only the practical and the
> dangerous truly press.

> **Safety**
> Older workshops often hide dangerous leftovers: old petrol and solvents, lead batteries, unknown
> chemicals in unlabelled cans, sometimes asbestos in very old materials. Treat unlabelled and unknown things
> as hazardous waste, lift heavy things with your legs, and get help for truly heavy machines. Take the
> dangerous things to the hazardous-waste drop-off, never the household waste.

> **Weekend Project: the first pass in an inherited workshop**
> 1. Set aside a day, and bring someone to help if you can — it's easier together.
> 2. Clear only the memory-free things first: screws, consumables, duplicates, broken stuff.
> 3. Put everything charged in a pile of its own, "to choose from," with no decisions today.
> 4. Photograph the workshop as it was, and individual things you want to remember but not keep.
> 5. Pick a later, calm day for the memory pile. It must not be rushed.
