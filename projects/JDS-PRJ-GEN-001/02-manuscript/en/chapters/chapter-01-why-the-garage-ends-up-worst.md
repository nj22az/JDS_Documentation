# Why the Garage Always Ends Up Worst

[[CARD step=1 of 10 | time=One morning | task=See the garage clearly — before you touch a thing | need=A notepad and a pen]]

You've cleaned the kitchen. You've sorted the wardrobe — maybe even flipped the whole bookshelf
one rainy Sunday in January. But the garage? The garage stands there, untouched, door down,
quietly growing a pile you've stopped really seeing.

You're not alone, and you're not lazy. The garage ends up worst for one simple reason, and
once you understand the reason, the rest of this book gets a great deal easier.

## The problem: the garage is the house's overflow valve

Think about where things go in a home. Whatever has a fixed place stays in its place.
Whatever doesn't has to land somewhere — so it drifts outward, away from the rooms where you
actually live and where guests can see. First into the hall, then the wardrobe, then the
basement, and finally the garage.

The garage is the last stop on that journey. It's the overflow valve: everything that didn't
fit anywhere else drains into it. The tyres you keep meaning to swap. The paint can that was
almost empty. The box from a move six years ago. The bike that's three sizes too small. Nobody
ever decided the garage would become a warehouse — it just happened, one item at a time, on
evenings when your hands were full and you only needed to set something down.

Three things make the garage especially brutal:

1. **Nobody sees it.** The door is closed to the world. What isn't seen never gets
   prioritised.
2. **The stuff is heavy and grimy.** Tools, oil, tyres, garden gear — not soft clothes you
   fold in a quarter of an hour.
3. **Everything is a "maybe."** "Maybe I'll need that bolt." "Maybe I'll fix it." The garage
   is full of futures that never arrived.

Here's the part that matters: the problem isn't that you have too few shelves or the wrong
bins. The problem is that there was never a *system* deciding where things belong. Without a
system, every object becomes a tiny crisis of decision, and we duck it by simply putting the
thing down. A thousand ducked decisions make a pile.

Notice the contrast with the rest of the house. In the kitchen, the forks live in the drawer
and the plates in the cupboard. You never have to think, which is exactly why the kitchen holds
its order almost by itself. The garage has no such obvious home for anything. Where does the
sander live? Where do the ratchet straps belong? When the answer is "don't know," the answer
in practice becomes "anywhere" — and anywhere soon means everywhere.

## What the mess actually costs you

A cluttered garage feels free, because the price never shows up on a receipt. But it costs you,
every week, in four ways.

It costs **time.** You hunt for the right screwdriver, for the summer tyres, for the charger
that goes with the trimmer. Ten minutes of searching here and there adds up to hours over a
year — hours you'd rather have spent on the actual job.

It costs **money.** When you can't find something, you buy it again. Almost everyone who empties
a garage discovers duplicates they forgot they owned: a second impact driver, yet another roll
of masking tape, a third hammer. You already paid — you just didn't know where the thing was.

It costs **safety.** Things stacked high and loose fall. Cords and clutter on the floor trip
you. Flammable liquids scattered around are a real hazard in the middle of your house.

And it costs you **the car.** The most common thing in a garage often isn't a car — it's
everything else, while the car sits outside scraping ice off the windshield on winter mornings.
The garage was built to protect the most expensive thing you park, and the clutter has crowded
out its whole purpose.

None of these costs is dramatic on its own. Together they're heavy, and you pay them every day
until the system is in place.

## The method here: stop cleaning, start building a system

The most common mistake is to walk into the garage one Saturday with a bin bag and just
"start cleaning." You shuffle things around, run out of steam by lunch, and on Sunday it looks
much the same. That's not your failing — you attacked the symptom, not the cause.

The rest of this book rests on a single idea: **you don't clean the garage once; you build a
system that keeps the order for you.** The system has five steps — the method professionals
call **5S** — and you'll meet them throughout the book:

- **Sort** — decide what stays, what goes, and what belongs somewhere else.
- **Set in Order** — give everything that stays a fixed place: zones, shelves, a pegboard.
- **Shine** — make a clean floor and bench the normal state, not a yearly achievement.
- **Standardise** — set a few simple rules so order becomes the default.
- **Sustain** — keep the system alive with a short routine, ten minutes a week.

Don't worry about memorizing them. Just notice that four of the five steps are about everything
*after* you throw things away. Throwing out is the start, not the goal. The goal is a garage
that helps you.

The difference between cleaning and building a system is the difference between a surface and a
habit. A cleaned surface lasts until the next evening your hands are full. A system lasts because
it decides, for you, where the thing goes — and then the right move is also the easiest one. This
whole book is about making the right move the easy move, so that order doesn't demand discipline;
it just follows from how the garage is built.

## Good news: this is fixable

I'll tell you when it clicked for me. I'd spent twenty years keeping other people's engine rooms
in order — and I still couldn't park in my own garage. One Saturday I stopped shuffling things
around and just looked, and I found three moving boxes I hadn't opened since the day we moved in.
Six years. I had not needed one single thing inside them.

That told me everything. It wasn't the stuff; it was that nothing had a place, so everything
landed wherever it fell. The boxes weren't the disease — the missing system was, the same thing
I'd have written up as a finding on any ship.

And that's genuinely good news, because it applies to you too. If the mess comes from a missing
system rather than from your being a messy person, it can be solved — systems can be built. You
don't have to change your personality. You decide where things belong, once, and then let that
order run itself.

> **Workshop Rule**
> If you didn't know you had it, you don't need it.

> **Weekend Project: look before you touch**
> This first project isn't about throwing anything out. It's about seeing.
> 1. Walk into the garage with a notepad and a pen — not a bin bag.
> 2. Write down the five biggest things you *know* shouldn't be in there.
> 3. Find one item — just one — that has served no purpose in a full year.
> 4. Set it by the door. You don't have to decide yet. You've started seeing again.

> **Safety**
> Before you move anything heavy: garages are often badly lit and dotted with slick patches of
> oil or meltwater. Turn on every light you have, and wipe up wet spots on the floor before you
> carry anything. Order starts with not slipping.
