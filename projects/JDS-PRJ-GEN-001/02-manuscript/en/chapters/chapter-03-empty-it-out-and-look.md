# Empty It Out and Look

Now the real work begins, and it starts with a move that feels backward: you're going to take
things *out* of the garage before you decide what leaves. You're going to empty it, and you're
going to look.

This is your first weekend in the **Sort** step. Before you can choose what stays, you have to
see what you actually have — all of it, gathered together, in daylight.

## The problem: you can't see your own garage anymore

When something has sat in the same spot for long enough, you stop seeing it. The brain saves
effort by ignoring whatever never changes. The shelf in the corner becomes a gray smudge. The
box under the bench becomes part of the wall. You can walk past the same broken string trimmer
for three years without once thinking the thought: *that needs to go.*

This is why clearing in place works so badly. As long as the thing stays where it has always
stood, you make the same non-decision you've made a hundred times: you leave it. To see, you
have to break the pattern, and the simplest way is to carry the thing out into the light.

There's a second reason to empty out, too. As long as things stand packed together, they hide
one another. You can't see the shelf behind the bikes, or the floor under the boxes, or how
little you actually keep once it's all laid out in a row. A full garage lies about its own
capacity. Emptied, it tells the truth: this is how big the room is, and this is how much I really
have.

## The method here: out, in categories, in daylight

In this step you do three things, in this order.

**Carry it out.** Bring everything loose out onto the driveway or the floor in front of you — on
tarps if the ground is wet. Anything you can move should come out, so you see the room empty and
the things gathered. Heavy fixed items — the workbench, a car lift — can stay, but everything
loose comes forward.

**Group like with like.** Don't dump things in a jumble. Put everything of the same kind together:
all the tools in one place, all the paint in another, all the garden gear in a third. Now
something important happens — you see that you own seven tubes of caulk, four hammers, and half a
shelf of dried-up paint. Scattered through the garage, they hid. Gathered, they tell the truth.

**Look.** Stand still for a moment and take it in. This is the whole point of the chapter. You're
not moving anything back in yet, and you're not throwing anything out in a panic. You're letting
the quantity speak. Most people are honestly surprised — not by any single item, but by how much
of the same thing they've accumulated without knowing it.

Notice that you still haven't made a single throw-it-out decision. That comes in the next chapter.
This weekend is only about getting everything into the light and seeing it for what it is. Emptying
out and looking is the groundwork that makes the decisions easy.

## Three kinds of things that always hide

As you empty out, three kinds of things will surface that you'd half-forgotten existed. It helps
to recognize them in advance.

The first is **duplicates.** Spread across the garage they looked like one of each; gathered, they
turn out to be three sets of spark plugs, four tape measures, a heap of half-full screw jars.
Duplicates are the easiest things to clear later — you genuinely don't need four of everything —
but you only see them when they're lying side by side.

The second is **dead projects.** The broken chair you were going to glue. The bike you were going
to fix up for a kid who's now grown and moved out. The box of parts for something you no longer
remember. Dead projects take up a lot of room and a lot of guilt, which is exactly why they're
buried deepest.

The third is **things that were never yours.** A neighbor's ladder. A box from a friend's move. A
thing you're storing for someone who's forgotten it as thoroughly as you have. This is often the
easiest of all to let go of — it just needs to go back where it belongs.

You don't have to do anything about them yet. Just notice them as they appear, and let each get
its own little pile. Seeing them is half the job.

## An example: the bench that was a myth

Maria was certain her problem was too little bench space. She'd even put a new workbench in her
online cart. Then she emptied the garage one Saturday, category by category, and laid it all out
on the driveway.

That's when she saw it. The bench she already had wasn't too small — it was buried. Under the
piles lay nearly 2 metres (6 ft) of clear work surface she hadn't seen in years. She didn't need a new
bench. She needed to see the one she had. That's why you empty first: half the problem turns out
to be things hiding what you already own.

> **Workshop Rule**
> Gather everything of one kind in one spot. Seven of a thing you thought you had one of is the
> truth you need to see.

> **Weekend Project: empty one wall**
> You don't have to empty the whole garage in a weekend. Take one wall.
> 1. Choose the wall or corner that feels worst.
> 2. Carry everything loose out in front of the garage — on tarps if it's wet.
> 3. Lay it out in categories: tools together, paint together, garden together, "don't know"
>    together.
> 4. Stand and look for two minutes. Count how many you have of the most common thing.
> 5. Leave it all out until the next chapter, when we sort. If it rains, carry it in by category,
>    not in piles.

> **Safety**
> When you carry things out: lift with your legs, not your back, and never carry so much that you
> can't see your feet. Don't set heavy things up high "just for now" — temporary becomes
> permanent, and a can falling from a head-height shelf hurts. Heavy things low, light things
> high.
