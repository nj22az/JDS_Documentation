# 1880: The Hell Ship

## I. The Fastest Thing on the Water

She is eleven years old this autumn and already a legend, which is a rare thing for a ship or a person. Built at Dumbarton for old Jock Willis — White Hat Willis, they call him, for the topper he wears everywhere, rain or shine — and launched the same year the Suez Canal opened, which turned out to be the unkindest joke the sea has played on a sailing ship in living memory. Steamers go through the canal now. Clippers still have to round the Cape, the long way, the hard way, the way the Company's own ships went two centuries before her. Cutty Sark's tea-racing days lasted eight years before the steamers ate the trade whole. Eight years was enough. Men in this bar still argue about her best crossing the way other men argue about a prizefight they saw once and have never once tired of describing.

She's out there tonight, or somewhere near it — Java Sea, last anybody heard, hauling coal and jute now that the tea is gone, doing a tramp trade beneath a ship of her quality, though nobody says that part too loudly in front of the men who've sailed her. A ship doesn't stop being fast just because the cargo got less romantic.

* * *

## II. What the Wire Says

The word comes in off another vessel days ahead of any post — a captain's gossip passed hand to hand down the docks the way it always travels, faster than anything official. Cutty Sark, they're saying. Bad business.

How bad, someone asks.

Man dead. Mate did it. Captain helped him run.

The bar wants the whole of it, the way a bar always wants the whole of a thing that's gone wrong on a famous ship. It comes out over the evening in pieces, one sailor's account correcting another's, until something like the shape of it settles.

* * *

## III. Smith and Francis

The mate's name is Sydney Smith. Bully, by every account that's reached London — the kind of officer who finds a reason to work a man past reason and calls it discipline. The seaman is John Francis, signed on in Cardiff, willing enough but green at the work, which on a ship like this one is close to an unforgivable sin. Smith rode him from the first week.

It comes to a head in a gale in the Indian Ocean. Francis, ordered about twice in the chaos of the storm, disobeys — whether from confusion or defiance, no man telling the story agrees. He comes at the mate with an iron bar. Smith wrenches it away from him and puts it across his skull instead. Three days later, Francis is dead of it.

An old rigger at the end of the bar, who has sailed under men like Smith more than once, spits before he speaks. Difference between discipline and murder is who's still holding the bar when it's over, he says. Every hard mate in creation will tell you the other man swung first. Half of them are even telling the truth.

* * *

## IV. What Wallace Did

Captain Wallace confines Smith to his cabin — twenty-five years old, his first command, a young man trying to do the correct thing by a bad situation. Correct lasts until they raise Anjer. There, by every account reaching this bar, Wallace disposes of the iron bar himself and lets his mate go over the side onto a passing American ship, gone, no trial, no answer for a dead man three days cold in the ship's own wake.

The crew will not forgive it. When new orders come to sail on for Japan, they refuse. Wallace claps four of them in irons to get the ship moving at all.

And then, the sailor telling it says, and pauses, because this is the part that quiets even a Wapping bar on a Tuesday — becalmed in the Java Sea, dead flat, not a breath of wind for three days, nothing to do but sit with what he'd done. Fourth morning, he comes up on deck, speaks a few words to the man at the wheel, and steps over the rail. Calm sea. They had a boat in the water inside a minute. Never found him. Sharks, most likely, where he went in.

The bar is quiet for a moment that has nothing to do with the ship's reputation and everything to do with a twenty-five-year-old who made one panicked decision to protect a man who didn't deserve protecting, and paid for it with more than his career.

* * *

## V. Two Old Company Men

At the corner table, two men who spent their working lives on Company pensions before the Crown took the whole business over have been listening without much visible feeling.

A murderer let walk free by his own captain, one of them says, shaking his head. Wouldn't have happened in my day. A factor who let a man escape justice on Company business would have answered for it himself, and answered hard.

His companion is less interested in Wallace than in the arithmetic of the thing. What was the dead man worth to the voyage? he asks. Able seaman's wage, I'd guess. Green at that. The ship's out one hand and a captain, and gained nothing for either loss. Bad management, whatever else you want to call it.

Neither of them asks what happened to Francis's family, or whether anyone told them, or whether anyone ever will. It is not the kind of question men at that table have spent much of their lives asking about anyone.

* * *

## VI. What Becomes of Smith

It is two years before the rest of it reaches this bar, carried the same way — ship's gossip, a line in a newspaper somebody reads aloud. Smith is caught in London at last, recognised on the street despite a new name, and stands trial at the Central Criminal Court for the killing of John Francis.

Manslaughter, the verdict comes back. Not murder. Seven years, most of it breaking stone on the breakwater at Dover.

Seven years, the old rigger says, when the news reaches the bar. For putting a bar through a man's skull and letting your captain drown himself covering for you.

He'll be back at sea before some of us are dead, someone else says. Men like that always are.

Nobody in this bar asks what becomes of the family Francis left behind. The newspapers that followed Smith's trial with such interest do not think to follow that thread either.

* * *

## VII. What the River Carries

Cutty Sark herself is back on the river tonight, moored downstream, home from whatever tramp voyage has occupied her since — nobody at this table has kept close enough track to say which ports, only that she came back under a captain nobody's heard a bad word about yet, which these days counts as good news. She is, by any measure the men in this bar care to use, one of the most beautiful things ever built to cross water. She is also, in the telling that's followed her these two years, a ship men still call cursed, and both of those things are true at the same time without either one cancelling the other out.

The river does not rank ships by their beauty or their body count. It carried tea for her glory years and carries the news of her disgrace with exactly the same indifference, the same tide, the same patient work of moving whatever is put into it out toward the sea — coal, jute, gossip, grief, a captain who couldn't live with what he'd done, and a mate who managed, somehow, to live with it for another forty years.
