# 1701: Good for Business

## I. Low Tide

The tide is going out on the afternoon they hang him, and everyone in Wapping knows what that means. Execution Dock works on the water's schedule, not the court's. Low tide, so the gallows stands clear of the mud and the crowd can get close, and by five o'clock half of London looks to have walked down to the river to watch a Scotsman die.

The bar empties out an hour before, most of the regulars gone to find a good spot on the foreshore or a window with a view. Boats are anchored thick off the north shore already, hired out by the hour, packed with people who paid good money to watch a man choke. A few streets over, someone is selling meat pies to the queue. Nobody in London has agreed on much this spring except that Captain Kidd's hanging is worth the walk.

The pub keeps its regulars anyway — the ones with money, who don't need to stand in mud to feel like they got their money's worth. Three of them are Company men, and they haven't moved from their table all afternoon.

* * *

## II. The Company Table

They come in most weeks, this table. Clerks and factors between voyages, men whose coats are a little better than the room around them and who make sure everyone notices. Tonight there are three: a senior factor freshly back from Bombay, a young writer not long out of his apprenticeship, and an older man nobody has ever heard called anything but Mr. Sillitoe, who sits on the Company's court of committees and likes it known.

Sillitoe orders the good wine, not the house ale, and doesn't lower his voice to do it.

About time, he says. Two years this business has hung over every ship we've sent east. Two years of the Mughal's people asking why an Englishman is robbing them on the water while the Company swears it has nothing to do with piracy.

The factor from Bombay nods into his glass. Aurangzeb doesn't care whose flag the man sailed under, he says. All he sees is an English captain taking an Indian ship. Every time it happens, our factors answer for it. Doors get closed. Goods get seized. I've sat in rooms in Surat while men shouted about Kidd who had never heard the name a year before.

The young writer, still new enough to say what he thinks before he's learned not to, asks: But he had a commission. The King signed it himself, near enough. Doesn't that count for something?

Sillitoe looks at him the way a man looks at a nephew who has said something at dinner that will need correcting later, privately.

It counts for exactly as much as it's convenient for it to count, he says. He was given a license to hunt pirates and came back looking like one himself. Whether he is or isn't matters a good deal less than whether hanging him fixes the problem. And it does fix the problem. One man on a rope, and every court in Surat can tell itself England deals with its own.

* * *

## III. The Missing Passes

The factor from Bombay lowers his voice slightly, though not so much that the table can't still hear him over the noise from the street.

I heard something odd, he says. About the French passes. The ones that were meant to prove the ship he took was a lawful prize, sailing under enemy colours. His lawyers asked for them at trial. Nobody could find them.

Sillitoe's face does not change. Papers go missing, he says. It happens.

They'd have cleared him, the factor says. Or gone some way to it.

Then it is fortunate for a great many people that they did not turn up in time, Sillitoe says, and drinks his wine, and does not elaborate, and the young writer looks between the two older men and understands, for the first time in his short career, that there is a version of the Company's business that is never written into any ledger he will ever be shown.

Behind them, two watermen are arguing loudly about whose boat gets the better mooring for the view, and it very nearly comes to blows before a third man wades in and settles it by pointing out that from where they're standing they'll see nothing but the backs of ten thousand heads regardless, so they might as well stop shouting and share the ale between them.

* * *

## IV. The Sound of It

The window is open for the noise. It comes up from the river in stages — a long low murmur that is ten thousand people talking at once, then a swell that means something is happening, then a silence heavy enough to hear from three streets away, then the swell again, bigger.

Sillitoe checks his pocket watch as though the hanging were a shipping schedule. About now, he says.

The silence comes a second time, and holds longer, and someone out on the water lets out a cry that isn't celebration exactly but isn't grief either — the sound a crowd makes when a rope has snapped and a half-hanged man has dropped back to the boards still living, and has to be walked up the ladder a second time to finish what the first attempt didn't. The young writer's hand goes still around his cup. Sillitoe's does not.

Bad luck for him, the Bombay factor says. Twice.

Good luck for us, Sillitoe says. Once was going to be memorable enough. Twice, they'll be telling this story in every coffee house in London by morning, and not one word of it will be about the Company's trade rights in Bengal. That's rather the point of a hanging, when you think about it. Gives everyone something else to talk about.

The second silence comes, and this time it does not break into a swell. It just ends, the way a held breath ends, and the ordinary noise of the evening comes back up underneath it — boatmen calling for their fares, someone laughing too loud, the pie seller already announcing he's nearly sold out.

* * *

## V. What the River Keeps

By full dark the crowd has mostly gone home, pleased with itself, already turning the afternoon into a story to tell at breakfast. Down on the mud, a cage of iron bars waits empty a little longer, built to hold what's left of a man somewhere the tide will reach twice a day — a reminder, the court called it, for the next captain who thinks a commission is the same thing as a conscience.

Sillitoe pays for the wine and does not leave a large tip. He has the look of a man whose evening has gone exactly as planned, which it has.

Bengal will quiet down now, he tells the table, standing, straightening his coat. Give it a season. The Mughal's court likes a hanging as much as anyone does, so long as it's the right man swinging.

And if he wasn't the right man, the young writer says. Quietly. Not quite a question.

Sillitoe looks at him for a moment with something that isn't unkindness, exactly. Closer to patience. Learn this now and it'll save you grief later, he says. The Company doesn't require the right man. It requires a man, and an ending, and ships that keep sailing on schedule. Kidd was simply the one available.

He leaves. The factor from Bombay finishes his glass at his own pace and follows.

The river takes the noise of the evening the way it takes everything — the cheering, the pie seller's last calls, the creak of the empty cage settling on its chains, the tide already turning to come back in. It does not keep score of who was guilty and who was merely useful. It has never once been asked to, and it would not know how to answer if it were.
