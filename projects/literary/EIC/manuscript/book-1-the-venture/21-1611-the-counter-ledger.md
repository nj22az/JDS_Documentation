# The Counter-Ledger (1603–1611)

> **1603–1611**
>
> *“The ships keep the wage. I keep the leaving.”*
>
> — Maggie, keeper of the Pelican

***

## I. What Men Leave

The first keepsake after Tom's thimble is a horn button.

It comes off a coat already missing two others. The boy worries it loose while he drinks, works the thread until it parts and puts the button on the counter beside his Company's paper.

Till I come back, he says.

Maggie looks at the coat. You will want that before Gravesend.

I've two left.

Then you are rich in buttons and poor in sense.

She takes it anyway.

The custom is older than the Company. Men have left pipe-stoppers, buckles, bent coins and once a good knife whose owner spent the whole passage borrowing worse ones. A keepsake is a wager small enough to fit in a fist. The house holds one end. The sea holds the other.

Before the charters and eastern voyages, most wagers returned within a season. A Baltic man came back with tar in his beard. A coastal hand came back without a boot and took his buckle home to mend the replacement. Sometimes a wife collected the object instead, face prepared before she entered, and Maggie fed her before explaining what the paid-off crew had said.

The new voyages take years. The objects remain long enough to acquire dust.

Maggie puts the horn button into the narrow fault beneath the bar. Matthew Bell's folded account lies against the back of the oak, his full name written outside in Arthur's careful hand. Tom's brass thimble rests beside it. Blood has dried at one corner of Bell's page. Ale cannot reach the gap, even when men spill a full jug directly above it. Maggie has tested this without meaning to.

Her hand goes to the sharp knife in her apron, then stops.

The keepsake records the leaving. A notch records the failure to return.

There is one exception already in the wood. Maggie cut Tom's mark before dawn, while his ship still waited and fear had not yet learned patience. Since then she waits. A vessel may be overdue, storm-damaged, in another harbour or gone. Rumour does not choose among them. When a man comes home, he may have his keepsake and no mark is cut. When the sea keeps him past every honest hope, she cuts once.

***

## II. The First Claim

The horn button's owner does come back.

He enters the Pelican fifteen months later with yellow skin, two fewer teeth and a purse containing less than his mother was promised. Maggie recognizes him only after he asks for the button. The boy who left had cheeks. This man has hollows and a habit of standing where he can see the door.

Food first, she says.

I can pay.

That was not the question.

She gives him broth with barley and the heel of yesterday's loaf. He eats too fast, stops when the stomach objects, waits and begins again more slowly. Only after the bowl is clean does she ask for the names.

Not numbers. Names.

The Company's paid-off paper says twenty-three deceased before the Cape and seven thereafter. He remembers nineteen Christian names, three nicknames and one man called only Welsh by everybody aboard. For the other seven he has faces, berths and counties, but no names he trusts.

Maggie does not supply dignity he did not have at sea. She enters Welsh in the room's memory and makes the returned man repeat the others until the regulars can carry them too.

Then she gives him the horn button.

He turns it over. You kept it.

That was the arrangement.

Did you cut one for me?

No.

You did not know I was coming back.

No.

Then why not?

Because I did not know you weren't.

The button was the wager. A cut would have been the verdict. The sea has returned his body. It does not return the months to his mother, the teeth to his mouth or the thirty men to anybody. No mark says only that he is here, not that the leaving has been cancelled.

The boy sews the button back onto his coat before he goes. It does not match the new ones.

Men sometimes ask Maggie which cut belongs to which sailor. She can point to many: Tom beside the knot in the grain, the one premature mark; a pair of brothers close enough that the knife broke the ridge between them. Others have lost their owners. She remembers a face but not its mark, or a mark but only that the boy's paper named a ship beginning with Saint.

This does not shame her. The counter-ledger does not pretend to the Company's exactness. The Company's book can give the month, wage and deduction while losing the person entire. Maggie's wood keeps the opposite truth: somebody sat here, ate, feared and did not come back. When memory can supply the name, the room says it. When memory fails, the cut does not acquire a convenient one.

An honest blank is still an entry.

***

## III. The Empty Stool

Barnaby keeps his promise by Whitsun.

The cough does not take him in the Pelican. It gives him that much. He dies in the narrow room behind a sail loft with a folded rag on his chest and two apprentices arguing in whispers over who must tell Maggie.

Both come. She gives them bread and dripping because they have been awake all night. Then she makes them begin again when they call him the old sailmaker.

Barnaby, she says.

Barnaby, they say.

The parish puts him in Wapping churchyard. Carter pays for enough ale that every man who carried the coffin can drink without converting grief into credit. Josiah drinks two shares and complains that a man ought to get a better plot after forty years in English canvas. Nobody answers. Elias puts Barnaby's cup upside down on the stool nearest the hearth.

The next evening a stranger from Limehouse reaches for that stool.

Taken, Maggie says.

The man looks at the empty seat. By whom?

Barnaby.

He waits for the joke. None comes. He chooses another place.

The cup is put away after a week, but the stool remains empty. It becomes awkward on crowded nights. Men stand with ale in their hands while good oak holds nobody. Carter suggests moving it against the wall until the mourning has had a decent term.

Who decides the term? Maggie asks.

Carter does not volunteer.

The room begins keeping things that are not inside the fault. A stool can be an entry. So can a name said properly. So can the dark shape in the grain of the centre table, scrubbed so often that only the person doing the scrubbing knows where to look.

Paper is not the only material that can hold an account.

That is fortunate. Paper belongs too easily to the man with the locked room.

***

## IV. The Fault

Twice a year, when the weather is dry enough, Maggie empties the gap beneath the counter.

She does it before dawn with the door bolted and the shutters fast. Bell's page comes out first. She unfolds it once, no farther than the old creases permit, and checks the blood-dark corner for damp. She cannot read every line, but Arthur read it aloud that first night, and memory has kept what schooling did not: Mara between cordage and spoiled timber; the Fletcher debt; account disputed; no discharge permitted.

She knows the page by position as much as words. Names above. Sums to the right. Bell's confession below, where his hand begins to slope because fear or haste has reached it.

The thimble leaves a small green shadow against the oak. Maggie rubs it clean with salt and a corner of her apron. The brass is plain, worn on one side by Tom's mother. Maggie never uses it. A kept tool is not therefore available.

Other objects move through.

A copper farthing with a hole bored through the king's face goes back to a sister after fever. A small key is collected by a man who returns to find the chest it opened sold for rent. A bone die remains three years, then five, until a widow recognizes the drilled corner and carries it home in silence. Maggie gives her eel pie before she brings the die out. Grief does not improve because the order is reversed.

The fault is not a grave. It is a harbour with a poor tide. Things wait there until somebody with a claim can take them.

Bell's account has no claimant who can safely keep it. Tom's thimble has one, but he is beyond the Cape or under the water or alive somewhere paper can reach and Maggie cannot. Those two stay.

When the gap is empty, she puts her fingers into the narrow dark and feels the oak. No rot. No wet. One splinter that catches the skin each time. She leaves it. A hiding place too smooth looks made.

Then everything goes back: page flat, thimble behind it, the temporary wagers nearest her hand.

She wipes the counter above them though it does not need wiping.

***

## V. Stone and Oak

The room changes by repair, which is how a room changes without permission.

Rushes near the hearth begin to hold more mud than they hide. Men come through the door in greater numbers now: Company crews, chandler's hands, porters from new warehouses, boys sent down from the City with sealed instructions and no money for supper. The rushes sour within a day. Maggie has them swept away and buys worn flagstones from a house being pulled down upriver.

The mason says the stones do not match.

They need to lie flat, she says. They do not need to agree.

The new flags reach from the hearth to the first tables. The old boards remain at the counter and beneath the centre table. Bell died on wood. Maggie does not preserve the stain as a relic; she simply refuses to let a builder improve the event out of the room.

The bar grows too short next.

On pay nights men stand three deep. Ale crosses above other men's shoulders. An elbow knocks a candle onto the floor and Ned stamps it out before the rush basket catches. Carter offers good oak at cost and a joiner who can replace the whole counter in two days.

Before the joiner arrives, Maggie puts Bell's page inside her dress and the waiting keepsakes in her locked flour chest. For two days the fault holds nothing but its own dark.

Extend it, Maggie says.

The joiner crouches beneath the old wood and finds the fault with his thumb.

This length is split.

It has held twenty years.

It will hold better replaced.

Then make the new part hold the old.

He looks at her, then at the mallet on the shelf, and chooses craftsmanship over argument.

The longer bar turns at the short end and adds room for six more men. For a year the new oak is pale beside the old. Sleeves, palms, spilled ale and Maggie's rag darken it by degrees. The seam remains visible if the light comes sideways.

Underneath, the original length stays exactly where it was. The notches acquire neighbours. The fault remains dry.

The Company would call this enlargement. More room, more custom, better return from the same house.

Maggie calls it six more men she can feed before somebody asks them to sign.

***

## VI. The Years in Wood

Pell ships again in 1608 on the *Ascension*.

He leaves no keepsake.

Already left two fingers in Bantam, he says, holding up the damaged hand. The sea has security enough.

The *Ascension* does not return when expected. Then it does not return when lateness becomes worry. Men bring rumours: sighted at the Cape; put into an island; taken by the Portuguese; safe in Bantam; all hands dead. Each account arrives confident and leaves contradicted.

Maggie enters none of them into the wood. A rumour does not earn a cut merely by crossing an ocean.

When the Company's own list finally places the *Ascension* among the ships not returned, she cuts Pell's notch. One loss. One mark. The exact manner can wait for a man who knows.

Elias dies the following winter, not at sea but of everything the sea left in him. His back fails first. Then his lungs. He spends his last month giving orders nobody needs and correcting knots tied by hands that have not touched rope in years. The room buries him near Barnaby.

His stool does not remain empty. There are too many old men and too few places near the fire. But for one winter every man who sits there is told whose back polished the rail. By the next, men tell newcomers that Elias would have judged the matter differently. His body has become a standard measure.

Arthur stops coming long before anybody declares him absent. At first his place at the end of the bar is saved through one drink, then half an evening, then not at all. His last chalked wager remains on the slate after both the named storm and the date have passed. Maggie leaves it until damp draws the figures into a white blur, then washes the slate herself. No answer comes back.

By 1611 the Company is no longer a desperate adventure men discuss as though it may vanish before the next rent day. Its carts claim the centre of Wapping Wall. Its warehouses make shadows where children used to play. Boys enter the Pelican carrying printed articles instead of promises copied for one voyage.

One of them sits at the long end of the new bar with rain in his hair and no money for food. Maggie puts stew before him. Only when he has eaten does she ask whose paper is inside his coat.

Company, he says.

Do you want the sea?

He gives her the answer hunger and a clerk have prepared together.

She makes him answer again after bread.

When he leaves, he puts a wooden toggle on the counter. Maggie places it in the fault. Her hand finds the knife, then leaves it in her apron. No news yet.

Her fingers pass close to one older groove, deep and pale once, dark now with eight years of ale and air. Tom's mark. The thimble waits behind it. No reliable word has reached the house from the man who left it.

It is the first mark she ever cut before the sea answered. She has not cut early since.

The room grows. The Company grows. Beneath the old length of bar, where neither enlargement reaches, the first account remains disputed.
