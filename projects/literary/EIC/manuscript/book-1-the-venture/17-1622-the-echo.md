# The Echo (1620–1622)

> **1620–1622**
>
> *“The sea came home in him. Home went back to sea.”*
>
> — Jack Mercer, Deptford, 1626

***

## I. The Door

Tom never sits with his back to a door.

He does not know this until a clerk in Surat points it out.

They are in the factory dining room after the evening tally. Eight men, one long table, shutters open to release the heat. Tom arrives last and moves a junior merchant's cup so he can take the chair at the end nearest the entrance.

You always do that, the clerk says.

Do what?

Take the door.

It has the air.

So does the window.

The window is too narrow for a man.

The clerk laughs because he thinks Tom has made a joke.

Tom eats facing the room.

Afterward he notices the habit everywhere. In the warehouse office he stands between visitors and the threshold. On deck he takes the position from which he can see both companionway and rail. In a market coffee room he changes benches when three armed Portuguese merchants enter, not because he expects violence but because the old position leaves them behind him.

The body makes these decisions before the mind receives them.

In 1603 there was a door behind Tom and Silas Rook before it. A woman who could not speak stood on the other side. Tom placed himself between them and took a backhand across the face. Years later a boarding pike opened the same eyebrow from the other direction, as if the scar had been waiting for a second author.

Under strain, Tom touches the place.

Two fingers. One brief rub above the eye. He does it when weather closes, when a factor gives an order without enough boats, when a boy steps into a bight and does not yet know the rope has begun deciding for him.

The men read the gesture as thought.

Tom experiences it as nothing.

One night a warehouse guard brings in a thief.

The accused is a child carrying three handfuls of spilled rice inside his shirt. Twelve years old, perhaps. Bare feet. A cut at one knee. The guard pushes him through the office door and asks Tom where to hold him until morning.

Tom takes the door without thinking.

The boy sees a scarred Englishman blocking the only exit and becomes completely still.

Tom feels his fingers at the eyebrow.

For a moment the room changes. Not into the Pelican exactly. Rooms do not remember for men. Men force rooms to carry what they bring. The child is not Mara. The rice is not Bell's page. Tom is not the young man who stood against the mob.

But the door is still a door.

How much rice? Tom asks.

The guard shows him.

Charge it to spillage.

He stole it.

Then improve the sweepings.

Tom steps aside.

The boy does not move. Safety offered by the man who blocked the door still looks like another order.

Go, Tom says.

The child runs.

Tom remains in the doorway long after there is nobody left to hold.

## II. The Signature

The Company sends a new form in 1621.

It is called a declaration of ordinary loss. Masters, pursers and boatswains are to sign at the end of every passage, confirming that shortages in water, stores, rope and sail arose from weather, necessary consumption or the natural dishonesty of sailors rather than negligence by Company officers.

Tom reads it twice.

The clerk waits with sand ready.

Where are the amounts?

Attached after landing.

Then the declaration comes after landing.

Procedure requires signatures before cargo discharge.

I cannot declare a number I have not seen.

You are not declaring the number. Only the nature of any loss.

Any loss has not happened on this page yet.

The clerk points to the signature line. Every other officer has signed.

Tom's hand refuses to move.

It is not fear of writing. He signs wage books, orders, inventories and punishments. His name exists in Company rooms from London to Bantam. The aversion belongs to the white space above the line, the place where another man's meaning can be entered after Tom's hand has made it true.

He remembers Bell across the counting-room table, asking whether Tom would sign if he knew the debt was designed never to close.

Tom signed the next morning.

His father's ruined knee became rent, physician, burial and interest beneath that signature. Jack's father's broken body became four pounds. A hand does not need to understand a machine to start it.

Tom turns the form over and writes on the back.

*No declaration made until quantities are attached and read.*

He signs beneath that sentence.

The clerk refuses it.

Tom leaves the paper on the desk.

For three days his pay is held. On the fourth, the chief factor summons him and explains that administrative order requires trust between servants of the Company.

Trust whom? Tom asks.

The process.

Which man is that?

The chief tells him not to be insolent.

Tom touches the scar.

The ship cannot clear without a boatswain's declaration. The factor cannot replace Tom before the tide. Power is not always the larger office. Sometimes it is the man whose missing name stops the page.

The amounts are attached.

Tom finds two coils of sound rope entered as ordinary loss. The purser meant to sell them privately after discharge. Tom removes the coils from the shortage, signs the corrected declaration and watches the clerk sand his name.

The ship clears.

The next form arrives six months later with a new clause permitting estimated quantities before landing.

The machine has learned from his refusal.

## III. Shore Leave

Tom takes shore leave once in six years.

Not market leave, when the ship remains inside every decision. Not the walk between factory and landing. A full day with no watch, no tally and no man expected to answer his whistle.

He wakes before the watch changes anyway.

The hired room is quiet. A bed, a basin, one chair, a door with a wooden bolt. Tom lies listening for rigging that is not there.

Without the ship's sounds, his body supplies them. A block knocking at the foretop. Water moving in casks. Canvas beginning to flog. He knows none is real. He listens harder.

At dawn he bolts the door, then unbolts it because sleeping behind a barrier makes the room feel smaller. He places the chair where he can see the entrance from the bed. When the keeper brings breakfast, Tom is already standing.

Food first, the keeper says.

She means before payment. The words strike another room inside him.

Mutton and mace. A rag over one shoulder. A blue square folded beneath a counter. Maggie asking whether he has used his father's whistle better.

Most days, he told her.

He has spent years proving the answer in situations she will never see and all that time knowing proof is not what she asked for.

Tom eats.

The bread is flat and fresh. The fish has been fried with spice. He tastes meat pie.

He leaves half the food and pays for all of it.

On the stair, the sole of his left boot opens at the toe.

It does not tear cleanly. The stitching has rotted under salt and lets go one hole at a time, so that the leather mouth opens and shuts with each step. Tom stops, lifts the foot and presses the sole back with his thumb.

The keeper watches from the doorway.

There is a cobbler two lanes inland, she says.

I have cord.

You have a boot that has already refused cord.

Tom looks at the broken stitches. She is right. This irritates him more than the boot.

In the street, men move without watches. A father carries a sleeping girl. Two women argue over fruit. A carpenter planes a door while the owner holds it upright. Ordinary life surrounds Tom with the intimacy of a language he once spoke and can now only understand when others use it.

He finds the cobbler by the boots hanging beneath an awning. The shop is open to the lane: a low bench, knives, lasts, pegs, a pot of blacking gone soft in the heat. The cobbler takes one look at the loose sole and holds out a hand for the other boot.

Only this one, Tom says.

Both.

The right is sound.

The cobbler bends it once. Three stitches lift along the welt.

Both, he says again.

Tom understands enough of the local trade speech to buy rope, water and a day's labour. Boots have not been necessary to that education. He points to the sun, then to the work, asking how long.

The cobbler points to noon.

Tom looks at the lane behind him. A man without boots cannot choose a direction quickly. He removes the left, then the right, and places them side by side on the bench rather than surrendering them directly. The cobbler gathers them without comment and points to a stool beneath the awning.

The stool faces the street. Tom turns it until he can also see the rear door.

The cobbler cuts the old thread. He does not hurry and does not explain. Each bad stitch comes free separately. The failure that looked sudden on the stair has been travelling around the sole for months: leather dried hard by salt, holes enlarged by strain, thread dark where water entered and pale where friction ate it.

Tom knows this kind of damage. A line parts at the final fibre and begins parting long before.

How much? he asks.

The cobbler names the price.

Tom pays half. The man counts it, puts it beneath a stone and continues. No gratitude passes in either direction. The rate has made room for silence.

For ten minutes Tom sits.

A handcart loses a pin outside the fruit stall. The owner replaces it with a nail and keeps going. A woman lowers a basket from an upper window on a cord polished by years of the same journey. Two boys carry a door between them while the carpenter walks backward, shaving one last high place without making them set it down. Somewhere behind the shops, somebody beats dust from a mat in a steady rhythm that becomes, in Tom's body, men hauling together.

He makes himself stay on the stool.

The awning line above him is wrong.

It has been taken twice around a peg and finished with a half-hitch that draws tighter whenever the cloth fills. The free end is already pinched flat. One gust will not bring the awning down. Twenty ordinary gusts will make the knot impossible to release, and then someone will cut good line because a bad turn has eaten it.

Tom looks away.

The cobbler draws waxed thread through the first sole.

Tom looks back.

The peg wants a round turn and two hitches, he says.

The cobbler follows his pointing finger, looks at the awning, then at Tom's bare feet.

Boots, the man says, and returns to them.

Tom lasts another minute.

Then he stands on the warm boards, reaches above the doorway and takes the strain of the awning across one forearm. The cobbler says something sharp. Tom holds up the flattened end, points to the place where it has begun to fail and reties the line so the load sits on the peg instead of the finish.

The awning lifts one inch and lies still.

The cobbler examines the knot. He pulls the free end. It releases. He ties it again himself, slower, and leaves it.

Tom returns to the stool.

The man works on the boots. Tom watches the street. Neither calls what happened payment.

At midday a food carrier passes with flat bread under cloth, a pot of lentils and a jar of sour milk. The cobbler buys one portion and divides it beside the blacking stone. He pushes half toward Tom.

I ate, Tom says.

The cobbler points at Tom, then at the food, dismissing the claim as irrelevant.

Tom pays for his portion. The man returns one of the coins.

Awning, he says.

Tom takes the coin back. Refusing a fair account would only make another kind of debt.

They eat without conversation. The bread is hot enough to soften the hard place at the corner of Tom's mouth. The lentils are plain. The milk is cool. Around them the lane continues its unrecorded exchange of work, food, carrying, waiting and repair. Nobody is being saved. Nothing is being proved.

At noon the cobbler gives Tom both boots. The left sole lies close. The right has been restitched before it could open. Tom puts them on and walks the length of the lane, testing the work.

He could return to the hired room. The chair still faces the door. The bed is paid through morning. A whole afternoon remains in which no man expects his judgement.

He goes to the river.

Boats require rope. Rope requires inspection. Within an hour he is helping a boatman reset an eye in a worn painter. Before the afternoon is half gone he has found a cracked block. By late light he has arranged replacement line through a broker who owes the factory nothing.

The times no longer agree because leave has no bell to make them agree. Tom notices only when the light changes. The painter leads to a frayed lashing at the thwart. The lashing leads to the cracked block. The block leads to a length of line stored in shade and sold without Company measure. Each repair names the next. By doing them, Tom turns an afternoon without orders into a watch only he knows he has taken.

At sunset he returns to the ship relieved.

The relief shames him less than the room did. A ship does not ask whether a man can sit still inside his own life. It gives him a line under strain and accepts the answer of his hands.

The sea carried home in him: door, food, thimble, blue cloth, Maggie's exact refusals.

Home carried back to sea: his habit of repair, his place at the threshold, the rule that a hungry boy eats before he answers.

Neither place releases the other.

## IV. The Letter Not Sent

Tom writes to Maggie seven times.

The first attempt begins in the Surat factory after his mother's parish letter arrives. He writes Maggie's name, then nothing beneath it. News of the death feels like a claim: feed me, forgive me, tell me I still belong somewhere. He folds the blank page around the name and puts it in his chest.

The second begins with Jack.

*The boy came home alive.*

This is true. It also arranges Jack's survival as evidence for Tom's defence. He hears himself entering the nineteen dead in smaller writing below it. He tears the page.

The third says the debt was corrected.

The fourth explains the paymaster's collar.

The fifth contains only the words *You were right*, which appear humble until Tom reads the demand hidden inside them: now tell me I am no longer wrong.

He burns that one.

The sixth is a proper letter. Weather. Surat. Cotton. His mother's death. Jack's work in Deptford, learned from a sailor newly arrived from England who saw the boy outside a rigging shed. Tom writes two pages without once saying why he has written them.

He signs late.

The signature makes the letter into something that may travel. Until then it was only thought arranged on paper.

Tom carries it to the packet clerk.

Which ship? the clerk asks.

Tom names the westbound vessel.

Fee?

The clerk names it.

Tom pays, then takes the letter back before the clerk can enter it.

Not ready?

No.

The clerk has handled merchants' promises, dying men's instructions and confessions addressed to wives who will receive them after the sender has found courage elsewhere. He does not ask.

The seventh letter begins in 1622 after the boy at the companionway reties the coil and goes to eat.

Tom writes:

*I remembered before I knew what remembering required.*

He sits with the sentence until dawn.

Then he adds:

*I do not know whether that matters to you. It matters to the orders I give.*

For once the page does not offer survival as acquittal. It asks for nothing. That should make it sendable.

Tom folds it.

Does not sign.

The unsigned letter remains beneath his mother's parish notice. Two messages in the same chest: one that travelled too late and one kept from travelling at all.

Silence is not the absence of an entry.

It is an entry renewed every time the ship sails west without the page.

## V. The Thimble

The thimble appears when Tom is most tired.

Not as a vision. As an error of the hand.

He reaches into a pocket for his knife and expects brass beneath his fingers. Opens his sea chest and looks toward the corner where his mother kept thread. Sees a glint beside a deck bolt and bends before recognising a washer.

The real thimble is in Wapping.

Unless Maggie sold it.

She would not.

Unless the Pelican burned.

He would have heard.

Unless she threw it into the Thames after he left.

She picked it off the flagstones.

Tom does not know this last fact. He remembers the sound of brass striking stone and the door closing behind him. Everything after belongs to hope pretending it is inference.

At night the scene replays with changes.

Sometimes he picks it up. Maggie tells him the wager is closed and turns away.

Sometimes Jack picks it up and carries it aboard. The thimble rolls loose during the Madagascar squall and disappears through a scupper.

Sometimes Mara stands behind the bar in dark silk, though Tom has never seen her in dark silk and has not seen her at all since the night she vanished. She puts the thimble beside Bell's page and bolts the door from within.

Tom wakes touching the scar.

He begins carrying a brass sailmaker's palm in his coat. It is larger than a thimble, flat and practical, its dimpled metal set in leather. He tells himself the familiar brass stops the hand searching.

It does not.

Men think the palm belonged to his father. Tom never corrects them. A false explanation can become privacy if nobody profits from it.

## VI. The Echo

In 1622 a young sailor freezes in a doorway.

The ship is taking water at anchor after a cargo boat strikes the quarter. Not enough to sink her. Enough to make every order urgent. Men run pumps, shift stores and rig a fothering sail over the damage.

The boy stands at the companionway holding a coil, unable to decide whether the order sends him below or aft.

Tom sees the hesitation.

Move!

The boy does not.

Tom crosses the deck and stops before striking distance.

Years ago he would have used the hand. Jack on the yard, his weight on the pale buntline. Fear broken by force before the rope broke itself. That blow saved Jack and remains a blow.

Tom points with the whistle.

Look at me. Which line?

The boy names it.

Where does it lead?

Aft.

Then go aft.

The boy moves.

Tom hears his own voice and his father's inside it. Beneath both, Maggie: do not call the taking a rescue. Beneath Maggie, Bell asking one quiet question across an unsigned page. Beneath Bell, a woman behind a door who has no English word available and a room deciding what her silence means.

None of these people is present.

All of them alter the order.

By midnight the leak is controlled. The boy brings back the coil properly flaked and waits for praise.

Tom examines it.

Tie it again.

The boy's face falls.

Tom touches the first turn.

This one crosses. Under load it binds. Again, and then eat.

The boy reties it. The line runs clean.

Good, Tom says.

The word still feels expensive. He spends it.

The boy goes to the galley.

Tom takes the doorway and watches the ship settle around the repair. He is forty-one. The scar aches before weather. His name stands beneath more Company orders than he can remember. Somewhere west, a woman he loves may still hate what he became. Somewhere east, other ledgers are closing around names he has not yet heard.

The sea repeats nothing exactly.

It carries the sound forward changed.
