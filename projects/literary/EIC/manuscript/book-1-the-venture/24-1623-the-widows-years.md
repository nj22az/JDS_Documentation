# The Widow's Years (1603–1623)

> **1603–1623**
>
> *“Some accounts are kept because payment is coming. Some are kept so nobody can say nothing was owed.”*
>
> — Anne Bell, Shadwell, 1623

***

## I. Longer Than a Wife

By 1623 Anne has been Matthew Bell's widow for twenty years, which is longer than she was his wife and nearly long enough for people to think widow is a trade.

Perhaps it is.

It has tools. A key worn flat at one tooth. A rent book with the first landlord's name struck through and his son's written above it. A good black dress that was black before Matthew died and has now been turned once at the cuffs. A way of standing inside an open door that makes a collector understand he has been admitted and may therefore be put out.

Both girls are women. The elder reads faster than Anne and keeps figures in a hand that begins like her father's before slanting stubbornly into its own. The younger remembers every face that has asked for money and none of the reasons offered. They earn. They contribute. Anne still counts the household as three because survival has taught her that independence is often several people refusing to let one another fall.

On Thursday afternoons she walks from Shadwell to the Pelican.

Maggie puts broth at the end of the bar without asking whether Anne has eaten. Anne brings whatever the house has sent home with her since the week before: an apron with a new tie, a shirt cuff turned, a rent demand read twice, a name remembered from a paid-off crew. They do not call these visits. A visit is what a woman makes when she has time to sit.

Today Anne carries three papers tied in thread and a child's shoe with the sole opening at the toe.

Maggie looks at the shoe.

Yours are grown.

Not every child is mine.

Maggie puts the bowl down first.

***

## II. The First Winter

Their friendship begins with food neither woman agrees to owe.

In the first winter after Matthew's death, Maggie brings barley broth to Shadwell every third day. Anne returns each pot clean with something repaired inside it. A split apron. A scorched cloth. Once, the tavern's best sheet, torn by a sailor who mistakes an upstairs room for a ship in weather and attempts to reef the bed.

Maggie sends a loaf with the next pot.

Anne sends back two pewter buttons and a note in the elder girl's large hand: *Sheet mended. Buttons supplied. One loaf too much.*

Maggie tears off the last sentence and uses it to light the taproom candles. Then she sends dripping and two onions.

Neither wins. That is why it lasts.

The girls eat before the women discuss Matthew. At first Anne permits no other order. A child who has watched adults lower their voices around her learns to hear death in an empty stomach. Maggie understands this without being taught. She arrives, fills bowls, waits for spoons to scrape, and only then reports what the parish has done with Rook's name or what somebody at the stairs claims to know.

Most reports are useless. Silas seen at Deptford. Silas on a collier north. Silas drowned off Gravesend. One week he is three places and dead in two. Anne asks who saw him. The answer is always a man who heard another man.

Do you want me to keep bringing the stories? Maggie asks.

No.

The next week Anne says, If a story has a name inside it, bring the name.

That becomes another procedure.

Maggie hears the river. Anne tests what it says. A name, a ship, a date, a person who will repeat it sober. If one part is missing, the story does not enter the house.

Years later, when nobody has brought anything true, Anne understands that waiting for news of a murderer has been eating time the girls can use. She stops asking.

Maggie stops bringing it.

Their friendship survives the loss of its first subject.

***

## III. What Women Charge

The account between them grows complicated because neither will put a price on the work that matters.

Anne will price six inches of hemming, a morning spent reading a lease and the ink needed to copy a demand. She will not price sitting beside Maggie when the anniversary of a Swedish son's impressment comes round and Maggie wipes the same clean place on the counter until the grain rises.

Maggie will price ale, pie, a room upstairs and broth carried out in a covered pot. She will not price the night the younger Bell girl burns with fever and Maggie sits at the pallet changing cloths while Anne sleeps for the first two hours in three days.

The fever comes in the ninth winter.

Coal costs more that week because the wind has pinned barges below the bridge. The physician wants payment before he enters. Anne pays him and knows at once there will be no meat until Sunday. Maggie arrives with beef bones, leeks and half a sack of coal contributed, she says, by a customer who lost a wager.

What wager?

That I would ask permission.

Anne is too tired to refuse properly.

The child lives. Afterwards Anne enters every farthing in the household book: physician, coal, linen spoiled, two days' work lost by Joan, candle ends, vinegar. Beside Maggie's name she leaves a blank.

Joan finds it.

What goes there?

Nothing she would accept.

Then why write her name?

So I remember the blank belongs to somebody.

The same blank appears in Maggie's reckoning. Anne never sees that book because it is not a book. It is a crowd of cuts beneath the bar, keepsakes in a fault in the wood, Bell's page folded beyond spilled ale. Yet Maggie knows which winter Anne misses Thursday because the rent rises, which girl needs shoes, which neighbour has taken ill and therefore which household Anne is quietly feeding in addition to her own.

Along the Wall, the same work passes hand to hand without ever entering a wage book. Hours at a sickbed. Children minded while a mother works. Washing laid out for a man who returns without wages. A loaf divided one place farther than the baker intended.

## IV. The Name Maggie Spends

In 1612 Maggie comes to Shadwell after closing, anger still making her hands exact.

Anne opens the door. Maggie has brought the last quarter of a pie under a cloth.

Who ate?

Tom ate first. The boy ate most of what remained.

Which boy?

Maggie tells her about Jack, the injured father, Carter's debt and Tom's berth. She tells her what she called Tom before putting him out.

Anne waits until the pie is warm again. The girls are nearly grown now and need no instruction to lay plates. Anne and both girls eat. Maggie does not. Anne cuts the remaining crust in half and leaves one piece before her until she does.

Only then does Anne say, He is not Matthew.

I called him Harcourt.

You used Matthew's death to sharpen the name.

He used the same words. The boy would sign with or without him.

That was Matthew's lie too.

Then I was right.

Anne folds her hands around the cup.

Right is not the same as exact. Harcourt made the paper hungry. Matthew fed it six years and called himself a hand. Tom found a boy already inside it and called the taking protection. I hear the likeness. I also hear where it ends.

Maggie's face closes.

You want me to fetch him back.

I do not want a grand act made for my benefit. You taught him the difference and put him out. Let the difference work if it can. But do not turn my dead husband into a cudgel because you are frightened of losing a living man.

Maggie looks down at her empty hands.

Too late.

For the words, yes.

And the rest?

Anne takes another bite of pie.

The rest usually takes longer.

Maggie does not ask forgiveness. Anne does not offer it. When she leaves, the empty pie dish is clean.

***

## V. Claims

By 1623 women come to Anne with papers because she is slow enough to distrust every line and quick enough to know which line will eat.

She is not a scrivener. A scrivener charges for words whether they help or not. Anne charges for the hour when the woman can pay and enters the hour when she cannot.

The child's shoe belongs to a sailor's wife from Ratcliff whose husband died homeward of fever. The Company has accepted the death and retained the wages pending proof that the claimant is his lawful widow. The two facts have occupied adjacent desks for five months without meeting.

The wife cannot leave three children alone to wait at the pay office. She cannot take them because the youngest cries indoors and clerks use noise as a reason to move poor people outside. She has asked Anne to read the demand. The refusal sends her toward another desk. Anne will not send her alone.

At the Pelican, Anne eats her broth. Then she unties the three papers.

One is a marriage line from the parish. One is a pay note carrying the dead man's mark. One is the Company's refusal, which does not refuse. It requests further satisfaction as to identity and leaves the kind of satisfaction blank.

Maggie reads over Anne's shoulder.

What do they want?

For her to tire.

Can she prove he drank here?

Drinking is not marriage.

It is to some men.

Maggie remembers two sailors paid off the dead man's ship. One lodges near Limehouse. The other works a lighter with Ned. Both saw the wife at the ship before sailing. One carried a letter from the husband to her on the outward leg and can identify the direction.

Anne turns over the Company's paper and writes five headings in the clean spaces: name, ship, date, knowledge, address.

Joan will make a fair copy tonight. Maggie will send word to the two men. Anne will take the wife to the pay office at opening, before a clerk has accumulated enough unfinished work to make another woman's claim feel like an offence.

And the shoe? Maggie asks.

The child must wear something while we wait.

Maggie measures the split sole against her palm and calls upstairs for Joan, who knows a cobbler willing to mend children's shoes in exchange for dinners he pretends not to need.

The chain forms without a committee. Tavern, widow, daughter, lighterman, witnesses, cobbler, dinner. None of it appears in the Company's account of wages eventually released.

That account will say the widow supplied sufficient proof.

***

## VI. Nobody Pays

They finish near dusk.

The two sailors have come, eaten and put their marks beneath statements Joan Bell reads aloud. The shoe has gone to the cobbler. The children and their mother are in Anne's rooms with stew enough for the evening. At first light Anne opens her own door, leaves the two older children with her daughters and takes the youngest and his mother to the pay office.

They arrive before the office door is unbarred and find twelve women ahead of them.

Nobody has been given an order. The order exists anyway, marked by shoulders against the wall, baskets at heels, children on a folded cloak and a baby asleep inside a shawl. A woman darning a stocking tells Anne to stand after her and before the blue basket. The basket guards a place no clerk recognises and every woman present will defend.

When the door opens, the line becomes a room.

It is narrower than the number of claims requiring it. Plaster has fallen from one corner in thin shells, exposing the lath beneath. The wall behind the benches is polished dark at shoulder height by coats that have waited there. A cold hearth holds yesterday's ash. One high window admits light without warmth, and beneath it a clerk's rail divides the people who have brought papers from the people permitted to delay them.

There are two benches. The women fit twenty bodies onto space intended for twelve, children counted as laps until they refuse. The Ratcliff widow sits with the youngest on her knees. Anne stands behind her and keeps one hand on the packet inside her coat.

The clerk does not appear with the door. A boy enters, puts an inkstand on the desk and goes away. Another man crosses the rear room carrying a book, sees the waiting women and closes the inner door. The room hears a bolt settle. Nobody complains. Complaint this early spends strength before it can purchase anything.

The woman with the sailor's cap resumes her stocking. Beside her, another turns the frayed edge of a cuff under with a thumbnail and holds it there until the first woman passes the needle. Thread travels down the bench. A woman near the cold hearth has brought a square of sailcloth and cuts patches from it for three different knees. She takes no coin. One of the mothers keeps the offcuts for soles.

Anne has brought work too: a child's shirt with one sleeve opening at the seam. She lays it across her forearm and begins. The Ratcliff widow looks at her.

You expected this long?

I expected them to hope for longer.

The woman with the stocking glances over.

Keep the marriage line in your own hand until he finds the death, she says. Once both are behind the rail, they can lose them together.

Anne nods. She had planned the same. She had not known the sentence belonged to others.

The women teach without announcing a lesson. The clerk nearest the window reads marks but dislikes seals broken at the edge. The clerk at the inner door will say a ship's book has not arrived when he means it is on the shelf behind him and he does not want to climb. If a claim is refused, ask him to write what remains wanting. If he will not write it, repeat the missing thing loudly enough for the next woman to hear. Never surrender the parish original when a fair copy will answer. Never say a husband had debts until the wage is acknowledged, because a debt waits more patiently than a widow and will be admitted first if invited.

Anne listens. She has spent twenty years learning how one man's paper eats. The room has learned how a hundred papers chew.

By midmorning the youngest child has cried himself hot. His mother apologises. Anne takes him, and the woman cutting sailcloth gives him her wooden bobbin. When he drops it for the third time, a girl offers a crust in exchange. He accepts with a merchant's gravity.

Food changes the mother before it changes the child. Anne sees her watching the crust.

She takes bread and a cold onion from her bag. Maggie packed both without asking who would eat them. Anne breaks the bread, gives the larger part to the widow and keeps the onion between them.

I am not hungry, the widow says.

That is useful. Eat before it passes.

The woman obeys. Around them other packets open without ceremony: an apple divided four ways, cheese pared thin, a heel softened in a cup for the baby. No meal is offered to the room. Every person merely discovers that what she has brought reaches one place farther than intended.

At last the Ratcliff widow is called.

Anne goes to the rail with her. The clerk looks first at Anne, who is older and holding the packet, and directs the question to her.

Your husband's ship?

Her husband, Anne says, and moves half a step aside.

The widow names the ship herself.

The clerk finds the death in a pay book. The entry is one line: name, rating, wages accrued, fever, dead homeward. A man's last months have been made short enough to fit beneath a ruler. The clerk touches it, then asks for proof of marriage.

The widow holds out the fair copy Joan made. Anne retains the parish line.

Original, the clerk says.

Upon acceptance of the copy.

I cannot accept what I cannot compare.

Anne shows him the original without releasing it. He compares the hands, annoyed by the distance of one arm. Then he asks who knows the claimant to be the same woman.

Anne places the two sailors' statements on the rail. Their marks stand beneath words read aloud before they touched ink; their addresses and shipboard knowledge are entered beside them. One saw the widow at the sailing. One carried her husband's letter outward.

They are not present, the clerk says.

Your request did not require their bodies.

It required satisfaction.

What part remains unsatisfied?

The clerk turns the refusal over as though its blank back may contain the answer his office omitted five months ago. Behind Anne, the room continues its work. Cloth draws through fingers. A child whispers a counting game. The woman with the blue basket has returned and the place kept for her has closed neatly around her skirts.

The muster must be compared with the pay ledger, the clerk says.

Compare it.

The ledger is not in this room.

Where is it?

In examination.

Anne looks past him. A broad book rests on the shelf beside the inner door, its leather back lettered with the dead ship's name.

Is examination the shelf behind you?

He turns before he can stop himself. The room sees. A small sound moves along the bench, not quite laughter and more expensive to the clerk.

He takes down the book.

The two entries agree. That should finish it. Instead he says the officer authorised to release the wage has gone to dinner.

When will he return?

When his dinner is finished.

The clerk closes the pay book with the widow's proof still beneath his hand.

Anne does not move from the rail.

Enter that the proofs have been received.

The claim is not concluded.

Then enter what has happened, not what has not.

He tells her to sit. She tells him she will sit when the papers are back in the widow's hand. The room does not look up. That is its assistance: twenty witnesses refusing the appearance of a disturbance while making it impossible for him to pocket the pages unnoticed.

He returns the originals and writes a receipt for the copies. Anne reads it to the last word before yielding the space at the rail.

The authorised officer's dinner lasts through the next bell and part of the one after. The benches exchange occupants but not knowledge. A mother carries another woman's baby to the yard. Anne closes the shirt sleeve. The Ratcliff widow learns which pay day begins before sunrise and which entrance opens first, then repeats both facts to a younger woman.

When the inner door finally opens, no heralding occurs. A fed man sits. The clerk places three accepted claims before him and seven to one side. Anne watches the Ratcliff papers enter the smaller pile.

The officer reads the witness marks, checks the matching lines and signs. Payment will not be made today. The wages must be drawn against the next appointed issue, less the dead man's recorded advances and any lawful claim already lodged. The widow's face empties at the first sentence and begins returning at the second. Delay has changed shape. It now has a date.

Anne asks for the date on the receipt.

The officer looks at the clerk. The clerk looks at the room. He adds it.

They leave with no coin. The youngest sleeps against his mother's shoulder. When the widow begins to thank Anne and cannot finish without crying, Anne gives her the packet to carry. Work steadies the hands better than consolation.

I know the entrance now, the widow says. And the hour. And not to give them the parish line.

She has learned the room's account. She will bring it back when another woman asks.

By the time Anne returns to the Pelican, the light is going. Maggie wipes the end of the bar.

What do I owe you? Anne asks.

For what?

The witnesses ate.

They were hungry.

Joan's time.

Joan's time is Joan's to price.

The broth.

You mended the cellar apron.

That was last Thursday.

Then we are behind.

They have been behind for twenty years.

Anne takes out her household book. Matthew's name appears on its earliest pages in the small entries of a living man: coal carried, candles bought, twelve shillings received, sixpence missing and found in the other coat. After his death the entries continue without him. Rent. Bread. Shoes. Fever. Work. Two daughters becoming women one paid hour at a time.

Maggie looks at the narrow columns.

Do you still reckon what his death cost?

No.

Why keep it, then?

Anne closes the book.

Some accounts are kept because payment is coming. Some are kept so nobody can say nothing was owed.

Beneath Maggie's hand, behind the oak, Matthew's other account remains folded in the dark. It does not absolve him. Anne's household book does not condemn him. The two pages perform different work and neither is asked to finish the dead.

Outside, a woman knocks and waits because she has not yet learned that the Pelican permits women with urgent papers to use the latch.

Maggie reaches for it.

Anne is closer.

I can manage a door, she says, and opens it herself.
