# Foreword

I have never seen the year 1603, nor 1888, nor any hour that ran its course before I drew breath. No man has. We are each of us nailed to our own small plank of time and set adrift on it, and we are not consulted about the water.

And yet I have stood in the cold and the dark with my whole life written down in another man's book, and waited on a wage that some thing far larger than me, and wholly careless of me, would decide whether to pay. I have watched the machine work. Every man who ever sold his hours has watched it work. That much has not altered in the four centuries this book crosses, and it will not alter in the four that come after.

So let me say the plain thing first, at the front, before a single boy signs a single paper.

This is a work of fiction. The men and women in these pages were never born. I built them out of ink and salt and the flat need to put someone where I could not go myself — into rooms I have only imagined, onto decks I have only read of, through deaths I have been spared. Their names are inventions, chosen to carry a living weight through dead years and nothing more. If you meet in one of them a face you knew, or a grief you have carried up your own stairs a landing at a time, that is not because I set your life down on paper. It is because the river runs the same through all of us, and always has. Any resemblance here to persons living or dead is the resemblance one grey wave bears to another: real enough, and meaningless, and gone before your hand can close on it.

But the water is real. The history is real. The Company sailed; the docks burned; ten million starved while the ledgers balanced to the farthing; the ships went out heavy with men and came home light of them. Those things happened, and they happened to people who were never invented. I have set at the back of this book a plain chronology of what is true, so that you may hold the made-up against the real and find the seam for yourself.

What I have done, in the end, is a smuggler's work. I have taken the cargo of one ordinary modern life — my own, its cold mornings and its long unbalanced accounts, the particular ache of being a small thing inside a vast one — and I have run it ashore in older centuries under other men's names, because the truth of it goes down easier when it is wearing 1626, and because history is not a thing you were meant to study through glass. It is a thing to be lived through, from the inside, by someone with a pulse. I could not send you your own self into the dark. So I sent Tom. I sent Maggie. I sent the whole invented crew of them on ahead of you, to stand where you would have stood and feel what you would have felt, and to come back — those that came back — and swear to you that it was so.

Read them, then, as your own kind. That is the whole of what they are.

*— Nils Johansson*
