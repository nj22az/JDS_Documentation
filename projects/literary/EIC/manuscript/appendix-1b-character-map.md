# Character Map: The People and the Threads

This map tells the whole story out of school. It names every thread the book only ever whispers, so read it the way you would read the last page of a mystery: afterwards.

The room is the protagonist; the people are its weather. But five threads run through the four centuries, and the people carry them — mostly without knowing it. Three of the five are the narrator's property alone: no character ever states, learns, or discovers them, and the book keeps that rule to the last page. This appendix is the one place they are written down in full.

* * *

## The Five Threads

**The Keepers.** The bar's own lineage — a trade, not a bloodline. Maggie (born Margareta, from a small fishing village on the coast outside Stockholm) runs the house in 1603 and is still running it when Daniel comes home wrong in 1626. The young keeper of 1701 never met her, but the woman who trained her was trained by Maggie herself — the trade passed down like a good knife. From 1770 the keeper answers to Hannah, and keeps answering to it across spans no one woman could cover: the name is what the trade carries, given to whoever's daughter or niece takes over the bar. The Hannah pouring gin and tonics in 2019 is the fourth of that name.

**The Cache.** Maggie starts it in 1603: a gap under the bar counter where she cuts one notch for every young man who signs and ships out. Four objects join the notches across three centuries, each left by someone at the moment of their hardest choice, and no one who leaves one ever knows about any of the others. Tom's mother's thimble (1603). Daniel's true account of Amboyna (1626) — the sentence the Company's deposition refused to hold. Nathaniel's testimony about Kidd's buried French passes (1701). Gwen's wartime notebook (1940). Only the building holds all of it, until Hannah finds the hoard during a cellar refit in 2019 — counts it, understands none of it, and leaves it exactly where it lies.

**The Work — the Canton thread.** A martial art, a bonesetter's trade, and a folded wooden fan, carried from a Canton factory lane to a Wapping laundry yard by the war the Company started over opium. The lane's master splinted the very wrists his art broke — dockhands, brawlers, and, around 1772, one foreign sailor named James Holman, who never knew what lane he had been lucky in. Hale's old master (1839) was taught as his teacher's teacher before him, in the line that lane had always made. Wei Zhang carries the art, the fan, and the white bowl to London; his daughter Su carries all three into a November alley in 1888. No character ever traces the lineage. It lives in the repeated image: the gentlest hands in the lane belonging to the most dangerous man in it.

**The Johansson line.** The narrator's longest ledger entry, stated once and never again. Maggie's son by her first husband Johan takes the King's coin in the Swedish navy before she ever crosses to London; the name Johansson comes back down the generations and steps off a timber boat in 1892 as Erik Johansson, the big unhurried Swede with the sea-bag of salt-stiff shirts, whose father came from a small fishing village on the coast outside Stockholm — the same village, never named. Erik marries Su Zhang. Their great-great-granddaughter keeps three unconnected things in a terraced house in Bow in 2019. Neither Erik nor Su nor anyone else ever learns that the woman who cut the first notch under the bar was his ancestor.

**The room itself.** The lightest thread: damage and tenants, never explained, only inherited. Louie's chair — broken by a flung chair in a 1701 mooring argument, propped level with a folded playbill, still standing in its corner in 1888 with nobody left alive who could say why. The lopsided table, scarred in an 1839 scuffle, wedged level at last by an oblivious American tourist's coaster in 2019. The fuchsia — carried from the Brazils in a biscuit-crate by the rigger Doyle for his wife Peg in the 1780s, sold as guinea cuttings by the nurseryman Lee, blooming at the Prospect's window ever since; the Hannah of 1888 presses a rooted cutting on Su the winter after the alley, a gift with no questions in it. And the gin thread: genever poured onto the flagstones in grief in 1626, drunk patriotic under a Dutch king, throttled by the Gin Act, and finally married to quinine in 1858 — the gin and tonic, the one imperial institution in the book still in daily service.

## The Timeline, Peopled

**PART ONE — THE VENTURE**

**1603 · The Boy Who Signed.** **Tom**, a boy with twenty pounds' bounty and a paper in his pocket, deciding at the bar whether to sign. **Maggie**, keeper — Margareta of the Stockholm coast, twice widowed, who gives him the odds straight: forty-three back out of five hundred. **Rook**, a gambler who puts a woman's debt on the table like a stake, and **Mara**, who refuses to be property with thrown ale, a kicked stool, and a mug across a wrist — fighting for herself, not for Tom. Maggie's one sentence raises the room. *Threads: the Keepers begin; the Cache begins as notches; the Johansson line is seeded in a single narrator's aside.*

**1603 · Dutch Courage.** **Hendricks**, the Dutch waterman who has seen the VOC's arithmetic up close and gives Tom the truer warning — that you can win a fight but not a Company — and who alone remembers Maggie arriving. Tom signs anyway, and leaves his mother's thimble with Maggie "till I'm back." He never is. *Cache: the thimble, first object in.*

**1626 · The Man Who Came Back Wrong.** **Daniel Vale**, factor, the one man shipped to Batavia while ten Englishmen were beheaded at Amboyna, home with the water still behind his eyes. **Nell**, his wife, who walked three years to find him and lets him be new. **Carter**, the ships' chandler whose grief and appetite share a table — the first of the Wapping trade families. **Pryce**, the Company's polite man with the polite offer. Maggie, older, still keeper, shows Daniel the gap under the counter. *Cache: Daniel's true copy, second object in. The gin thread opens with genever poured on the flagstones.*

**PART TWO — THE GALLOWS YEARS**

**1696 · The Price of a Man.** **Esther Finch**, lodging-house widow, who half-guesses her quiet upstairs boarder **Adams** sailed with Every on the Fancy, and lies to **Tanner** and the Company's bounty rather than be purchased — the month six substitutes hang at Execution Dock. She throws the Mughal gold into the river: deliberately not a Cache item. The river keeps it, not the building.

**1701 · Good for Business.** **Nathaniel Cross**, hiding under the name **Fenner**, a Kidd crewman in the crowd on hanging day, who overhears **Sillitoe** of the Court of Committees explain what the Company requires — a man, and an ending — and learns the exculpatory French passes were buried on purpose. The young keeper, trained in Maggie's line, tips her head at the gap. **Louie**, an uninvolved bystander, takes a flung chair across the head. *Cache: Nathaniel's testimony, third object in. The chair begins its two centuries in the corner.*

**PART THREE — KINGS OF BENGAL**

**1757 · A Soldier's Arithmetic.** **Sergeant Coates**, back from Plassey, weighing his pension against the truth of a battle that was bought before it was fought, and going on record with the Gazette man anyway. Clive and Mir Jafar at the edges, real and imagined within the record.

**1770 · What Mulvey Saw.** **Mulvey**, a Company clerk home from Bengal with the famine's arithmetic — the drought was God's, the famine was the Company's — who recognises **Aldridge**, a grain-hoarding profiteer, at his ease in the bar and confronts him. A **Lascar**, one of the thousands the Company ships and abandons, in clothes that aren't his. **Hannah** keeps the bar: the first we meet of the name the trade will carry for two and a half centuries.

**1774 · Too Big to Sink.** **Naylor**, tea-warehouse tallyman, thirty-one years of exact and ignored counts, ordered to tally the bailout hoard out to Boston — who writes his nephew **Ben** the warning that the cheapness is the hook, signed, knowing letters get opened. **James Holman**, American mate off the Nantucket oil trade, two years on the Company's own Canton ships where the coolie gangs renamed him **Homang** — a name given freely by men doing harder work than his own, and never corrected. His wrist, wrecked by a tea-chest around 1772, was set in a factory lane by the gentlest hands he was ever under. *The Canton thread's anchor: the foreign sailor in the lane is him.*

**1790 · Forty-Seven Days** *(interlude)*. **Alec Munro**, whose brother **Davy** is on the Bounty's list of the loyal but not on the newspaper's list of the saved — and who claims the connection out loud when a stranger asks the room. **Doyle** the rigger and his wife **Peg**, two springs back, put Britain's first fuchsia on a Wapping sill; the nurseryman Lee pays a guinea a cutting. Burke's losing prosecution of Hastings runs underneath, third year of seven.

**PART FOUR — THE POPPY**

**1839 · What Pemberton Called Trade.** **Mei**, searching London for the husband who never came home, who hears **Pemberton** say "Lintin" and "receiving ship" and understands at once what his cargo is and what it cost her. **Dr Hale**, naval surgeon, three years off Canton, who once watched a lane's master splint the wrists his art had broken with a folded fan and strips torn off his own hem — the gentlest violence he ever saw — a master taught as his teacher's teacher before him. **Hannah** keeps the bar. *The Canton thread's middle link; the lopsided table takes its scar.*

**1858 · What Harding Would Not Say.** **Captain Harding**, home from the Mutiny, offered a DSO by a beribboned **colonel** on condition the official Cawnpore story holds — who repeats the true number in front of the journalist instead. **Hannah** — this one's line is "I pour drinks; I don't collect stories," and she collects them all. The Company dies by Act of Parliament; the gin and tonic is born the same year.

**1880 · The Hell Ship** *(interlude)*. **Coombs**, ordinary seaman of the Cutty Sark, who saw the mate Smith kill the seaman John Francis and watched Captain Wallace help Smith escape and then walk into the Java Sea — and who weighs the owners' blacklist and testifies anyway.

**1888 · The Watchman's Daughter** *(interlude)*. **Su Zhang**, nineteen, laundress, keeper of her father's four rules. **Wei Zhang**, chandler and master of the work, who carried the art, the fan, and the white bowl from Hong Kong. **Sau-Ling Zhang**, her mother, whose God keeps books at a loss, on purpose, forever. **Lee**, Su's brother, dead at seven of a fever the doctor did not hurry to. **Long Liz** and **Kate** — the real Elizabeth Stride and Catherine Eddowes, drawn from the record and extended with affection — customers first, then the third and fourth names on a list. **Dr. Reginald Cray**, Company surgeon retired, invented: what he was on the other nights of that autumn, no one — Su included — ever learns, and the case goes into the Thames unopened. **Erik Johansson**, four years later, up the shop steps with a sea-bag of salt-stiff shirts. **Hannah** presses a fuchsia cutting on Su across the bar. *Four threads pass through this one room in one chapter: the work, the Johansson line, the keepers, and Louie's chair, still propped in its corner.*

**PART FIVE — AFTERLIVES**

**1940 · A Warden's Watch.** **Gwen**, ARP warden of Stepney, counting twenty-nine thousand bombs on the docks, whose post is also a vigil: her son **Peter** is an AFS fireman inside the fires she counts. A scared young **Canadian** airman learns the room's oldest trade — courage borrowed across a bar. **Hannah** keeps the taps running under the raids. *Cache: Gwen's notebook, fourth and last object in.*

**2019 · What the Suit Didn't See.** **Hannah**, fourth of that name, who during a cellar refit finds the notches, the thimble, the true copy, the testimony, and the notebook — and leaves all of it exactly as found. **The suit**, who moves money from one column to another and keeps a slice, and sees nothing. An American tourist's husband wedges a coaster under a lopsided table, oblivious, and ends a 180-year wobble. And across the river in Bow, a great-great-granddaughter of a Causeway chandler and a Swedish sailor keeps three things she has never connected. *Every thread ends here, and only the reader — and the building — knows it.*

* * *

The people above who are invented, are invented entirely; the real ones — Kidd, Every, Clive, Mir Jafar, Bligh, Burke, Hastings, Stride, Eddowes, Smith, Wallace, Francis, Lee the nurseryman — appear only at the edges of the invented lives, inside the boundaries the record supports. The full accounting of which is which is in the Author's Note; the full accounting of when is in the Timeline. This map is only the room's own answer to the question it gets asked most: who else has sat where you're sitting?
