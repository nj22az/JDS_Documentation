# 1790: Forty-Seven Days

## I. The News

It reaches the bar the way most news reaches the bar — a sailor with a newspaper three days old, reading it aloud badly, stumbling over the long words because he never had much schooling and the printer never met a sentence he couldn't extend.

Captain Bligh, he announces, is alive.

Nobody asked whether Captain Bligh was dead, but the room goes quiet anyway, because a sentence like that promises a story, and a bar on a wet March evening will take a story from anywhere it can get one.

Cast off the Bounty back in April last, the sailor reads, squinting. Him and eighteen men, put over the side in an open boat by his own crew, with a sight of biscuit and a few casks of water. And now word's come — landed at Portsmouth, alive, every man with him alive but one.

* * *

## II. Alec Munro

He's been at the bar since before the sailor came in with his newspaper, nursing an ale that's gone flat in the time it's taken him to drink it, and if anyone in the room had reason to look at him twice they'd see a young man doing a very poor impression of someone who doesn't care about the gossip.

His brother signed aboard the Bounty three years back in Deptford, seventeen years old and mad for the sea the way younger brothers get mad for whatever their older brothers haven't already claimed — Davy, quick with a rope, quicker with his mouth, the kind of green hand a hard voyage either makes or breaks. Alec hasn't had a letter in over a year. Nobody's had a letter from that ship in over a year; that's rather the trouble with a voyage to the far side of the world.

So when the word Bounty comes up in a Wapping bar on a wet Tuesday, Alec Munro sets his ale down very carefully, the way a man sets down something he doesn't trust his hand to hold otherwise, and listens to every word of it like his life depends on the arithmetic — because in a way he hasn't yet let himself say aloud, it might.

* * *

## III. The Arithmetic

A boat, someone repeats. How big a boat?

The sailor checks the paper. Twenty-three foot.

Twenty-three foot. The word goes round the room like a coin. There are men in this bar who have worked barges longer than that just to cross the Pool of London, let alone an ocean.

An old waterman at the end of the bar, the kind of man who has spent forty years reading tides the way other men read faces, does the sum out loud before anyone asks him to. Twenty-three foot, nineteen men, and whatever they could carry between them. He shakes his head slowly. That boat wouldn't ride a foot of freeboard with all that weight in it. One bad sea and they're feeding fish, every one of them.

How far did they sail her? someone asks.

The sailor finds the number in the paper, mouths it twice before he trusts himself to say it aloud. Three thousand six hundred miles. Near enough.

The bar goes properly silent this time. Three thousand six hundred miles is most of the way round the world by some men's reckoning, and they did it in an open boat with a compass, no charts worth the name, and — the sailor reads on — six ounces of bread and a gill of water a day between them, rationed out by the captain himself with a pair of scales he'd rigged from a pair of pistol balls and some string.

Forty-seven days, the paper says. Landed at Timor half-starved and half-drowned, all of them, and not a man of the eighteen lost to the sea. One killed by natives on some island early on. The rest walked off that boat on their own legs.

Alec says nothing through any of it. The arithmetic on the table is Bligh's arithmetic — the eighteen men who stood by him, not the deck Davy stayed on, which by every account not yet printed still has no arithmetic done for it at all. Eighteen men accounted for tells him nothing about the nine or ten who didn't sail with them. It only tells him there's a second sum still waiting to be done somewhere out past all this counting, and nobody in London has the numbers for it yet.

* * *

## IV. The Company Men

Three Company men have been at their usual table through all of this, more interested in their supper than the news, until the old waterman's numbers reach them.

Forty-seven days on six ounces of bread, one of them says, mostly to his companions. There are factors in Bengal who'd call that a light diet for a man doing nothing more strenuous than sitting at a desk.

His companion, older, laughs without much humour in it. Discipline, he says. That's what carried them. A captain who holds the line on rations when the men are half-mad with thirst — call it cruelty if you like. It's the only thing standing between order and everyone drinking themselves dead by the second week.

You'd know, the first one says, and there's an edge under it that might be a joke and might not be.

I would, the older man agrees, unbothered. I've watched men die of thirst in Bengal who had a river not twenty feet from them, because nobody with the authority to ration it did. Half of what your newspapers call tyranny is just a man doing the arithmetic nobody else wants to do.

* * *

## V. The Tyrant Question

Word gets round the room, as it does, that the men who cast Bligh off did it because they couldn't stomach him a day longer — his tongue, mostly, by every account that's reached London so far, rather than his fists. He wasn't a flogging captain, not by the standards of the service. He was a man who could take the skin off you with words instead, and do it in front of the whole crew, and do it again the next morning as though nothing had been said.

The old waterman has opinions about this too, though nobody asked. A man can survive the lash, he says. Takes a particular kind of cruelty to make nineteen men choose an open boat and near-certain drowning over one more week under your command. That's not a captain the men turned on lightly. That's a captain the men decided the ocean was safer than.

And yet he got them to Timor, the younger Company man says. Every one of them. Whatever he did to earn that mutiny, he did something right after it too.

Both things can be true, the old waterman says. Doesn't happen to make either one of them false.

He looks round the room then, the way a man does when a thought's occurred to him mid-sentence. Anybody here have kin that sailed on her? No more weight on it than he'd put on asking about the tide table. Ship that size, three years out — somebody in a room this size is bound to have a nephew or a cousin aboard her.

Nobody answers straightaway. Alec Munro's hand goes still around his tankard, and for one long moment the safest thing in the world would be to say nothing at all — to let the question pass the way a hundred questions pass through a bar full of strangers every night. It would cost him nothing. Nobody here knows Davy's name. Nobody would ever connect a quiet man nursing a flat ale to a story about mutiny that hasn't even settled into its final shape yet.

My brother, he says instead. Signed aboard her in Deptford. Three years back.

The room's attention shifts to him the way attention shifts in a bar — briefly, more curiosity in it than judgement. Which side of it did he end up on? the old waterman asks. Not unkindly. Simply the next question, the one that matters more than any other question asked in this room tonight.

I don't know, Alec says, and it is, he realizes as he says it, the truest sentence he's spoken all year. I don't know if he's one of the eighteen who lived, or one of the others, wherever they've got to, or a dead man on some island nobody in London's ever heard of. I only know he was aboard her, and now she's back, and he isn't on the list of names in that newspaper.

Nobody has an answer for that. It isn't the kind of thing a bar can fix with an opinion. The old waterman, who has had an opinion about everything else tonight, simply nods once and lets the silence stand, which is, in its way, the most respectful thing anyone does for Alec Munro all evening.

* * *

## VI. What the Bread Was For

Someone near the fire, half in his cups, asks what in God's name they were doing out there in the first place, that far from anywhere, in a ship that size.

Breadfruit, the sailor with the newspaper says, still reading. That's what the whole voyage was for. Sailed clean round the Horn near enough to Tahiti to fill the hold with breadfruit saplings, meant to carry them on to Jamaica. Cheap food for the plantations, so the paper says. Feed the slaves on something that doesn't cost the planters a shilling more than the growing of it.

The older Company man does not react to this the way the younger one does. The younger one goes quiet, turning his cup slowly. The older one simply nods, as if this were the least surprising fact in the whole story.

Sound economics, he says. Every voyage the Crown funds these days has a ledger under it somewhere, whatever the newspapers dress it up as. A man drowns for king and country. The king and country, as it happens, mostly wanted cheaper bread for men in chains three thousand miles from here who were never asked whether they wanted it grown.

Nobody at the Company table finds this worth further comment. They finish their supper. The bar goes back to arguing about rations and tyranny and whether nineteen men in an open boat is the greatest feat of seamanship anyone here has ever heard tell of, and not one voice in the room spends any more of the evening on the plantations, or the men in chains, or what the breadfruit was actually for. That part of the story doesn't carry the way the boat does. It never really has.

* * *

## VII. What the River Doesn't Carry

The newspaper gets passed from table to table until the print wears soft at the folds and somebody finally uses it to light a pipe. The talk moves on, the way talk does, to the price of coal and a dog fight promised for Saturday and whether the frost will hold.

Alec Munro leaves before any of that, out into the wet dark, no wiser about his brother than he was when he came in — only wiser now about the shape of the not-knowing, which is its own kind of arithmetic, and the only kind anyone in London can give him tonight.

Outside, the Thames does what it has always done, indifferent to which of tonight's arguments will still be remembered by anyone in a week — the mutiny or the mercy, the tyranny or the arithmetic, the twenty-three-foot boat or the three thousand miles behind it. It carries the ordinary traffic of a Tuesday night same as any other: coal barges, a late wherry, the tide turning on schedule whether anyone in this bar has settled the question of Captain Bligh's character or not.

It has never once cared how a story ends, only that something is always moving through it toward the sea.
