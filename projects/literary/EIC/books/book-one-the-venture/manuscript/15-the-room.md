# The Room

Above the wood store the candle burns all night, every night, because the dark is where the room waits, and Daniel has learned to ration the dark the way he rations everything now.

The room is coral stone and has no window, and it is three years ago, and it is also always.

There is a table, and a man behind the table who asks the questions, and a clerk beside him who writes down the answers, and the writing is the part Daniel cannot stop watching, even now, even here — the pen moving patiently whether he speaks or screams, because the paper does not need him to speak. The paper was finished before he was brought in. His answers are a formality the ink is waiting to receive.

The question is always the plot. Who ordered it. When it was to be. Who else. There is no plot, Daniel says, and the man behind the table nods, unhurried, unoffended, the way a man nods at a sum that hasn't come out right yet, and the cloth goes over Daniel's face again, and the water comes again.

Nobody who has not had it done can be told what it is. It is not like drowning; it is drowning, on dry land, in a chair, at a speed of another man's choosing — the body's whole animal machinery screaming its one message while somewhere above the water a voice keeps asking, mild as a schoolmaster, who ordered it, when it was to be, who else. Then the weight on his belly, and the water coming back up, and the room again, and the pen, patient, waiting. Then again. Hours are the wrong measure. There are only two times in the room: the water, and the question.

Through the wall, for two days, he can hear Towerson. Not words. Just the fact of him — the best man Daniel has ever served under, going the way everyone goes in that room, by lengths. On the third day the wall goes quiet, and the quiet is worse, and some days later — he has lost the count by then, the one thing he has never lost before or since — they come in and look at him the way men look at stock, and one of them says a word to the other, and the word is Batavia, and no one ever tells him why. He has been trying to work it out for three years: what he said, or didn't; what he was worth, or wasn't; why the axe for ten men and the boat for him. There is no answer. It was an entry in a ledger he will never see, made by a clerk with a patient pen, and it is the only mercy anyone showed at Amboyna, and it was not mercy. It was paperwork.

* * *

In Wapping the room keeps its own hours.

It does not come when the pamphleteers would have it come, at the sight of a Dutch coat or the word in a headline. It comes on the small hinges: a kettle poured too long at the hearth downstairs, the particular patient scratch of Mara's pencil against the slate, a chair scraped over flagstones in a certain way, wet cloth wrung out. The first week, Maggie's girl doing the washing sends him out of the yard white and blind, and nobody understands, least of all him, until he is sitting on the stairs with his head between his knees hearing a voice — his own, apparently — explaining to nobody that it was the sound of the water going back into the bucket, that in the room they poured it back into a jar between times, that the pouring meant it was going to begin again. After that, Mara does the wringing when he is out, without being told, without telling anyone she has changed the order of her mornings. He never learns she knows. She never learns he never learns. The house absorbs the arrangement the way the house absorbs everything, silently, into the general fabric of its keeping.

He tests himself against the room the way you test a healing wrist: small weights first. He can say Amboyna now. He can hear it said. He can sit with his back to the door for the length of a meal, on the good days, if the door is Maggie's. What he cannot do, and has stopped attempting, is the count. Every man who has been at sea can count seconds honestly, a skill beaten into middies with a log-line, and Daniel could count with the best; and in the room he counted, because counting was the one thing they could not take off him, the proof there was still a clerk in the chair and not only an animal — one, two, three against the water, numbers laid like planks over the screaming — and somewhere in the second day the numbers went, all of them at once, and he has never got them back past ten. He keeps the Company's ledgers at Surat for two years after with a boy to do the sums. Nobody asks why. The Company is not particular about faces, and it had, in his case, particular reasons not to be particular.

The candle burns down. The tide turns below the window — his window; there is a window; he has counted its panes more times than there are panes — and the grey light comes up the river the way it does, and the room lets him go for another day, the way it does, keeping its lease, promising nothing.

Downstairs, the smell of bread. Mara's pencil. The bolt going back. A woman's voice, level, foreign, twenty years unsurprised, saying something ordinary to the morning.

He gets up. That is the whole of what can be said for a long time, and the house says it for him, daily, in its own dialect: he gets up, and the room he comes down to has windows, and the water in it is for washing, and the only pen at work is keeping honest score against the brewer, and no paper in the building was finished before he arrived.
