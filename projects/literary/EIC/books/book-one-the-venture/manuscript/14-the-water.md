# The Water

It takes Daniel three days to talk. Three days of sitting, drinking ale he can't taste, flinching at the door. Maggie puts food in front of him. He pushes it round the plate. The other patrons give him room. The river does its business outside the window; the ale stays good; Daniel sits.

On the fourth day, Carter comes in.

He is fifty now, wider than ever, the yard behind him the biggest between the Tower and Blackwall, and he has known for three days exactly who is sitting at Maggie's counter, because Carter knows everything that moves through the docks; it is his trade and his nature and by now nobody can say where one ends and the other starts. He has stayed away three days, which for Carter is a fast of biblical severity, out of something he would call respect and Maggie, watching him finally come through the door, would call the respect running out.

He pulls up a stool. Orders two ales. Slides one across.

You're Company. Amboyna.

Not a question. Daniel doesn't look at him. Drinks some of the ale. They killed them, he says. His voice is flat, worn smooth from saying it too many times already. They killed all of them.

I know, Carter says, quieter than his usual, which is still not quiet. We heard.

They arrested us on false charges. Said we were plotting with the Japanese mercenaries to take the fort. There was no plot. They dragged us out at night. Ten Englishmen. Ten Japanese. Put us in a room and asked questions, and when we didn't answer the way they wanted—

The bar has gone quiet around them. Men who weren't listening are listening now.

Daniel's sentence stays broken where it stopped.

* * *

What he says, when he says more, he says in pieces over that evening, and the room takes each piece without moving, the way you stand still for a man carrying glass.

Water, he says. They used water. And lets the rest of the sentence go where the first one went.

Towerson was our head of factory. My superior. They took him first — two days before he broke. Made him watch the rest of it after. He told them there was no plot, that we were merchants, not soldiers. They'd already decided. Orders from Amsterdam. The charges were a formality. The questioning was procedure. The killing was the actual point.

How many? Carter asks.

Ten Englishmen. Beheaded. The Japanese were burned. I wasn't there for it — they'd shipped me to Batavia first, a prisoner, two years before they let me go. Then the Company gave me Surat: a desk, a ledger, heat enough to make a man want to die, and nobody who would say the word Amboyna out loud. And now home — as if home were a thing a man could still use, once he got back to it.

He drinks. His hands are steadier now — as steady as they're going to get. Something in his face has shifted. Not relief. More like a man who's opened a door he's kept shut a long time and found the room behind it exactly as bad as he remembered.

Carter, for once, has nothing to say. He looks at Daniel. At his ale. At the river through the window, grey and slow and not caring either way.

What he does instead is stand, walk to the counter, and take down the squat stone bottle the house keeps for the Dutch skippers' trade — genever, juniper spirit, the stuff the river's Hollanders take against the cold — and uncork it, and pour the whole of it, slowly, onto the flagstones. Nobody stops him. Nobody charges him for it either. It is a small, useless, entirely understood gesture, and the smell of juniper hangs over the room for an hour afterwards like a verdict.

At the far end of the bar, Hendricks sets down his cup half finished.

He is past seventy now, grey and stringy, and he has drunk in this room since before Carter had a shop — since before the Company had a ship, if the room wanted its history exact, which tonight it does not. He gets up without haste and puts on his hat. He is not angry. He is not anything a man could name. He has simply understood, quicker than the room did, that the juniper on the floor was poured at him too, and that there is no useful sentence left for him to say in here tonight.

Nobody stops him. Nobody looks at him going. Maggie, behind her counter, keeps her hands moving on the tankard she is drying and lets her oldest friend on this river walk the length of her room and out of her door under a verdict he has earned no syllable of, because the house's law, her own law, twenty-three years cut into the underside of the counter, says the room decides what the room decides, and a keeper who overrules her room on the hard nights soon keeps an empty one. It is the correct decision. She makes it with her hands full and her face still, the way she has made every correct decision that ever cost her something. The door lets in the cold and takes him out, and that is its own kind of verdict, and a colder one, because the men at Amboyna were Amsterdam's men and this one only ever hauled timber.

The smell of juniper hangs over the room for an hour. It will be a long generation before anyone in this house asks for the Dutch spirit by name again.

* * *

Carter watches Daniel a moment longer, and then, because he is a merchant and cannot help it even now, he starts — gently, almost kindly — to mine him. Which ports. Which of the Dutch factors gave the orders. How they took the fort, and with what, and how many soldiers it wants to hold a spice island against a company that means to have it. Not cruelty. Carter's grief is real. But grief and use live in the same room in a man like Carter, and the questions keep sliding, without his quite meaning them to, from *what did they do to you* toward *what can I do with it.* Daniel hears the turn happen. He has a good ear for it now; the men in the coral room had the same ear, turned the other way. He stops answering. Puts his sentences down where he put the others.

And Carter — this has never happened before, and the room, which knows him to the bone, misses it entirely — hears the turn too, half a second behind Daniel, in his own mouth, in his own warm voice, and recognises the sound of it, and where the recognition comes from he could not say: a wake thirty years ago, maybe, a hand shaking on a correct price. He shuts the great ledger behind his eyes. He buys the next two ales and asks nothing further all evening, a fast of severity his own wife would not believe, and walks home along the wall in the dark by the longest way, past his yard, where the coils sit pale in their racks, honest, holding or not holding, telling you which before they're paid for — and for the first time in a life of loving the goods he stands at his own gate and wonders, briefly, at the edge of the lamplight, what his rope has been holding, all these years, at the far end of the Company's arithmetic. The wondering does not survive the night. It never does, in men fed by the arithmetic. But it visited, and the visit is entered here, in the only book that takes such entries.

The Spice Islands are finished for us, Carter had said, at some point in the mining, by way of consolation or geography. Company's pulled out. Dutch have the forts, the soldiers, the monopoly. Nobody's fighting them for a nutmeg tree.

No, Daniel had answered. Not there.

Nobody at the bar asked him what he meant. The ones who wanted to know were afraid to, and the ones who weren't afraid didn't want to know, and between those two parties the whole trade of empire has always been conducted, at this counter and every other, with the ale good and the river going about its business outside the window.
