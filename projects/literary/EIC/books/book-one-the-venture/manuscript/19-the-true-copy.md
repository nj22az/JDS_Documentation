# The True Copy

The deposition he signed sits in a box in Leadenhall Street, and it is a true document, and it is a lie, and the space between those two facts is where Daniel has lived every day since he put his name to it.

Some weeks after Pryce, on an evening the bar is nearly empty — the frost bettor gone home solvent, the graveyard cough abed, Nell at the widow Prentice's mending by her own candle — he asks Maggie for paper and ink. She brings them and asks nothing, the way she brings ale to a shaking man; there are hours in this trade a woman learns not to put questions into. But she registers, with the part of her that has kept this room thirty years and misses nothing that crosses its counter, what the asking means. The Company asked him for his account and wrote a different one. The Company's man offered to buy the account outright, and was refused. Now the account has come, on its own feet, at closing time, to the only counter on the river it trusts. She sets the inkwell down as if it were ale, and moves off down the bar to let a man write.

He sets it down plainly, the thing the clerk's pen would not receive. Ten Englishmen and ten Japanese. Towerson two days under the water before he broke, and made to watch the rest of it after. The coral room with no window, the cloth, the patient pen that was finished before he was ever brought in. The word Batavia, and no reason given for it, then or since. At the foot he writes his whole name, unshortened, because he has signed enough lies to know exactly what one is worth as a witness, and this is meant to be the other thing. It takes him most of the evening, and when it is done he has told the truth entire exactly once, on one sheet of paper, in a language no Company clerk can summon him back to amend.

Then he does not know what to do with it. He has just refused good money to keep this quiet, and here he sits having turned the quiet into an object he must now hide or burn — a true account with nowhere to go being only a heavier kind of the very thing Pryce came to buy.

Maggie watches him from the far end, drying the last of the tankards, and conducts, in the time it takes to dry two of them, the largest deliberation of her keeping life.

Twenty-three years, no one has been shown the gap. Mara knows it exists, the way a first mate knows the master's private locker exists: position, never contents. The notches are Maggie's own, cut for her own reasons, in her own tally-script; the thimble is a wager held for a dead man; what else has drifted back against the old wood in a quarter-century of keeping is the house's, and the house does not publish. She has never once thought of the gap as anything but hers — and discovers now, watching a ruined honest man fold a true paper smaller and smaller because the world has no drawer that will take it, that she has been wrong about that for some time. The gap is not hers. She is its keeper, which is a different office. A keeper's own box lives upstairs, with the lace and the ring and the letters from Karlskrona. The gap under the counter belongs to the room — to the boys who went out of it and the accounts that could not be settled in it — and the room has just produced, at her counter, at closing, the truest account it has ever been owed.

She takes the rag off her shoulder and wipes a length of counter that does not need it, and comes down the bar, and without looking at him tips her head at the underside of the counter, where the wood has a gap a hand can just fit into.

He ducks and reaches and finds it already tenanted — a crowd of small notches worn soft at the edges, some scored across with a fine second line whose meaning he does not know; and pushed back where a draught has kept it dry, something small and metal his fingers touch and, understanding it is not his to know, leave alone.

What a house keeps is the house's own business, Maggie says, to the counter, to the room, to the twenty-three years, in the voice she prices barrels in. And has been longer than you've been alive.

He wedges the paper back against the old wood, beside the small metal thing he did not look at, where a hand would have to know to look for it. It will not raise Towerson off that beach; it is not justice, nor anything with a name so large. But it is the true copy, lodged where no Company pen can reach in and stand it on its head, and that is more than the box in Leadenhall Street will ever hold. He straightens, and something in him that has been on its feet since the coral room sits down at last, a little.

* * *

She locks up alone that night, Mara gone up before her, and stands a moment at the dark counter with her palm flat on the wood, above the place.

A thimble and a testimony. A boy who went out and a man who came back. Between them, in the dark, the notches — hers, the count she keeps because somebody must, the ledger of the priced-and-shipped that no counting-house on earth maintains. She thinks, unavoidably, being the age she now is, of the question that has begun arriving with her knees on the cellar steps: what becomes of a house's keeping when the keeper stops. The gap does not answer. Gaps never do; that is their office; they keep, and keeping is silent, and the silence is the whole service. But standing there in the dark above two objects and a crowd of marks, Margareta of the northern water, keeper of an English room, makes the decision she has been circling since the letter came down the tide from Karlskrona and her own column reopened at a gain: that the trade will be taught. Formally. To hands young enough to outlast her, chosen with the whole of her judgement, bound to nothing but the room. Not because she is sentimental about counters. Because she has just watched the Company file its version of ten dead men, and watched her own counter quietly out-archive Leadenhall Street, and a woman who has seen that once does not leave the succession of the truer library to chance.

The tide turns at the bottom of the stairs. The house holds what it holds. In the morning she will speak to Mara about a girl.
