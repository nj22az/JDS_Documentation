# Carter's Rope

The wake is held in the tavern by the stairs, because old Carter drank there forty years and it would have been eccentric to mourn him anywhere else.

The son stands where the father used to sit. Twenty-six, big already in the shoulders and the voice, in his good coat, receiving the parish. He has buried the old man that morning in the churchyard where the Carters go, and he has done it properly — paid bearers, a real sermon, drink for every man who ever hauled a coil for the family — and the room approves of him for it out loud all afternoon, and he receives the approval the way he receives everything: fully, warmly, with both hands, at slightly too great a volume for the occasion.

And in the middle of it — in the actual middle, between one condolence and the next, with his father four hours in the ground — a mate off a Baltic trader edges up and asks, apologetic, whether the yard could still supply two hundred fathom of two-inch by Thursday, the old man having promised it before he was taken bad, and young Carter hears his own voice say yes, Thursday, and the tar with it, and hears himself name a price, a good price, a correct price, and watches his own hand shake on it.

He is ashamed. He goes on doing it. Three more orders find him before the candles are lit, drawn to him through the crowd of mourners the way water finds a hole, because the waterfront cannot stop needing things and everyone in this room is the waterfront, and by dusk he understands something about himself that he will spend the rest of his life deciding how to feel about: that grief and trade can stand in him side by side, at full strength, neither one diluting the other, all day. He loved his father entirely. He priced the two-inch correctly. Both these things are true, and they do not so much as jostle each other, and he suspects — looking around the room at men who are weeping with genuine feeling while keeping half an eye on which of the widow's effects might come up for sale — he suspects this is not a flaw in him particularly. It may be a flaw in everyone. It may be a flaw in nothing, and simply what people are, on rivers, where the tide does not stop for anything either.

Maggie, refilling him, says only: your father took his first order at his own father's wake. In that chair. She says it without softness, as fact, which is the only comfort that would not have insulted him, and he looks at her with sudden complete attention, and files the sentence where he files everything, in the great ledger behind his eyes that never loses an entry.

* * *

The shop takes him over the way weather takes a coast.

His father ran a tidy yard: rope, tar, canvas, tallow, hooks and twine, sold across a counter to men he had known since boyhood. The son keeps all of that and cannot leave it alone. Within a year there are two apprentices and a horse. Within two there is a second store-shed, leased from the wharfinger on terms the wharfinger tells his wife he cannot entirely reconstruct, having agreed to them somehow between the second drink and the third. Carter does his business everywhere, all day, at the yard and on the quays and up the ladders of ships and — chiefly, and by strong preference — at the tavern by the stairs, which he treats from the start as an annex of the shop: samples on the settle, a coil of new hemp by the door for men to put their hands on, contracts agreed by handclasp at the counter with Maggie or Mara as unpaid unofficial witness, an arrangement both women tolerate because he is honest, pays his slate on the nail, and brings custom in behind him like gulls behind a plough.

His secret, so far as it is one, is that he loves the goods. Not the money — or not only the money, whatever the room says when he is out of earshot — the goods. Rope is honest. It holds or it doesn't, and it tells you which before it's paid for. He says this often, to anyone, uninvited, running a length of new cable through his fists like a man reading scripture, and the thing that makes it bearable in quantity is that he means it: he will condemn a coil out of his own stock, at his own loss, on the feel of it, and has, and the waterfront knows he has, and that knowledge is worth more custom than any price he could post.

He courts the Company the way he courts everyone, loudly and with patience. Their ships victual at the bigger yards downriver, the established men, and Carter bides, and supplies the small stuff nobody fights over — twine, tallow, lamp-black — flawlessly, for four years, until the winter a Company husband is let down badly on a cable contract with the fleet already fitting, and someone at a counting-house desk says: what about the Wapping man, the loud one, the one who condemned his own coil. He delivers the cable in nine days, walks every fathom of it through his own hands first, and prices it — this is the part the waterfront retells — not low, but exactly at the market, to the penny, because a panic price gouged from a man in trouble is a debt, and Carter does not lend. After that the Company's boats are at his stairs every season, and the yard doubles, and the horse gets a companion.

He is not a deep man and knows it cheerfully; he has never had a thought about the Company's business abroad that lasted longer than the order it arrived with, and when the talk at the bar runs to the Bandas, to factors turned out and convenient fevers, he listens with his face and does his sums with the rest of him. Rope wanted in the Indies is rope bought in Wapping. That is as far east as Carter's conscience has so far had occasion to travel. The room does not hold it against him. Half the room is fed, one way and another, off the same arithmetic, and at least Carter does his out loud.

Only once in these years does anyone see the other thing in him. A boy is drowned off the stairs in the spring of 'twenty — a wharfinger's apprentice, fourteen, gone under a lighter in front of twenty men — and it is Carter who goes in after him, coat and boots and all, and Carter who carries what comes up, and Carter who pays, unasked and refusing thanks with something close to anger, for the burying. And it is Carter, that same evening, red-eyed at the counter, who quietly secures the lighterage contract the drowned boy's master had been minded to move elsewhere, because the master is in the room and in grief and in need of steadying, and Carter steadies him, and the steadying and the securing happen in the same conversation, in the same tone, with the same warm hand on the same shoulder.

Grief and use, side by side, at full strength. Maggie watches him do it and says nothing, then or ever. The room casts its parts, and it has cast this one young, with decades left to run, and how the part ends — what a man like that does when the grief in the room is finally his own kind, when the thing needed off a broken man someday is not a contract but the truth — is a question for another part of this book, and for a grey afternoon eight years off, coming up the river even now at the speed of the seasons, riding low.
