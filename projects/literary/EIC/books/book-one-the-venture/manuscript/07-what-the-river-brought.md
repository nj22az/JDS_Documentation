# What the River Brought

Two years is nothing on a river. The tide does it four times a day.

The bar does two winters and two summers the way it has always done them: barrels in, barrels out, the snow bettor losing in December and recouping in January, the tar arguers arguing, the graveyard cough by the fire getting neither better nor worse, which the room chooses to read as a victory. Mara learns the cellar, then the brewing calendar, then the quiet art of pricing a stranger before his coat is off, which she turns out to have arrived already knowing, having practised it her whole life for lower stakes than ale. The two women run the house now the way a good crew works a boat: nothing said, everything caught.

News comes up the river in its usual condition — wrong, then loud, then old. The new King makes peace with Spain, and the watermen argue about whether peace is good for trade with the fervour of men who will not be consulted either way. A third fleet is subscribed; a fourth talked of. The Company is becoming a fixture, an annual weather: ships out in the spring, prices in the autumn, boys at the bar with papers in their pockets in every season, and under the counter, low, where you would have to duck to see it, the crowd of small notches grows by ones and twos, cut always the morning after, always without comment.

Then, on a wet Tuesday in May, a boy comes running up from the stairs with the word that ships are standing in past Blackwall, Company ships, the fleet, the fleet — and Wapping empties onto the waterfront the way it emptied for the going, two years and a lifetime ago.

Maggie does not go out to the stairs. She sets the good barrel and cuts bread and stands behind her counter, listening to the crowd noise change shape, and Mara, watching her from the cellar door, understands for the first time that the keeping trade has exactly one hard day in its calendar, and this is it: the day the river brings the bill.

* * *

They come in through the evening in twos and threes, the returned. Thinner, browner, older by more than the arithmetic, with the particular bright unsteadiness of men who have been given their whole pay at once and cannot yet believe in floors that hold still. The room takes them in and stands them drinks and asks, and asks, and asks, and the questions have two sizes: the big loud public ones — what was it like, is it true about the elephants, how much pepper — and the small quiet ones, said close, one name at a time, which is the real business of the evening, conducted at the counter's end like exchange at a rate everyone knows and nobody posts.

Pell comes in at dusk.

She knows him before the door has shut: the two-fingered hand, the third voyage now behind him that he signed for in this room, cheerfully unable then to say why. He is greyer. The hand has learned new work-arounds; she watches it manage his hat, unconsciously clever. He does not join the loud tables. He comes to the counter's end, to her, directly, with the level walk of a man discharging the last duty of a voyage, and she knows what he is carrying before he opens his mouth, because men do not walk like that to bring you good news.

Mistress, he says. You'll remember a writer. Tom.

I remember every man who leaves a thing with me, she says. Say it plain.

Bantam, Pell says. The flux, first autumn out. He was three weeks sick and working two of them — kept the purser's books propped on his chest when he couldn't sit, because lying idle frightened him worse than dying, is what I think now, though none of us would have said it then. He wrote fair to the end. There's men out there with no line in any book at all. Tom's in two: the muster and the burials, both in his own hand but the last.

The room's noise goes on around them, huge and far away, like weather on the other side of a wall.

He kept my name off the bosun's lists, Pell adds, as though it has just occurred to him, as though it is not the thing he crossed the room to say. Twice. Small thing. Wasn't small.

Maggie stands with her hands flat on the counter. What there is to feel arrives in its proper order, which she knows by now to let it do: first nothing, a whited-out nothing like fog on glass; then the fact, taking its seat; then, last and worst and briefest, the picture — a boy with foam on his lip and ink on his fingers, working hard not to know what he knew, drinking half a tankard in one pull and stopping thinking and starting doing. She lets the picture sit exactly as long as it takes to breathe in and out once. Then she puts it where the others are kept.

Did he say anything, she asks, because the living always ask it, knowing better.

Pell considers lying, and she watches him decide against, and values him for it more than he will ever learn. Men with the flux don't leave sentences, he says. He had the purser write his father. That letter went with the Hector's packet; it'll be in Essex by now. He looked after his ink better than his boots. That's the whole of what I have, mistress. It was two years ago and half the world away, and I've carried it the whole way back because he stood a room's length from me once and bought a debt off a table with everything he owned, and a man remembers the room where he saw that.

He puts his coin on the counter for his ale. Maggie pushes it back with one finger.

Tonight the house buys, she says. For the carriage.

* * *

She does the thing there is to do at the end of the evening, when the returned have been walked home or bedded down in corners and the room is hers again. Mara stays without being asked, stacking, wiping, present, and says nothing at all, having learned from the mistress of the style that there are hours when company should have the manners of furniture.

Maggie ducks under the counter, to the low place, the gap behind the old wood.

The thimble sits where it has sat for two years, back against the timber, out of the damp, beside the crowd of notches. She does not take it out. She had thought — on the bad nights of the first winter, when the wind came up the river like news — that this would be the moment the thing was for: that she would take it out and hold it and let it be him. Kneeling here now, she finds the truth is otherwise, and simpler, and harder. It was never going to be collected. She knew that the night he set it down; she said as much, in the only words she had — repeating the wager without accepting it — and he heard her and signed anyway, and that is the whole transaction, complete as a paid slate. The thimble is not him. The thimble is the wager, and wagers stand where they were laid.

What she does instead is take the knife from her apron and put one small line beside the notch she cut the morning he signed. Not a new notch: a line across, a settling-mark, the sign her mother's people used on tallies at home when a debt closed. Nobody else on this river reads that mark. That is acceptable. Most of what is written in this room is written for the room.

Then she stands, and her knees inform her about the two years in their own language, and she looks at Mara, who is watching her with the mug handle worrying between her fingers the way it does when she thinks nobody sees.

He would have come back insufferable, Maggie says. Salt on his coat. Stories at my counter every night for forty years, each one longer than the last.

Yes, Mara says.

We are spared that, at least.

Yes, Mara says, and neither of them smiles, and both of them nearly do, and the grief slips into the room on the arm of the joke the way grief prefers to arrive among working people, decently dressed, not making a scene.

The fleet's pepper makes the subscribers rich again; the prices do what the merchants hoped; the Gazette-men and the ballad-men do the voyage over in ink and it comes out heroic, which the returned men in the bar read with great attention and no comment. In Essex, a granary-keeper who found his son a good opportunity reads a purser's letter and is not seen at market for a month. And in one tavern by the stairs the count under the counter stands at whatever it stands at — the keeper has stopped counting and never stopped cutting — with one small settling-line now among the many, the first of its kind, and not, the river being what it is and the Company being what it is becoming, anywhere near the last.
