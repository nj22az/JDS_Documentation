# The Rain

The rain has not let up in three days. It comes in cold off the North Sea, turns the mud of Wapping Wall to something a man could lose a boot in, and beats against the tavern shutters loud enough to talk over.

Maggie is up before the light, because the light in November is a late riser and the work is not. The fire first, raked back to its embers and fed until it takes; then the floor; then the barrels, walked and tilted and listened to, because a barrel tells you what it holds the same as a man does, by the sound it makes when you knock. The room at this hour smells of last night sleeping it off: old smoke, spilled ale gone sweet in the rushes, tallow, wet stone. She opens the door to the morning for the length of time it takes to throw a bucket of slops at the rain, and the river comes in at her — mud and tar and cold and the particular grey smell of the tide going about its business — and she shuts it out again the way you shut a door on a talkative neighbour. The river has been trying to get a word in all her life. She has never once needed it to finish a sentence to know what it was going to say.

The brewer's man comes at eight, complaining about the rain as though it were personal. The joiner's boy comes about the shutter hinge and is sent away twice for quoting a price no honest hinge has ever cost. A waterman she has known ten years pays down half of what he owes, unasked, which means his luck is in; a rope-yard lad is sent by his master to fetch the morning jug, and stands dripping at the counter while she fills it, staring at the shelf of pewter like a boy memorising a country he means to live in someday. The morning trade of a river tavern is not trade at all, mostly. It is the parish taking its own pulse.

She serves them all and hears everything and appears to hear nothing, which is the first skill of the keeping trade and the last one anybody notices. By noon she knows the rain will hold through tomorrow (the watermen's knees say so), that a collier is aground on the Blackwall shoulder (two lightermen came in laughing about it), and that a ship is expected up on the evening tide — one of the Company's, victualling for the spring fleet, and where a ship comes in, men follow, and where men follow they want a drink. So she has the girl from the bakehouse bring a second basket of loaves, and she broaches the better barrel, and she gets on with it.

Three years before, on the last day of a dying century, an old woman put her seal to a piece of paper — the Queen, Elizabeth, giving two hundred and eighteen merchants leave to trade east of the Cape. The old woman is dead now: gone in the spring, mourned, buried, succeeded by a Scotsman. The paper is doing fine. Papers usually do.

Maggie has opinions about the paper. She keeps them the way she keeps the good brandy, out of sight and for occasions.

* * *

She runs the place. Owns it, more likely, though nobody asks and she never says. Past forty, hands red from lifting and scrubbing, eyes the blue-grey of a cold tide. Not a face men call pretty. A face men are careful around. Before a man has his cloak off, Maggie has generally taken his measure and found the figure small. She has buried two husbands and talks about it as other women talk about the weather. Men die. You pour the ale, sweep the floor and get on with it. She will take the last farthing from a man who can pay and press bread on one who cannot, and dare either to remark on the difference.

Margareta, she was, from a fishing village outside Stockholm: one of nine, daughter of a net-mender and a woman whose hands were more brine than skin. She grew up half in a boat, married a fisherman named Johan and worked the northern water beside him — herring in their season, the long light in summer, the ice singing under the sledge in winter, a life you would not call soft and she has never once called sad. A slow illness took him: a winter, a spring, most of a second winter, the big patient man getting smaller in the bed while she salted fish to pay for candles. Their son, old enough by then to want a deck of his own, took the King's coin in the Swedish navy the summer after they buried his father. He stood in the doorway with his sea-bag and could not make his mouth work, and she did it for him — go on, then — because one of them had to be able to speak and it was never going to be the boy. Husband under the frozen ground, boy handed to the sea: Maggie did not stay to keep a house full of ghosts.

She came south on a timber ship, certain that a harbour city on a great river must need something a strong back and a level head could supply. It needed barmaids. In time it gave her an English waterman to marry, a good one, steady, safe as houses, and then took him off Africa when the river work failed and he signed deep-water. Two husbands buried; one son somewhere on a Swedish deck. She has learned to pour the drinks and not wait at the window.

To the river she is Maggie — an English scrap of Margareta, the only piece of the old name light enough to carry. Margareta stayed on the northern water. Maggie runs the bar.

What the bar knows about all this could be written on a farthing with room left for the King's head. The bar knows she is a widow, foreign, fair with the chalk and murder with the slate, and that when two men square up in her room she can end it with a look nine times in ten. The tenth time is what the poker under the counter is for. Nobody has ever seen her use it. Everybody is sure somebody else has.

The room has also, over the years, worked out her scale. English, level, for prices and verdicts. Silence, for offence — a silence with a draught in it, into which men find themselves apologising without being asked. And, twice in a decade, the old language: fast and soft, like water over stones, at which point the wise drink up and the wisest are already in the street. Nobody knows what the words mean. Translation has never once been required.

And once a year, at the Bartholomew-tide fair, she buys a ribbon. Good silk, a hand's length, chosen with twenty minutes of frowning, as though provisioning a voyage. No living soul has seen her wear one, and no living soul has asked, the asking being priced, by general agreement, somewhere above what any man present can afford. Where the ribbons go is her own business. Most things are. It is the one purchase in her year that serves no purpose at all, and the room, which loves her in the unstated way rooms love the women who keep them, is quietly glad every August to see her make it.

* * *

The ship comes up on the evening tide, as promised, and the evening fills the room, as promised: dockworkers off the wet quays with their hands like old leather, a pair of them already deep in the winter's standing argument about the price of tar; the man with the graveyard cough who takes the stool nearest the fire and pays in exact coin; the one who has sworn all week it will snow before morning and has money on it, and checks the door each time it opens with a bettor's honest anguish. The tar under argument is Carter's — the rope-and-tar chandler off Wapping Wall, a fair-dealing man with a yard, a young son at home, and a settled belief that the world holds together with cordage and clean paper — and Carter himself drinks one table off and lets the argument run, the argument being free advertisement. By the door, over a half-pint made to last, sits Ned, a young lighterman of the stairs, a year out of his apprenticeship and still paying rent on his stool with politeness. And at the counter's end there is a sailor nobody has placed yet: salt-stiff, quiet, two fingers gone off his left hand, come up alone from Blackwall on the afternoon tide, drinking slowly, in no hurry at all to be worked out. Maggie has priced him already, because pricing is the trade — deep-water, paid off, carrying something heavier than his pay — and has decided, in the same appraisal, to let him carry it in peace. Steam comes up off wool coats in the fire's reach until the whole room wears a low haze, and the candles burn holes in it, and the noise settles into the good workable roar of a house doing what it was built for.

He comes in around dusk, shaking rain off a cloak that has seen better years. Young. Twenty, perhaps twenty-two — near enough the age of her own boy, on whatever cold deck the Swedish Crown keeps him, and she notes that likeness the moment he crosses the sill and puts it where she puts all such arithmetic, face down, out of the working light. Tar beside his nails, a pale seam across one palm where a wet line once ran faster than his grip. Not a deep-water man, not yet, but no stranger to a deck; coaster wrists, she prices them, and prices them young. He orders ale, and she pours it without asking, which means he has been in before: three times this fortnight, always alone, always an hour of slow drinking with his eyes on the middle distance, always the pocket of his coat touched twice before leaving, the way a man touches a thing he is afraid to lose and more afraid to open.

She has his measure in other particulars too, because taking measures is the trade. He carries a second, cheaper treasure: a penny memorandum-book, wet at the corners, into which he writes words. He collects them off watermen and porters the way other boys collect scars — stops a man mid-fare to ask what the post is rightly called, and the hitch, and the difference between a wherry and a skiff, and thanks him, and writes it down on the spot, tongue between his teeth, whistling one thin note through the gap in his front teeth while he does it. The bar debated him for a week and settled, provisionally and kindly, on poet. He also tells jokes. They are terrible — long, honest, carpentered things with the ending visible from the start — and he tells them the way he does everything, leaning in, delighted, certain, and the room groans like a hull and lets him buy the next round, and he is somehow better liked for the badness of them than a wit would be for wit.

Tonight he opens it. He takes the paper out and smooths it flat on the bar and reads it the way a man reads a letter from someone he loves, or a sentence handed down by a judge.

Maggie finishes the pour she is on, sets the jug down, and comes over. She stands in front of him with the rag over her shoulder and her arms folded, and looks at him the way a mother looks at a boy who has just told her he means to run off and join a circus.

You are going to sign it, then.

Tom folds the paper and tucks it away. Tomorrow. Governor Smythe himself will be there. A governor and twenty-four committees. The berth is before the mast, common seaman, second voyage. Joint stock — means the risk is shared. So is the reward, if there is any.

If there is any, she says. Not a question.

The first fleet went out two years ago. Four ships. Came back with pepper. Real pepper, from Java.

She snorts. She has heard every version of this from every young man who has sat at this bar with a piece of paper and a fire in his belly. How many men sailed?

Four hundred and eighty, near enough.

And how many were dead before they rounded the Cape?

He drinks. Foam on his lip. I don't know.

More than a hundred. Before they had reached India. Scurvy, flux, fever. She says it without heat — the terms of a transaction. Those are the odds. Take note of them.

The investors nearly doubled their money.

The pepper is lovely, I'm sure. She leans in close enough that the grey in her hair catches the candlelight. How old are you?

Twenty-two.

Been to sea before?

Coasters. Newcastle twice. Calais, once. I can hand, reef and steer.

Your father taught you.

Tom looks at the paper instead of her. He did.

Right. She straightens up. So you know enough water to be useful and not enough ocean to know what is waiting. You are going to sign a paper that sends you round the Cape in a wooden box full of scurvy, to a place where the Portuguese and the weather and the food will all take turns trying to kill you.

Tom says nothing. The fire cracks, sends sparks up the flue. Down the bar the tar argument has reached its nightly summit, where both men agree the price is a scandal and disagree only about whom to hang for it.

What are they paying you?

Eighteen shillings a month.

You will see nine.

Tom's head comes up.

I know paper, she says. Nine to you. Nine to Master Harcourt until your father's account is clean. Food and clothes charged besides, I'll wager, so the number can learn to walk away while you sleep.

It is eleven pounds, six shillings and fourpence.

She gives him a long look. A debt exact to the penny is a debt someone means to keep alive. Your mother know?

My mother is dead.

Your father?

Was boatswain for Harcourt's Levant ships. Smyrna, Scanderoon, sometimes Venice. Twenty-three years. The last passage took his knee. Harcourt says stores were missing when they paid the ship off — cable, two coils of running rigging, a kedge anchor. Things lost in the storm that broke him. Things in his charge.

And now Harcourt has put money into the East India voyage.

Tom nods.

Same man, Maggie says. New seal.

He found me the berth. If I take my father's place, half-pay goes against the account.

Found you the chain and called it work. She wipes the bar in front of him. Not the pepper, then. What else is on the manifest?

Silk. Indigo. Cotton. Cloves from the Moluccas. Nutmeg from the Banda Islands. Worth a hundred times what they cost in London.

Cloves. She almost laughs. You're going to die for a clove, Tom.

Something flickers under his brightness. He knows he is about to do something foolish and is working hard not to know it. He drinks half his tankard in one pull. When he sets it down, he has stopped thinking and started doing.

* * *

She leaves him to it. That is the part of the trade nobody teaches and nobody escapes: you give them the odds once, plainly, and then you fill their cup, because the alternative is an empty room and boys who sign anyway, uncounted, somewhere down the street where the ale is worse.

At the far end of the counter she pulls the next jug and watches him in the long tin mirror of the pewter shelf. He has taken the paper out again. He is not reading it now. He is holding it flat under his palm the way you hold a bird still, or a wound closed, and looking at the fire, and being twenty-two, which is a thing everyone in this room did once and no one in this room would do again for pepper, silk, indigo, cotton, cloves, nutmeg, or the whole of the Governor's stock.

The rain keeps on. The man by the door loses his snow bet for the third night running and pays it, disgusted, and doubles it for tomorrow. The cougher by the fire coughs. The tide turns at the bottom of the stairs, and the ships out on the black water swing at their anchors to face the new direction, all together, obedient, like a congregation standing.

The evening might have stayed an ordinary one. Most of them do.
