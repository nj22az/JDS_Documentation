# The Sailing

The fleet does not go for weeks, because fleets never do. That is the first thing the songs leave out.

Ships are victualled, and then re-victualled, because the beer went bad in the casks; a mainyard is condemned and replaced; the muster bill is posted, taken down, amended, posted again. The Company's second voyage sits in the river through the turn of the year like a guest who has said his goodbyes and not yet reached the door, and Wapping, which lives off exactly this, feeds on it happily: every rope-walk working, every chandler's boy running, every bar along the wall full of men spending advance money that is not, when a man thinks about it, which he does not, entirely his.

The tavern by the stairs does its winter trade, and does it, this year, with four hands behind it instead of two.

Nobody discusses the arrangement. One morning in December the girl from the wager is simply carrying jugs with a slate pencil behind her ear, and the room, after a decent interval of sidelong looks, decides it has always been so. She learns the pour in a week and the slate in two, and the third week Maggie catches her correcting the brewer's man on his own arithmetic, gently, the sum done in her head while he was still licking his pencil, and the two women look at each other over his bent back with the particular satisfaction of a bargain neither will ever admit was one. She has a room upstairs, wages on Saturdays, and a bag that stays packed for another month yet, standing by the bolt, until one evening in January it is quietly not packed anymore, and no remark is made about that either, by anyone, which is the only condition under which it could have happened at all.

The big man comes in some evenings, stands at the counter's end, drinks his penny pint and goes. The room held its breath about it the first three times. By the fourth it had stopped noticing, which is how terms become furniture.

Tom comes back twice.

The first time is business: pay off his slate, collect a shirt Maggie had mended and would not be paid for mending, sit an hour with his ale and his lists — a sea-chest bought secondhand, a knife, a quilted cap, wool stockings from a stall his mother would have haggled at, and did not that thought arrive out of nowhere and sit down across from him like an old dog. He shows nobody the lists. Everyone behind the counter reads them anyway, upside down, professionally.

The second time is the evening before the fleet drops down to Gravesend, and it is the evening he has been writing in his head since the courtyard in Philpot Lane, and like everything a man writes in his head, it goes otherwise.

He had a speech. Something about the odds, and about how a man may know them and go all the same, and how that is not the same as not listening; something with an apology in it that was not quite an apology, and thanks in it that were not quite thanks; he had, God help him, polished it. What happens instead: the room is full, the night is loud, a fiddle has materialised from somewhere and is being played at rather than played, and Maggie has no time for speeches and less than no time for polished ones. She puts his ale in front of him. She looks at him once, all the way through, in the manner of a woman confirming an inventory. And the speech dies in him honourably, because he understands, one whole day early, that it was for himself.

Eat first, she says. It is stew night. You will not see stew again this side of Africa.

So he eats, at the counter, elbow to elbow with the two-fingered Dragon's man — signed again, sailing again, cheerfully unable to say why — who gives him, between mouthfuls, the whole of a survivor's wisdom: keep your feet dry as you can, your mouth shut as you can, and your name off every list a bosun offers to put it on, because no list a bosun keeps was ever kept for your benefit. Write small, the man adds, tapping the counter. Paper runs out before the ocean does.

Mara brings the bread. There is a moment — the room sees it without watching, the whole art of a bar being exactly this — when she sets the loaf down by his plate and the two of them are within a hand's width of each other for the first time since the night the coins rolled among the stools. Nothing is said. Something has to be done, so she does the thing she has decided on, having thought about it, on and off, for a month: she cuts him a slice, the heel, the good one, and puts it on his plate, and looks at him while she does it. It is not love and it is not owing. It is the thing between people who were in the same weather once. He has the sense — barely, and only because the eye she blackened has finally healed and taught him something on the way — not to say anything either. He eats the heel. That is the whole transaction, and both of them are content with the terms, and the fiddle scrapes on, and the evening folds them back into the general noise.

At the end of it, cloak on, at the door, he turns around once. Not for a speech. He has learned about speeches.

Mind the thimble, he says.

Mind your figures, writer, Maggie says, and does not look up from the counter she is wiping, and the door lets in the cold and takes him out into it, and that is the last this room ever holds of Tom entire: the latch settling, the fiddle finding its tune for six bars together, the smell of stew and tallow and wet wool, a heel of bread gone from a plate.

* * *

The fleet comes down on the morning ebb three days later, and Wapping turns out for it, because Wapping always turns out for it.

The stairs are packed. The wall is lined. Boys sit on mooring posts; a pieman works the crowd; the watermen hold their boats off in the stream, cargo forgotten for an hour, because there is no cargo on this river today that outranks the spectacle of it. And down they come off the Deptford reach with the tide under them and the wind on the quarter, one, then two together, then the last — four ships, sails filling and spilling and filling as the reach bends, colours out, drums somewhere aboard beating the hour or the mood or nothing at all, the water peeling back white at each stem. Whatever else is true — and this book is a long account of what else is true — it must be said plainly, once: a fleet going down on the tide is among the most beautiful things the world has yet arranged, and every soul on the stairs feels it, including the ones it has already widowed, including the ones it will.

The crowd finds its voices. Men bellow names at the passing hulls, and here and there a small figure at a rail bellows back, and which figure belongs to which name is anyone's guess and nobody's concern; the shout is the thing. The pieman sells out. Somewhere along the wall a woman is crying with a baby on her hip, and the woman next to her, a stranger, has put an arm around her without either of them turning their faces from the river.

Mara stands at the top of the stairs, apron still on, jug still in her hand, having walked out mid-pour with the whole room and thought nothing of it. She watches the second ship, because the writers muster on the second ship; she has taken the trouble to know that and would not confess it under oath. At this distance the men at the rails are the size of pen-strokes. One of them may be looking at the stairs. It is not knowable. She lifts the jug a hand's width anyway — a gesture between a wave and a toast, deniable as everything she does is deniable — and holds it until the ship is past, and then goes back in to finish the pour, because the room did not stop owing itself ale just because the world got briefly beautiful.

Hendricks watches from his barge, standing, hat off, the tide chuckling under the flat bottom. He is not sentimental about fleets; he has cursed this one's boats out of his fairway all winter. The hat is not for the ships. It is for the roll of names on board that nobody has read out yet, the one the clerks will total properly in two or three years, the arithmetic he gave the boy across a counter in November. He puts the hat back on before anyone can see, and pieces of his morning resume around him. Pieter does not stand beside him today. Some days are too plain for the dead; they come on the quiet ones.

Maggie does not go out to the stairs.

She hears it all through the open door — the crowd, the drums, the shouted names — and she stays where she is, behind the counter, wiping what does not need wiping, straightening what was straight, and if her eyes go anywhere it is once, briefly, to the window, and once, briefly, down, to the old wood under her hands, beneath which, in the gap, a brass thimble sits in the dark beside a crowd of notches, the newest of them still pale as a scar. She learned this the hard way, on another shore, in another language: you may wait at windows or you may keep a house, and the women who do the first do not always manage the second, and somebody must keep the house, because the boys who do come back come back to it.

Ships are loud going and quiet coming. It is the quiet you learn to listen for. She said it once, years ago, to a woman sobbing on her counter, and it was true and no comfort, which is the family most true things belong to. The going fleet takes its noise down the river a mile at a time — the drums first, then the crowd, then the gulls that followed the crowd — and the room refills, and the fiddle from three nights ago turns out to live above the cooper's and starts up again as if it had never stopped, and by evening the tavern by the stairs sounds exactly as it sounded a year ago, before any of this, which is to say: full, warm, loud and short several particular voices, the absence of which nobody mentions and everybody pours against.

By the window, the stool stands empty a while, its crack settled into shape. Then the room needs it, because rooms always do, and somebody drags it to a table, and it goes back into service among the living, which is what stools and rooms and rivers are for.

The tide turns at the bottom of the stairs. Out past Gravesend, in the widening grey, four ships lean east.

Part One of the venture is closed and entered: one debt bought, one girl on wages, one warning given and not taken, one name in a register, one thimble in the dark, one notch. The Company's books for the same period record four ships victualled and dispatched. Both sets of accounts are accurate. Only one of them will ever be audited, and this book is the audit.
