# Mara's Trade

The girl is in the yard when Mara goes out for wood, folded into the gap between the stack and the wall where the rain does not quite reach, asleep the way animals sleep in the open: all the way down and ready to run, both at once.

Fifteen, sixteen. Shoes that were made for somebody else and a lip split three days ago by the colour of it. Mara stands in the doorway with the wood-basket on her hip and does not wake her, because waking a sleeper like that from the front is a good way to be bitten, and because she needs a moment herself. It is like being handed a mirror with five years taken off it.

She goes back in, fills a bowl with the end of the stew, cuts bread, carries it out, and sets it on the chopping block with a knock of the spoon against the rim — a sound that means food in every language there is — and then withdraws to the far side of the yard and sits down on the step and looks at the river wall, presenting her back the way you present your back to a strange dog: I have teeth and I am not using them.

The girl eats the way Mara once ate. There is no other verb for it; eating is too calm a word.

Running from a master, Mara says to the wall, when the bowl sounds done. Not a question, and in the flattest voice she owns, the voice with nothing in it to argue with.

A silence with calculation in it. Then: from a place. In the Minories.

Mara nods at the wall. She knows the kind of place, and the kind of debt such places keep on their girls, entered in a book the girls never see, growing by rules the girls never made — bed, board, the dress you were ruined in, all of it chargeable, all of it compounding, a sum built to be unpayable because the sum is not the point; the owing is the point. She had five years of a cousin arithmetic. She knows it from the inside out.

There's a woman here, the girl says. They say she bought a girl's debt once. For a coin. They say the girl's free and works for wages. Her eyes on Mara now, sideways, hungry in the second way, the way food does not fix. They tell it in the Minories, that story.

Do they, Mara says.

They tell it like a saint story, the girl says, and then, with the terrible bluntness of the young: are you her?

* * *

And so, for the first and only time, sitting on a step in a wet yard with the river going about its business over the wall, Mara tells it — the whole of it, her version, the one nobody has ever asked for. The room downstairs tells the wager as Tom's story or as Maggie's, depending who is buying. Both tellings are true. Neither one is hers.

São Tomé first, because the girl should know what the sea costs before she ever prices it. A green island that smells of rain and cut cane, a father she does not remember, a mother who boiled sugar for the Portuguese until the sugar took her hands and then her chest and then the rest, a grandmother who buried her and looked at Mara with honest arithmetic and said: there is nothing on this island for you but what took her. So: passage. Taken honestly, from the first man, a Lisbon supercargo with kind eyes and a fair contract, half to be paid in service at the far end — and he would have kept it fair, she believes that to this day, which is the bitterest part of the whole accounting, because a bargain does not have to be broken to ruin you. It only has to be sold. The kind-eyed man's luck went bad in Antwerp, and his paper went to a factor to cover a loss, and the factor's clerk added carriage and interest in a fine secretary hand, and the second man sold it on a run of cards to a third, who added his losses too, because why not, who checks, and the third staked it against a Cornishman's winning streak in a dockside room in Wapping — and at each change of hands the sum grew and the girl attached to it was shown to nobody, because you do not show the cargo, you show the paper.

I could read the whole time, she tells the girl. Every bill of them. Not one man ever asked.

She tells the crossing plainly, which is the only mercy she gives it, and the girl listens with her arms around her knees and does not make the mistake of crying, which raises her further in Mara's ledger. She tells the doors: how you count them in every room before you count anything else, the doors and who is nearest to each, because rooms belong to people and doors belong to rooms. A door is not a way out. A door is a thing someone else owns. Count them anyway. Counting is the difference between cargo and a person: cargo is moved, a person keeps a map.

And the night itself, last, briefest, because it was the shortest part and everyone else has already worn it smooth with telling. A boy stood up for her who had no means and less sense, and she has long since forgiven him the rescue, because he paid honestly for it and because he is dead now, entered in two books far away, and the dead may keep their gallantries. A room stood up after him — for its own sentence, for its own woman, not for her, and she knew it at the time and it made the standing better, not worse: a room that stands for its own law can be lived in; a room that stands for a girl's face falls down again when the face changes. And a woman bought her whole compounded unpayable sum for one coin off the floor, in front of witnesses, and then — this is the part the Minories version leaves out, and it is the only part that matters — never once, in five years, called the coin to mind. Not by a look. Not on a bad day. Not in the currency of gratitude, which is the longest debt of all and the favourite coin of everyone who has ever done Mara a kindness before that one.

Free is not the coin, she says to the girl. Free is nobody keeping the account. Remember that when you choose where to run to. Run to the ones who forget what they paid.

* * *

The girl sleeps that night in the wood store, on sacking, with bread in her pocket, and is gone before light — properly gone, north, with a half-loaf, the name of a Whitechapel laundrywoman who owes Maggie a favour, and the small hoarded sum Mara presses on her at the yard door, which is not a loan, and is not charity, and is not explained.

The girl, being young, tries to thank her.

Don't, Mara says. Pass it down the line. That's the whole trade. Then, because the girl is looking at her with saint-story eyes again and it has to be stopped: I threw ale in a man's face and broke a mug on his wrist for my own reasons on a night I expected to die. The rest was other people and luck. There are no saints on this river. There are people who keep accounts and people who tear them up, and you may be either, and you will meet both. Go on.

The yard door closes. The bolt goes home — her hand does it without her; some habits are load-bearing and she has stopped apologising to herself for them.

Inside, Maggie is up, of course, at the fire, of course, having known everything since yesterday, of course, because nothing enters this house without the keeper hearing its weight on the boards. She does not ask. What she says, without turning around, is: wood store will want new sacking. And Mara says: I'll see to it. And that is the entire conversation, and both women hear all of it, the said and the unsaid alike, and the house settles around them toward morning.

At the counter, waiting for the kettle, Mara takes the mug handle out of her apron pocket and turns it over once in her fingers — old habit, worn smooth now, the glaze gone soft at the break like sea glass. The trade she was taught in this house has no name and does not appear on the slate. It is done at yard doors before light, in flat voices, in small pressed sums, in accounts deliberately forgotten. She was its cargo once. She is its keeper now, one of two. The river, over the wall, goes on carrying everything to the sea, including, by now, well started on her way north under a clearing sky, one girl with bread in her pocket, counting the doors of every house she passes.
