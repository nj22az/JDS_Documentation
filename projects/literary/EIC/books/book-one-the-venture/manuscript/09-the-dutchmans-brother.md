# The Dutchman's Brother

The news comes true the way he said it would, which is the worst thing news can do to a man in Hendricks's position.

It arrives in instalments, spaced out over years, each one small enough to argue about: an English factory on one of the Banda islands closed on a legality nobody can quote; a ship's cargo condemned in a Dutch court for a fault in papers that were faultless when they left London; an English agent dead of a fever that his shipmates, home again and drinking here, pronounce with heavy looks to have been a very convenient fever, very sudden, very Dutch. Each instalment makes the room a little harder to drink in if your vowels come from the wrong side of the North Sea. Nobody blames Hendricks, exactly. But the word Dutchman has begun to arrive at his end of the counter with a small new weight on it, an ounce or two of the Bandas in every use, and he feels the added freight the way his barge feels an extra passenger, and trims for it, and says less on the bad evenings, which for a man of his construction is hard work.

Once — one evening only, in all those years — it goes further. A broken trader, three days off a ship that came home without his goods or his partner, stands in the middle of the room and puts it to Hendricks directly, loud, with drink doing half the talking: your lot, your butchers' company, your brother's flag — and the room goes still in the manner of rooms deciding which of its two or three possible selves to be.

It is the old docker who settles it, without standing and without heat, from his stool by the fire. That man's hauled my timber thirty years, he says, to nobody in particular. His lot's a barge. Sit down or go home.

The trader sits. The room exhales into its cups. Maggie draws Hendricks his pint unasked, and with it, unprecedented, a measure of the good brandy she keeps for occasions, set down beside the ale without one word of comment, which is the most anyone has said to him all evening and the only thing that needed saying. He drinks the ale. He leaves the brandy standing a long while, the way you leave a hand resting on your shoulder.

* * *

The chest comes in the spring of 'fourteen.

A countryman finds him at his mooring: a skipper out of Amsterdam with timber for the yards, a stranger with a family name Hendricks half-knows, who has carried, along with his lading, a commission from a warehouse clerk on the Prinsengracht. There is a chest, the skipper says. Held these eleven years against a claim nobody made. Marked for a P. Hendricks of the London river, care of his brother. The warehouse is clearing its dead stock. He says that last part and then hears it, and has the grace to look at his boots.

Hendricks signs for his brother's sea-chest on the deck of a stranger's ship with the tide pulling at both their moorings, and pays the carriage, which the clerk's commission requires, and which is so exactly like his countrymen — eleven years, and the carriage still due — that he laughs one short laugh through his nose, and the skipper, understanding completely, laughs the same laugh, and they part on it.

The dead send their luggage last. He says it to Maggie that evening, the chest on the floor between them in the empty back room she has lent him without being asked for it, and a candle, and no company but hers, which he has also not asked for and which has also arrived.

You'll want it opened or you'll want it sunk, she says. Decide before you cut the cord. Not after.

Opened, he says. I sat four nights with what's in it. The chest can do me no worse.

* * *

It is an ordinary chest, and that is the whole cruelty of it. A spare shirt, gone to yellow, folded by hands that folded well — their mother's teaching, still legible in cloth after everything. A clay pipe, mended once with wire. A Bible in Dutch with the family's names in the front in three inks, the newest entry Pieter's own wedding that never happened, a girl's name half-written and scratched through, some story Hendricks never got and now never will. A knife. A whetstone. Sea-boots, re-soled. The kit of a man who meant to work, save, come home and be ordinary, which was always the whole of Pieter's rich-in-the-head plan once you boiled the shine off it: a barge of his own, a wife, Sundays.

And at the bottom, wrapped in a rag with more care than anything else in the chest, one stone.

Grey, sea-smoothed, the size of a goose egg, of no kind Hendricks knows, and he knows stone the way watermen know ballast. Nothing written on it. Nothing about it at all, except the wrapping, which says it mattered, and its position, which says it mattered most. A stone from an island, maybe. From a beach where something happened, maybe. The piece of the story Pieter could not say, maybe — or a keepsake of the girl with the scratched-through name, or a charm bought off a Java trader against fevers it did not stop, or nothing, a stone, a thing a sick man's hand closed on once and would not give up. Eleven years Hendricks has lived with the missing piece of his brother's last account, and here is either the piece or another absence shaped like it, and there is no one left alive to say which.

He sits with it in his palm a long time. Maggie keeps the candle trimmed and her opinions trimmed likewise.

You could ask after it, she says at last. There are Bandas men through here every season now. Someone might know the beaches.

He weighs it, and she watches him weigh it, and it is a heavier weighing than the stone accounts for. Then he shakes his head, slow, and wraps the stone back in its rag.

If I ask, he says, someone will tell me. Sailors are kind; they'll build me an answer out of whatever they have. And then I will have a story with an end on it, and the end will be theirs, not his. He puts the stone in his coat, on the side where the bottle rides, so the two of them can keep company. My brother got to keep one thing he never had to explain to anybody. I find I am not willing to take it off him now.

Maggie says nothing for a while. Then she stands, and collects the candle, and says, in the doorway, not quite looking back: my second husband went down off Africa. No body, no chest, no stone. You are a rich man, Dutchman, by the accounting used in this house.

It is the most either of them will ever say about what the other one carries. It is also, he thinks later, on his barge, in the dark, with the stone and the bottle riding together over his heart, more than most men are given in a lifetime of friendship, and he holds the thought the way he holds a fair tide: gratefully, and without once saying so aloud.

The Bible goes on a shelf in his room ashore. The shirt he wears, once, on the anniversary, and then folds away in their mother's manner for good. The pipe he smokes. The boots fit. That is what brothers are in the end, on rivers: a set of things that still work, a name in three inks, a stone with no story, and a man on a barge who is no longer the only one who knows there were two of them.
