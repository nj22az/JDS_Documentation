# The Walls Get Higher

Years pass. The bar fills and empties and fills again — same cycle, same ale, slowly improving.

Carter's back most evenings, treating the bar like an annex of his shop, greyer now and louder to compensate, and tonight he's got news, and Carter with news is Carter at full volume.

Company's moving everything to India, he tells the room in general. Spice Islands are finished. But India — India's where the money's going now.

What money? A dockworker, not looking up from his cup.

Cotton. Carter says it like the man's slow. Indian cotton — light as air, and it takes a dye like English cloth never will. The great houses have started asking after it by name: calico, they call the plain sort; chintz, the printed. Comes off the Coromandel coast, and the few men who got in early are quietly making fortunes nobody's put a proper figure to yet.

And nobody minds? somebody asks. The weavers, the wool lot?

Not yet, Carter says. Not enough of it comes in to frighten Parliament, nor to set a Spitalfields man breaking anyone's windows over it. That's a fight for our grandsons, if the stuff keeps arriving the way it's lately started to. For now it's only enough for men with warehouses to notice, and quiet enough that the noticing is still worth money. Which is exactly the moment to be in it.

Behind him, two men are arguing with real heat over whether a barrel of Carter's own tar was short-measured last month, and Carter, mid-sentence about Coromandel and calico, breaks off just long enough to shout that it was not short-measured, he weighed it himself, before picking the cotton thread back up without missing a beat.

Daniel isn't listening. He's at the end of the bar with Nell, and it is a different quiet than the one he came up from the ship with. Companionable. The quiet of two people who've said the worst of it out loud and are still sitting next to each other. They come in less often now — there is a cottage at Shadwell, and a garden Nell governs like a small firm colony, and two evenings a week Daniel keeps the books of three honest tradesmen who can wait for their sums while a man steadies his hand — and when they do come in, Maggie pours without asking, and the count, Nell has confided to her alone, across the counter, in the flat invoice voice the two of them do their real business in, has come back past twenty.

Rook does not come in anymore. He died two winters back, ashore, in a bed, of nothing in particular — an ending nobody on the waterfront would have priced, and the room drank to him once, briefly, without speeches, and his standing place at the counter's end took a month to stop being his. The wrist forecast rain to the very last, and was right the day before it stopped, which the room agreed he would have counted a debt properly closed. At Christmas the room waited without saying what it was waiting for, and the carol went unsung, and that was his stone.

* * *

That first winter after the juniper, Maggie had walked down to the mooring below the stairs on a Sunday, in the cold, with the good brandy in her apron pocket, and stood on the frost-slick planking until Hendricks looked up from his ropes and let the silence decide what it was going to be.

They drank it on the barge, out of his two tin cups, off the house's ground entire — which was the whole point, and both of them knew it, and neither said it. In the house, the room's verdict stood; her law, cut into the underside of her own counter, and she would not overrule it. On the water there is no house's law. There is the river's, which does not sort the guilty from anyone, and two old foreigners who have been keeping each other's names for thirty years drank good brandy under it in the cold and talked about ice — the state of the ice in the northern harbours this winter, the ice being very bad — and the ice stood guard, as ice does in her family's correspondence, in front of everything else, and did its office, and by the second cup there was nothing left in front of.

He still comes up the stairs on the quiet afternoons. He is old now the way barge-timber is old, past counting, still floating. He drinks his pint at his end of the counter, and at the end of it takes one measured pull from the stone bottle, which the house no longer stocks and he no longer offers to anyone, and now and then, on the emptiest afternoons, he says Margareta, and she lets him, and that is the whole of what the years have left of it, and it is enough, and they are both of them too old and too northern to call it anything.

* * *

The girl is called Joan. Fifteen, a lighterman's orphan, quick with sums and slow to speak, chosen by Maggie out of a winter's careful watching and vouched by Mara, whose reading of people is the house's other ledger. She scrubs, hauls, chalks, pours; that is the visible apprenticeship. The true one happens at the margins, in the trade's own dialect, one law at a time.

You do not ask a shaking man what he wants. You put ale in front of him.

Take the coin gravely when taking it is the mercy. Refuse it gravely when refusing is.

Night debts are paid in daylight, or they are not debts. They are weather.

Read the room before the man; the room tells you what he'll cost. Count what must be counted and keep the count where it keeps. And this, on a slow Tuesday, both of them ducked behind the counter over spilled tallow, the girl's candle throwing their shadows up the shelves — Joan's eye caught, for half a moment, by the low dark line of the old wood where something might or might not be a gap; and Maggie, watching her not ask, marked the not-asking with satisfaction, and said only, in the voice she prices barrels in: a house is kept by the next pair of hands. That is the whole secret, and it is not a secret. Mind your candle.

What Joan is being given, though she will not have the shape of it for years, is a library and its office. She will keep this room through decades this book does not reach; she will train, in her own late years, a small fierce woman who will train another; and somewhere far down that line of hands — long after every name in this part is worn off its stone — a keeper who never heard of Margareta will tip her head at the underside of a counter, on a hanging day, for a hunted man with a true paper, and the house will do again what the house was taught, this winter, by a woman showing a girl where the tallow went. None of them will know. Houses do not require their keepers to know. They require them to keep.

* * *

The Company keeps growing. Bigger ships, bigger warehouses, busier docks. Sir Thomas Roe secured the factory at Surat years back; Bombay will come later, a wedding gift from a Portuguese dowry that nobody at the time will understand the size of. The factories have started growing walls. Nobody in this bar has said it out loud yet, but a factory stores goods, and a fort controls territory, and the difference is starting to show in the letters home.

Just a wall, the letters say. Bricks and mortar. Nothing to worry about.

The letters arrive at Leadenhall Street and are filed. The other library takes no letters. It takes what it has always taken — notches, wagers, true copies, the priced-and-shipped, the debts kept open on purpose — and it holds them in the dark against the old wood while the walls out east get higher, and the room above it fills and empties and fills, and a keeper grown old shows a girl where everything is kept, and the river, which was here before the Company and will be here after it, turns twice a day at the bottom of the stairs, taking no position, keeping no books, indifferent as ever to everything this house has spent a lifetime refusing to leave uncounted.
