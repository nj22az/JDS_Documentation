# Rumour of Spice

It comes up the river the way such things always come: wrong first, then loud, then printed.

A whisper in May, off a Zeeland flyboat, passed hand to hand along the waterfront faster than any manifest — trouble at Amboyna, English blood, torture in it somewhere. Nobody can say what an Amboyna is. By evening half the room has decided it is a ship and the other half a man, and a waterman with a cousin in the Downs is standing drinks on the strength of the cousin having heard the same word, which settles nothing and cheers everyone. Maggie serves through it and believes, on principle, none of it. News is like cargo. What arrives first is what floats.

What floats, that spring, keeps floating. The whisper does not sink the way most whispers sink; it circles, and thickens, and starts agreeing with itself, which is the sign she has learned to watch for. Then the Company's own packet is in, and the word stops being a whisper and becomes an account: a factory on a clove island, ten Englishmen and ten Japanese soldiers taken up by the Dutch on a charge of plotting, questioned — the word the first accounts use is questioned, and the word the later accounts use is not — and executed on the beach. Heads off. A conspiracy confessed to under water and fire and signed under the same, which the dead men's own letters, smuggled out, swear in the margins they never made except to stop the pain.

By midsummer it is pamphlets, and the pamphlets are illustrated, and the illustrations are not careful, and the thing has a name that will outlive everyone in this room: the Amboyna business. The Massacre, the ballads say, needing the shorter word for the metre. London is angrier than Maggie has seen it since the old Queen's wars. Preachers take it up. Apprentices break Dutch windows in the Minories, windows belonging to weavers and sugar-bakers who could not find Amboyna on a chart with both hands, and the watch is doubled, and the Privy Council makes noises, and the Company — the English one, the one whose men are dead — discovers that a massacre, whatever else it costs, is worth more than pepper at the subscription table, and prints accordingly. Maggie reads the pamphlets the way she reads all cargo manifests, checking what is listed against what she knows of the hold. The dead men are real; Pell, who has touched at those islands, goes quiet in a way that is real; the outrage is real too, and also, she notes, very conveniently shaped, fitting the Company's older grievances the way a made key fits a lock. Both things true at once. She is fifty-eight years old and has stopped expecting the world to sort itself cleaner than that.

* * *

For Hendricks the summer is a long low room with no door out of it.

He is not blamed, exactly, this time either. Twenty years the room has known him; the old docker's sentence still stands over his end of the counter like a bye-law. But he feels the parish looking at him sideways to see how he takes each fresh pamphlet, and the truth is he takes them badly, and not in the direction the parish is watching for. His countrymen did this. He reads the accounts twice through, the confessions taken under water, the pen recording while men screamed, and he thinks of a fever-room off the Ratcliff road and a thing watched that could not be put into words, and for one whole evening he sits at his end of the bar with the stone bottle untouched and does the sum he has been declining to do for twenty years, the one where the missing piece of Pieter's last account is given a shape by other men's testimony.

He does not finish the sum. He stands up from it the way a man steps back from an edge, deliberately, one step. Then he asks Maggie, who has been polishing the same tankard for a quarter of an hour at his end of the counter for no reason either of them names, for the good brandy — the first time in twenty years he has ever asked — and she pours two, and they drink them standing, on the house's side of the evening, to nothing stated.

They will use the paper on us for fifty years, is all he says, at the end. Your Company. When it wants a war and cannot afford one, it will wave Amboyna. Watch.

The pamphleteers want the war now, Maggie says.

They will not get it now. Paper keeps. He corks the bottle with his thumb, old habit, though the bottle is hers. That is what your boy never heard me say, all those years back, because I had not learned it yet myself. I told him the Dutch would win the islands. True. Here is the truer thing: nobody wins a massacre. It goes into a drawer, and it works for whoever opens the drawer, forever. The dead are the only ones done with it.

* * *

Two years pass, and the drawer stays open on every counter in London, and the room learns to live with it there, the way rooms do.

The house is not what it was, and is more than it was. Maggie's knees have opinions about the cellar steps now, delivered daily; Mara runs the floor entire on the heavy nights, and the brewer's man and the joiner's boy and the whole morning parliament of the parish have made, each in their own time, the small silent adjustment of taking their questions to her first. Rook's twenty years are nearly run: greyer, slower through the door, the sling long gone and the wrist gone weather-glass — he can tell rain by it, and does, unasked, at the counter's end, and is more often right than the almanac. The snow bettor has passed his book to a son who bets on frost. The graveyard cough by the fire changed owners twice and persists, an office rather than a man. The room casts its parts, and the parts outlive the players, and only a keeper polishing the same counter for a quarter of a century is positioned to notice, and she keeps what she notices where she keeps everything.

The fleets go out. The fleets come in, or come in short, or do not come in. Under the counter the notches gain their ones and twos every sailing season, and here and there among them the small crossing lines, and the gap keeps what it keeps in the dark, back against the old wood: a wager standing, unclaimed, twenty-three years now, because the woman who holds it does not tear up accounts that are not hers to close.

And then, on a grey afternoon in the autumn of 'twenty-six, with the light flat through cloud and the river low enough to show the mud, a ship comes up on the tide, riding deep, sails slack, in no hurry at all — and word comes off it before the crew do, one name, passed hand to hand along the waterfront faster than good news ever travels, and the name is the one from the pamphlets, the one in the drawer, and it is walking up from the stairs toward her door in the shape of a thin man in clothes made for somebody bigger, and Maggie, watching him come through the window she does not wait at, wipes her hands on her apron and sets a tankard on the counter, because whatever the river has decided to bring her now, the house is open, and the ale is good, and that has been the whole of the answer for twenty-three years.

Part Two of the venture closes here, at the sound of the latch. What comes through the door is Part Three.
