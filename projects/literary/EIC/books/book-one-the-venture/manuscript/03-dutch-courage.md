# Dutch Courage

Hendricks has been on the water since before it was light, which in November is no boast, and off it since dusk, which is.

Timber down from the Surrey side, two loads; then hides; then a lighter's worth of barrel staves for a cooper who counted them twice in the rain, out loud, insultingly, and found the count good because it was Hendricks's count. Thirty years on this river. He knows the Thames between the bridge and Blackwall better than he knows his own hands, and he knows his own hands very well, because they are what the thirty years were paid into: knuckles like a sack of hazelnuts, a rope-burn scar gone silver, two fingers that ache before rain and have not been wrong about it since the old Queen's day. The river is not a mystery to him and not a friend. It is a colleague. They have an understanding: he does not tell it lies about what it is, and it has not yet killed him.

He walks up the stairs from his mooring with the day's coin in his boot and his thirst in its usual order, and pauses, as he always pauses, at the top, where the tavern's shutters leak their light out onto the mud. Inside there will be noise, and Englishmen, and the particular pleasure of a room that does not want to hear him and will hear him anyway. The bottle rides in his coat against his ribs, squat and stoneware and half full — genever, juniper spirit, the smell of his mother's kitchen and his brother's wake and every important thing that has ever happened to his family, all of it in one short bottle with a thumb-worn cork.

Pieter comes up the stairs with him most nights now. Dead men are light; they keep no schedule; they wait at the places you were last happy with them and fall in beside you without asking. His brother sailed east with the new Dutch company's second fleet, rich in his head, loud with it, and came home two years later the colour of candle-fat, and died in a lodging house off the Ratcliff road raving about islands, and about a thing he had watched done there that he could not put into words, though he tried, at the end, when the fever gave him no choice but trying. Hendricks sat with him four nights. He never had the whole of it. He had enough. There are words a man cannot finish and you learn the shape of the missing piece from the break.

He goes in.

* * *

The room is full and lively and bruised. He reads the bruise before his coat is off — furniture missing from the far corner, rushes scrubbed in a half-circle, a boy at the bar with one eye closing and a folded paper in front of him, the keeper watching the whole room the way she watches it on the bad nights, from behind stillness. Something happened here this evening. Something big, by the gap it left. He orders nothing, because he never has to.

An hour before him, the door had opened on noise of a different kind: five or six men clumping in from the waterfront, loud, salt-stiff, converting their wages into ale as fast as Maggie could pour it. One of them missing two fingers on his left hand and not seeming to have noticed, which tells you something about what he has noticed instead.

The Dragon's lads, one of them announced to the room. Just in. Lancaster let us go.

The room went quieter — not silent, quieter. These are men of the Red Dragon. The first fleet. Men who went out and came back, though more than a hundred were dead before the fleet had rounded the Cape.

They are not lying about what they saw. But they are editing. Pepper, gold, markets, colours — not the scurvy, the flux or the men put into the ground before India. You tell the story that makes the voyage sound worth it, because if it wasn't, what were you doing out there? Two of them are already arguing — loudly, and with real conviction — about whether the cloves from Ternate beat the cloves from Tidore, a question neither is remotely qualified to settle and neither will surrender this side of morning. A third sings the front half of something and hums where the words should be. A fourth drinks in silence and cries while he does it, without fuss, without shame, the tears simply going about their business while the ale goes about its, and his mates leave him to it with a care so complete it looks like ignoring. There is a manners to surviving. They have all learned it in the same school.

The one with the missing fingers notices Tom. The paper. The hunger in his face, visible even out of one working eye. He has seen that look before — probably worn it himself.

You're signing on. Not a question.

Tomorrow. Second fleet. Writer.

Good. We need men who can count. He holds up the ruined hand. Exhibit A. Knife fight in Bantam. Over a woman, if you can believe it — a woman and a sack of pepper. Don't get into knife fights out there, lad. The climate doesn't agree with bandages.

Was it worth it? Tom asks. The voyage. Was it worth it?

The sailor considers a question he has not let himself ask. The pepper was good. Very good. The rest of it — ask me again in ten years. If I'm still here.

Maggie brings bread and cheese to the Dragon's men. They fall on it like wolves. She watches them eat, counting the cost of pepper in men.

They were lucky, she tells Tom quietly, refilling his ale. Lancaster knew what he was doing. Most captains don't.

But the Company's sending the next lot anyway.

Of course they are. That's what companies do. Some come back, some don't, and the Company doesn't much care which — the Company cares about pepper. And money.

And money, she agrees. Money does not bring back the dead.

* * *

Hendricks takes his place at the counter's end, and the pint arrives without being asked and without warmth, which from her is a welcome.

You talk too much, Dutchman.

Only when the boys listen too little, Margareta.

Something in her jaw answers to the name nobody else here has heard. That name stayed north of the water, she says.

Then it travelled badly, he says, and drinks. They have been doing some version of this for years. He was on the stairs the day she came off the timber ship — a grown widow among the deck cargo, one bag, a face that had already decided not to be surprised by England — and he heard her name said properly once, by the mate who handed her down, before the river wore it smooth to Maggie. He keeps the original the way he keeps the bottle: for use on occasions, in measured pulls. She would deny that she permits it. He would deny that he rations it. They understand each other very well, two foreigners grown old on an English river, neither of them ever once tempted to say so out loud.

He drinks the house ale like everyone else, and at the end of the pint he takes one measured pull from the stone bottle, and the English at the bar call it Dutch physic and want nothing to do with it, and all of this is as it always is. What is not as it always is: the boy comes to him. One eye closing, split lip, carrying his ale down the bar with the careful walk of a man whose ribs have opinions, and sets himself on the next stool like a question.

You were on the river before the Company. That right?

Before your Company was a thought in a merchant's head. Hendricks turns his pint on the wood. I was hauling timber when the Portuguese owned everything east of the Cape and the English were not allowed within a mile of it.

And now, Tom says.

Now your Company wants to break that. Good luck.

They already have. First fleet came back with pepper. The charter gives us fifteen years. Nobody else trading east of the Cape.

Hendricks makes a sound that might be a laugh. On paper. And what happens when you get to the Spice Islands and find the Dutch already sitting on them?

The Dutch?

The Dutch. Last year, while your first fleet was still at sea, they chartered their own company. The VOC. Heard of it?

A little.

Not enough, then. More capital than you. Better ships. Better guns. And they are not gentlemen arguing over pepper in a London room. They mean to take the trade while you sit here with your twenty pounds a year and your good ale.

Lancaster was there, Tom says. He made deals.

Lancaster made deals. The Dutch made others. Hendricks picks up his pint, and for a moment the room's noise goes on without him. My brother Pieter sailed east with one of their fleets, rich in his head. He came home with a fever and died in a lodging house off the Ratcliff road, raving about the Company's islands and a thing he had watched but could not put into words. I never had the whole of it out of him. Enough, though. Do not mistake my accent for their flag, boy. A man may know his countrymen and know them for wolves.

What do they do?

When two charters claim the same island? Burn the post. Take the ship. Call the rival a criminal and the taking punishment. Paper is clean. Men do the dirty part.

The bar has gone properly quiet. Even the Dragon's men are listening now, pretending not to. The crying one has stopped crying, which is somehow worse.

Your fleet began dying before it reached the Cape, Hendricks says. Now you mean to trade where the Dutch are already buying guns.

Tom stares at his ale. The brightness has gone out of his face.

Hendricks looks at the blackening eye and split lip — the room's whole evening written on one young face, the bruised account of a story he arrived too late to see and has already mostly guessed. A big man in a tavern is honest danger, boy. You can see him come. A tavern may even fight back. He takes one measured pull from the stone bottle and corks it with his thumb. The thing you sign for in the morning has no eyes to blind, no wrist to break. No room has ever stood up to a paper. That is the whole difference between a fight and a Company. One of the two you can win.

* * *

The evening wears on. The Dragon's men get loud again: bad singing, one man crying quietly into his ale once more, his shipmates guarding his quiet like a coat they are holding for him. The snow bet is being tried for a second time — no snow yet, and the man who staked his supper on it is reminded at length.

Maggie brings Tom bread and cheese he didn't ask for.

Eat. You will not eat like this for months. My second husband told me. Salt beef till it walks off the plate.

Your second husband.

Died six years back, off Africa. Never found the body. My first was a fisherman, and a sickness took him in his own bed. My second was a riverman, safe as houses, and the sea reached all the way to Africa for him. It has a humour to it. Just not a kind one.

Tom eats. Outside, the ships at anchor are dark shapes on darker water, masts swaying, rigging making small sounds like men who cannot sleep.

Hendricks has gone silent at his end of the bar. The boy will go. The boy will sign. He is twenty-two, and the Dutch are still only a story. Hendricks has watched this exact evening happen more times than he has fingers left to count it on, and he has learned what the warning is actually for. It is not for stopping them. Nothing stops them; the ships would sail empty otherwise, and they never sail empty. It is so that later, on some deck at the wrong end of the world, in the moment the story stops being a story, one of them remembers that somebody told him, and knows the thing that is happening has a name, and was known, and was survivable enough to be reported in a bar by a fat Dutchman who got old. That is all the warning does. It is not nothing. Pieter had nobody to tell him, and Pieter, at the end, could not stop trying to be the somebody, four nights running, with the fever eating his sentences from the far end.

Maggie comes over one last time, refills his ale.

The charter was signed on New Year's Eve, she says. Three years back. Old Queen Bess is dead, and the Company she signed into being is still here, hiring. The queen is gone. The paper is not. She signs one. You sign another. Somewhere between the two, men die. The pepper comes back. The money comes back. The men do not.

He opens his mouth. Nothing comes out.

What he cannot say is that it was never the pepper. The brown millpond behind the Essex granary was, for ten summers, both a stretch of water a strong man could throw a stone across and the whole ocean. It had serpents beyond the map, a pirate cave under the willow roots and wreckers' fires on the far bank. He has always wanted to come through a door like this with salt on his coat and stories nobody else could tell. It is a child's answer to more than a hundred dead before the Cape. It is also the truth.

Then, instead of anything he might have said, he does something instead. Reaches into his coat and comes out with the one thing in it worth more than the coat — a plain brass thimble, his mother's, the only piece of her he kept when there was nothing else worth the keeping. He sets it on the bar between them, not quite meeting her eye.

Keep this, he says. Till I'm back.

Maggie looks at the thimble, then at him. She has held out her hand for enough keepsakes to know what it costs a man to leave one. She closes her fist around it as she would a coin meant to be kept apart from the till.

Till you are back, she says, repeating the wager without accepting it.

He finishes his ale, stands, puts on his cloak.

Hendricks stops him at the end of the bar and holds out the uncorked stone bottle. Tom drinks. His eyes water — juniper, fire, pine resin — and the Dragon's lads laugh, not unkindly, at his face.

For the morning, Hendricks says, taking the bottle back. Courage enough to reach the clerk. After that you will need your own.

Go and sign your paper, boy.

Goodnight, Maggie, Tom says on his way past the bar. She does not look up from wiping the counter.

At the door he pauses, hand on the latch, and does not look back. The cold takes him. The dark and mud swallow him.

The door closes. Maggie stands still behind the counter. The Dragon's men are asleep, or close to it. The fire is down to embers.

Hendricks finishes his pint, and takes, out of order, against a rationing he would deny exists, one more pull from the stone bottle, and leaves without a word. Outside, the rain has finally stopped. The ships stand black on the black water, waiting for boys. He walks down the stairs to his barge with his brother beside him, and the tide, which has heard every warning ever given on this river and carried every boy who ignored one, comes in quietly over the mud, on schedule, taking no position.

Tom will sign in the morning and get on the ship. He has heard the cost and goes anyway.
