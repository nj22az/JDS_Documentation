# The Woman Who Came Looking

She has been walking, in one sense, for three years.

Through dock offices where clerks ran a finger down lists and shook their heads at her — the wrong lists, she learned later, always the wrong lists, the lists were never once the right ones. Through mission churches where kind men prayed over her for a sailor's widow before she could explain she had not agreed to be one. Through the doorways of lodging houses from Deptford to Ratcliff, following a name that changed its story with every mouth that carried it. She has been told he was dead, twice, by men who seemed sure of it. Told he was mad, once. Told, by a clerk at a Company door who had no reason to lie to her, that he had taken another wife out east and would not thank her for the coming. She came anyway. You do not turn back from that kind of walking.

What kept her on the roads is nothing a ballad would take. Not a vision, not a vow. It was the arithmetic of the alternative: that she would be at her sister's in Chelmsford, kneading bread, on the day the knowing arrived by some carrier's mouth, second-hand, softened for her, already shaped by others into whatever they had decided she could bear. She had been given her life's news that way once before, when her father went, and she had concluded, quietly, at nineteen, over the kneading trough, that she would not receive news that way again. She would go and stand where the news was made. It has cost her three years, her savings, one good pair of boots a year, and the last of what her family calls her sense. She has audited the cost the whole way, being a factor's wife, and finds it fair.

The last mile is the strangest. A carter at Aldgate, hearing the name Amboyna, says: there's a man off that business drinks at the tavern by Wapping stairs, so they say. And after three years of walking, one mile is suddenly too short: she slows on the wall, past the chandler's, past the rope-walks, because there is a version of her that wants never to arrive, that would rather walk beside the hope forever than spend it, and she gives that version a hundred yards of the wall, out of respect, and then takes the hope back and spends it.

She comes in on a Friday. Not young, not old. Dust on her shoes, a thin shawl, the particular set of a woman who has walked further than she meant to because there was no choice about it.

She stands in the doorway, and she does not ask whether Daniel is here. She has done all her asking already, on the road; asking is for people who still have a question left. Her eyes move along the bar, and they stop — not on his face, which the three years have changed past any easy knowing, but on his hands, flat and shaking on the counter. She knew him by his hands before she let herself trust the face. Whatever was holding her together on the walk here falters, just for a second.

Daniel doesn't see her at first — he's watching his ale, the way he does most hours now. Something makes him look up. He sees her. His face does something complicated: guilt and relief and a kind of frightened tenderness all trying to happen at once.

Nell, he says. Just the name.

She crosses to him without running. Sits on the stool beside him. Doesn't touch him. Doesn't cry. Just looks — the thinness, the shaking hands, the general look of a man taken apart and reassembled by somebody who didn't much care whether the pieces fit.

Daniel, she says again. Same word. Different weight.

I'm sorry, he says, voice cracking on the second word. I couldn't write. I sat with the paper and couldn't think what to say. How do you tell someone you're not the man they remember?

You write the letter, she says. That's how. You send it and let the person who loves you decide if you've changed too much. You don't make that call for them.

They killed everyone, Nell. Held me under until I thought I was dying, and then again, and the only reason I'm here is they decided Batavia instead of the axe. I don't know why. I've been trying to work it out for three years.

She puts her hand on his arm. He flinches — not from her, from the touch itself, a thing he's out of practice at surviving. She doesn't pull back. Holds it there, light, the way you hold a bird you're afraid of frightening off.

You're here, she says. Sitting in a bar, alive, and I found you. That's enough. That's the start of enough.

I'm not the man you married.

No, she says. And I'm not the woman you married either. We can be new people together, or old people who don't fit anymore. But we work that out here. Not through three more years of silence.

Maggie sets down bread, cheese, something that might be stew, and leaves without a word — she knows which moments in her bar are not hers to be part of.

Daniel eats. Nell watches him, and for the first time since he walked through that door, his hands are still.

* * *

She takes a room two doors along, at the widow Prentice's, because whatever the ballads would prefer, a wife does not move into a wood-store loft above a tavern, and because — she says this to Maggie across the counter on her second evening, flat as a delivered invoice, and Maggie ranks her permanently upward on the spot — the man sleeps alone yet, and will, until he asks otherwise, and he is not to be hurried by so much as the sound of my boots on his stair.

She comes each evening. She talks; he listens; it suits them. She does not perform hope at him, which he could not have borne, and does not perform grief either, which he could have borne even less. She tells him Chelmsford things: her sister's children, the price of wool, a scandal concerning a churchwarden and a beehive that takes her three evenings to tell properly and is worth all three. She is, Maggie observes from her professional distance, doing the thing the trade calls building a fire in a wet hearth: small dry sticks first, laid without hurry, the flame nursed low and shielded, everything in its order, no great log of feeling thrown on to crush it. The room watches it happen over the weeks the way it once watched a wager, silently, keeping score in its own fashion, and there is not a regular at that bar, of all its leathery and unsentimental congregation, who does not privately consider the fire's progress the best news on the river.

His hands, on the good evenings, are still now for an hour together. The count has not come back past ten. She has learned about the count. She does not know yet what she will do about it, but she is a woman who walked three years on the strength of refusing to receive her life second-hand, and the ones the room would bet on, if the room were asked, are not the Dutch.
