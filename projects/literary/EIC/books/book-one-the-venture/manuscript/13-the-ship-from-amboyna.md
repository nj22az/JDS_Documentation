# The Ship from Amboyna

The ship comes up on a grey afternoon, riding low, sails slack, in no hurry at all, and the word comes off it before the crew do — one name, passed hand to hand along the waterfront faster than good news ever travels.

Amboyna.

Nobody on the dock has the whole of it yet. Just the name, two years out of the pamphlets and never once out of the drawer, walking ashore at last with mud on its boots.

The room fills early that evening, the way rooms fill when there is something to not-talk about. The tar argument is conducted at half strength. The frost bettor takes no positions. Men drink with their shoulders and watch the door without watching it, and Maggie pours and says nothing and knows more than any of them, because she watched the ship's boat come in to the stairs from the window she does not wait at, and counted the men who came up, and saw which one the others left a distance around, the way sailors leave sea-room round a thing they cannot name.

He comes in an hour after his shipmates. Walks like a man not sure the ground will hold. Slow. Careful. Thin in a manner that is not hunger, or is not only hunger: the thinness of someone who has stopped eating because food has become a problem instead of a comfort. His clothes are English but old and hang wrong, made for a bigger man than the one left inside them.

He sits at the counter. Does not order. Puts his hands flat on the wood, and the hands tremble, and his eyes fix on the grain of the counter without seeing it — looking at somewhere behind the eyes instead, a room with no windows.

Maggie knows him.

It takes her a moment, because the face has been taken apart and reassembled by somebody who did not much care whether the pieces fit, but the bones of it are in her ledger: Daniel Vale. Factor's clerk, then factor. Sat where he is sitting now — two stools along, if she is exact, and she is always exact — eight years ago, with a paper in his pocket and ink on his fingers and a fire in his belly, bright and quick and courteous, asking her good questions about the Indies trade and writing her answers, she would swear it, on the inside of his skull in a fair small hand. She gave him the odds. He thanked her for them, which almost none of them do, and signed anyway, which all of them do. There is a notch under this counter with his name on it, so to speak; she cut it the next grey morning, one among the crowd. She has poured for a quarter-century of departures and learned every shape a return can take: the loud ones, the rich ones, the ones on crutches, the ones in Pell's level-walking category with news too heavy for the door. This shape is new. This is a notch walking, a return that did not return, a man delivered back to the parish with the man left out.

Mara has seen him too, from the far end, and stops where she is, jug in hand, reading him the way she reads everyone, and what her face does is the thing it almost never does: it stops working. She has counted his doors for him before he knows the room has doors. She looks at Maggie. Maggie is already moving.

You do not ask a shaking man what he wants. You put ale in front of him. She said it to Mara once, years ago, first winter, a lesson over a broken sailor neither of them ever learned the story of, and Mara gave it back to her word-perfect a year later over another one, and it is the whole of the house's theology in two sentences. Maggie draws the pint and sets it down on the counter inside the ring of his flat trembling hands, quietly, the way you set a thing down near a startled animal.

Drink, she says. Not a suggestion.

He drinks. One pull, then another. The tremor slows. Does not stop. Slows is the best this bar can do for him this afternoon, and the bar knows its own limits and has never once been ashamed of them.

* * *

He does not speak that evening. He drinks the one pint over two hours, and Maggie lets it stand at one, judging — correctly, as the week will prove — that this is a man who has been made to swallow against his will and now rations everything that crosses his lips, even mercy. At closing he stands, sways, finds the floor where it said it would be, and produces, with terrible care, from some inner pocket, a coin.

Maggie looks at the coin. Eight years ago she watched this man count change like a lawyer, cheerfully, to the farthing. The hand holding the coin now is a stranger's, whittled, older than the rest of him, and it holds the coin out with a kind of desperate correctness, as though the whole architecture of the world has come down to this: that a man pays for his ale, that there is a counter, that there is a woman behind it, that some transactions still stand.

She takes it. Some mercies are had by refusing payment. She has been keeping this room long enough to know the other kind: the mercies had by accepting it, gravely, at the posted price, so that a man may believe one system somewhere is still in working order.

Lodging, she says. Not a question either. Above the wood store. Mara will show you.

He looks at her then, the first time his eyes have come up off the counter all evening, and there is a long delay in them, a letter taking years to arrive, and then something that was Daniel Vale looks out for a moment at something it half-remembers.

The odds, he says. His voice is a ruin, worn smooth from saying too few things too many times. You gave me the odds. At this counter.

I did.

They were generous, he says, and follows Mara up the stairs, one at a time, both feet to a step, like a man learning the trick of land again, and Maggie stands behind her counter with his coin in her fist and does not put it in the till for a long moment.

Twenty-three years she has cut the notches and taken the keepsakes and settled the accounts of the ones who did not come back. Nobody ever told her — not Hendricks, not Pell, not the whole long schooling of the river — that the hardest entry in the book would be a man the sea gave back.

The tide turns at the bottom of the stairs. Above the wood store, a candle burns all night; she sees the line of it under the door on her last round, and leaves it burning, and goes to bed with the house's newest account open in her head, no sum against it yet, the ink still wet.
