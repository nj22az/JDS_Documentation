> **1626**
>
> *"I have been drowned twice now. The water was gentler the second time."*
>
> — Daniel Vale, factor, 1626

---

# The Deposition

Before the Company sends a man with money, it tries the cheaper thing. It asks Daniel, very correctly, to give a deposition.

The letter comes to the tavern, which tells him the Company knows where he sleeps, which he files without surprise; the Company has always known where its people sleep, it is the first entry in every ledger. The appointment is for a Tuesday, at a counting-house off the stairs, at his convenience, which means Tuesday. Maggie reads the letter upside down while wiping the counter, an accomplishment she would deny possessing, and says only: eat first. Mara says nothing and puts his coat where he will find it brushed.

A clerk is waiting in a back room — a candle, a good sheet of paper, an inkwell, and the particular patience of a man who has taken down a great many accounts and believes not one of them is any business of his. He is polite. He is nearly kind. He tells Daniel to take his time, to say it in his own words, that the Company wishes only to have the matter correctly set down.

Daniel says it in his own words.

They tie a cloth over your face, he says, and pour water onto it until the whole of your body is certain it is drowning, and then they stop, and ask their question, and then they begin again. They drowned us by inches. For days.

The pen moves. Daniel watches it move, the way he watched the other pen move, and he reads it upside-down across the table the way you learn to read anything being written about you: *The prisoner states that he was examined with some severity.*

That is not what I said, Daniel says.

It is the substance of it, the clerk says, not unkindly, and dips the pen. The Company has no need of the — he chooses the gentleman's word — the colour. Only the facts. And the fact is that you were questioned, and released, and are here to speak of it, which is more than every man can say.

Daniel names Towerson. The pen writes that a factor of the establishment was among those who did not survive. Daniel says ten men beheaded on a beach. The pen writes that the affair was attended by loss of life on both sides — because there had been a scuffle when they were first dragged out, and a Dutch soldier took a cut across the hand, and so it is both sides now, forever, in the only copy anyone will keep.

By the time it is done it is a true document. Every sentence in it is a thing that happened. It is also a lie of the completest kind — the kind built entirely out of facts, that stands the truth on its head with each of them in turn — and Daniel signs it, because a man who will not sign is a man with something to hide, and he has already learned what is done to men the paper decides have something to hide.

* * *

Walking out, he understands that he has now been drowned twice. Once in a coral room by men who wanted the truth out of him, and once in a lit English office by a mild man who wanted it kept in. The water was gentler the second time. That is the only difference he can find between them, and he looks for it the whole way home, along the wall, past the chandler's, past the stairs, in the rain that has started the way it always starts here, without conviction, as though the sky were keeping its hand in.

He has met the clerk before. Not the man — the man is new to him — the office of the man. There was a clerk at his own muster, years ago, some dry courteous fellow at a table in a courtyard who told him to make his mark plain, and Daniel had thought, then, being twenty and clever, that the clerk was the least of the Company, its smallest and dullest part, a hinge, a nib. He knows better now. He has seen the Company from the far side, from underneath, from the wrong end of its paper, and he understands what the room in Amboyna and the room off the stairs have between them, the shared engine under the different weathers: the pen that does not need you to speak. The Dutch clerk's paper was finished before Daniel was brought in. The English clerk's paper was finished before Daniel sat down. The one wrote him toward an axe he was spared, the other writes him toward a quiet he has not agreed to, and both of them, he would swear it, will balance at the year's end to the farthing, initialled, filed, and never once read again by anybody until the reading can do no harm.

In the tavern, Maggie takes one look and pours without asking, and does not ask, that evening or ever, how the Company's correctness had gone. She has her answer in how he holds his shoulders, and in the fact — noted, filed, kept for the account she does not know yet she is keeping — that Daniel Vale, who pays for everything with terrible care, who has made a religion of the till and the posted price and the systems still in working order, walks past the counter that evening without producing his coin, for the first time since he came up from the ship. Some part of him has stopped believing the ledgers are honest. She marks the date. It is not a small thing to lose, for a bookkeeping man. It is also, though neither of them can know it yet, the beginning of the only entry that will matter: a man must stop trusting the official copy before it occurs to him to make a true one.
