# The Son

The Swedish crews come through two or three times a year, with the timber.

Maggie serves them like anyone: ale, bread, the reckoning chalked fair. She does not tell them what she is. A few of the older skippers know anyway — the Baltic trade is a village with a very long street — and have learned that the knowledge buys nothing: she will speak the language across the counter if spoken to, one measured sentence at a time, and it comes out of her level and unhurried, like coin counted from a drawer she keeps locked between customers. The young ones sometimes try to draw her — a countrywoman keeping an English bar, there must be a story — and get the bread, the ale and the level look, in that order, and give up, and are served none the worse for trying.

Seventeen years she has kept the drawer locked. What is in it: a boy in a doorway with a sea-bag, unable to make his mouth work. Go on, then. She has never once regretted saying it, which is a different thing, she has learned, from it not costing anything. Regret is for wrong turnings. This was a right one that went on hurting, the commonest article on any river, stocked in every size.

He would be — she does not do this sum often, and does it now, on a slow Tuesday in the spring of 'twenty, because a Swedish mate is standing at her counter turning his cap in his hands in a manner she has seen before, on Pell, on men who walk level and carry things — he would be six-and-thirty this year.

Mistress Margareta, the mate says, in the old language, carefully, like a man stepping onto ice he has been assured will hold. I have a thing for you from Karlskrona. It has been some time finding you. And he puts a letter on the counter: travel-worn, sea-stained at one corner, her name on it in a hand she has never seen in her life and knows absolutely, because it is Johan's hand, thirty years dead, come back down through a wrist Johan never lived to hold steady over a page. Fathers write in their sons whether anyone teaches it or not.

She looks at it and does not touch it.

You'll take ale while I read, she says, in English, to the whole room, in her ordinary voice. Mara — the barrels. And Mara, who misses nothing, takes over the counter with a shoulder-turn that closes it like a door, and Maggie goes into the back room where the candle is, and sits down with the letter, and is, for the first time in some years, alone with her whole name.

* * *

It is one page, written close, in Swedish that smells of the navy: plain words, ranks, no flourishes, a man reporting.

He is alive. Boatswain now, crown service still, out of Karlskrona. He begs to inform her — the letter genuinely says this; she can hear the pen gripped too hard — he begs to inform her that he is married these nine years to a good woman, a rope-maker's daughter, and that they have a son, six years old, sturdy, named Johan for his grandfather. He is teaching the boy boats. He writes that the Baltic was hard in the late wars but the service fed them. He writes about ice, at some length, the state of the ice this winter past, the ice being very bad, and she understands the ice completely: it is the part of the letter where the words about the ice are standing in front of the words he cannot get out, the way a man stands in front of a door he is minded to guard.

Then, at the bottom, above the plain signature, three sentences not about ice:

A man on a Lübeck ship said there was a Margareta keeping a tavern on the London river, by some stairs. I did not know for certain it was you, but I have carried the not-knowing long enough, and a boatswain learns: a line you do not throw cannot be caught. If this finds no one, no matter. If it finds you — the boy asks about his grandmother, and I have had nothing to tell him, and I would like to have something to tell him.

Maggie sits with the page in both hands for a long time. The candle does its work. Through the wall, faintly, the room does its work too, Mara's voice moving through it, the fire ticking, the whole familiar machinery of the house running without her, which is a thing she built and is suddenly, fiercely glad of, because it means nobody comes looking.

Seventeen years she has told herself the story with the ending already on it: the boy handed to the sea, the sea being what it is, the arithmetic being what it is; a woman who pours for sailors' mothers all her life learns not to exempt herself from the odds. She had settled it. Filed it. Cut, so to speak, the line across the notch — and here is the tide, without warning, without asking, bringing the whole account back up out of the water, alive, married, sturdy, teaching a boy boats, worried about ice.

A letter is a tide. It brings what it brings, and it does not take requests. She says it out loud, alone, in the back room, in Swedish, and hears what her own voice does on the words, and lets it, once, since nobody is by.

* * *

The answer takes her four evenings, which for a woman who does a day's accounts in her head between the last customer and the bolt is a fact she keeps from everyone, including, mostly, herself.

The difficulty is not what to say. The difficulty is the size of the drawer once it is open: seventeen years of things that would frighten the boy — the buried English husband, the bought debt, the notches, the whole freight of a life he has no shelf for — against three sentences that ask only for something to tell a six-year-old. In the end she writes as the navy taught him to write, plainly, one page, a report. She keeps a tavern by the stairs; the house is sound; the trade is good. She is well. England is wet. She puts in one thing for the rope-maker's daughter — the recipe rendered into Swedish, the fish done the way his father liked it, which a wife can use and a boy can eat. And for the boy himself, at the bottom, above the signature, one sentence in the old language, the one her own mother sent her out the door with fifty years before, which does not translate and is not going to be translated here, because it belongs to them.

She signs it Margareta, whole.

The mate carries it in the spring, with the timber fleet, refusing the coin she offers for the carriage with a formality that tells her the whole street of the Baltic village will know her business by Michaelmas, and she finds she does not mind, which surprises her more than the letter did.

His letter goes upstairs, into the small box that crossed with her on the timber ship — under the lace her mother made, beside the ring Johan hammered from a Riksdaler the winter they married. Not under the counter. The gap under the counter is the house's book, and it keeps the house's accounts: the boys who go out and do not come back, the wagers left standing. This is a different ledger entirely. This is the column, she thinks, putting the box away, that a woman is permitted to keep open on purpose — at a gain, for once, at a gain, forever — and if a letter comes back down the tide next year, or the year after, it will find her here, keeping a house, not waiting at any window. Or so she tells the window, wiping it, twice, on her way down to the evening trade.
