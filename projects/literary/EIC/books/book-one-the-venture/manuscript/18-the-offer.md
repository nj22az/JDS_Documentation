# The Offer

Word travels. It always does, in a bar this close to the docks — what Daniel told Carter is halfway round Wapping within the week, dressed up a little more with each telling, though nobody who's added to it has managed to make it worse than the plain truth already was.

The Company hears it too. Word like that always finds its way back to Leadenhall Street eventually, and Leadenhall Street does not care for it. The pamphlet Amboyna is the Company's Amboyna: an outrage in its keeping, deployed at its choosing, pointed where its interests point. A live factor in a Wapping tavern retailing the unlicensed version — the passes, the questioning, the numbers, the deposition that says severity where a man said drowning — is not an outrage. It is a leak.

He comes in on a Tuesday, a man Daniel's never seen before — precise coat, precise voice, the kind of politeness that has been trained rather than felt. Introduces himself as Pryce, on the Company's business, and sits without being asked, the way men do when they've decided in advance that the seat is theirs.

Mara serves him, because Mara has already priced him from the door and wants the closer look. She has spent a life reading men who deal in papers with people attached, and this one she reads in three strides: the coat says clerk, the hands say never-a-clerk, the eyes do the thing she has met only in the best of the breed — they inventory the room once, completely, without appearing to move, and then decline to look at anything again, because the first look took everything worth taking. He thanks her for the ale correctly. He tips correctly. Everything about him is correct in the exact measure that spends least and secures most, and walking back to the counter she catches Maggie's eye and gives her the flat quarter-inch of a nod that the two of them have used for twenty years, at the door of trouble, meaning: this one. Watch.

The Company is grateful for your service, Pryce says. Batavia. Two years a prisoner, and you came home able to hold a pen and keep a ledger, which is more than some men manage after less. Surat's been good to you, I hope.

Daniel says nothing. He has learned, this past year, that saying nothing is the safest answer to most sentences that start with the Company is grateful.

There's a difficulty, Pryce goes on. Talk. In bars. About Amboyna. About the passes, the questioning, the numbers. The dead are not helped by accuracy, Mr. Vale — I have always found that the hardest thing to make a grieving man believe, and the truest. The living are sometimes ruined by it. It does the Company no good, and it does you no good either, dwelling on it the way you have been. We could improve your situation considerably. A better posting. A clerk's wage doubled. Whatever debts your family carries, cleared. In exchange for — Pryce chooses the word with visible care — quiet. Just quiet. Let the thing rest where it lies.

Daniel looks at him for a long moment. You want me to stop telling people what happened to Towerson.

I want you to consider your own future, Pryce says. Nothing more sinister than that.

Ten Englishmen went into a room, Daniel says. Ten Japanese. I watched them take the first one out, and I heard the rest of it through a wall for two days before they came for me. You are offering me money to stop saying that sentence out loud.

I am offering you a better life than the one you've currently arranged for yourself, Pryce says, unbothered. Most men would take it without needing it explained twice.

Daniel picks up his ale. Drinks half of it before he answers, giving himself the time Nell taught him to take, instead of saying the first thing that arrives.

No, he says, finally. Not because I want to be poor. Because I've already paid what this cost once, and I'm not selling it a second time.

Pryce doesn't argue. Men like Pryce never argue with an answer, only note it. He finishes what's left of his own drink, sets a coin on the bar for it precisely, and stands.

You'll have the same posting tomorrow that you had yesterday, he says. Nothing changes because of this conversation. I'd only ask you to consider how much less it costs everyone if the story simply stops being interesting.

He leaves. The door lets in the river's evening and takes him out into it, and the room, which has been not-listening with its collective hackles up, breathes again by degrees.

* * *

Maggie has served every kind the river sends. She adds this kind to the shelf where she keeps the others and studies it that evening while the work does itself under her hands.

Rook, all those years ago, was force wearing a body: you could see him come, as Hendricks said, and a room could stand up to him, and did. This one is the other thing, the thing Hendricks meant, wearing a good coat and paying precisely: a paper with a man attached. There was no threat in anything Pryce said, and that is the report she would give a magistrate, and the report would be true, and it would be a lie of the completest kind, because the threat was the whole of him — in the trained politeness, in the debts he knew of unasked, in the posting that would be the same tomorrow, said in the voice of a man who could have made it otherwise between the coin and the door. Force respects stated terms; she learned that at this counter twenty-three years ago from a man with a ruined wrist. What does paper respect? She polishes the question with the tankards and gets the same answer off every one of them: nothing it isn't made to. Which is why, though she could not tell you she knows it yet, her house keeps a gap under the counter where paper of a different kind sleeps against the old wood — and why, some weeks from this Tuesday, on a near-empty evening, when a ruined bookkeeping man asks her quietly for pen and paper, she will bring them and ask nothing, and this book will reach the entry the whole of it has been kept for.

Daniel sits a while with the offer where Pryce left it, turning it over slowly, the way he turns over most things now, before setting it down for good. Then he looks along the counter at Nell, who heard every word from the far end and did him the courtesy of not coming to stand behind him while he answered, and she looks back, and neither of them says anything, because there is nothing to say that the refusal has not already said better.

That night, for the first time since the coral room, Daniel Vale sleeps four hours together, and the candle above the wood store goes out before midnight, untended, unneeded, and nobody sees it happen except the house.
