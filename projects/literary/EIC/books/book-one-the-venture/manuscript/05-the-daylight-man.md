# The Daylight Man

Morning is the honest hour in a tavern. Whatever the night said, the morning counts.

Maggie counts it on her knees, with a brush. Two chairs that will want the joiner, one that will want the fire. A jug in pieces where Mara threw it, the shards gone pink where the ale and the blood dried into each other on the rushes. A mug that died against a man's wrist, its handle sitting on the counter where its killer set it down, curled like something that had washed up. The rushes themselves are done for in a half-circle by the far table, trodden and bled on, and the whole room smells of the night before: stale ale gone sweet, cold ash, wet wool nobody wore home, and under all of it the tide, coming in at the bottom of the stairs with its low smell of mud and rope.

She works, and she listens to the ceiling. The girl is awake. Has been since before light, by the boards. Not moving about, not trying the door. Just awake, the whole weight of her sitting perfectly still overhead, and Maggie lets her be, because there is a kind of tiredness that sleep is no use to and company is worse. There is bread on the table at the top of the stairs and a jug of small beer beside it. The girl will come down when the girl comes down.

Tom is gone. Went at first light with his paper, half his face the colour of a thundercloud, walking like his ribs had opinions, and if the Company clerk asks about the eye he will lie, and the clerk will not ask. The Company is not particular about faces. It is buying backs.

The tide turns. The morning trade comes and goes: two watermen, a rope-yard boy sent to fetch his master's beer, the old docker, who takes his stool by the fire and says nothing about last night at all, which is his manner of saying a great deal. He was the first man on his feet when the room stood up. He is drinking today at yesterday's price. He does not know that, and Maggie has no plan to tell him.

Rook comes at noon.

She knows him through the window first, the size of him against the light, and stands her brush against the wall and comes round to her own side of the counter, because there is a right side to hear this from, whichever way it goes. Behind her, without being told, the old docker sits up out of his doze. On the stairs, the boards stop being quiet. So the whole small room knows, before the door opens, that the daylight has arrived, and everyone in it has made their arrangements.

He has to duck to come in. She had forgotten that, or the night had made it seem ordinary. Sober, in the flat grey light, Silas Rook is a different account of the same man: coat brushed, boots cleaned, the ruined wrist carried in front of him in a seaman's neat sling, and his face, above the beard, holding the particular stillness of a big man who has done all his thinking before opening the door. Half-blind he was, when the cold took him out of here. His left eye is still angry at the edges. He sees her with it anyway.

He does not come to the counter. He stops in the middle of the room, in the trodden half-circle where the rushes are ruined — a man standing in his own evidence, and knowing it, and choosing to stand there anyway.

You said daylight, he says. And sober.

I did.

Here is both.

The room is very quiet. Not last night's quiet, which had a crowd in it. This is three people and a fire, and a door anyone could reach, and none of that would matter for a moment if the man in the middle of the floor had come to spend the rest of his strength. He could break her over the counter with the one hand he has left. Everyone present knows it. That is not what force is for, this morning, and everyone present knows that too, but the knowing has to be done all the same, in the open, like a price read aloud.

Say your piece, Maggie says.

The debt stands, he says. Bought and witnessed. He turns his head and takes in the old docker without heat, one witness, noted, filed. I don't come about the debt.

Then you've had a walk for nothing, if it's the girl.

Something crosses him at that, the first thing since the door. Not anger. Distaste, oddly, as though the suggestion were beneath the morning's business. The girl carried another man's bad luck, he says. It's been paid off her. I've no more claim on her than on the weather, and no more wish for one. He says it plainly, and Maggie, who has kept a bar long enough to hear most kinds of lying, hears none in it, and files that away with everything else she knows about the men force makes.

What, then?

He crosses to the counter at last, unhurried, and lays on it, one at a time, with his good hand, four coins. Not a purse set down grandly. Four coins, counted out like a man settling with a chandler.

Two chairs, he says. The jug. The rushes. If the mug was the house's, say so, and I'll make it five, though I hold the mug was spent against me, and a man doesn't pay for the shot that hits him.

From the stairs, where she has been standing long enough to have heard the whole of it, Mara says: the mug was mine.

She comes the rest of the way down with the tray of morning bread against her hip, and she does not stop walking, and she does not look away from him either. She crosses her own half-circle of ruined rushes to set the tray on the counter, close enough that his shadow is on her, and the room holds its breath in the old stupid way rooms do, and nothing happens. Nothing was ever going to happen. That is what her walk says, every plain step of it: you are weather to me now, and I have been rained on by better.

It is Rook who moves his eyes first. He looks at the tray. At the counter. At the coins.

Then the mug's paid where it fell, he says, to nobody in particular, and that is as close to the other thing as the morning is going to get, and everyone in the room old enough to shave knows there will be nothing closer, and that this is not an apology, because men like Rook do not carry those, and if he ever tried to lift one it would look wrong on him, like a fiddle in a blacksmith's fists. What he carries instead is terms. He is a man who has lived his whole life by the strength of his hands and the exactness of his word, and last night a room he had priced wrong called his word due, in front of the river, and this morning he has come to pay it because the alternative is to be a man whose word doesn't pay. That is the whole of it. It is not virtue. It is bookkeeping, of the kind his sort of life is kept in.

Maggie sweeps the coins off the wood and into her apron, one motion, no counting. Counting would be an insult, and she has decided, in the last quarter minute, not to insult this man again unless it is useful.

There's a fifth thing, she says.

He waits.

Night debts are paid in daylight, or they are not debts. They are weather. You've paid in daylight, so hear the rest in it. She sets her two hands flat on the counter. The girl works here now, for wages, same as anyone. This house serves the river, and you're the river's, so it serves you. But the table you played at is done serving you. Not this year, not in ten. If that's agreeable, your ale is a penny, same as anyone. If it isn't, there's the door, and we'll both know what your word was worth by which way you walk.

The old docker has stopped pretending to doze. On the counter the bread is still steaming faintly, the ordinary good smell of it sitting strangely across the whole transaction, the morning's plain life going on directly underneath the settling of the night's account, which is, when Maggie thinks about it later, how every account in her life has ever been settled: over bread, before the beer, while the tide does something enormous and indifferent at the bottom of the stairs.

Rook looks at the far table. The one his coins were swept from, the one Tom went backwards over, the one with his whole bad evening still written on it in scrubbed wood and new scars. Something works behind his face, arithmetic of a kind, pride and thirst and the long cold walk back to the wharf all weighed against a penny and a rule.

A penny, he says, and puts it on the counter.

She draws his ale. He drinks it standing, by the counter's end, the sling resting on the wood, and he says nothing more, and nobody troubles him, and the room, degree by degree, lets its breath back out and returns to its morning. The rope-yard boy comes back for his master's second jug and squeezes past the big man without knowing what he is squeezing past. Mara sets the loaves along the shelf with her back to the whole of it. The fire ticks. The tide turns again below the stairs, and the light on the ceiling goes from water-light to plain day.

He finishes, sets the pot down soft, and goes out, ducking under the lintel, and the door lets the cold in and him out, the exact reverse of the night before, and somehow the same motion.

He will keep the terms. Not because the room could make him — no three men on this stair could make Silas Rook do anything on the best day of their lives — but because he set them in his own ledger the moment he said *a penny*, and men like him die before they blot that book. He will drink here, off and on, for twenty years. Foul weather and fair, pressed for work and flush with it, he will stand at the counter's end with his penny, and be served, and trouble nobody, and in all that time he will never once, not once, sit down at that table. The regulars will stop knowing why. The table will not.

Maggie takes the mug's handle off the counter, where it has sat since the girl who broke it set it down, and turns it over once in her fingers.

Yours, she says.

Mara looks at it a moment. Then she takes it, and puts it in her apron pocket, and goes back to the bread, and that is the whole ceremony: a curl of glazed clay passing from one woman to another over a scrubbed counter, worth nothing, priced already, paid for last night by the only person in the room the debt was never hers to carry.

The morning gets on. The morning always does.
