# The Signing

Morning comes grey and wet. Somewhere behind the tavern a cockerel crows — a farmer's bird, or an optimist's; in Wapping the distinction is not always clear.

The stool by the window is empty. A tankard ring fades into the table.

Maggie takes a knife from her apron and scores the underside of the counter, low enough that a person must duck to see it. There is a crowd of marks down there — one for every young man who sat with a paper in his pocket and walked out towards a ship. She stopped counting long ago. She keeps cutting them. It gives her hands something to do besides wait.

Tom's thimble goes into the gap behind the old wood, where a notch cannot be mistaken for a promise. Few of the keepsakes there have been collected. She does not look at the others while she places it. Looking at them is a thing she permits herself twice a year, the way some women permit themselves church, and for the same reasons, and with the same results.

Then she straightens, and puts the knife away, and goes to see what the night has done to her chairs. The rest of that morning belongs to another chapter of this book, and to a large man with a sling and four coins; this one goes with the boy.

* * *

Tom walks to the City with the river on his left and his ribs disagreeing with him about the pace.

It is a fine walk if you have not spent the night being throttled. Wapping Wall first, the mud sucking at his boots, the wharves already loud: coal coming ashore, fish going up, a rope-walk's long shed humming somewhere behind the houses, tar smoke drifting over everything like the district's own weather. Past the stairs, where a pair of watermen argue a fare with a passenger who has the face of a man losing an argument he started. Past a chandler's window full of lamp-oil and twine. Under the Tower at last, the stone of it black with rain and indifferent to him in a manner that feels, this morning, personal — one eye swollen to a bright slit, a tooth loose against his tongue, everything he owns either on his back, folded in his coat, or stacked in a neat column by a docker's elbow in a bar he could not look at as he left.

He walks it the way he walks everywhere, a half-step quicker than needful, collecting. The granary taught him that. His father keeps the great tithe granary behind the millpond, has kept it forty years, a silent, exact, entirely honest man whose whole conversation is jokes — terrible ones, delivered flat across the supper table like rent — because jokes are the one language he ever learned for the things underneath. Tom's mother was the other tongue in the house: a parish seamstress with a lap full of other people's linen and a game she played with her boy from the time he could talk, a word a day, found, traded, spelled out in the flour dust on the kneading board — she had letters from a vicar's wife she'd sewn for, and was prouder of them than of anything she owned except the boy she taught them to. The fever took her when Tom was fourteen, between a Tuesday and a Sunday, and the house went silent except for the jokes, which kept coming, worse than ever, because they were load-bearing now. Tom kept up the game alone. A word a day, written fair. He would not know how to stop; stopping would be a second funeral. The thimble was hers, and the letters were hers, and the penny book is hers too, at one remove, and none of this has ever once been said aloud in the granary house, where the language for things underneath remains jokes, and the jokes remain terrible, and the love they carry would founder a barge.

The City closes over him by degrees: the streets narrow, the smells change coin — less tar, more horse; less fish, more smoke — and the men change coats. In Wapping a good coat is a story. Here it is a uniform. By Fenchurch Street he is the worst-dressed man in every crowd he joins, and the paper in his pocket has begun to feel lighter than it did, and he cannot tell whether that is a good sign or the first symptom of a mistake.

Philpot Lane is short, steep-shouldered and unremarkable, and the Governor's house does not announce itself. That is the first surprise. He had imagined — he does not know what he had imagined. Colours. A crowd. Something with trumpets in it, or at least a flag. What there is: a door standing open on a courtyard, a porter with a list, and a queue.

The queue is the second surprise, because it is a queue of men exactly like him.

A farm-shouldered boy who has never seen the sea and keeps saying so, to nobody, as though confessing it early might get it waived. An old hand, grey-stubbled, signing for his third voyage, who sleeps standing up with his chin on his chest and wakes each time the line moves, moving with it, never fully surfacing. A man lying about his age in both directions at once, somehow. Two brothers not speaking to each other, whose quarrel has the well-kept look of a thing they intend to take around the world. And a dozen more, wet wool and pipe smoke and nerves, boots shifting in the mud, every third man patting some pocket where some paper lives. Nobody swaggers. It is too early, and too damp, and the thing at the head of the queue is too much like the thing at the head of every queue: a table, and a man behind it.

The clerk is the third surprise, because Tom had expected the Company to look like something, and instead it looks like Mr. Cade.

Fifty, spare, dry as a ledger's spine. A good pen, a better inkwell, a register the size of a paving slab, and a voice that has read the same articles aloud so many times the words have worn smooth in it, all the corners gone, a prayer said by a man who no longer hears himself pray. He does not look up between men. He looks up at each man once, at a fixed point in the proceeding, for a fixed purpose, and Tom, watching from four places back, works out what the purpose is before his turn comes: the clerk is matching the body to the wage. Stout goes to the hold. Clever goes to the pen. Desperate goes wherever the last gap is.

The articles, read at speed, are these: obedience to the General and his officers; watch and ward as required; no private trade above the allowance; wages as agreed, paid as the Company shall direct, with such part held back as the Company shall think fit; and the Company to be held blameless — this passage smoothest of all, the pebble worn longest — for perils of the sea, of climate, of war, of accident, and of whatsoever else God in His providence may send. Nobody in the courtyard appears to hear this list as a description of their own death. Tom hears it. He signed nothing yet; he could still walk; the thought arrives, does its circuit, and leaves by the door it came in. Behind him the farm boy is asking the old hand whether the food is truly as bad as they say, and the old hand, surfacing, says: worse, and better than the water, and goes back under.

Then it is Tom.

Name. Age. Parish. Calling. He answers, and the pen makes its small dry noises, and his life goes into the register in a hand better than his own, which stings a writer more than it should. The clerk reaches the fixed point and looks up: one measuring look, forehead to belt. The eye is taken in — the swollen slit of it, the split lip below — and is not asked about. Whatever it is to Tom, to the register it is nothing. The Company is not particular about faces. It is buying backs, and hands, and in the present case a head with figures in it, and Mr. Cade has seen a thousand black eyes queue in this courtyard and signed every one of them, because the sea does not care what a man did with his last night ashore and neither does the ledger.

Writer, the clerk says, finding the line. Twenty pounds per annum. You keep a fair hand?

Fair enough, sir.

We shall see. He turns the register and holds out the pen, and says the thing he says, the sentence smoothed like all his sentences by ten thousand uses, and no less true for it: Make your mark plain. The Company pays no blurred names.

Tom takes the pen. It is a good pen, better than any he has owned. And here, at the bottom of everything, under the bruise and the ribs and the child's ocean behind the granary, the plain wonder of the thing catches him unprepared: that this is how it is done. No trumpet. No oath on a blade. A wet courtyard, a queue, a table, a man with a register — and a name going down in ink, one among two hundred, by which a boy is converted into an asset of a company chartered by a dead queen, entitled to twenty pounds a year and whatsoever else God in His providence may send. He writes his name. He writes it plainly, and well, with the loop on the T his father taught him on a kitchen table with a stick of charcoal, showing off a little at the last, because it is the one thing in this courtyard he does better than anyone in it.

The clerk blots it, reads it, and — the only unmeasured motion in the whole proceeding — gives the signature itself a second small look, the look a wheelwright gives a stranger's good joinery.

You'll do, Mr. Cade says, to the name rather than the man. Imprest money at the table by the gate. Muster at Woolwich when the bill is posted. Next.

And that is the whole of it. Seven minutes, queue to gate. At the second table a boy younger than Tom counts coins into his palm — an advance against wages he has not earned, warm from a box, the Company's first money and the exact sensation, though nothing in the courtyard says so, of a hook going in. Tom closes his hand on it. Behind him the farm boy is spelling his own name for the clerk, twice, and getting it different each time, and Mr. Cade is saying, without heat, patient as tide tables, that the mark will do, that many good men sign with a mark, that he should make it plain.

* * *

He does not go back to the tavern. He tells himself several reasons, on the walk east — his ribs, his lodgings, an errand to a slop-seller about a sea-chest — and knows the true one, which is that he cannot yet stand in front of the woman who holds his mother's thimble and show her the ink on his fingers. Time enough. The fleet will not sail for weeks. There will be an evening for it, an evening he is already writing in his head, in which he says something to her that makes the whole of it sensible, and she says something back that is not the odds.

Instead he walks down to the riverside at Ratcliff and stands a while in the wet, looking downstream at the fleet fitting out — hulls black and busy, yards crossed, boats swarming them like flies at a horse — and lets the two feelings have him at once, because nobody is watching and one eye is closed anyway: the fear, cold and correct and counted in the hundreds before the Cape; and underneath it, unkillable, disgraceful, twenty-two years old, the other thing. The serpents beyond the map. The wreckers' fires. The door he means to come back through, salt on his coat, with stories nobody else can tell.

The tide is making. The ships lean on their moorings, all together, like a congregation growing restless. In a courtyard off Fenchurch Street his name is drying in a register between two hundred others, and in a bar by Wapping Old Stairs there is a new notch on the underside of a counter, cut by a woman who stopped counting long ago and has never once stopped cutting.

The charter is a piece of paper in a locked room in the City. The Company, this wet morning, is a queue of damp men, a dry clerk, a box of warm coins and a good pen. It does not look like the thing Hendricks described. It does not look like anything at all. That is the last thing Tom thinks about it before hunger moves him on, and it is the truest, and he will have an ocean's worth of nights to understand it: the machine was never going to look like a machine. It looks like a table, and a queue, and a man saying next.
