# The Return (1612)

> **1612**
>
> *“Don’t come back until you remember what a human life is worth.”*
>
> — Maggie, keeper of the Pelican, 1612

***

## I. The Changed Room

Nine years is a long time on the river. In the ledgers of the East India Company it is an eternity.

By the winter of 1612 the Pelican has changed without anyone quite watching it happen. New flagstones near the hearth. The rushes swept away for good. A longer oak bar, to hold the swelling tide of dockworkers. The Company that hired boys out of this room for a desperate venture is a power now, trading in silk, indigo and blood; off the Surat bar its ships have just beaten a Portuguese fleet at Swally, and the Portuguese grip east of the Cape has begun, audibly, to crack.

While the Company grows, Wapping pays the toll.

Elias, the old docker whose word was once law, was broken by a lifetime of other men's cargo and buried three winters past. Barnaby kept his promise: Wapping churchyard by the Whitsun of 1604, and his stool by the fire has stood empty ever since, by a treaty nobody ever said out loud. Arthur no longer drinks in the Pelican. Within a week of Matthew Bell's murder he accepted a copying place in Harcourt's house, meaning at first to learn what Bell had learned and do something cleaner with it. The Company discovered what an exact man was worth. Now he has a desk in Leadenhall Street, where he drafts the exact species of paper that traps poor boys in endless debt — timelines, penalties, wage assignments, exact to the penny. A murder opened the place. Nine years later Arthur is still filling it. Only Josiah remains precisely as he was: at the end of the bar, still complaining about the Portuguese and the Dutch, a fixture the room has long stopped hearing.

Maggie stands behind the counter, slower on her feet than she was a decade back, wiping the wood with a rag. Beneath the bar the hidden crowd of pale notches has multiplied — dozens of cuts now, one for every boy who signed a paper and never came home. Matthew Bell's folded account remains behind them, his full name on the outside, Tom's inherited debt buried inside.

There is a meat pie in the oven behind the bar — mutton and mace, the way one particular boy liked it. Word came up the river this morning that the Company fleet is in and paying off. Maggie has told herself the pie is for the house. The house knows better than to touch it.

***

## II. The Boatswain

The heavy oak door opens, bringing in the sharp chill of the Thames.

The man who walks in moves with the heavy, rolling balance of a deep-water sailor. Thirty-one, his face weathered to old leather by the Indian sun, a jagged white scar through one eyebrow — a Portuguese boarding pike at Swally, for anyone who knows how to read that handwriting. He wears a heavy boatswain's coat.

Maggie stops wiping the bar. She looks at the scarred man, searching the hardened face for a naive twenty-two-year-old who once stood between a lynch mob and a terrified girl.

Tom, she says. Her voice is perfectly flat. Her hands grip the rag tight enough to whiten the knuckles.

Maggie, Tom says. His voice has dropped an octave, scraped raw by years of shouting orders over the ocean.

He does not walk to the bar. He crosses the new flagstones and sits down, without asking, on the stool nearest the fire. Barnaby's stool. The room lets him, and both of those things mean something.

He sets a square parcel on the bar. Oilcloth outside, blue cotton within, printed in red with a climbing flower no English loom has learned to make. The cloth is not enough for a dress and too fine for wiping tankards. Tom bought it from a Gujarati broker outside Surat and carried it dry through one monsoon, one Portuguese attack and four months round the Cape without deciding what it was for.

Maggie touches the edge with one finger.

Table cloth, Tom says.

For which table?

One that doesn't have men cutting tar on it.

Then you have brought it to the wrong house.

She folds it again, more carefully than he did, and puts it beneath the counter. Neither says thank you. They have both understood.

You survived, Maggie says, coming down the length of the counter.

The Company pays me to survive now, Tom says, touching the boatswain's whistle at his neck. I keep the rigging tight and the men moving. Just like my father did.

And Pell? Her eyes go to the doorway behind him, as if the quiet sailor might yet be shaking out his coat. Did he come through the Portuguese guns?

Tom looks at the fire. The flames stand in his hardened eyes and find nothing to catch. Pell shipped out on the Ascension, four years back. They never saw battle. The scurvy got him before the Cape. His jaw tightens. They put him over the side, and the purser entered him in the ledger as cargo. Just like he said they would.

Maggie closes her eyes, one brief second. Another notch under the bar.

And your father?

Tom takes the boatswain's whistle from his neck and lays it on the counter. Not his own, Maggie sees now. The silver is worn thin on one side where another man's thumb rubbed it for years.

Dead these four winters. The knee went black at the end. Harcourt sent a physician and put the fee on the account.

Of course he did.

I buried him with the old whistle. Tom turns the one on the counter. This was the spare in his chest. He made me promise I'd use it better.

Have you?

Most days.

Maggie goes to the oven and brings out the meat pie. She sets it in front of him hard enough to make the crust settle, then puts down a knife and no fork, because the boy she remembers used his hands. Tom looks at the pie and then at her.

For the house? he asks.

The house has eaten.

He cuts it. Steam carrying mutton and mace reaches him and takes nine years out of his face. The first mouthful is too hot. He eats it anyway.

While he eats, Maggie notices his hands: rope-burn scars, a crushed nail grown crooked, two pale circles where fever sores healed. He notices hers: the swollen joint of the thumb, the way she braces one palm against the counter before lifting a full pitcher. Neither comments. Affection in this room has always preferred an inventory to a declaration.

The upper hinge of the storm door complains when a gust worries it. Tom hears it, looks up and stops eating.

Been doing that long?

Only when the wind blows.

So every day.

He finds the tool box beneath the stairs without asking where she moved it, because she did not move it. The pin in the hinge is too small. Tom draws it, wraps it once in a shaving of oak and drives it home with Maggie's bung mallet while she holds the door square. They work without speaking, each knowing when the other needs the weight shifted. Nine years ago he stood against this door while Silas Rook tried to make the room a gallows. Now he repairs the same iron with his father's whistle against his chest.

When the hinge sits true, Maggie opens and closes the door once.

Better, she says.

It is the nearest she comes to telling him what she thinks of the boatswain's coat.

## III. The Boy in the Thin Coat

An hour later the repaired door opens again. A boy steps inside, shaking freezing rain from a thin coat. Sixteen at the most, shivering, looking around the Pelican with wide, terrified eyes. He looks exactly the way Tom looked nine years ago, except that Tom at twenty-two had a coastwise sailor's hands and the stubborn confidence of a man who had already met weather. This boy has a cooper's cuts across his knuckles and the soft balance of somebody who has never stood on a moving deck.

Tom stands up from the fire and gestures to the empty stool beside him. Come sit by the hearth, Jack. Warm your bones.

Maggie frowns, looking from the scarred boatswain to the shivering boy. Who is this, Tom?

Jack looks at Tom before he answers, which tells Maggie almost everything.

Jack, he says.

Only Jack?

Jack Mercer. My father works the seasoning sheds at Carter's yard.

Worked, Tom corrects gently. Until the accident.

Jack's face closes. The accident was six months ago and has already been reduced, in Carter's books, to a date beside a loss. A stack of green timber shifted when a sling parted. Jack's father lived, which meant the yard had somebody to charge for the pieces that went into the tide. He can lift a spoon with his left hand now. Nothing heavier.

Tom signals for two pints. Maggie sets one before Jack and puts water in the other place.

He is sixteen, Tom says.

Ale won't make him older.

Jack takes the water and drinks it because he is thirsty enough not to defend himself.

Tom has spent the walk from Carter's yard telling him what a boatswain needs. Men who can learn. Men who obey the first order because at sea the second order may arrive after the mast has killed somebody. Jack has repeated this to himself until obedience sounds like a skill rather than the absence of one.

Show her the knot, Tom says.

From his pocket Jack brings a short length of tarred line. He makes a bowline slowly, tongue against one tooth, then another faster. The second is neat and holds when Tom puts his weight through it.

Learned this morning, Jack says.

He learned three knots, Tom says. Quicker than half the men the Company gives me.

Maggie examines the bowline. Who told you to come here?

Tom did.

Who told you to sign?

Jack looks toward the fire. Nobody had to tell me. There's six of us in two rooms. My father's account is four pounds and growing. Carter's steward says they will take the bed if there is nothing against it by month's end.

And you want the sea?

The question surprises him. Adults have asked whether he will sign, whether he is sound, whether his teeth are firm, whether he understands that advances may be retained. Nobody has asked what he wants.

I want the wages, he says first.

That was not my question.

Jack turns the line round his fingers. I want to see it. The other side. Tom says the water at Bantam is green enough to see the anchor go down.

It is, Tom says.

And he says a man can come back boatswain if he learns.

Tom looks at his father's whistle. Maggie looks at Tom.

Can you swim? she asks Jack.

No.

Most sailors can't, Tom says. It only makes the dying take longer.

Jack laughs because Tom does. Maggie does not.

## IV. With or Without Me

Carter comes in while Jack is practising a rolling hitch on the leg of the table. The chandler has grown prosperous without growing quiet. His coat is good broadcloth now. Tar no longer lives beneath his nails; three other men carry it for him. He sees Jack, then Tom, then Maggie's face, and wishes with visible force that he had chosen another door.

The boy is not mine, Carter says before anybody accuses him.

No, Maggie says. Only the debt.

His father spoiled twelve lengths of Riga pine.

His father was under them when they fell.

And was paid through the quarter while he healed. Carter removes his gloves one finger at a time. I have a yard to keep. If I forgive every loss because the man beneath it was hurt, I will have no yard and none of them will have wages.

There it is, Maggie says.

There what is?

The room inside you where grief becomes use.

Carter does not yet know how many forms that room can take. He only knows she has found something inside him and dislikes being found.

Tom steps between them, not physically — he learned long ago that standing between is sometimes the easy part — but with the calm voice he uses when two watches are close to knives.

Jack goes to the City tomorrow to sign for the Company's next fleet. I told him I would find him a berth on my crew. His wages go against the yard account until it is clean.

All of them? Maggie asks.

Half. Same as mine were.

Were?

Tom's eyes flick down. The original eleven pounds is nearly paid.

Nearly.

There were additions.

Physician's fee, she says.

And rent advanced while I was away. Burial. Interest when a voyage ran beyond the year.

Nine years at half wages and Harcourt still owns a piece of the month.

Not for much longer.

Maggie points at Jack. Put his father's four pounds against your half.

Tom stares at her.

If you are keeping him alive, she says, buy the chance with your own wages.

That is not how the books work.

Books are made by hands.

I have my father's account.

And Jack has his. That is very convenient for every man whose wages remain his own.

Jack has gone still. The knot lies unfinished between his hands.

Tom lowers his voice. He will sign with or without me. On another deck he gets a boatswain who takes his advance, sells his rations and beats him for not knowing work nobody taught him. On mine I can keep him alive.

There is always a *with or without me*, Maggie says. It is how the man holding the paper makes the man holding the boy believe there was never a choice.

This is not Harcourt.

No. Harcourt knew what he was doing.

The words land harder because she does not raise her voice.

Tom turns away, then back. You think I should leave him to whichever ship takes him?

I think you should stop calling the taking a rescue.

Pell would have lived with a better boatswain.

Pell had Lancaster.

Pell had scurvy.

And Jack will have fever, flux, rotten water, Portuguese guns and whatever order comes down from a merchant who has never tied the knot in his hands. Which of those are you?

The man who can teach him when to duck.

Then teach him. Pay him. Bring him home. But do not take half his wage for Carter and name yourself kind because another man might take all of it.

Carter puts both gloves on the table. The debt is lawful.

So was the woman in Bell's book.

That silences him.

Jack looks from one adult to the next, seeing perhaps for the first time that the arrangement presented to him as weather was made by people in rooms. He pushes the unfinished knot away.

I asked Tom for the berth, he says.

Maggie's face softens, which is not the same as yielding. I know.

My father needs it.

I know that too.

Then why are you angry with him?

Because he knows all of this and has learned to speak as though he does not.

The silence that falls over the bar is absolute.

Maggie stares at Tom. At the whistle. At the scar. At the competence she was proud of an hour ago and the use he has chosen for it. To survive the Company he has become part of its machinery. That does not make every thing he does wicked; it makes every wicked thing easier to describe as necessary.

Maggie does not pour the ale.

She reaches under the bar, into the dark gap beside the pale notches, and brings out a plain brass thimble. She walks around the counter, and drops it on the flagstones at his feet.

You're not a sailor anymore, Tom, she says, and her voice shakes with a sudden, profound disgust. You're Master Harcourt. You're Silas Rook. You're the room telling itself the next death was already decided. Another big man using paper to send somebody else's boy where you would not send your own.

I haven't got a boy, Tom says. It comes out small, and human, and too late.

No, Maggie says. The sea made certain of that too.

Tom looks down at his mother's thimble shining in the firelight. He does not pick it up.

The door is behind you, Maggie says, turning her back on him. Don't come back until you remember what a human life is worth.

The door opens. The door closes. The cold moves through the room and settles.

Carter collects his gloves.

What happens to the yard account? Maggie asks.

The chandler looks at Jack, and for a moment the man who put a belaying pin beneath Silas Rook's boot stands inside the merchant's expensive coat.

If the boy signs, half wages until it is discharged.

And if he does not?

Carter's answer takes too long.

The steward will proceed against the rooms.

Maggie nods. Not approval. Entry.

## V. The Meat Pie

Jack is still standing near the threshold, dripping, too frightened to sit. Maggie looks at him for a long moment. Then she goes to the oven and brings out the meat pie she has been telling herself all day is for the house, and sets it on the bar with a pint beside it, and nods the boy toward both. Eat, she says. Not a suggestion. The boy will sign in the morning whatever anyone in this room does tonight; the paper has him, the way papers do. But tonight he will eat like a guest in this house, not like cargo.

The pie has a quarter missing where Tom ate. Jack notices and understands who it was baked for. He sits anyway.

Maggie cuts him a piece large enough for a working man and watches until he takes the first mouthful.

Is he a bad man? Jack asks.

Tom?

Jack nods.

No.

You called him Rook.

Bad men seldom disappoint you, Maggie says. They arrive complete. Eat.

Jack eats. Halfway through, his shoulders come down from his ears.

Maggie takes the tarred line from the table and undoes the rolling hitch he left unfinished. She does not know the knot. She knows what unfinished work looks like. When Jack has cleaned the plate with bread she gives the line back.

Tom will be at the hiring hall tomorrow, she says. If you still mean to go, make him show you the articles before you put your name under them. Ask what is held back. Ask who pays if your kit is lost. Ask whether the surgeon's fee comes out of your wage if another man breaks your head. Ask twice when the first answer has no number in it.

Will it change anything?

Perhaps not.

Then why ask?

So the clerk has to hear himself answer.

She finds a scrap of paper and has Arthur's successor at the end of the bar write the questions because Jack can recognize his name and little else. The clerk writes reluctantly. Men who make their living from paper dislike being asked to make paper troublesome.

Jack folds the questions into his coat.

At the door he turns. If Tom takes me, will you put a mark under there?

Maggie does not look toward the hidden notches.

Only if you do not come home.

Then don't cut one yet.

I never do.

After he has gone, she stoops, picks the thimble off the flagstones, and puts it back in the gap behind the old wood, in among the notches, where she keeps what the sea owes her.

Outside, Tom is standing beneath the eaves across the lane. He sees Jack emerge and does not call to him. Jack sees Tom and, after a moment, crosses the mud of his own accord. They walk toward Ratcliff together, the boy half a step behind until Tom slows enough to put them side by side.

## VI. The Articles

The Company's hiring room is full before daylight. Boys, men, wives, creditors, two surgeons and a row of clerks behind a rail. Wet coats steam in the press. Every person in the room has come carrying a different necessity, and the clerks mean to turn all of them into the same form before dinner.

Jack stands in line with Maggie's questions inside his coat. Tom waits beside him in the boatswain's coat and discovers that rank is a kind of door: the men at the rail call him sir, though nobody aboard will, and boys in the line look at Jack as if he has arrived with the answer.

At the third desk sits Arthur.

Nine years have improved his coat and drawn the rest of him narrow. No weather book. No wagers. His fingers carry ink in the same permanent bruise Bell wore. Tom has not seen him since Arthur went back upstairs to take the vacant chair.

Arthur sees the scar through Tom's eyebrow, the whistle, the boy at his side. His eyes move once toward the folded questions showing from Jack's coat.

Name? he asks.

Jack Mercer.

Age?

Sixteen.

Arthur looks at Tom.

Sound enough for foremast work, Tom says. Learns quickly.

Arthur begins the entry. Advance to the household account of the father, four pounds and—

How much is the kit? Jack asks.

The pen stops.

Reasonable cost as supplied, Arthur says.

That is not a number.

The line behind Jack becomes attentive.

Arthur looks at the paper in the boy's hand. Who wrote the questions?

A man in the Pelican.

Which man?

Did not ask his name.

This answer pleases Maggie at a distance.

Arthur sets down the pen. Cost varies by need. A man who owns a sea chest is not charged a sea chest. A man requiring shoes is charged shoes.

At whose price?

The purser's.

Can the purser charge anything?

No.

What can't he charge?

Arthur has copied the clause a thousand times. For the first time somebody has asked him to locate its edge.

The clerk at the next desk tells Jack to make his mark and clear the rail. Arthur ignores him.

Tom, he says, you brought the boy.

I brought him to read the paper.

Yesterday you brought him to sign it.

Yesterday was longer than I expected.

The line behind them shifts. A woman holding a toddler asks the clerk at the next desk what burial costs are recovered if her husband dies before wages accrue. The clerk does not know. A tarred man at the back asks whether a lost knife is valued new or used. Necessity has begun asking questions in several voices. The room will not finish by dinner.

Arthur draws the articles back. He adds a ceiling to the kit charge: twelve shillings unless Jack separately marks an additional item at issue. He writes that replacement goods are to be valued at their condition when lost, not at the cost of new stores. These are small alterations. They do not make the voyage safe or the debt just. They make two empty spaces less hungry.

Harcourt's steward, waiting behind the rail for the assignment, leans over Arthur's shoulder.

You have no authority to vary the master's recovery.

I have authority to state what the Company will deduct before it pays the assigned half. Your master may sue the boy's injured father for anything beyond it.

With what result?

Arthur looks at the steward. A public hearing of the account.

The steward straightens. Four pounds will suffice.

Tom hears Maggie in the silence after that: books are made by hands.

Two shillings from my monthly wage against the same account, he says.

Jack turns. No.

Instruction allowance, Tom says to Arthur. My expense for training an unready hand. Credit it to Carter until the four pounds close, then pay it to the boy.

You don't owe it, Jack says.

No. That is why it can be given.

Arthur writes the allowance. The ink turns a late thought into a term other clerks must obey, at least until somebody higher notices. He has used Bell's chair for nine years. This is not redemption. It is one line leaning the other way.

Jack makes his mark. Then, embarrassed by the shape of it, asks Arthur to write his whole name beside it. Arthur does, slowly, and reads every altered clause aloud.

At the door Jack asks Tom whether they are going back to the Pelican.

You can.

Are you?

Tom looks west. The walk is not far. The distance is exactly one sentence spoken by a woman who knows him.

No.

Why?

Because changing one paper is easier than admitting why it needed changing.

Jack understands none of this. He is sixteen and has a ship to see. They turn east together.

Fourteen years will pass before Tom comes through that door again.
