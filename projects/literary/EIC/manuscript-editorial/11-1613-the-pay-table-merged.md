# 1603–1614: The Pay Table

> **1613**
>
> *“He kept me alive once with his hand and every day with his voice.”*
>
> — Jack Mercer, Deptford, 1626

## I. The River Lets Go

Jack's first work aboard is to carry his own debt down a ladder.

The sea chest costs nine shillings under the limit Arthur wrote into the articles. Inside are two shirts, one pair of stockings, a wooden bowl, a spoon, a blanket that smells of another man's sleep and a knife valued as new though its handle has been cut with somebody else's initials. The purser points to each item while a clerk enters it. Jack keeps Maggie's questions folded inside his coat and asks the price twice.

The purser looks at Tom.

Answer him, Tom says.

The second price is eightpence lower than the first.

Jack does not feel victorious. He feels the ship noticing him.

Men notice a boy who arrives beside the boatswain. They notice more when the boatswain makes the purser account for a spoon. By the time Jack has dragged his chest below, he is Tom's pet, Tom's clerk and Tom's little wife, according to three men who have not asked his name. One steals the blanket before dusk. Jack finds it beneath a gunner's mate and pulls until the man opens one eye.

Go ask your father for another.

Tom is six feet away, inspecting a frayed stay. He hears. Jack knows he hears because nothing said on deck escapes him. Tom does not turn.

Jack pulls again. The mate sits up and gives him the blanket with a smile that promises the matter is not finished.

Later, when the watch changes, Tom finds Jack trying to tie his chest shut with the bowline he learned in Wapping.

Wrong knot.

It holds.

So does a locked door. Can you open it under load?

Jack cannot.

Tom shows him a slipped hitch and makes him tie it until his fingers find the turn in darkness. He does not mention the blanket.

You heard him, Jack says.

Yes.

You could have made him give it back.

Yes.

Why didn't you?

Because tomorrow I may be on the other end of the ship.

Jack pulls the hitch free. He understands the lesson and hates the man for making it true.

The vessel drops downriver with the morning tide. Wapping passes as wet roofs, smoke and a confusion of stairs. Jack stands at the waist searching for one sign among a hundred. He cannot see the Pelican. He sees Carter's yard, or thinks he does: timber stacked behind the seasoning sheds, cranes black against the low sky, the place where his father lies in two rooms with a hand that can lift a spoon.

Four pounds. Two shillings from Tom every month. Half Jack's wage until the account is clean.

Numbers behave better ashore. On the moving deck they refuse to stand still.

At Gravesend a boat brings the last letters. None is for Jack. Tom receives a folded page bearing the Company's seal, reads it once and puts it inside his coat.

From Maggie? Jack asks.

No.

Did you tell her we sailed?

She knows what ships do.

The river widens. England lowers behind them. Jack waits for the moment the sea begins and cannot find it. Brown water becomes grey, grey becomes green, and the land is simply smaller each time he looks.

* * *

## II. Above the Deck

The first time Tom sends Jack aloft, the ship is barely clear of the Downs.

The order is to loose the foretopsail. Jack knows every word separately. Together they mean climbing into a wind that has already taken his cap.

Up, Tom says.

Jack puts one foot on the shrouds.

Not the ratline with your hands. The shroud. Ratline parts, you fall. Shroud parts, we all have larger trouble.

Jack changes his grip.

Up.

The word follows him. It is not encouragement. Tom has a voice made to cross weather, low in ordinary speech and enormous when lifted. It finds Jack through canvas cracking, blocks knocking and forty men pretending not to watch the boatswain's boy discover height.

At the futtock shrouds the climb turns outward. The deck is no longer a place but a shape below him, narrow and unreasonable. Water moves on both sides. Jack locks both arms and stops.

Keep moving, Tom calls.

I can't.

Nobody on deck laughs. Tom's silence prevents it.

Jack, look at the mast.

He presses his face to tarred rope.

Not down. Not out. One hand. One foot. Then the others.

Jack moves one hand.

Again.

One foot.

Again.

The voice builds a ladder where the ratlines have failed. Jack reaches the top, crawls over and lies against the platform with his eyes shut.

No sleeping, Tom calls. Loose the sail.

A man beside Jack shows him where the gasket lies. Jack's fingers are too stiff to free it. The man reaches across, then stops when Tom shouts from below.

Let him find it.

Jack finds it.

The sail opens with a violence no cloth should possess. It bellies, strikes him with its edge and nearly tears the line from his hands. He swears. The men laugh now, because the danger has become ordinary enough to permit it.

Coming down is worse. Gravity knows which country it belongs to.

At the deck Tom examines Jack's hands. Tar in the palms. One split nail. No rope burn deep enough to cripple him.

Again at the afternoon watch, Tom says.

Jack thinks this is cruelty until the second climb frightens him less. By the fifth, his feet begin to know where the ship will be before the roll arrives. By the tenth, another new boy watches him for the route.

Tom does not praise him. Praise makes a sailor listen for praise when he should be listening for wind.

He corrects everything.

Your thumb outside the coil.

Never stand inside the bight.

Feel the line before you look at it.

Tie it again.

Again.

Again.

Jack hears Tom in his sleep. He hears him while eating, while pissing over the lee rail, while hauling until the skin opens beneath his fingers. On the seventh day he turns at another man's shout because all voices have begun to sound like orders.

On the ninth, he makes his first dangerous mistake.

A buntline jams while the foretopsail is being taken in. Jack climbs to clear it, folds himself across the yard and puts his weight on the jammed line where it has worn pale against the block. Tom sees the colour from the deck.

Off that line! he roars.

Jack freezes.

Move!

The urgency fixes him harder. Tom is up the shrouds before Jack understands he has begun climbing. He comes over the top, works himself astride the yard and strikes Jack once across the shoulder with an open hand hard enough to break the grip fear has made.

Jack folds across the spar. Tom catches his coat, drags him inward and wraps Jack's arms round the yard.

The pale buntline parts on the next roll.

One end whips away into empty air.

Tom's face is close to Jack's. Not angry. Worse: afraid, and already turning the fear into instruction.

When I say move, you move.

You hit me.

Yes.

You could have said the rope was bad.

I did not have time to improve the order.

Below them the sea passes, green-black and without opinion.

Tom makes Jack climb down first. On deck he puts the severed rope into the bosun's store and enters it against ordinary wear. He does not charge it to the boy who stood on it.

* * *

## III. The Voice

There are kinder men aboard than Tom.

The cook gives Jack the bottom of the porridge pot, where the oats have caught and taste almost roasted. The old quartermaster teaches him to judge speed from the bubbles passing the hull. The sailmaker lets him sleep in the loft one wet afternoon while pretending not to see him.

None of them keeps him alive as often.

Tom's voice stops Jack before a block drops where his head is going. It sends him across the deck before green water comes over the waist. It tells him when to flatten, when to climb, when the familiar line in his hand has become the wrong line because darkness and panic have altered everything around it.

The voice is not kept for Jack. It works the entire ship.

Men hate it, obey it and repeat it in smaller voices down the watches. Tom sees the flaw in a knot from twenty feet. He smells loose wet hemp before it parts. He can wake from the deck beneath a coat and know by the changed pitch of canvas that a sheet is running.

He can also make a grown man feel six years old for coiling against the lay.

When a sailor named Pritchard drops a marline spike from aloft and misses Jack by the width of one hand, Tom has Pritchard at the mast before the spike stops rolling. He does not strike him. He makes him stand the boy's next two watches and carry Jack's work as well as his own.

Pritchard takes the punishment out on Jack in words.

Boatswain's little debt, he calls him. Two shillings buys a loud father.

Jack has not told anyone about the allowance.

He finds the purser's book open that evening while stores are issued. Beside Thomas Fletcher is the monthly deduction, entered exactly as Arthur wrote it. Beside Jack's name is the assigned half wage. The whole ship knows because books have no privacy from the men who keep them.

Jack waits until the night watch.

I told you not to pay it, he says.

Tom is splicing a length of line beneath the stern lantern.

I heard you.

They call me bought.

Men aboard call the moon a whore when it gives poor light. The moon keeps its account.

I'm not the moon.

No.

Then stop it.

Tom draws the splice tight. I can stop one mouth. The others will learn to whisper. Which do you prefer?

Jack has no answer that does not sound like surrender.

Tom gives him the line.

Find the join.

Jack runs both hands along it and cannot.

That is what good work looks like, Tom says. Not the man who paid for the rope.

It is almost comfort. Tom ruins it by making Jack splice the next length three times.

Most of the voyage is not weather or punishment. It is one watch followed by another, each made from small discomforts that nobody will remember if the ship arrives.

Jack wakes for the forenoon watch already wet.

The damp has come through the deck above, gathered on a beam and fallen steadily onto his chest. His shirt holds a dark patch from collar to belly. The hammock sags beneath him, one shoulder pressed against the ship's side and one knee against the sleeping man beside him. On the other side a pair of bare feet rests level with Jack's face. Their owner has wedged himself between two guns and snores whenever the hull rolls to larboard.

Jack tries to sit. The hammock folds him in half.

Tom strikes the sole of his shoe.

Watch.

I'm awake.

Then begin resembling it.

There is nowhere to dress except where he has slept. Jack works damp arms into his coat while the next hammock brushes his hair. Somebody has taken his left stocking from the line and left a strip of oakum in its place. He finds the stocking under Pritchard's head. Pritchard does not wake while Jack pulls it free, though his smile suggests a complicated kind of sleep.

The way forward is blocked by men leaving the morning watch and men rising for the day, all of them with the same needs and none with room to meet them. At the beakhead Jack waits behind six sailors while the sea appears and vanishes through the rails. Privacy consists of facing outward. The man ahead finishes, ties his breeches and warns Jack to hold the stay when the bow drops.

Jack thinks this advice insulting until the bow drops.

He returns with both shoes wet and one hand smelling of tar. A sailor at the pump tips a bucket of seawater across the deck and catches Jack to the ankles.

Now the rest matches, the sailor says.

Jack scoops a double handful from the scupper and throws it back. The ship rolls. The water misses the sailor and strikes Pritchard in the thigh.

Pritchard looks down at the spreading mark.

That boy's aim will make a gunner.

It is the first joke of the watch, and because Jack has helped make it, he is allowed to laugh.

The ration issue begins beside the main hatch. Each mess brings its wooden tally and whatever cups it has not lost. Biscuit is tipped from a cask onto sailcloth. Some pieces are whole, some split, some reduced at the edges to meal and droppings. The purser's assistant weighs the share for each mess, calls the number of mouths and makes a mark in the book.

When Jack first comes aboard, he thinks a ration belongs to the man who eats it. After a month he understands that it belongs first to the page. A man may be standing at the cask with an empty hand, but if the page says sick, absent, punished or dead, the biscuit can pass through him without stopping.

Tom gives Jack their mess tally.

How many?

Jack counts the men. Pritchard; the two at the pump; the lookout not yet relieved; Tom; himself; five more waiting with cups or still working.

Eleven.

Again.

Jack counts again. Eleven.

The assistant calls ten.

Eleven, Jack says.

The assistant does not look up. Ten on the mess.

Tom takes the tally, turns it over and puts one black thumbnail against a cut near the end.

That is eleven.

That is the boy.

I have observed him.

Half wage, the assistant says. It is entered.

Jack feels every man in the line hear it. Pritchard's name for him returns before Pritchard can speak: boatswain's little debt. Two shillings buys a loud father.

Tom's voice remains level.

The wage book pays Carter. The victual book feeds Jack. Unless the Company has found a way for half a boy to do a whole watch, weigh eleven.

The assistant looks toward the purser, but the purser is occupied arguing over a cheese that has become liquid inside its rind. He adds the biscuit.

Jack carries the share away.

Does half wage mean half everything? he asks when they are beyond the line.

It means half your wage.

And the two shillings?

Mine.

The food?

The ship's expense. It must keep alive what it intends to use.

Jack turns this over. It sounds less like kindness than arithmetic, which makes it easier to accept.

Their beer comes next. The first casks were small and good enough to make men sing after the evening issue. This cask gives a brown, sour drink with threads that cling to the cup. The assistant calls it beer because that is what the book calls it. Pritchard calls it rope with ambitions.

One of the pump men lifts a thread on his finger.

Splice it, Tom.

Too weak, Tom says.

The men laugh. Jack laughs late because he is surprised Tom has made the joke.

Water is worse in a quieter way. The cask smells of wet cellar when the bung comes free. The first dipper carries black specks. The second carries a pale shaving of wood and something soft that nobody examines. Men complain, but they hold out their cups. The good water is already behind them in the river mouths of England. This water has crossed every mile they have.

The cook takes his share to the galley and sets barley in it before the taste can become an argument. He salts the pot lightly and keeps the broth thin enough to pour. Jack crumbles his biscuit into a cup and waits while the pieces surrender. Hard biscuit breaks teeth; softened biscuit reveals inhabitants. Three small beetles rise to the surface.

Pritchard leans over his shoulder.

You have more meat than the rest of us.

Jack lifts one beetle with the spoon and drops it into Pritchard's cup.

Half wages, he says. Full meat.

The two pump men laugh first. Then the lookout, climbing down hungry from the mast. Pritchard looks at the beetle, looks at Jack, and drinks without removing it.

Waste nothing, he says.

That is the next joke of the watch. This time Jack knows in time that he belongs inside it.

Tom makes him count the water measures before he eats. Ten are drawn. The eleventh comes up short because the cask gives only a wet knock when the dipper reaches the bottom.

Put the shortage in the book, Tom tells the assistant.

The cask is empty.

That is the shortage.

It issued full when opened.

It does not issue full now.

The assistant says evaporation, leakage and bad cooperage as though three explanations add up to one measure of water. Tom waits. Waiting is the quiet form of his voice. At last the assistant writes beside the issue.

Jack watches the pen.

What happens if he doesn't write it?

At the next reckoning, the book has more water than the hold. The book wins until men become too thirsty to agree.

And if he writes it?

Then the cask loses the argument.

Tom tips half his cup into Jack's.

I have mine.

No, you have counted yours. Drink.

Jack drinks. The water tastes of the cask, the dipper and Tom's refusal to discuss what he has done.

The watch works forward from the ration line. First the pumps. Jack takes one handle with Pritchard opposite him. The pump wheezes, catches and brings bilge water up in brown surges that smell worse than the cask water and are easier to trust. Nobody has entered this water as drink. Nobody asks it to be anything but foul.

They pump until the carpenter calls the well low. Then buckets come over the side and the deck is flooded. Men scatter sand. Others push flat stones back and forth on their knees, scouring grease, blood, spilled broth and the black tracks of bare feet from the planks. Jack works a stone no larger than his palm. The sun climbs. His wet shirt dries stiff with salt while his knees become wet instead.

Pritchard crawls past and brushes Jack's back. Men begin smiling at nothing.

Jack keeps scrubbing. A twist of oakum swings behind him from the band of his breeches, tarred at the end like an animal's tail. He sees its shadow each time the sun clears the sail. He lets it remain.

The old quartermaster asks whether London boys grow that way or only shipboard ones.

Only the handsome ones, Jack says.

The tail survives another length of deck. Then Jack crawls beneath a wet line, catches it in one hand and, while Pritchard reaches for the bucket, tucks it into the back of Pritchard's belt. The exchange passes without anyone naming it. Two minutes later the quartermaster asks Pritchard the same question.

Pritchard looks behind himself and finds the oakum.

The deck waits.

He bows.

Improved by sea air, he says.

Even Tom's mouth alters at one corner.

The laughter does not excuse the dropped marline spike. It does not erase the names or the debt. It only gives Jack a place among men who have too little room, too much work and four hours in which to become tired of one another. Belonging aboard is not being spared. It is being answered.

When the deck is clean, Tom sends Jack to the weather shrouds with a serving mallet, spun yarn and a pot of tar. A length above the deadeyes has begun to chafe where rope works against rope. The damage is no longer than Jack's thumb. Left alone, Tom says, it will become the length of his arm and then the height of the mast.

So replace it.

With what?

New rope.

Where does new rope come from?

The store.

And when the store is empty?

Jack looks east, where the sea has no warehouse in it.

Tom puts the spun yarn in his hand.

Preserve first. Replace when preservation fails.

Jack lays the yarn against the shroud. Tom makes him begin again because the turn is loose, again because he has trapped water beneath it, and once more because the serving runs against the rope's lay. On the fourth attempt the mallet draws each turn close and hard. Tar coats Jack's fingers and works under his nails. The ship rises beneath him in a rhythm his body now anticipates.

Below, the ordinary life of the watch continues. A bucket travels aft. A sail is patched on deck. The cook insults the purser's cheese. Somebody sings three lines of a song and cannot remember the fourth, so the entire waist supplies a different one. The versions disagree about whether the woman in Plymouth owns a tavern, a husband or a knife.

Jack looks down at the serving.

The turns sit together so closely that he cannot see where the last begins.

Find the join, Tom says.

Jack runs one tarred finger along the work. This time he can.

There.

Better.

It is the smallest word Tom owns that can mean praise.

Near noon the heat thickens. The deck seams soften underfoot. Men who complained of cold rain in the Channel now work bare to the waist and curse the sun. Jack's mouth dries before he finishes one coil. He glances toward the water cask, though he knows by the bung lying beside it that there is nothing left until another is brought up.

Tom sees the glance.

Finish the coil. Then shade.

Jack coils with the lay, each circle flat inside the next. Pritchard passes and does not disturb it. This restraint is almost an apology.

The bell ends the watch. The relieving men rise from the dim places below, marked by hammock rope and sleep. They blink at the white deck. Jack looks toward the hatch and thinks of his own hammock, the wet patch perhaps shifted from chest to face, the feet waiting beside it. The space now seems less like punishment than permission to become unconscious.

Food comes first. The cook ladles the last thin barley broth into mess cups. Jack drops in the final piece of his biscuit and presses it down with a spoon. Around him men eat quickly before the next order can claim their hands.

One sailor does not.

The man sits in the full noon sun with both arms crossed over his chest. His biscuit remains untouched beside the cup. Broth soaks halfway through it, leaving the upper edge dry.

Jack points at the biscuit.

If you don't want that, Pritchard will eat it and the beetles.

The sailor looks at him without understanding. His face is grey beneath the heat.

Cold, he says. God help me, boy. I am cold.

Jack looks at the sun. Tom looks at the untouched biscuit.

## IV. Nineteen

By evening the sailor is shaking beneath two blankets. By morning four more men cannot stand. The surgeon calls it the flux and orders the sick separated, an instruction for which the vessel has no space. Men sleep between guns, beneath boats, against bulkheads wet with their own breath. Separation is a word belonging to houses with spare rooms.

Tom clears the sail room and slings hammocks close enough that the sick turn together when the ship rolls. He orders vinegar splashed on the boards. He burns foul bedding until the captain objects to Company property going over the side as smoke.

Dead men draw no wage, Tom says. Choose your account.

The captain allows the fire.

Jack carries water. At first this seems easier than going aloft. Then the men begin asking him to stay.

One is nineteen and has a sister in Limehouse. One has a wife who believes he is on a coastwise run to Bordeaux. One took the Company's advance, lost it at dice before sailing and now laughs whenever the fever climbs because he remembers the expression on the clerk's face. Their names are Ellis, Brown, Cooper, Venn. Jack repeats them until sleep rubs the edges off.

The surgeon moves from hammock to hammock with a lancet and a basin. Tom watches him bleed the first three and stops him at the fourth.

It is the accepted treatment, the surgeon says.

They have lost water from both ends for two days.

The fever must be relieved.

Relieve one more into that basin and I will make you drink it.

The surgeon appeals to the captain. The captain looks at the men able to work, counts how many answer to Tom's voice and tells the surgeon to try water first.

Tom has no medicine. He has observation and the memory of every voyage that killed men in the same order. He has the cook keep the thinnest barley broth on the fire, salted lightly and carried warm from hammock to hammock in cups. Some men keep it down. Some do not. Nobody calls this knowledge because no gentleman wrote it in a book.

On the fifth day Jack wakes cold beneath the tropical sun.

Tom sees before Jack speaks. He takes the bucket from Jack's hand and presses two fingers beneath his jaw.

How many stools since dawn?

Jack is too tired to be embarrassed. Three.

Tom puts him in the sail room.

I can work.

No.

You said men obey the first order.

That was the first order.

Fever strips the ship to sound. Timbers complain. Water moves in casks. Men breathe as though hauling something uphill. Jack lies among them and understands for the first time that a vessel can continue while the people inside it stop.

Tom brings the water himself.

Jack tries to sit. Tom puts one hand against his chest and holds him down. The hand that struck him on the yard is careful now, which makes Jack angrier than the blow did.

Don't, Jack says.

Drink.

I don't want your two shillings.

Drink.

I don't want—

Tom tips the cup against his mouth. Jack drinks or drowns in a very small quantity of water. Most of it stays down.

Why me? Jack asks when he can speak.

You were nearest the bucket.

The answer is a lie and a kindness and Tom's refusal to name either one.

Men die around them.

The first is Ellis. The surgeon's mate sews him into his hammock with a round shot at the feet. Tom makes the clerk enter the full name before the body goes over. The clerk says the muster number is sufficient. Tom stands beside the book until the letters are written.

Ellis.

Brown.

Cooper.

Venn.

The list reaches nineteen before the fever releases the ship.

Jack is not on it.

On the first morning he can stand, he climbs to the foretop without being ordered. Halfway up, his legs begin to shake. Tom's voice rises from the deck.

One hand. One foot. Then the others.

Jack does not look down.

## V. The Next Weather

The ship changes after nineteen burials.

Hammocks hang farther apart because there are fewer men to fill them. Watches lengthen. Boys do work that belonged to able seamen at the Canaries. Nobody calls Jack Tom's pet now. The word requires an innocence the voyage has used up.

Pritchard gives Jack a strip of leather for his palms and claims it was spare. The cook stops pretending the burnt oats are an accident. The quartermaster lets Jack cast the log and accepts his count without checking it twice.

Tom gives him nothing except more work.

At dusk he takes Jack to the foremast and makes him lay one hand against the wood. The vibration changes as the ship rises. Rigging speaks through the mast: every stay carrying its own degree of strain, every sail pulling at a different height.

What do you hear? Tom asks.

Nothing.

Feel, then.

Jack closes his eyes. Beneath the ordinary shudder is a small repeated knock.

Block at the foretop?

Tom waits.

No. Lower. The brace.

Which brace?

Jack follows the tremor through the mast and points.

Lee fore brace.

Tom nods once.

They renew the worn strop before darkness.

Weeks later the African coast lies somewhere beyond the western horizon. The master speaks of passing Madagascar before the monsoon turns. Heat sits on the deck after sunset. Flying fish strike the hull in the night. Clouds build where the sky has been empty all day.

Jack has learned enough to watch Tom watching them.

Bad weather? he asks.

Weather, Tom says.

That is not an answer.

It is the only one the sea has given.

Tom checks the braces himself before the watch goes below. Jack follows without being told.

The boy who left Wapping could tie three knots and could not swim. The boy in the rigging can feel a worn line through the mast and knows that neither fact makes him safe.

Above them, the first high cloud draws a white hook across the dark.

* * *

## VI. The White Hook

The white hook in the western sky is gone by morning.

In its place stands a wall.

It is too far away to seem large. A low darkness below, a hard white edge above, the whole shape sitting where sea and sky have spent weeks refusing to admit they are separate. The master studies it through a brass tube and says the weather will pass astern.

Tom looks at the sails.

Jack looks at Tom.

This has become another instrument aboard: the angle of the boatswain's head, the amount of time he spends answering a simple question, whether he inspects a line with his eyes or puts his hand on it. This morning Tom touches everything.

He checks the foremast wedges. He sounds the standing rigging with the handle of his knife. He makes Pritchard climb to the foretop and examine the topmast cap, then goes up after him and examines it again. He orders the fore braces cleared from the pins and recoiled where they can run without catching a man's ankle.

The master watches from the quarterdeck.

You expect trouble, Fletcher?

I expect rope.

We have rope.

Then I expect it to work.

The master does not enjoy the answer. He enjoys less that Tom has already made the men shorten the loose ends of their clothing and move spare spars off the forward deck. Preparedness can look like insubordination to a man who has just predicted fair passage.

By noon the wind falls.

Canvas hangs against the masts. The sea becomes long, dark glass lifting under the hull without breaking. Heat gathers beneath the courses and stays there. Men speak quietly because the absence of wind has taken away the sound that usually hides them from one another.

Jack is sent aloft to stop a block knocking against the foretopmast. He carries a short lashing between his teeth and climbs through air thick enough to drink.

From the foretop he can see Madagascar as a low bruise to the west. It is the first land in weeks, if land is what it is. The coast shows itself only when the ship rises: a dark line, then nothing, then a dark line again.

Below him the vessel is stripped of illusion. Seams, patched canvas, pale scars where fittings have moved under strain. Nineteen fewer men. A ship does not become whole because land is near.

Jack secures the block. Before descending, he looks toward the weather.

The wall has moved.

No longer sitting at the edge of the world, it has eaten half of it. The white upper edge is gone. Beneath the black cloud, the sea has turned the colour of lead freshly cut.

Jack starts down.

Tom calls from the deck. Stay there. What do you see?

Rain.

Where?

Everywhere.

That is not a bearing.

Jack braces one arm round the mast and forces himself to look again.

West by south. Moving north.

The master lowers his tube. Passing astern, he says.

Tom does not answer him. Watch the sail, he calls to Jack.

There is hardly enough wind to shape one.

That makes the small movement easier to see.

Jack watches the foretopsail. The lower edge hangs slack. One corner lifts, falls, then lifts again though the deck beneath it has rolled the other way.

The clew is moving, Jack calls.

Which?

Weather clew.

The master turns toward the binnacle.

The wind has not reached the deck. It has reached the upper canvas.

Tom's voice crosses the ship.

All hands. Shorten sail.

The master says, I have not given that order.

Tom looks at the black wall, then at nineteen empty places where men should be running.

Give it now.

## VII. Before the Wind

The order comes late and everybody obeys it quickly.

Men spill out of the waist and up the shrouds. The courses are clewed. Topsail sheets are eased while clewlines and buntlines come in. Canvas that hung dead a moment before begins breathing in short, irregular pulls.

Jack remains at the foretop because Tom told him to watch. Pritchard climbs past and throws him a gasket.

Move, boy.

Tom told me—

Tom wants the sail in before he wants your opinion.

Jack crawls onto the yard. Six men lie across the spar, its timber taking their weight while their arms gather canvas in hot, heavy folds.

The first wind arrives from the wrong direction.

It touches the back of Jack's neck. The ship is still moving on the old air, such as it was, while the new air comes across the quarter. The topsail shivers between the two.

Jack looks down.

Tom stands beside the foremast, one hand on the stay. His face is turned upward.

The sail fills backward.

Not fully. One deep inward breath, enough to put every seam in relief and make the yard kick beneath Jack's chest. The men swear and flatten themselves.

Then it empties.

Jack sees the mast move.

All masts move. Tom taught him this. A rigid mast is already broken. But the foretopmast does not return along the same path. It bends away and comes back with a small second motion at the cap, like a loose tooth tested by a tongue.

Jack shouts.

The wind takes the word.

He shouts again. Foretopmast! At the cap!

Tom has seen it.

Down from the yard! he roars. Lay down! Move!

The men come off the spar. Jack reaches the top and stops because Pritchard is below him on the futtock shrouds.

Tom points.

Backstay. Slide it.

Jack throws one leg over the aft lead of the rigging and descends more by controlled falling than climbing. Tar burns through the leather on his palms. His feet strike the deck.

Tom catches his shoulder, turns him and puts a line into both hands.

Lee fore brace. Take it aft. Do not belay until I tell you.

The line is already under strain.

Why me?

Because you felt it before. Go.

Jack runs aft with the brace, threading between men hauling the fore course up. He reaches the station beyond the mainmast where the old quartermaster and two fever-thinned sailors wait for hands enough to control the yard.

Ten seconds is not a measure anybody makes during danger.

Later Jack will build it from actions.

He takes the brace.

One.

The quartermaster tells him to keep his thumb out of the coil.

Two.

Tom orders the weather brace eased.

Three.

The first rain hits, drops large enough to burst in the dust between the deck planks.

Four.

The foretopsail fills backward again.

Five.

The yard tries to swing. Jack and the quartermaster haul the lee brace to bring it round.

Six.

Somebody forward shouts that the cap is opening.

Seven.

Tom looks up.

Eight.

He takes one step away from the mast.

Nine.

The wind arrives.

Ten.

The foretopmast goes.

* * *

## VIII. The Going

It does not snap like a stick.

It comes apart as a system.

The cap tears loose first. Iron leaves timber with a scream. The topmast bows forward under the backed sail, carrying yard, canvas, blocks and a confusion of rigging that for one suspended instant still resembles a mast.

Then the whole weight falls toward the bow.

Men run without choosing directions. The sail catches wind on the way down and drags the broken spar sideways. A stay rips through the foretop rail. Blocks strike the deck hard enough to bury their corners. The yard lands across the forward boats and breaks one clean through.

Jack is aft of it because Tom put him on the lee brace.

The brace jumps in his hands.

The quartermaster lets go. One of the other men does not. The line strips skin from his palms and pulls him face-first across the deck. Jack sees the running coil open beside his ankle.

Thumb outside, Tom's voice says inside him.

He steps clear as the line tears past.

Forward, Tom is not clear.

A parted backstay lashes round his left arm and tightens as the fallen topmast goes over the side. Tom is dragged to one knee. The next pull will take him against the rail; the pull after that will take the rail with him.

Jack has Tom's knife.

He does not remember taking it. The handle is in his hand because Tom gave him the lee brace and the knife hangs beside every order Tom gives. Jack runs forward while other men run aft.

The deck changes angle beneath a wall of rain. He falls, keeps the knife and loses a piece of skin from one knee. The backstay tightens. Tom's shoulder strikes the rail.

Cut it! Tom shouts.

The line is tarred hemp thick as Jack's wrist.

Jack cuts.

The blade makes almost no mark.

Not across. With the lay!

Jack turns the knife and drives its point between the strands. The rope opens enough to show pale fibres within. He saws through one bundle, then another. Every movement of the wreck in the water closes the rope against the blade.

Tom's arm has gone dark below the turn.

Jack puts one foot against the rail, holds the knife in both hands and pulls upward through the last strand.

The stay parts.

The severed end goes over the side with such force it cuts a smoking line across the wet rail. Tom falls backward onto Jack. For a moment they are both on the deck beneath water that is rain and sea together.

Then Tom is moving.

He does not thank Jack. There is no time in which gratitude would not be another obstruction.

Clear the wreckage! Axes forward! Hands off every line until you know where it leads!

His left arm hangs badly. His voice does not.

The fallen mast pounds the hull. Each strike shakes the deck. If it is allowed to remain, the broken spar will drive through the planking or drag the ship broadside to the wind.

Men take axes to the surviving rigging. Every cut releases a new violence. One block flies upward when its load goes and catches Pritchard beneath the jaw. He disappears without a sound under the wrecked sail.

Jack sees his boot.

He starts toward it.

Tom catches the back of his coat with the good hand.

No.

He's under there.

The mast is over the side.

He's alive.

You do not know that.

I know his boot.

The ship rolls. The broken spar lifts in the sea and slams back against the hull. Beneath the canvas, the boot moves.

Jack tears free.

This time Tom follows.

They crawl beneath the edge of the sail while the quartermaster and three others haul it clear by inches. Pritchard lies between the fallen yard and the splintered boat, blood running from his mouth. Jack gets both hands beneath his shoulders. Tom grips the coat at the waist with his good hand.

Together they drag him out before the mast strikes again.

The axes finish the last stay. The topmast, yard and sail fall away into grey water and vanish behind the ship, still joined, still briefly looking like something built by men.

The foremast remains standing below the top.

Nothing above it does.

* * *

## IX. Under Bare Poles

The squall passes in less than an hour.

That is the insult of it.

The black wall goes east. Rain thins. A broken sunlight opens across the water and makes every wet surface shine: the severed rigging, the split boat, the blood at Pritchard's collar, the empty place above the foretop where half the mast used to be.

The sea looks refreshed.

The ship looks robbed.

Pritchard lives. His jaw is broken and three teeth lie somewhere beneath the forward gun. The surgeon binds his head and says he may never chew on that side. Pritchard asks whether the lost teeth are to be charged as Company stores. The words are mud in his mouth. Jack laughs before he can stop himself.

Pritchard does too, then regrets it.

Tom's shoulder is not broken. The backstay has cut a dark groove round the upper arm and pulled the joint until every movement makes his face empty. The surgeon offers a sling. Tom uses it for one watch, then takes it off because a boatswain pointing with his right hand sends men toward the wrong rope.

The master holds an inquiry before the deck is dry.

He asks why sail was not shortened sooner. He asks whether the foretopmast was unsound. He asks who last inspected the cap. Each question travels carefully around the fact that he said the weather would pass astern.

The clerk sits at the cabin table with his pen waiting.

Tom gives him no shelter.

The upper wind changed before the wind at deck level. The foretopsail backed twice. The topmast moved out of true at the cap. I ordered men down and put Mercer on the lee brace. The mast failed before the sail could be taken in.

Mercer saw the movement? the master asks.

Yes.

A boy?

A lookout aloft.

Was the cap defective?

It is in the sea.

Then we cannot know.

We know it failed.

The master turns to Jack. How long between your reporting the movement and the loss of the mast?

Jack looks at Tom.

Answer the question, Tom says.

Ten seconds.

You counted?

No.

Then how can you state ten?

Jack gives the actions. The line in his hands. The quartermaster's warning. The first rain. The orders to ease and haul. The shout from forward. Tom stepping clear. The wind.

The clerk stops writing midway through because actions do not fit the narrow space left for the answer.

Approximately ten seconds, he writes.

The master asks whether Jack would have remained aloft without Tom's order.

Yes.

And been lost with the mast.

Yes.

The question waits for gratitude. Jack has gratitude. He also has Tom's blood beneath his fingernails from opening the backstay.

Tom would have gone over if I hadn't cut him loose, he says.

The clerk looks up.

The master does not want this second truth. It complicates the shape of command. A boatswain saves a boy; a boy does not save a boatswain. One is discipline. The other looks like dependence.

Tom puts his injured arm on the table.

Enter it.

The clerk enters both.

* * *

## X. A Shorter Mast

For nine days the ship moves under reduced sail while the crew builds what the sea allows.

They have spare timber, but no spare foretopmast equal to the one lost. Tom chooses a shorter spar and has the carpenters shape it. The riggers fit new stays. The quartermaster serves the lines. Jack works wherever a narrow body can reach.

This is rigging without metaphor. Measure, splice, parcel, serve. A rope too short does not become long because the ship needs it. A weak eye does not become sound because the captain signs an order. Every piece tells the truth when weight arrives.

Jack likes the work.

It is the first thing aboard he likes without also fearing it.

Tom shows him how to bury the ends of a splice so the join carries strain along its length. Jack's first is bulky. His second is neat and weak. Tom cuts both open.

The third holds.

Good, Tom says.

It is the first praise Jack has received from him.

He tries not to show what it does.

At sunset they stand beneath the shortened mast. The vessel looks wrong, one shoulder lower than the other, but the new rig draws.

Why the lee brace? Jack asks.

Tom flexes the fingers below his injured arm.

It needed another hand.

There were men on deck.

Not men who saw the mast move.

You believed me.

You were aloft. That was your work.

The answer refuses heroism. It gives Jack something better: his observation belonged in the decision.

You knew it would go?

I knew it might.

Ten seconds.

Nine would have done.

Jack looks up at the shorter spar, the new stays and the place in the sky where the old mast failed.

And eight?

Tom considers.

Do not build a life around finding out.

Weeks later the water changes colour.

It is not yet the impossible green Tom promised in Wapping. But birds begin appearing at dawn, flying with purpose instead of resting lost on the yards. Weed passes the hull. One evening the air carries smoke from land nobody can see.

Jack stands at the foremast with one hand against the wood. The new rig speaks in a higher voice than the old one. He can feel his own splice carrying its share.

Behind him, somewhere off Madagascar, a foretopmast lies descending through dark water with ten seconds still inside it.

* * *

## XI. Green Water

The water turns green before dawn.

Not the grey-green of the Channel or the black-green beneath a storm. This is light held inside water, clear enough that when the lead goes down Jack watches the pale line descend farther than any line should remain visible. Small fish turn around it like filings around a magnet.

Anchor ready, Tom calls.

Jack is already at the cable.

Bantam lies ahead beneath a morning haze: low roofs, palms, river mouths and more ships than the approach has room to explain. Javanese prahus with high sterns. Chinese junks broad as houses. Portuguese vessels carrying crosses and cannon. Gujarati traders, Malay boats, Dutch hulls, English hulls, fishing craft moving among all of them without asking which king signed which monopoly.

The smell reaches the ship before the harbour boat does.

Smoke. Mud. Fruit opening in the heat. Pepper sharp enough to sit behind the eyes. Drains. Wet timber. Human beings living close together and refusing to become the clean, distant East painted on the Company's maps.

Jack forgets to work.

Only for a breath. His hands stop on the cable while a boat crosses beneath the bow with six men paddling in perfect time and a child sitting among baskets of green fruit. The child looks up at the scarred English ship, its shortened foretopmast and the white faces along the rail. To him, Jack understands, they are the strange arrival.

Tom's voice finds him.

Mercer.

Jack grips the cable.

The sea is on the wrong side of you again.

Jack hauls.

The anchor goes down through the green water. He watches until the iron becomes a dark, shrinking shape and the shape disappears into brightness.

Tom sees him watching.

Like I said, he says.

Jack does not ask whether this makes the voyage worth it. Tom once asked Pell the same question and received the only honest answer available: a count of the dead.

Nineteen men are absent from the rail.

The water remains beautiful.

Both facts stand.

* * *

## XII. The Factory Gate

The English factory is no workshop.

No saws. No seasoning sheds. No rows of men shaping the same object. It is a compound of warehouses, rooms, walls and clerks, a place where goods are made into English property by being weighed and written down.

The first lighter comes alongside before the ship has finished securing. Bantam men bring rice, water, fruit, baskets of small fish, chickens tied at the feet and jars whose contents nobody aboard can name. They work in Malay, Javanese, Portuguese and the quick gestures of men who know a buyer will pretend not to understand a price he dislikes.

Tom speaks enough market Portuguese to keep the water casks honest and enough Malay to ask who owns the boat. Jack knew neither thing about him.

Where did you learn that? he asks.

Here.

Who taught you?

Men who charged less after I stopped sounding English.

Tom's words are rough and practical. He uses no language for friendship if a language for water will do.

The first cargo opened is not pepper but breakfast.

The cook has spent the passage making old food change shape. Biscuit pounded into broth. Salt beef boiled, cooled and boiled again. Pease made thin enough to drink when the teeth hurt. He receives the Bantam provisions with the suspicion of a man who has learned that anything green may be rotten underneath, cuts each fruit before he trusts the basket and puts the rice through his fingers looking for grit.

Then the galley begins to smell of food that has not crossed an ocean.

Rice lifts white beneath the pot lid. Small fish go into a pan with oil and something sharp enough to make the men sneeze. One chicken is killed for broth for the sick mess; another is divided so finely that every bowl receives proof of it and almost none receives meat. The green fruit has a skin Jack cannot name and flesh the colour of a candle flame.

He bites too quickly. Juice enters the cracks in his lips and burns. His gums, softened by months of biscuit and salt meat, leave a little blood on the pale flesh.

Tom catches his wrist before he takes a second piece.

Slow.

I'm hungry.

Your belly has had longer to forget this than your mouth has.

Around them, men make the same mistake in different ways. One swallows half a bowl of rice and has to sit with his head between his knees. Another chews fish until there is nothing left to chew, unwilling to let the taste end. Pritchard works softened rice through the side of his mouth where the jaw meets badly and closes his eyes as though fresh food has accused him of something.

The cook gives Jack a smaller piece of fruit and watches until he eats it properly. No one jokes about the blood. Too many mouths aboard have begun bleeding when the weather turns, and men who once compared scars now conceal the places where their bodies are quietly coming apart.

Last comes the water.

It has been carried from a river above the town and settled in clean jars. The cook fills the dipper. Jack expects cask taste: oak, slime, the sour edge of water issued because it cannot yet be condemned. This tastes of almost nothing. He drinks, stops, and drinks again.

Enough, Tom says.

There is a whole boat of it.

There was a whole ocean of water beside us. You could not drink that either.

Jack gives the dipper on. The next man takes it in both hands.

The bowls are collected before the boat goes ashore. A clerk enters rice, fish, fruit, two chickens and fresh water against the ship, each item with a number. He does not enter the cracked mouths, the stomachs that must be taught abundance by spoonfuls or the nineteen men who require no bowl at all.

On shore, the heat has weight.

It settles onto Jack's shoulders the moment he leaves the boat. His shirt clings before he reaches the gate. Flies find the salt at his eyes. The market beyond the Company's walls is loud with metal, poultry, bargaining, wheels, prayer and a hammer striking the same patient note somewhere out of sight.

The stone landing rises beneath him.

Jack catches the post. For months every surface has yielded to water. Now the quay appears to lift, settle and lift again while the harbour remains level. His knees answer a roll that is no longer there.

Tom waits beside him without amusement.

How long?

An hour. Sometimes a day.

You had it?

Everybody has it. Some men are too proud to hold the post.

Jack releases the timber only when the land agrees to stay beneath him. By then the boatmen have passed with the empty provision jars and nobody except Tom has noticed.

Inside the gate, sound changes. English voices. Dutch voices through another wall. Clerks making lists beneath deep eaves while porters move cargo through the sun.

A factor meets the ship's officers in a clean shirt with white cuffs. He wants to know the pepper capacity, the state of the guns and whether the lost foretopmast delayed them enough to affect the return season.

Tom gives him the damage and the days.

The factor asks how many men died.

Nineteen.

The clean cuff moves across a page.

How many remain fit for loading?

Tom looks at the hand, then at the factor.

You asked two questions as though they were one.

I have two columns.

Then leave space between them.

The factor does not understand the objection. He enters nineteen under loss and the surviving hands under available labour. The page is accurate.

Jack watches it become a lie without changing a number.

Outside the office, bales wait beneath tarpaulins and pepper lies in sacks stacked higher than a man. This is what the voyage crossed the world to reach. Not green water. Not the market. Not the child in the harbour boat.

Sacks.

The Company measures success by how deep the ship sits when it leaves.

* * *

## XIII. The Rope Market

Jack's first afternoon ashore belongs to the ship.

Tom takes him through the market looking for hemp, coir and timber fit to strengthen the shortened rig before the return passage. Jack expects Tom to choose. Instead Tom makes him handle every coil.

What do you feel?

Rope.

Then the rope knows more about you than you know about it.

The seller laughs. He is an old Gujarati merchant with a beard dyed red at the chin and a younger man beside him who translates only when misunderstanding threatens the price.

Jack bends the line. Coir is lighter than wet hemp, harsh under the fingers and made from fibre he has never seen growing. One coil has been laid evenly. Another hides soft sections beneath a tight outer turn.

This one, Jack says.

Why?

Same lay all through.

You have opened ten feet.

Jack looks at the merchant. May I?

The younger man translates. The merchant gestures that Jack may ruin as much rope as the English intend to buy.

Jack opens another section. Sound. Another. Sound. At the centre he finds a join so carefully buried his fingers pass it twice before the change in thickness gives it away.

He looks at the merchant.

The old man nods once.

Good work, Jack says.

The translator gives him the words. The merchant answers at length.

He says good work does not become better because an English boy discovers it.

Tom buys the coil.

On the walk back Jack sees riggers working beneath an open shed. No Company gate. Three men serving a stay, a woman measuring cordage against marks cut into a post, two boys twisting small line from loose fibre. Their movements are fast without being hurried. A customer brings a broken boat fitting. The woman names the work, the price and the day. The customer argues. She points to the marks. He pays half.

Jack stops.

Tom has gone six paces before he notices.

That a rigging shop? Jack asks.

Close enough.

They work for themselves?

They work for whoever pays.

Same thing.

No, Tom says. But closer.

The woman sees Jack looking and holds up the end of the stay. The splice is longer than the one Tom taught him after Madagascar, tapered so gradually the standing part scarcely thickens.

Jack steps beneath the shed. She puts the rope into his hands and says something he cannot understand. When he looks at the translator, there is none.

She takes his fingers and places them on the buried ends.

The lesson requires no shared word.

Jack feels where each strand disappears.

The woman takes the stay back, points at Jack's eyes and closes her own. Then she gives him the splice again. Jack understands and shuts his eyes.

Without sight, the join is larger. His thumb finds the first buried end, then loses the second beneath the lay. He opens his eyes. She repeats the gesture. He closes them again.

He starts from the standing part this time. Sound rope. A slight rise. One strand thinning beneath the next, its fibres spread instead of cut square. Another turn and the second end. The splice tells the hand what the eye accepted too easily.

She lets him look.

From a basket beneath the bench she takes three short pieces of coir. One has been cut clean. One has been worked enough to soften it. The third is already beginning to open at the end. She puts them before Jack, separates the fibres with a blunt marline spike and makes the taper slowly: fewer fibres at each turn, the end losing itself by degrees rather than stopping inside the rope like a knot hidden under cloth.

Jack reaches for the spike.

She gives it to him.

His first tuck crosses the lay. She pulls it out. His second leaves too much fibre together. She rolls the swelling beneath her palm, then rolls the sound splice, and makes him do the same. The good one passes under the hand like one long thought. His has a word in the middle of it.

Again, Jack says, though she cannot know the word.

She knows the request.

On the third attempt he stops looking at the loose ends and watches where the standing rope wants to open. The spike enters with the lay instead of against it. He divides the fibres, buries half, rolls the work, feels the remaining hardness and opens it once more. The woman does not help. She serves another customer's line while Jack pulls his own mistake apart.

Tom waits outside the shed with the purchased coil over one shoulder. He could translate the lesson into an order. He does not.

Jack finishes the taper. The woman takes the short line, grips one end in each hand and loads it with her weight. The splice lengthens, settles and holds. She gives it back, turns Jack's hand palm upward and lays the work across it.

There is still a rise where his thumb can find it.

She points to the bench.

Again.

This time Jack laughs. The woman has already turned to the next length.

Tom does not call Jack back until the factory bell rings for the boat. Jack leaves the practice splice on the bench because it is not sound yet and follows with the shape of the hidden ends still present in his fingers.

* * *

## XIV. The Capstan

The accident happens because the pepper must be aboard before rain.

Clouds gather over Bantam in the afternoon, high and white at first, then dark beneath. The factor sees weather and calculates spoiled cargo. He orders two lighters unloaded together.

The ship is still short nineteen men.

Sacks rise from the lighter in cargo nets. The foremast tackles carry what the shortened topmast cannot. At the capstan, men walk the bars round while Jack tails the hauling line and watches each load clear the rail.

The first lighter empties. Rain begins beyond the roadstead, a grey curtain moving across the green water.

Faster, the factor's clerk calls from the deck.

Tom tells the men to keep the same pace.

The clerk points at the weather.

Tom points at the load hanging over six people in the lighter.

Same pace.

The next net catches beneath the channel. The capstan stops. Men lean against the bars, holding the weight while somebody below shouts that a sack has slipped.

Jack moves to take another turn on the tail.

The line jerks.

Two fingers of his right hand enter the riding turn.

There is no pain at first. Only knowledge: his hand is where rope has decided to become solid.

Jack shouts.

Tom is on the other side of the capstan. He sees the trapped hand and drives his shoulder against a bar.

Back it! Half a turn!

Men push the wrong way out of habit.

Other way! Back it!

The capstan moves a fraction. The load drops toward the lighter. Men below scatter. Tom holds the bar while Jack tears his hand free.

Then pain arrives.

It is white and immediate. Jack kneels with the hand pressed beneath his other arm, making a sound he already wishes unmade.

Tom does not touch him.

Show me.

Jack cannot.

Show me.

The last two fingers lie at an angle fingers do not choose.

The surgeon sets them while Jack bites a folded strap. He binds the hand to a narrow board and says the bones may knit straight if the boy does not use them.

How long? Tom asks.

Six weeks.

The return loading ends in four days.

Then he will be useless for the return loading.

Jack hears the word.

Tom hears Jack hear it.

He is injured, Tom says.

To a surgeon those conditions are often identical.

The surgeon enters the examination and splint in his medical book. The purser, who has followed the blood below with the wage ledger, enters something else.

Dressing and linen supplied at the surgeon's table. Sixpence against wages.

The setting of the bones belongs to the ship. Harcourt's articles make the rag around them a recoverable store.

Tom puts his good hand flat on the page. The other shoulder still pains him from Madagascar.

The injury occurred in Company loading.

The linen was supplied to the servant.

Then charge it to the loading account.

The purser pulls the book away. The articles provide for necessary dressing and medical stores.

Jack remembers the hiring room. He remembers Maggie's question: whether the dressing comes out of his wage if another man breaks his head.

The capstan broke my hand, he says.

The purser looks relieved to have found the correct defendant.

Then the capstan may pay you back.

He sands the entry.

Tom could tear out the page. He does not. Jack can see the choice pass through him: the old answer, force against the immediate man, and the newer knowledge that another clean page will be made upstairs.

Name the fee correctly, Tom says.

It is correctly named.

No. Call it what it is. Linen charged to the hand damaged while earning the cargo.

The purser leaves the line unchanged.

The rain reaches the ship. Pepper comes aboard beneath tarpaulins. Jack's hand swells inside the splint until each heartbeat feels trapped there.

The factor's cargo stays dry.

* * *

## XV. Enough

Jack spends six weeks learning what work remains when one hand is bound.

He can carry messages. Count coils. Hold a marline spike beneath his arm. He can sit beside Pritchard, whose broken jaw has healed crooked, and insult the cook's broth while Pritchard insults it back in a voice permanently changed.

He cannot go aloft.

That is the hardest part.

The repaired rig draws above him, including the splice he made. Other men put their weight on it. Jack stands below and feels useless in the exact place where he first discovered useful work.

Tom gives him the rigging inventory.

Jack can read numbers and his own name. The descriptions defeat him.

Read it to me, he says.

Tom does.

Every evening they take one line. Jack learns *hemp*, *coir*, *served*, *new*, *worn*, *issued*. The written words begin attaching themselves to things his hands already know.

Why bother? he asks. The purser keeps the book.

That is why.

Jack looks at the bound fingers.

Good rope tells the truth when weight arrives. A ledger requires somebody to make it.

At the end of the sixth week the surgeon removes the board. Two fingers move. Two do not move fully and never will again. Jack learns to close the hand around a line by placing the crooked fingers first.

The grip is ugly.

It holds.

The night before the ship leaves Bantam, Tom finds him at the rail watching harbour lamps move across the green water.

You wanted the other side, Tom says.

I saw it.

Worth the voyage?

Jack considers the market, the woman rigger's buried splice, the nineteen names, the topmast, the sixpence drying in the purser's book.

Parts.

That is most honest answers.

I'm not signing again.

Tom remains beside him. He does not say Jack may change his mind. He does not explain the wages of an experienced hand or the berth that might wait after one successful voyage. The Company has enough men who can make a second paper sound inevitable.

What will you do? he asks.

Rigging.

Ashore?

Where I can see both ends of the rope.

Tom looks out at the ships, each one held by lines running into darkness.

Learn the work first.

Teach me, then.

Until England.

Until England, Jack agrees.

The ship sails on the morning tide, lower in the water with pepper and higher at the foremast because the replacement spar still looks new. Jack stands beside the cable as the anchor rises. The green water gives it back slowly: cable, ring, shank, fluke, each part becoming visible before it breaks the surface.

He has crossed the world to discover that seeing a thing clearly does not make it yours, and surviving a choice does not oblige you to choose it twice.

One voyage is enough.

* * *

## XVI. Homeward

The return voyage teaches Jack that going home is also ship's work.

There is no road beneath them becoming shorter. No steeple on the horizon for months. Only the same watches, the same shortened foretopmast and the same ocean now resisting in the opposite direction. The vessel rides deep and answers the helm slowly, as though profit has weight beyond its sacks.

Tom teaches Jack rigging until they reach the Cape.

Not the three knots from Wapping. Eyes, seizings, splices, serving, the different stretch of hemp and coir, how standing rigging takes strain and how running rigging must surrender it. Jack learns to estimate a coil by lifting it with the good hand and steadying it against his chest. He learns to use the two crooked fingers first so the others can close over them.

The dressing-and-linen sixpence sits in the wage book.

Every time the purser opens the ledger, Jack sees the small charge. It has become larger than sixpence because it is the only part of his hand the Company has agreed to remember.

The purser also enters half Jack's monthly wage against Carter's account. Beneath it he enters two shillings from Tom, described as instruction allowance. Jack watches these numbers without yet understanding how quickly four pounds should disappear beneath them.

How much remains? he asks after the Cape.

The purser turns the book away.

Accounts are settled in England.

That isn't a number.

It is the answer available aboard.

Jack has heard that sentence in several forms since signing. The answer available. The term as written. The necessary charge. Each one means the number exists somewhere the speaker does not intend to open.

Tom tells him to wait for the pay table.

You know what remains? Jack asks.

No.

You have been paying it.

Yes.

And never counted?

I counted what left my wage.

That is half an account.

Tom looks at him.

Jack has learned this tone from Tom and is surprised to hear it in his own mouth.

The boatswain takes the purser's wage book, turns to a blank margin and begins reconstructing the debt. Jack's assigned halves. Tom's two shillings every month. The original four pounds. No interest permitted beyond the amount Carter's steward accepted at the hiring rail.

They calculate twice.

The account closed two months before the ship reached the Cape.

Every deduction since then has gone somewhere else. Jack's articles direct Tom's payment to Jack after closure. The purser has continued entering it against Carter's satisfied assignment instead.

Tom returns to the purser.

The purser examines their sum and says shore assignments are not his department.

You make the deduction.

At the instruction attached to the articles.

The instruction is discharged.

Then Carter's steward should have sent notice.

Did he?

No notice reached this book.

Tom puts one finger on the continued deductions.

Then whose money is this?

The purser closes the ledger.

A question for the pay office.

Tom's hand remains on the cover a moment after the purser's does.

Pay table, he says to Jack.

The words are a promise and a postponement. Jack has lived long enough under both to know they are not the same.

* * *

## XVII. The River Returns

England arrives as smell.

Coal smoke. Cold mud. Wet wool brought aboard by the pilot. After Bantam's fruit and drains, after the open ocean, the Thames smells narrow and used. Jack stands at the rail breathing it as though dirt can be a kind of welcome.

The river has not waited for him.

New scaffolds rise beside old yards. Hulls crowd the reaches. Company marks appear on warehouses that belonged to other merchants when Jack left. At Blackwall, carts queue before the ship has made fast, ready to remove the pepper before the men who carried it have been paid.

Jack searches the shore for his family.

Nobody is there.

He did not know which week they would arrive. His mother cannot leave his father alone to wait upon a rumour. Jack understands all of this and still experiences the empty quay as an accusation.

Tom has no one waiting either.

He behaves as though that was arranged.

The crew musters on deck. The clerk reads the dead by number until Tom interrupts him after the third.

Names.

The clerk finds the amended list.

Ellis. Brown. Cooper. Venn. Nineteen men return to England as spoken words and no bodies. Pritchard stands with his jaw crooked. Jack flexes the two fingers that do not fully flex. The voyage has brought home everything the pay office knows how to unload.

The captain praises discipline, endurance and a profitable cargo. He says the Company will remember the service.

Men look toward the warehouse where the pay table waits.

They know exactly how the Company remembers.

* * *

## XVIII. The Table

The pay room is long, low and built to make waiting men feel numerous rather than important.

A rail divides the sailors from three clerks and a table covered in ledgers. One man reads the account. One counts coin. One witnesses the mark. Behind them stands the paymaster, clean at the cuffs, available for irregularities and therefore absent from ordinary conversation.

The line moves slowly.

Every man arrives with a voyage and leaves with a subtraction.

Advance. Slops. Knife. Tobacco. Bedding. Medical attendance. Loss of issued property. Money paid to a wife who swears she received less. Money assigned to a creditor who swears he received exactly what is written. The clerk never raises his voice. Arithmetic has no need.

Pritchard is charged for one replacement shirt lost when the foretopmast took his chest. He asks whether the sea has opened an account. The clerk tells him disputes must be submitted in writing.

Pritchard cannot write.

Next.

Jack reaches the table near dusk.

Name?

Jack Mercer.

The clerk finds him.

Gross wage. Half assigned to Carter's yard against four pounds. Kit nine shillings. Dressing and linen under the medical-stores clause, sixpence. One blanket issued—

One blanket was in the kit.

The clerk checks. One blanket. Corrected. Balance payable—

Stop.

The line behind Jack shifts.

He puts Tom's reconstructed sum on the table. The paper carries numbers in Tom's hand and descriptions Jack copied himself from the wage book because he has begun to distrust any account he cannot partly read.

When did Carter's four pounds close?

The clerk looks at the assignment page.

It has no closure date.

Then how long did you take my half?

Until discharge from the creditor.

The debt was paid two months before the Cape.

Do you have the creditor's discharge?

No.

Then it was not discharged.

It was paid.

Those are different conditions.

Jack hears Maria's lesson without knowing her: a true debt and a release order can occupy the same morning. Paper records power. It does not replace it.

He puts his crooked hand on the table.

You took the money.

At the proper instruction.

Where did it go after the four pounds?

The clerk turns to another ledger. Carter's assignment receipts stop at four pounds. The deductions from Jack's wage continue.

He turns another page.

The extra is not credited to Carter. It is not returned to Jack. It is not entered as Company income. The figures vanish between books with every clerk having completed his own correct line.

The clerk calls the paymaster.

The paymaster examines the pages. He is a broad man with a narrow mouth, old enough to know that a missing sum becomes dangerous only when someone important asks where it went.

The boy may submit a petition, he says.

I'm here, Jack says.

A petition is a document.

So was the deduction.

The line behind Jack has stopped being impatient. Men lean forward. Several have assignments of their own.

The paymaster lowers his voice.

You will receive the balance stated. Any adjustment follows examination.

How long?

As long as examination requires.

That isn't a number.

It is not a question with a fixed answer.

Tom steps to the rail.

The paymaster sees the boatswain's coat and changes his expression by one careful degree.

Mr Fletcher, this is an accounts matter.

Good. Account for it.

The boy lacks a discharge.

Your books show four pounds received.

His deductions continued under a valid assignment.

To whom?

The paymaster looks at the clerks. None offers him a name.

The money has been held pending reconciliation.

Where?

In the accounts.

Which account?

The paymaster closes the ledger.

Tom reaches across the rail, takes him by the collar and brings him forward until the clean cuffs strike the table.

Coin stops moving in the room.

Tom does not hit him. He holds him against the wall behind the clerks with one fist at the throat of his coat. It is the old answer, force against the immediate man. It is also the only language in the room the paymaster has not prepared a form to delay.

I asked you politely, Tom says.

You assaulted an officer of the Company.

Not yet.

The paymaster looks past him for help. The sailors at the rail look back with the disciplined attention of men obeying no order at all.

Find the account, Tom says.

* * *

## XIX. Nowhere

The account is found in seven minutes.

It is not one account. It is a temporary balance, an unallocated receipt, a carried remainder and a discrepancy awaiting advice. Each name describes the same money at a different desk. None says Jack.

The paymaster straightens his collar while two clerks calculate.

Tom stands beside Jack now, no longer touching anyone. His violence has opened the books. It has not made what is inside them honest.

The overcollection is eleven months of half wages, less an adjustment for the month in which the debt closed, plus two of Tom's instruction payments misallocated after closure contrary to Jack's articles. The clerk tries to retain the dressing-and-linen sixpence from the recovered amount.

Jack points to the original wage line.

You already took it there.

The clerk checks.

He has tried to charge the injury twice.

He crosses out the second sixpence without apology.

The paymaster orders the balance paid.

Coin lands on the table in small stacks. Jack counts it twice. Tom does not help. A man who has made a room open its book must be allowed to finish reading it himself.

The two payments made after closure sit separately.

Jack pushes them toward Tom.

Yours.

The articles made them yours.

They were meant to close my father's account. It was closed.

Then I paid badly.

Take them.

No.

Jack closes his crooked fingers over the coins and puts them into Tom's palm. The grip makes refusal difficult.

I did not ask you to pay my father's debt.

No.

You did it anyway.

Yes.

It helped.

Yes.

It did not make the paper kind.

No.

They have learned to keep two truths standing without forcing either one to kneel.

Behind them, Pritchard has returned to the rail with three other sailors and a man who can write. The lost shirt is becoming a petition. Somebody asks when a dead man's medical charges stop. Somebody else asks where his wife's assignment was held during the extra four months at sea.

The pay room will not close on time.

Jack pockets his wage.

Outside, cold rain begins.

* * *

## XX. Deptford

Carter's steward waits under the warehouse eaves.

He carries a receipt stating the four-pound account is satisfied. The page is dated months after the money reached Carter's book, because closure becomes real only when a clerk finds time to acknowledge it.

Jack reads the number himself.

The steward offers him work at the seasoning sheds. Light duties until the hand strengthens. His father's place cannot be held, but there is sweeping, tallying, barrel repair. Honest work, the steward calls it.

Jack looks at the Company carts taking pepper through the gate. Carter's ropes lifted the cargo. Carter's account sent Jack aboard to help fetch it. None of the steps required Carter to behave dishonestly.

No, Jack says.

Your family needs wages.

They'll have mine.

From where?

Deptford.

The old quartermaster has given him a direction: his own low shed near the King's yard, where he rigs between voyages and needs a learner with sea experience and does not mind crooked fingers if the knots hold. The wage is less than another Company voyage. It is more than sweeping. It belongs to work Jack has chosen after seeing it from both sides of the water.

Tom walks with him as far as the road divides.

The Pelican lies west. Deptford lies east and south across the river. Tom looks toward neither.

You going home first? he asks.

My mother will kill me if I don't.

Your father?

Jack folds Carter's receipt inside his coat.

He'll ask what it cost.

What will you tell him?

Four pounds. Nineteen men. Two fingers. Sixpence.

Tom almost smiles.

That is not how accounts are kept.

It is how this one is.

Jack asks whether Tom will come to Wapping.

No.

She might listen.

That is not the difficulty.

Then what is?

Tom looks back at the pay office. A collar pulled straight. A refund forced. One boy walking away with his wages. Easy evidence that a man has changed, if he wants easy evidence badly enough.

I would be going to show her this, he says.

You did do it.

Yes.

And that isn't enough?

Not for what she asked me to remember.

Jack does not fully understand. He knows only that Tom can cross oceans and not the short river road to one tavern door.

They clasp forearms. Jack's grip is awkward and strong.

If a Company man offers you a second berth, Tom says—

I know the answer.

Say it anyway.

No.

Good.

Jack walks toward the ferry with the receipt against his chest, his recovered wages in his coat and a rigger's direction in his pocket. Beyond the river lies Deptford: ropewalks, yards, ships he may help build without belonging to them.

Tom remains at the turning until Jack is out of sight.

Then the boatswain goes back toward the Company.

* * *

## XXI. Both Ends

Jack goes home first.

The two rooms are still there. Carter's steward did not take the bed. Rain has found a new path through the window frame, and his youngest brother has grown enough to occupy Jack's old place at the table, but the room has not been dismantled to satisfy four pounds.

His mother opens the door.

For one second she does not know him. The boy who left was narrow, soft-handed and frightened of a ladder. The person in the doorway is brown from weather, broader through the shoulders and holding one hand as though it belongs to somebody else.

Then she says his name and brings him inside by the coat.

His father sits beside the window with the injured arm supported on a board across his knees. He can lift a spoon now. He cannot close the hand around a cooper's adze. Carter's yard has found lighter tasks when available and no wage when they are not.

Jack puts the receipt on the board.

His father reads slowly, moving one finger beneath the words.

Satisfied in full, he says.

Yes.

How much did they take?

Four pounds. Then more.

How much more?

Jack sets the recovered coin beside the receipt.

His father looks at the money, then at the crooked fingers.

What did that cost?

Sixpence for the linen.

The old cooper does not laugh. He touches the splinted memory in Jack's hand without quite touching the fingers themselves.

That is not what I asked.

Jack gives him the longer account. Nineteen names. A foretopmast. Green water. Tom's two shillings. He does not make the voyage noble because he survived it. His father has already been injured once by an account that made damaged timber more important than the man beneath it.

When Jack finishes, his mother puts food in front of him.

He eats before anybody asks what comes next.

In the morning he walks to Deptford.

The old quartermaster is already in the low shed near the King's yard, with coils hanging from roof beams and tar warming beside the door. He looks at Jack's hand before his face.

Can it grip?

Jack closes it around a fid.

Can it splice?

Give me rope.

The quartermaster gives him two worn lengths that would otherwise become oakum. Jack opens the strands, marries them and buries each end the way the woman in Bantam showed him. His fingers are slow. The crooked pair must be placed before the sound ones can pull. The older man does not help and does not hurry him.

When the splice is finished, the rigger loops the rope through a roof ring and hangs his weight from it.

The join holds.

The quartermaster lowers himself.

Sea work?

One voyage.

Going again?

No.

Good. Men who dream of ships leave before they learn rope.

The wage is modest. The first month includes a charge for tools, stated aloud and written as a fixed number. Jack asks who owns them after the month.

You do, the quartermaster says.

Put that on the page.

The quartermaster looks at him, then writes it.

Jack signs with the mark he used at the Company rail and asks for his whole name beside it. This time he reads enough of the terms to know where every number ends.

Outside, the tide moves past Deptford toward Wapping and the sea. Inside the shed, Jack takes down a coil and finds both ends before beginning.

* * *

Behind the pay table stands another account, older than either voyage. It began the same week Bell died, eleven years before Jack ever signed anything, and it has been running underneath this room the whole time.

## XXII. The First Morning

Bell's chair is too low.

Arthur discovers this before he discovers anything hidden in the books. His knees press the underside of the desk. The left rung has been worn smooth by Bell's heel. When Arthur shifts his weight, the seat answers with a small dry complaint that makes both other copyists look up.

Neither says Bell's name.

Harcourt has given Arthur the dead man's sand shaker, penknife and pumice stone. The inkwell has already been divided between the two surviving desks. Bell's coat, cap and purse have been entered in Harcourt's inventory against deficiencies, though all three remain with the widow. The page missing from the private book is entered at a value Harcourt has not yet decided. A man's life has left one empty chair and one recoverable loss.

Arthur begins with the inventory.

*One penknife, horn handle. One sand shaker, pewter. One pumice stone, much used.*

He stops before the chair.

Does furniture pass to the widow? he asks.

The senior copyist looks at him. Furniture belongs to the room.

Arthur writes nothing beside it.

His first paid task is to make another fair copy of Tom's wage assignment. Bell's hand fixes the original debt at eleven pounds, six shillings and fourpence. Arthur's own witness mark sits below it, less than a day old. Half wages continue until discharge. Replacement, medicine, rent and interruption may be added. Each clause is lawful. Together they form a corridor with a door painted at the far end.

Arthur copies every word.

Then he opens the weather book he used to keep at the Pelican. The wagers on frost, tide and wind occupy the front. He turns it upside down and begins at the back.

*Matthew Bell. Dead before the account closed. Cap delivered to Anne. Private page not recovered by Harcourt.*

Below that he copies Tom's figure and adds what the fair copy cannot hold.

*Father's debt designed to continue. Son knows and signs because refusal will cost the rooms.*

It is not proof. It is not a petition. It is a true copy of the thing that happened around the document.

He closes the weather book when Harcourt enters.

Your hand is smaller than Matthew's, Harcourt says, examining the finished assignment.

Is that a fault?

It leaves room.

Harcourt taps the open space beneath the recovery clause.

Arthur understands the compliment.

At the end of the day he washes his hands in the yard. The ink comes away in grey ribbons, then in pale clouds when he rubs the first finger of his right hand with pumice. He keeps at it until the skin is pink and clean.

Bell's finger had looked bruised because Bell had stopped trying.

Arthur tells himself he never will.

* * *

## XXIII. Both Books

The private book grows by lines. The public books grow by shelves.

At first Arthur copies only matters he believes he may one day correct. A sailmaker's advance retained after the man's death. Three months of a widow's assignment resting between Harcourt's receipt and the pay office's acknowledgement. A surgeon's charge entered twice, once as care and once as stores. The facts are small enough to fit beneath weather.

He gives each one a number. He copies the number into no official index.

This feels like caution. Some evenings it feels like courage.

The work rewards him quickly. Harcourt discovers that Arthur can reconcile a cargo book against a pay book without making the people in either appear to have erred. A carried balance becomes an amount awaiting advice. A deduction without a recipient becomes a retained assignment. A dead creditor becomes an estate not yet represented. Nothing disappears. It merely receives a name under which no ordinary man will think to look.

Arthur is paid twelve shillings a week before Bell has been dead a year.

He uses the first increase to buy a better lock for the room where he sleeps. The weather book goes beneath a loose board. He keeps the key to the room on his person and the key to Harcourt's desks on a ring at work. One protects the true copies. The other opens the originals from which harm may be copied indefinitely.

In the spring of 1604, a woman comes for wages assigned by her husband before the Company's ship sailed. The ship is overdue. The man may be alive, dead or in a harbour no London clerk can spell. Four months have been deducted from his pay book. Harcourt's receipt book shows three.

The missing month is not stolen. Arthur finds it by dinner. It rests in a total carried forward from one venture to the next because the ship changed its expected year of return.

The woman has brought a boy young enough to sleep against her skirt while she waits.

Can you give me the month? she asks.

The money is not in this room.

Where is it?

In the account.

She looks around at the shelves as if one of them might be the account he means.

Can I take that home and spend it?

No.

Then it is not where I am.

Arthur prepares a direction to the pay office. Harcourt signs it after altering *pay immediately* to *examine and pay if found due*. Both instructions can produce the same coin. Only one admits which direction the delay runs.

The woman leaves with no money and a paper requiring another walk.

That night Arthur enters her husband's name in the private book. He records the missing month, the sleeping child and the distance to the pay office. He does not know whether she ever receives the coin.

This last fact troubles him, so he leaves a space.

* * *

## XXIV. The Useful Hand

Harcourt's affairs move into the Company without ever quite being sold to it. Money passes under a new seal. Pepper replaces currants in the warehouse air. Men who once traded to Smyrna begin speaking of Bantam as though saying the name gives them a key to it.

Arthur goes with the books.

At first he attends in Leadenhall Street two days each week to explain Harcourt's assignments. Then four. Then his desk remains there overnight and Bell's old chair stays in the counting room without him. Harcourt offers to send it. Arthur says the Company has chairs.

The one they give him is higher, broader and does not complain.

It also faces a hiring rail.

The Company asks Arthur to make one form out of six merchants' articles. He approaches the work as he once approached a gale: direction, force, duration, consequence. A sailor should be able to see what is owed. A creditor should be able to see what may be recovered. A purser should not have to guess which instruction governs.

Arthur sets the terms in order. Advance. Assigned portion. Equipment. Medical attendance. Replacement. Death. Desertion. Interruption. He removes contradictory phrases, gives the numbers straight columns and draws a witness line wide enough for a steady name or a poor man's mark.

He leaves *reasonable cost as supplied* beside equipment because no clerk can know in London whether a man will need shoes at Blackwall. He leaves *at the purser's valuation* beside replacement because no chair ashore can judge the condition of a knife lost at sea. He leaves discharge dependent on notice from the creditor because a ship's book cannot see a payment made in Wapping.

Each empty place has a reason.

When the form is finished, a committee man reads it once and says it is wonderfully clear.

Within a season, boys are bound to amounts nobody stated because Arthur has made the surrounding language easy to trust.

He sees the first completed forms when a purser's packet returns from the Downs. Nine shillings for a chest already cut with another man's initials. New value for a used blanket. Surgeon where required, whether the sailor asked for treatment or was carried to it insensible. Arthur marks the irregularities on slips and sends them to the proper desks.

The proper desks answer that particulars will be examined upon pay-off.

He copies each answer into the private book.

By then the weather wagers are gone. Tide tables give way to names. The book has become thick enough to distort its own binding. Arthur buys another and begins again, numbering the second from the first so no entry stands alone.

He could take both to a magistrate. He can already imagine what would happen. The entries show lawful assignments, delayed advice, permitted charges and mistakes corrected when discovered. They do not show one order to injure one man. They show a practice, which is harder to hang and easier to continue.

Arthur keeps copying.

The ink begins staying in his finger during the winter of 1606. First at the side of the nail, then inside the crease nearest the hand. Vinegar lightens it. Pumice takes the skin before it takes the colour. A woman who launders for the clerks gives him lye soap and says clerks always believe dirt will yield if addressed in the right terms.

He laughs. He used to be good at laughing.

By Candlemas the stain resembles a bruise.

He wears it to work.

* * *

## XXV. Additions

In 1608 a parish notice reaches Arthur concerning Fletcher, lately boatswain in Harcourt's Levant service.

For one instant he expects Tom's name. Then he reads the Christian name and understands it is the father.

The ruined knee has gone black. A physician attended. The man is dead. Burial was advanced by Harcourt's steward. Rent remains unpaid for the final month.

Arthur sets the notices beside the old assignment. Tom has been east for most of five years, sending half a wage into the account whenever the Company's books admit he is alive. The original sum has fallen. The additions lift it again.

Physician. Burial. Rent. Interest during the voyage's extension.

No single charge is invented.

Arthur asks Harcourt whether burial might be forgiven.

Forgiven by whom?

The account.

Accounts do not forgive, Harcourt says. Men do. Are you proposing to pay it?

Arthur knows the amount. He knows his quarterly wage, the cost of his room, the winter coal, the money he sends to a sister in Limehouse when her husband's work runs thin. He could pay this charge. He cannot pay the sailmaker's month, the double surgeon's fee, the widow's walk and every other line in the two private books.

No, he says.

Then enter it.

Arthur enters it.

In the private book he writes the same charges again, with different descriptions.

*A physician who did not save him. A burial his son did not attend. Rent on a room the dead man no longer occupies.*

He looks at the sentences and crosses them out. They are accusation, not account. He writes more exactly.

*All supplied. All added. The father is dead. The assignment continues.*

The shorter entry is worse.

For three nights he considers carrying the books to the Pelican. Maggie would keep them. Maggie would also ask what he means the keeping to do. He has not drunk there for years. Each month makes returning harder, and Arthur can no longer tell whether he would carry the books to help anyone or to be seen carrying them.

He stays in his room.

The books remain under the board.

At work, a junior clerk asks why Arthur sometimes copies a completed page before filing it.

To check the hand, Arthur says.

Whose?

Mine.

It is true, and therefore useful.

* * *

## XXVI. The Boy Who Asked Twice

Nine years after Bell dies, Tom stands on the other side of Arthur's rail.

Arthur knows him first by the way he occupies the space between the boy and the room. Then he sees the pike scar, the boatswain's whistle and what nine years of surviving have removed from the face beneath them.

The boy is called Jack. Sixteen. A cooper's hands not yet hardened into a cooper's trade. Four pounds assigned to Carter's yard because his injured father lived long enough to owe for the timber that fell on him.

Arthur has already entered half the wage when Jack asks the cost of his kit.

Reasonable cost as supplied, Arthur says.

That is not a number.

The sentence reaches across nine years and puts Arthur back in Bell's chair with an empty value beneath his pen.

Jack asks twice. The line behind him begins asking too. The hiring room, built to turn different necessities into one form, becomes for several minutes a room full of particular men.

Arthur caps the kit at twelve shillings. He values replacements at their condition when lost. At Tom's instruction he writes two shillings from the boatswain's wage against the four pounds until the account closes, then to Jack.

They are small lines. Arthur knows their size because he knows the form around them better than anybody.

He reads every alteration aloud before Jack makes his mark.

After the doors close, Arthur copies the articles into his private book. Not merely the clauses. He records that Jack asked. He records that Tom's allowance stops going to Carter at four pounds and thereafter passes to Jack. He records Harcourt's steward accepting the limit rather than hearing the account in public.

Then he drafts articles for six more boys.

None has Maggie's questions.

One owns shoes and is charged shoes because the purser will decide what counts as fit. One sends all but three shillings each month to a mother who cannot read the receipt. One is perhaps fourteen and states seventeen because seventeen is the age at which necessity becomes easier to witness.

Arthur reads aloud what the room requires and witnesses each mark.

When he rises, the right leg of the chair has pressed a numb line into his thigh. The mark fades on the walk home.

The mark on his finger does not.

* * *

## XXVII. The Clearer Form

Jack comes home in 1614 without returning to Arthur's rail.

News reaches Leadenhall as an irregularity report from the pay office. A boatswain laid hands on a paymaster. Sailors delayed the table with questions. An assignment continued eleven months after four pounds had been received, along with two of the boatswain's instruction payments. The surplus acquired four correct names in four correct books and no person the desks agreed was entitled to spend it.

Arthur recognizes Jack from the capped kit and Tom from the two shillings.

The report says the balance was located, adjusted and paid. It says no loss remains to the servant. It recommends clearer language governing discharge.

No loss remains.

Arthur puts his stained finger beneath the sentence. Jack has returned with two crooked fingers, though the report records only the dressing and linen charged against them: sixpence. Nineteen men are absent from the pay line. Tom's violence appears as the irregular act because it is the only part of the day one book can hold without opening three others.

The secretary asks Arthur to revise the assignment form.

Arthur drafts the change before supper.

On future articles, deduction will continue until written discharge is received from the creditor or an officer directs otherwise, whether or not the stated sum appears to have been reached. The clause prevents a purser from guessing. It prevents a pay clerk from releasing money twice. It makes each desk exact.

It also makes Jack's eleven lost months the rule for the next boy.

Arthur takes the draft to the secretary.

This is clear, the secretary says.

Yes.

No room for dispute.

There is always room, Arthur says.

The secretary hears professional caution and approves the page.

Arthur returns to his desk after the other clerks leave. He has bread, hard cheese and a small beer brought up rather than go home. He eats before opening the loose bottom of his travelling box. The private books no longer fit under one floorboard, so he moves them in pairs between his room and the office, never leaving the whole account where one search can take it.

He enters Jack's result.

*Four pounds satisfied before the Cape. Eleven months and two instruction payments continued. Surplus returned only after the pay table was stopped. Sixpence attempted twice. Jack asked twice again.*

Below it he copies the new clause he has just drafted.

Official and true versions sit in the same hand.

For years Arthur has believed the private line stands against the public one. Tonight he sees the arrangement plainly. The true book is hidden. The form goes to the hiring rail. One records the harm after a man has forced somebody to count it. The other already makes the harm orderly before he arrives.

He does not burn the form. He does not carry the books to Wapping. He does not resign the chair.

He closes the private account, locks it away and turns to the stack waiting for morning. The top page requires a witness line. Arthur signs where the rule tells him.

When he sands the name, ink gathers in the first crease of his right finger. He rubs it once with his thumb. The colour does not move.

By then it is no longer a stain he carries home from the work.

It is the part of the work that stays.
