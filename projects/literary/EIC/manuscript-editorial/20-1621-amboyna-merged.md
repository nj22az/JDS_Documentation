# 1621–1623: Amboyna

> **1621–1623**
>
> *“Not received into our store. Ask Mr Vale, who witnessed the unloading.”*
>
> — a margin note in an English account, Amboyna, 1622

## I. The Story Worth Telling

Daniel Vale goes east because he is good at sums and tired of being paid like a man who is not.

He is twenty-six, a merchant's clerk out of Rochester with four years of a London counting house behind him, a quick exact hand, and a wife of one year named Nell who can read his letters without help — a fact he boasts of, in the counting house, more often than he boasts of anything about himself. The Company is hiring factors for the eastward stations under the new treaty with the Dutch. The wage is triple. The advancement is real. The stories that come back off the ships are the kind men lean toward across tables.

That is the part Daniel will not say aloud, and Nell says for him, the last night, in the room over the chandler's.

You want to come back worth a story.

I want to come back worth a house.

The house is the story you tell me, she says. Go and get the other one. Write everything down. You are no good at telling; you are very good at writing down.

He promises a letter by every ship.

The voyage out is eight months of learning that a passenger-factor is cargo that dines with the captain. Daniel does not learn the ropes. He learns the manifests, which is his rigging, and by the Cape he can tell from the water book alone which cask was stowed green. At Bantam the Company sorts its new men the way a keeper sorts fish, and because Daniel's copies are clean and his totals are the kind that agree with other men's totals, he is given the posting the treaty has just opened and older men have just refused.

Amboyna.

The island comes up out of the east as a green steepness with its head in cloud, and the smell reaches the ship before the anchorage does — cloves, drying in the sun by the hundred thousand trees, a sweetness so complete it is almost a weight. Below the cloud stands the castle: coral stone, pale as bone, Dutch colours over the gate, guns counting the roadstead. And below the castle, in its shadow the way a chick stands in the shadow of a kite, the town — and in the town, renting its rooms the way the Company rents everything under the treaty, the English house.

The treaty is two years old. On paper, the two Companies have stopped burning each other's boats east of the Cape; on paper, the English take their third of the cloves and pay their share of the garrison that watches them take it. Daniel has read the articles on the passage out. They are drafted the way all good articles are drafted, each clause reasonable, the sum of them belonging entirely to whichever side holds the guns — and the guns, Daniel sees before his sea chest is ashore, are all one side's.

The English house holds a dozen men. The castle holds four hundred, and a company of Japanese soldiers in Dutch pay who drill on the strand in the evening, matchlocks and pikes moving in the clove-scented air like a sentence in a language nobody in the English house has troubled to learn.

Standing at the rail, the new factor does the only arithmetic available.

Then he goes ashore and reports to the man he has been sent to serve.

* * *

## II. The Best Man

Gabriel Towerson has been in the east longer than some of his clerks have been alive, and it shows the way weather shows in a sea wall: not decoration, structure.

He reads Daniel's letters of introduction standing, the way busy men read. He puts the letters down and looks at the man attached to them, longer than a chief looks at a junior, and says: What did you count on the way in?

Daniel tells him. The guns of the castle. The boats at the clove quay. The Japanese soldiers at their drill, and the number of them.

Any man counts the guns, Towerson says. Why the soldiers?

Because their wages are on somebody's book, Daniel says, and I have not seen the book.

Towerson smiles, on the side of his face away from the Dutch interpreter.

You will do, Mr Vale.

The work is a factory's work, and Daniel takes to it as a starved man takes to bread. Cloves in from Hitu and the leeward villages, weighed under Dutch eyes, divided under English ones, the third and the two-thirds entered in two houses whose books must agree and whose interests never do. Rice and cloth out. The garrison account, calculated in Amsterdam, disputed in Amboyna, paid in arrears everywhere: the English share of walls the English are not allowed to man, of powder pointed, as the assistants say after supper, in a direction nobody names.

Towerson runs the house the way the best men run anything caught between two powers: exactly, calmly, and out loud. Every dispute goes to paper. Every courtesy is returned. Twice in Daniel's first season the chief walks up to the castle over some quarrel of weights or wharfage, and comes down again having lost the point and kept the peace, and says the same sentence both times, hanging his coat on the peg.

The treaty holds, gentlemen. We are merchants.

The younger men repeat it as a joke when he is out of the room. Daniel does not. He has noticed the order of the words. Not: we are merchants, so the treaty holds. The treaty holds, so we are permitted to be merchants. Towerson knows which clause carries the weight. He has simply decided that a man cannot live crouched, and stands up straight inside the arithmetic, and teaches his clerks to do the same.

Going by lengths, Daniel writes to Nell, he is the best man I have served under. Then he crosses out going by lengths, because she will ask what the other measures are, and he does not want to write the castle into a letter she will read by a Rochester window.

* * *

## III. The Margin

The rice teaches Daniel what he is.

It is an ordinary cargo in the wet season of 1622: rice up from the coast for the factory and the garrison both, landed in a rain so hard the lighters cross the bay half-swamped. Daniel stands the landing because standing landings is his week's duty, and watches six sacks come ashore dark with bilge-water, and makes the decision a junior factor is paid to make: spoiled rice to the sickhouse, where men too fevered to taste will get the good of it before the rot does. He notes the hour, the porters — the tall one and the lame one, whose names he asks and writes — and the condition of the seals, and goes in out of the rain.

Two months later the account comes round for witnessing, and the six sacks are in it twice.

Once to the factory's store, at the landed price. Once to a private mess, at the landed price again. The same rice, eaten by fevered men in the sickhouse, has been sold to two different columns, and both columns expect Daniel's initials, because Daniel stood the landing.

The steward who kept the book is a heavy, friendly man who has been east eleven years and has a proverb ready: the wet season eats a little of every account, Mr Vale, and it is nobody's business to starve arguing with the rain.

Daniel looks at the page for a long time.

He is not brave. What he is, is unable — physically, the way some men cannot carry a tune — to initial a number he watched be false. The two talents look identical on paper. They are not the same talent.

He does not strike the entry. Striking it would start a war with a man he messes with. He writes, small, in the margin, in the cramped hand that presses too hard on the downstrokes:

*Not received into our store. Ask Mr Vale, who witnessed the unloading.*

And signs it — the little D, the surname driven into the page — and lets the account go forward to Bantam with the fleet's papers, where somebody, he supposes, files it, and nobody, he supposes, reads it.

Somebody reads it.

The answer comes back two ships later, by way of the Bantam market rather than the Company's bag: his margin note returned in a fair copy, the duplication marked in a strange neat hand, and a question attached that no Company clerk would think to ask. Whether Mr Vale will, in future, copy disputed landings before surrendering the originals to either factory.

There is no name on the question. There is a rate.

Daniel takes the paper to Towerson, because taking it anywhere else has a smell to it.

The chief reads it twice. You know what this is?

A broker.

The broker, Towerson says. Bantam prices her copies like physic and they cure like physic. He hands the page back. The Company will not pay for you to answer this, and I will not order you to. I will tell you what I have learned in thirty years of the two things, and you may do your own sums. Paper the Company owns tells the Company's story. Paper a man buys with his own coin is the only place his eyes are the ones that were there.

Daniel writes back cautiously — he has Company duties; he cannot undertake private employment prejudicial to his masters — and beneath the caution, in smaller writing, the truth of him: the cost of paper at Amboyna, and whether duplicates may be entered as an office expense.

The answer, when it comes, is six words and no comfort.

*If his masters pay for the copy, his masters own the copy.*

Daniel reads it in the doorway of the English house, in the smell of cloves and rain. Then he walks into the market and buys, with his own wage, a quire of paper and a stone bottle of ink, and the lame porter watches him carry them home under his coat like a man who has bought something it would be better not to drop.

* * *

## IV. The Names of Porters

After the rice, Daniel's pages change.

Not what they record — landings, weights, repairs, the garrison account, the small tides of a small factory. How they record it. Every entry now expects to be challenged. Every landing has its hour. Every dispute has its witnesses, named — porters, boatmen, the lame man and the tall man, people no Company account has ever bothered to make room for, because a company believes its own seal outweighs any number of hands that were actually there.

Daniel has stopped believing that, quietly, the way a man stops believing in a bridge he has watched crack.

The assistants chaff him for it. The steward has not forgiven him and shows it by praising his exactness to visitors, in the tone a man uses to praise a dog's hearing. Towerson says nothing, but the disputed landings begin, without any order being given, to fall on Daniel's weeks.

Copy them first, the strange neat hand had asked. He does. The copies go into a deal box with a plain lid, and the box goes under his bed, and some months, when a trusted ship is in, a copy goes west to the Bantam desk at the rate, and coin comes back that Daniel enters, in his private book, under the only heading that is honest.

*Paper of my own.*

The letters to Nell go by every ship, as promised. They are true, and they are edited, and he learns on the island what every man east of the Cape learns about the two: that a letter can be both, if it is written by a careful man, and that the caution is a kind of lie that mails itself. He writes her the green steepness and the clove sweetness and the sea like lit glass in the evenings. He writes her Towerson's jokes. He does not write the castle. He does not write that the boats are counted, or that the drill on the strand has moved, this season, from the evening to the morning, which a man who counts cannot help noticing means the soldiers now drill fresh instead of tired.

When do you come home? Nell writes, at the bottom of every letter, under her name, like a sum waiting to be carried.

After the next harvest, he writes back. He has written it three times now. The harvests come and go, and the third of the cloves goes into the English books at a profit that reads well in London, and Daniel Vale, who came east to be worth a story, sits up at night by a paid-for candle, copying disputed pages nobody has asked him to copy, in a house below a castle, on an island that smells of sweetness, adding himself line by line to the only account he fully owns.

He came east to be worth a story.

He is becoming worth a margin.

* * *

## V. The Audit of Small Things

The closing begins so quietly that only a counting man could see it, and the counting man spends half a year telling himself he is counting shadows.

The first entry is the searches. English boats have always been boarded at the castle's pleasure — once, lazily, a formality with the manners of a toll. In the dry season of 1622 the boarding becomes twice, and the second search is not lazy. Bilges are sounded. Rice is run through fingers. A soldier turns Daniel's landing-book over, holds it upside down, and shakes it, watching not the book but Daniel's face.

The second entry is the seals. Letters from Bantam have always come with the wax a little tired from the passage. Now the wax comes warmed and pressed back crooked, the impression doubled at one edge like a stammer. Daniel holds a cover to the light and understands that somewhere between the ships and the English house, a patient man with a knife and a candle is reading the Company's mail, and not troubling to hide it well — which is its own message. We read what you write. Write accordingly.

The third entry he finds inside the castle's own housekeeping, because the garrison account still crosses his desk for the English share. An order for lamp oil, doubled. A request, sent through to Batavia, for an interpreter of Japanese. A prohibition, posted at the water stairs without explanation, on boats leaving the roadstead after dark.

And once — brought to him folded in a fish basket by the lame porter, who will not say what he was paid or by whom — a list, in a Dutch clerk's hand, of the English house's stores. Their own powder, their own muskets, eleven of them, their own beer and rice and candles, counted to the piece by somebody who had stood inside their storeroom and taken his time.

Daniel sits up that night and lays the pages side by side on the deal box, in the light of his own candle.

Alone, each page is administration. Oil is bought. Interpreters are hired. Harbours have rules. Together, on one table, at one midnight, they are something else, and the something has a shape, and the shape is a door being bolted from the other side.

Eleven hundred miles west, a woman he has met only as a rate and a neat hand is laying the same pages side by side under strings, and reaching the same sentence.

He takes the sum to Towerson in the morning.

* * *

## VI. Reasonable Explanations

The chief hears him out entire. That is Towerson's way; he interrupts nobody, which the assistants call patience and Daniel has come to recognize as bookkeeping — the man lets every account be rendered in full before he audits a line of it.

Then he takes the entries one at a time, and one at a time they die.

The searches: there has been private trade in the roadstead, English private trade at that, and the castle is within its paper to look for it. The seals: the mails have been read since before Daniel was born; a crooked impression means only that the reader has grown careless, or busy. The lamp oil: the governor is building a new gallery, and galleries burn light. The interpreter: the garrison's Japanese soldiers have wages, grievances and contracts, and the castle has nobody who can hear them out; an interpreter is housekeeping. The prohibition on night boats: smuggling, Mr Vale, the answer to every harbour rule ever posted.

The stores list is the only one that slows him.

He reads it twice, and his face while he reads it is the face of a man doing sums he had hoped to be spared. Then he folds it and gives it back, and gives Daniel the sentence with it.

Any single page has a reasonable explanation. You have shown me six reasonable explanations standing in a row, and I will grant you that six is a number with a smell to it. But consider what you are asking me to do with your sum. There are twelve of us and four hundred of them, and the treaty is the wall we live behind — the only wall we have. If I act as though the treaty is dead, I kill it. I will not spend twelve men's safety on the arithmetic of lamp oil.

The treaty holds, gentlemen, he says, though only Daniel is in the room. We are merchants.

Daniel goes back to his desk. He does not argue.

Instead he does the only thing his talent knows how to do. He makes another copy of the sum, entire, every page, in his own bought paper and his own cramped hand.

And for the first time, the copy does not go into the deal box under his bed.

* * *

## VII. The Garrison Share

The quarrel that fills the season between the stores list and the silence is about money, because on this island everything is about money until the last week of its life.

The garrison account falls due at the turn of the year, and the English share is in arrears — genuinely in arrears; Daniel keeps the account and will not pretend otherwise even now. London is slow, Bantam is slower, and the castle's demand sits on Towerson's table gathering courteous reminders the way a wreck gathers weed. The governor's men press. The English house temporizes. It is the oldest, safest, most ordinary kind of dispute two trading companies can have, and both sides settle into it with something like relief, because an argument about arrears has rules, and religion, and precedent, and nobody has ever been marched up a causeway at night over interest.

Daniel works the figures honestly and finds the honest total, and in the working discovers the entry that will not leave him alone afterward.

The Japanese soldiers' wages.

They are in the garrison account — of course they are; the English share helps pay them, which means Daniel has entered them quarter by quarter for two years: so many men, at so much, victualled at so much, the neatest lines in the castle's whole demand. And they are in the English house's own letters too, the ones the assistants draft for Bantam when the ale is in them — but there they are not wages. There they are *the Japanese*, a word with a new weight on it, the hired blades the Dutch keep at our elbow, men of no nation and any master, the first thing an anxious Englishman's eye counts on the strand and the last thing he mentions in a report signed with his name.

The same men. Wages in one book. Suspicion in the other.

Daniel sets the two pages side by side one evening — he cannot help it; setting pages side by side is the whole of him — and sits looking at what the setting shows. Every party on this island is keeping two accounts of every other party. The castle enters the English as partners in the garrison book and counts their muskets in a private hand. The English enter the soldiers as a wage line and fear them in the post. The soldiers themselves, for all he knows, keep some third reckoning of the two pale nations that hire and suspect them in the same breath, and if they do, no one on the island can read it, and no one has tried. The interpreter the castle sent for has arrived, a narrow man off the Batavia ship who eats alone; his wage is in the new quarter's demand. Daniel entered it himself.

He tells Towerson only the honest total, and the chief pays what can be paid and disputes the rest, and the courteous reminders resume, and everyone is satisfied, because the arrears are a language everybody speaks.

But Daniel has stopped believing in the argument. An argument about money is what this island wears over whatever it is actually doing — he has watched too many of its pages walk in pairs to trust any single sheet again. Somewhere behind the arrears, in a ledger no one shows him, there is another account of the English house, kept in another hand, waiting for its season.

He is right, and being right does him no good at all, which is the way of margins.

* * *

## VIII. Two True Reports

The last packet from the Bantam desk arrives in January, through the market, the seals honest because the route was never the Company's.

It contains his own pages back, fair-copied — the neat hand has had his warehouse landings, his roof repairs, the rice allowance of the Japanese soldiers, all the ordinary exactness he has sold west at the rate — and beneath them, unsigned as always, an answer to the question he had sent in the autumn, the question that had begun as bookkeeping and become, in the writing, something nearer to divinity.

*If two true reports name different owners of the same store, which report do you keep?*

*Both. Ownership is the dispute. Do not edit it out.*

Daniel reads it three times. Then he writes the acknowledgement at the foot — *Both retained* — and puts the packet where the sum went, off the compound, out of the English house, into the keeping of hands the castle has never thought to count, because the castle, like the Company, believes paper lives in houses.

He pays the porters from his own wage. He enters the payment in his private book, under the honest heading, and sits looking at the page.

Two true reports. The castle's account of the season: administration, housekeeping, harbour rules. His own: a door being bolted. Both true, line by line. Ownership is the dispute — ownership of the island, of the treaty, of the twelve men sleeping around him in the dark of the house — and he has just obeyed the neat hand's instruction and declined to edit it out.

It is the last thing he sends west.

In February the packets stop being answered. The market route goes quiet, the way water goes quiet before it decides. The lame porter does not come. The fish basket arrives with only fish in it, which frightens Daniel more than the stores list did.

He begins sleeping badly, and counting the drill on the strand from his window, and finding the count higher.

* * *

## IX. New Year

The last ordinary supper is ordinary enough that nobody marks it.

Rice comes to the table in the pot it was boiled in, clumped at the bottom where the cook let the water run low. Two small fish have been fried whole and divided badly. The steward takes the head of one by seniority and then spends the meal complaining that the eyes accuse him. An assistant says they have cause. Another turns the fish on the dish so it can accuse Towerson instead.

The chief moves it back.

I am answerable for the house, he says. Not for the fish.

That distinction should go into the treaty.

Put it in the margin, Towerson says, and the table looks at Daniel.

He has earned this teasing. For two years he has corrected the supper account when a jar of oil is entered twice, asked whether six candles issued means six whole candles or six ends, and once made the steward restore half a measure of rice because the household book had charged a visiting boat crew for food they brought themselves. The men now appeal every small uncertainty to him. A cracked cup. A missing spoon. Whether the cook's thumb, found in the rice while he serves it, belongs under victuals or wages.

Daniel gives the same ruling each time.

Insufficient evidence.

The phrase has become useful. It excuses bad cards, empty bottles and every story told after the third cup. Tonight an assistant lays down a winning hand with such satisfaction that the others inspect his sleeves.

Insufficient evidence, the winner says.

Evidence is not the difficulty, Daniel says. Your arithmetic is.

When the rice is gone, the cards continue. The cook puts the pot near the door to soak. Somebody begins mending a split cuff at the table and is advised by three merchants who cannot thread the needle. The steward loosens his shoes beneath his chair. Outside, the roadstead moves through its harbour sounds: an oar against a thwart, a call from the water stairs, rigging answering a small change in wind. None requires the house to rise.

Daniel writes to Nell at the far end of the table while the others play.

*We had fresh fish tonight,* he begins. It seems too small after months without a letter ship and therefore exactly the thing she will want. He tells her about the accusing eyes and the marked card. He reports that his superior disclaims responsibility for marine judgement and that the cook's thumb remains unclassified. He can see her reading the page at the Rochester window, knowing which sentences are jokes without needing the jokes explained.

He starts to write that he expects to come after the next harvest. The sentence has appeared in three letters and become less true with every use. He scratches it through before the ink dries.

*I keep your question,* he writes instead. *I have not yet made the sum answer it.*

An assistant leans across to ask whether writing to one's wife during cards constitutes private trade. Daniel covers the page with his hand. The table objects that a man who copies everybody else's business has no doctrine of privacy. Towerson tells them to leave him enough marriage to come home to.

The word home passes without changing the room.

Daniel sands the letter, folds it and writes Nell's direction. He puts it beside the household packet for the next westward boat. Then he returns to the score and proves that the assistant who has been boasting of victory is down by four points.

They argue over the count until the lamp needs trimming. Somebody finds the missing spoon beneath the steward's plate. The cook's thumb is acquitted. Men take their candles and go to their beds still disputing the cards, and the English house settles around them with the small knocks, coughs and floorboard complaints of a place in which tomorrow is expected to resemble today.

The end begins with a question — the one mercy the island shows him: it lets the shape of the thing be honest from the first hour.

A Japanese soldier of the garrison — young, off his watch, alone — stands at the foot of the castle wall in the cool of the evening and asks a Dutch sentry about the watch. How many men stand it. When it turns. What the word is.

It is the kind of question soldiers have asked each other out of boredom on every wall ever built. It is also the kind of question a wall's owners may make into anything they need, and the castle, that season, is in need.

They take him inside. This is known in the town by morning, the way everything is known in the town by morning: not the fact of it, the absence — a man who drank at a certain stall does not come, and the stall-keeper's face is closed, and the strand drill forms up one short, and nobody looks at the gap.

For two nights, sounds come down from the castle that the town agrees not to have heard.

In the English house, the assistants play cards badly and lose count of the deals. The steward drinks. Daniel stands at his window counting nothing, because the thing he is listening for has no number, and on the second night Towerson comes and stands beside him, and the two men look up at the pale walls together.

They will ask him questions, Daniel says, until his answers fit a shape they have already drawn. And then the shape will need more men in it.

You have no evidence for that.

I have six reasonable explanations in a row.

Towerson is silent a long time. The sweetness of the cloves comes in on the land breeze, thick as ever. Nothing on the island smells any different from the day Daniel first raised its green head out of the sea, which seems to him suddenly the most terrible fact of the place: the trees do not even change their breath for what is coming.

We are merchants, Towerson says at last. Quietly. Not to Daniel — to the wall, to the treaty, to thirty years of standing up straight inside other men's arithmetic. The treaty holds.

On the third day the castle sends down an invitation, courteous as ever, for the English chief to come up: a matter touching the garrison account, best settled in person. Towerson puts on his good coat. At the door he pauses and looks back at the house — the low room, the cards abandoned, the clerks watching him with their unfinished faces — and Daniel, who witnessed the unloading, who names the porters, who cannot initial a false number, records his chief in the doorway with the terrible completeness that is his whole gift, and says nothing, and lets him go.

That night the soldiers come down for the rest of them.

They come with lanterns and with the courtesy that is worse than blows, and the arrests arrive dressed as procedure: a matter of questions, gentlemen; a formality; the governor regrets the hour. Daniel is taken between two Japanese soldiers whose wages he has entered with his own pen, through streets he has walked for two years, up the causeway he has audited stone by stone in the garrison account he was too exact to leave alone.

He counts the steps to the gate. There are one hundred and eleven.

* * *

## X. The Table

The room is coral stone and has no window.

Daniel notices the absence the way he notices everything, at once and exactly, and understands what it is for before anyone has spoken. A window is a clock. Light moves, and a man in trouble can hold on to its moving. This room has been chosen by somebody who thinks about what men hold on to.

There is a table. There is a man behind the table, neither young nor old, with the settled face of a conscientious schoolmaster, and there is a clerk beside him with paper, ink and sand. The paper is not blank. Daniel sees that in the first minute: sheets already close-written, laid in a squared stack, wanting only entries in the spaces left for them. He has prepared enough documents to know a prepared document across a room.

The questions begin gently, in fair English.

The plot. Who ordered it. When it was to be. Who else.

There is no plot, Daniel says.

The man behind the table nods, unhurried, unoffended — a man checking a sum that has not yet come out right — and makes a small sign, and the soldiers behind Daniel take his arms.

What is done then has a procedure, and the procedure is the most terrible part of it, because procedure means it has been done before, refined, taught. The chair. The binding. The cloth over the face, drawn snug with two little tugs, like a barber settling a towel. And then the water, poured with care, without anger, without hurry, while the body — his body, which has kept his columns and carried his sea chest and stood his landings for two years — abandons him entirely and becomes a single animal shriek of drowning that no resolve, no reason, no innocence can reach.

They stop. That is the other half of the procedure. They stop, and the cloth comes off, and the room reassembles itself — table, schoolmaster, clerk, stack — and the voice asks, mild as morning:

The plot. Who ordered it. When it was to be. Who else.

There is no plot, Daniel says, or the wreck of his breathing says it for him.

The man behind the table nods, and the pen, which has been waiting, does not write it down.

That is when Daniel understands the room entire. Not from the water; from the pen. It writes when he says certain things. It rests when he says others. The paper on the table has already decided what happened on this island, and the room exists to move his voice from the answers the pen ignores to the answers the pen is waiting for. Truth is not being sought here. Truth is being transcribed backward.

He is a copying man. They are making him into a page.

The cloth again. The water again. Hours are the wrong measure and he stops using them. There are only two times in the room, and he moves between them at a speed of another man's choosing.

At the end of the first session — sessions, another word from procedure, the schoolmaster actually says it, we will resume the session tomorrow, as though there were a syllabus — Daniel is taken down a passage he cannot afterward reconstruct and put into a cell with three of the others.

He does not tell them what is in the room. They will each learn it, and know he knew, and there is nothing in the knowing that helps a man. He sits against the coral, which is sharp and holds the day's heat, and does the only work left in his profession.

He begins to count.

* * *

## XI. The Wall

He counts because counting is what he has instead of prayer, and because a man who counts still owns the numbers.

Breaths. The guard change, which comes at intervals of about six hundred breaths in the day and eight hundred at night, when the watches grow careless. The drips of the wet season working down through the coral. The sessions — his own, and the other men's, which can be told by the order the cells empty and refill, by which assistant comes back able to walk and which must be carried, by who eats and who only sits.

On the second day they take Towerson.

Daniel knows it without being told, the way the town knew about the soldier: by absence, by the way the guards straighten, by a door heavier than the others. And then, because his cell shares a wall with the passage to the room, he knows it by the fact of him.

Not words. The wall is coral and a yard thick and words do not survive it. What survives is the fact — pressure, rhythm, the shape of a voice he has heard every day for two years, hanging its coat on a peg, losing a point and keeping the peace. The fact of Towerson goes on beyond the wall for two days.

Daniel counts.

Not the breaths. The lengths. Going by lengths, he is the best man I have ever served under — and the length of him is going on, hour upon hour, session upon session, two days of a man refusing to move his voice to where the pen waits. Every hour the wall holds is an hour the paper stays unfinished. Daniel counts it the way men at sea count a spent storm: not because counting helps, but because the number is the only witness they can offer.

On the third day the wall goes quiet.

The quiet is worse. Sound was the fact of him; the quiet is the fact of the paper, complete somewhere, sanded, squared. Daniel sits against the coral and discovers that he has lost the count — the breaths, the guard changes, all of it, gone between one hour and the next, and he cannot say when.

It is the one thing he has never lost. He does not get it back whole, ever.

That evening the guards' voices in the passage are loose and ordinary again. One of them laughs. The syllabus has moved past its hardest lesson.

The next morning they resume the session.

* * *

## XII. The Signature

The room has changed. It takes Daniel a moment to find the change, and then he finds it.

The stack of paper is thinner. Most of it has gone wherever finished things go. What remains lies before the clerk in a fair hand, and the schoolmaster turns it toward Daniel across the table, and Daniel reads — upside down first, out of habit, then righted by the clerk's careful fingers — his own answers.

They are not his answers. They are in his mouth's grammar, sprinkled with his true words — the landings, the stores, the hours he did keep — and threaded on a spine of confession he never spoke. Meetings he did not attend, arranged on evenings when he was standing landings he can date. An oath he never swore, sworn in a room he has never entered. His exactness has been harvested and rebound. Every date is possible. Every detail is his. The sum is a lie, and it is the best-made lie he has ever read, because he supplied the materials himself, one session at a time, answering the only questions his body left him able to answer.

Sign, the schoolmaster says. Not unkindly. He has never once been unkind.

And here is the fact, heavier than the water: he signs.

Not at once. He asks for it to be read aloud, and it is, patiently, every word. He says, once, that it is false, and the schoolmaster nods, and the cloth is not even lifted from its basin — it does not need to be; it needs only to exist, folded, damp, in the corner of his eye. The body decides. The body has been taught what refusing costs, and the body signs, and the hand that cannot initial a false number — the one incorruptible thing he brought east, the talent that made him, the margin that named the porters — moves across the bottom of another man's truth and makes it legal.

They did not know what they were taking. That is what he can never explain afterward, to the Company, to the clerk with the gentleman's word. The water took hours. The signature took the thing the hours were for.

The pen is sanded. The page is squared away.

Thank you, Mr Vale, the schoolmaster says, and means it.

Daniel is taken back to the cells, and does not speak, and watches, through the high grate, the pens of the yard clerks crossing and recrossing the bright doorway of the governor's office, and cannot stop watching them, and understands that he has left the room and the room has not finished with him. He has learned to watch pens. It is the room's parting gift, and it is for life.

He does not say Nell's name. He has not said it once, in all the sessions, in any of the spaces the paper left. It is the one entry they did not get, because they did not know to ask for it, and a man can keep what nobody knows to ask for. In the cell, in the dark, he holds the name without saying it even to himself, the way you carry a coal in wet weather.

They have his hand.

They do not have where he keeps her.

* * *

## XIII. Batavia

Some days later — he has lost the count; the days are a smear of grate-light and guard-noise — they come in and look at him.

It is a particular look and he knows it at once, because he has used it. It is the look a factor gives cargo when deciding between columns: sound or spoiled, keep or condemn, this manifest or that one. Two of them stand in the cell door and look at him that way, and confer, low, in Dutch he has learned enough of to follow and wishes he had not.

One of them says a word to the other, and the word is Batavia.

No reason attaches to it. No sentence is read, no mercy pronounced, no explanation offered then or ever. One word, passed between two men the way a bale is passed between two hooks, and the whole remaining shape of Daniel Vale's life is decided in a doorway by an entry he is not shown.

He is taken down to the water stairs at dusk with a guard on either side. The town is out of sight of the path they use, which is deliberate, and the boat is small, and the ship in the roadstead is Dutch and bound west, and nobody tells him any of this; he assembles it, entry by entry, because assembling entries is what is left of him.

From the deck, chained, he watches Amboyna go.

The castle first, pale on its height, its windows catching the last sun. Then the town, the roofs of it, one of which was his. Then the green steepness entire, headed in cloud, and over the water, impossibly, unforgivably, the sweetness — cloves, drying by the hundred thousand trees, the island's breath following the ship out over the roadstead as though nothing whatever had happened in it.

The others are still inside the walls. Towerson is still inside the walls. Daniel does not know what is coming to them, not for certain. What he knows now is only the arithmetic of the doorway: they were looked at like cargo, all of them, and the columns were two, and he has been entered in the one that sails.

He is the only Englishman leaving Amboyna by water that week.

He does not know what that sentence costs — what he said, or didn't; what he was worth, or wasn't; why the boat for him. There is no answer, because there was never a reason. There was an entry, made by a clerk with a patient pen, in a ledger closed to him.

The island drops below the sea's edge. The sweetness goes last.

Daniel Vale sits in his chains and begins, without meaning to, to count the ship's bells, and finds that the numbers still will not hold, and closes his eyes, and holds instead the one uncounted thing he saved out of the coral room, and sails west toward the word that was said over him.
