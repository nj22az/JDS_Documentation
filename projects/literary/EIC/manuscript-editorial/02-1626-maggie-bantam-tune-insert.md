# Proposed insert — 02-1626-the-man-who-came-back-wrong, Section XII
"The Wager Kept"

## Why here

This is the mending chapter's quietest scene: Maggie brings out Tom's
mother's thimble, says "Till you're back" as if no time and all of it has
passed, and pours him a pint without a word. It's the right register for
one more silent beat — and it gives an otherwise mysterious "why this
particular evening" a hidden cause, without ever stating it. Per the
continuity design's rule that these threads stay entirely implicit,
Maggie's own history (Swedish, a son taken by "the King's coin" to the
navy and never returned) is gestured at, never confirmed, and Tom's
Bantam song is never named as what it is.

## The deployed text, exactly as it stands

> On a quiet evening at the turn of the year, when the bar is down to its
> last patrons and the fire to its last hour, Maggie reaches under the
> counter.
>
> She sets the brass thimble on the bar between them, exactly where a boy
> set it twenty-three years ago, not quite meeting her eye.
>
> Tom looks at it a long time. He does not pick it up. Some wagers are not
> for taking back.
>
> Till you're back, Maggie says. As if no time has passed. As if all of it
> has.
>
> She returns the thimble to the gap, in among the notches and the folded
> truth, where the house keeps what the sea owes it. Then she picks up a
> rag, wipes a counter that does not need wiping, and pours Tom a fresh
> pint of dark ale.
>
> Nobody in the room notices anything. That is how the room knows it
> matters.

## Proposed insertion (new paragraph, placed immediately before "On a
quiet evening at the turn of the year...")

> Some evenings he does not know he is doing it. A few bars, no more,
> under his breath while he turns his cup — an old tune with no English
> words in it, one Maggie has never once heard him choose to sing
> outright, only ever catches him losing to without noticing. She does not
> ask what it is. She has her own songs that arrive the same way,
> unbidden, in a language nobody else in this room would know to be
> frightened by. But something in it, tonight, empties her hands of
> whatever they were doing, and she stands a moment with the rag
> forgotten, listening to four bars of a stranger's grief coming out of a
> man she has known since he was twenty-two, before she goes to fetch what
> she has been keeping for him.

## Notes

- This is the only place in the whole design where Maggie's Swedish
  history and Tom's Bantam history come near each other on the page, and
  even here neither is named — "her own songs," "a stranger's grief," no
  more. This keeps faith with the hard guard in the continuity design doc
  (`editorial/continuity-web-and-cast-reduction.md` §11): Maggie's own
  buried thread (her son, "the King's coin," the eventual Johansson line)
  stays exactly as unstated as it already is everywhere else in the book.
- The causal reading — that the tune is *why* Maggie decides, this
  particular evening, to bring out the thimble — is available to a
  careful reader and confirmed to no one, including Tom.
- Single insertion point, fully reversible, contingent on the Bantam
  interlude being approved.
