# 1603–1612: Maria's Passage East

> **1603–1604**
>
> *“A true copy is a second witness, and the second is harder to kill.”*
>
> — Maria, Bantam, 1604

## I. What the House Will Hold

Amsterdam receives Maria as London released her: in rain, among timber, under a description that is true only in the narrow place where truth and survival overlap.

The customs clerk at the quay reads *household goods, one lot, consigned* and looks past her twice. On the third look he finds a woman inside Pieter's coat and asks Joos de Vries whether the household is his.

It is now, Joos says.

The clerk has no box for that answer. He puts a line through the entry, writes another word above it and waves the consignment ashore.

Maria cannot yet read what he has made of her. This is intolerable.

She walks behind Joos through a city built like an argument against water. Canals hold the grey sky in strips. Bridges rise under handcarts and settle again. Houses stand shoulder to shoulder with their upper storeys leaning over the street, each one lifting a beam and hook from its gable so that cargo may enter by the window and never trouble the family door. Everywhere there are ledgers: chalk figures on casks, tally cuts in timber, bills nailed beneath oilskin, boys running folded instructions from men who own ships to men who will die in them.

The Company is not two years old and has already learned to look ancient. It has rented rooms in the old Bushuis on the Kloveniersburgwal, an armoury being taught commerce one locked door at a time. Pepper is weighed under guard. Men enter carrying samples and leave carrying permissions. In one room the directors decide where twelve ships will go. In another, somebody counts spoons.

Joos does not take Maria through the directors' door. He takes her to the rooms where victuals, bedding and human beings are entered for the fleet.

There are three lists on the desk.

Sailors. Soldiers. Merchants.

The clerk taps each heading with the end of his quill while Joos translates into Portuguese. There is no list for a woman. In the margins of earlier pages Maria sees the occasional short entry attached to an officer's name: a wife, a boy, each one an exception answering to a man already entered above.

Which man? Maria asks.

Joos's mouth tightens.

That is the difficulty.

No, she says. That is the price. Which man?

The clerk does not understand the Portuguese, but he understands a woman looking at his page too directly. He lays a blotting sheet over the lists as if she might steal a name with her eyes.

Joos leads her back into the yard.

There is a surgeon's widow sailing with one small son, he says. Her brother is a junior merchant at Bantam. He has petitioned twice, her passage is charged against his account, and I have guaranteed what the account does not cover. The owners agreed before they understood how many arguments agreement would require. At present she is the only woman in the fleet. The clerk may enter one servant beneath her name if I guarantee that servant too. You can sew. You can reckon. You speak Portuguese.

And she owns me until Bantam.

She pays you nothing, Joos says. The passage is the wage.

Joos will not use the word *owns*. Maria notices. It does not change the price.

What will the paper say?

Household servant.

Whose household?

The widow's.

After Bantam?

Joos looks back at the door through which the lists have vanished. After Bantam, the paper ends.

All paper ends, Maria says.

Not all of it ends where the people named in it expect.

That is the first useful thing anyone in Amsterdam tells her.

She agrees on three conditions. Her full name is entered. No advance is paid to the widow or to Joos on her behalf. The packet under Pieter's coat remains her own property, unopened except by her hand.

The clerk refuses the first because a servant does not require two names, refuses the second because no wage means no advance, and laughs at the third because private papers are no concern of his. Then he says the widow's exception has already consumed the Chamber's patience. If Joos wants another argument, all three may watch the fleet leave from shore.

Maria waits.

Joos puts his purse on the desk. Not payment: bond. If Maria deserts her service before Bantam, damages Company property or consumes stores beyond the allowance, the Chamber may recover the cost from him. The clerk names a sum designed to make principle expensive.

Joos agrees.

Only then does the clerk write *Maria de Sousa*, draw a hard dash through the advance column, and add *one packet of private papers* under effects.

Then he turns the book for Joos to witness.

Maria puts one finger on the page.

Read it to me.

Joos reads every word. The clerk's annoyance grows line by line. When he says *dienstmaagd* — maidservant — Maria sees again the word the customs man wrote above *goods* at the quay. Two strokes have carried her from cargo into service. She files the shape of them. Then she listens until the entry in the book and the entry in her head agree.

Only then does she let Joos sign.

* * *

## II. The Texel Roads

The widow's name is Lysbeth van Aalst. She is thirty, which grief has made look like forty, and has the dry, competent hands of a woman who assisted a surgeon because illness did not stop at the edge of his licence. Her son Willem is seven. He wets his bed when he is frightened and has learned that the death of a father is a promotion, so he tries to command everyone beneath four feet tall.

Lysbeth looks at Maria's coat, her ruined shoes, the packet she keeps against her ribs, and asks in slow Dutch whether she has ever cared for a child.

Maria understands *child*. She understands the question from the boy.

No, she says.

Lysbeth closes her eyes for a moment.

Can you keep him alive?

That depends what tries to kill him.

Joos translates. To Maria's surprise, the widow laughs.

They go north by lighter with the fleet's final stores, through cold water widening toward the island of Texel. The twelve ships wait in the roads beyond the shallows because Amsterdam's wealth is too deep in the hull to pass safely through Amsterdam's water. From a distance their masts resemble a leafless wood. Closer, they become a moving town: boats crossing between hulls, barrels going up, letters coming down, officers shouting about water, powder, biscuit, vinegar, cordage, late men and missing hens.

The ship assigned to Lysbeth is not *De Hoop*. Maria had not expected it to be, but she searches every stern until there are none left to search.

Joos brings her aboard and spends an hour arranging with the under-merchant what the clerk in Amsterdam has already arranged in ink. The under-merchant dislikes additional mouths until he sees the entry. Then he dislikes them officially, which is safer.

At the rail, Joos gives Maria a slate, a chalk stub and a thin sheaf of waste paper stitched through one corner.

For Dutch, he says.

For what else?

Joos considers this and adds a pencil.

He has done enough. Both of them know it. That is what makes the account dangerous.

What do I owe you? she asks.

Nothing.

That is not an answer.

It is the answer I would prefer.

Then it is the answer I refuse.

Willem chases a chicken past them. Somewhere below, a carpenter strikes the same fastening three times and swears at it in three different measures.

Joos rests both hands on the rail.

Learn what is in Pieter's book, he says. If it contains the name of a man who should answer for something, send the name home. If it contains only a sick man's fear, send nothing. That will close it.

That will tell us the cost, Maria says. It will not close the account.

He looks at her, and this time his face permits the shame.

Yes, he says. It will tell us that.

The boat takes him away before the fleet sails. Maria watches until he is a dark mark among other dark marks and then opens the waste-paper book.

On the first page she writes the four Dutch words she owns.

*Hout. Touw. Water. Thuis.*

Below them she copies her own name from memory, the letters as Bell made them on the page in Wapping and the clerk made them in Amsterdam.

She copies it twelve times, until the hand belongs to her.

* * *

## III. East of Everything

The voyage teaches by subtraction.

First the shore goes. Then fresh bread. Then privacy, clean water, uncracked lips, the expectation that a body carried aboard will be carried off again. The fleet loses sight of itself in weather and finds itself by signal. Sails split. Men fall. Biscuit moves when broken. A sailor who gives Maria the Dutch word for gull dies before they cross the line, sewn in sailcloth, weighted and committed to the water.

Maria learns Dutch because ignorance is the only danger aboard she can reduce by sitting still.

Willem begins with objects. Cup. Spoon. Shoe. Mast. Rat. Lysbeth supplies verbs. Eat. Wash. Hold. Sleep. The sailors contribute the language of weather, work and complaint. The under-merchant adds numbers when he discovers she can total a ration issue faster than his assistant. Portuguese has already given her the letters. Repetition gives them a new language.

The numbers become useful before the words do.

Every morning a bell calls the ship to provisions. The sound travels through plank and bone, reaching Maria before the light does. Men rise from hammocks slung so close that one man's shoulder lifts another man's back. Shoes are found by touch. Belts are fastened beneath blankets if there is room, and without them if there is not. The air below tastes of wet wool, old breath and the bilge moving under everything.

Lysbeth and Willem have the narrow berth built against the after bulkhead. Maria sleeps beneath it on two folded coats and a square of sailcloth, with Pieter's packet under her head. When the ship heels, her hip meets the chest. When it rights, her shoulder meets the wall. Privacy is the moment after a person turns away and before someone else turns back.

At the bell they carry their three cups and a wooden plate into the passage. The households wait behind the officers. The sailors wait behind the households, each mess with its sack. Biscuit comes first, struck against the edge of the cask so that the loose inhabitants fall out before the eating begins. Willem examines every piece.

That one moved, he says.

They all move.

Not that much.

Maria breaks the biscuit along a dark tunnel and holds the two halves toward him.

Meat, she says in Portuguese.

Willem laughs in spite of himself. Lysbeth does not. She has spent the first week insisting that her son eat around the grubs, the second picking them out, and the third watching him chew without asking what remains.

The rationer's call is faster than any lesson. Three mouths. Three biscuit shares. Three measures of small beer while it lasts. Water at morning and sunset, one measure to each name on the passenger entry. The assistant repeats the numbers and marks his slate without looking at the cups.

*Een. Twee. Drie.*

Maria says the words under her breath. One. Two. Three. The sound differs; the quantity does not.

The beer grows sour first. A rope of something pale clings to the dipper and stretches before it breaks. The water follows more slowly. It takes the colour of the cask and a flavour of green wood, then of wood shut too long in heat. Men hold their noses and drink. Willem asks whether rain tastes of barrel in Java. Maria tells him that rain tastes of roof, leaf, dust, whatever it touches before the mouth. He considers this proof that nothing in the world arrives clean.

On the fifth morning of contrary wind, Maria wakes because Willem is pressing two fingers into her shoulder.

Do not tell, he whispers.

There is no light. The berth above her is still, but the smell has already spoken. Willem stands in his shirt with both fists held against his belly. Behind him, the wool blanket is dark from the middle to the edge.

Maria sits up. Her head strikes the underside of the berth.

Your mother knows.

She is asleep.

Lysbeth's voice comes from above them.

His mother is not.

Willem closes his eyes.

There is no room in which to hide the linen, no spare blanket to put beneath him, no hour when the deck belongs only to women and frightened boys. Lysbeth lifts the wet things without accusation. Maria takes the lower corners. Together they carry the bundle through men dressing for the watch. Nobody needs to ask. A sailor makes space without meeting Willem's face. Another does meet it, then looks away because he has children somewhere or remembers being one.

They lower a bucket over the lee side. Seawater strikes the planks as Maria drags it aboard. The first washing takes away what can be seen. It leaves salt in the wool and stiffness in the linen. Lysbeth rubs the cloth between finger and thumb.

It will scour his skin raw.

Water, Maria says.

Fresh water is locked beneath an officer's key. Cooking receives it. The sick receive it when the surgeon insists. Bodies receive it through cups measured twice a day. Laundry receives none.

Lysbeth takes their morning measures before Willem can drink and pours one into the bucket. The boy watches the clear stream disappear into grey cloth.

I am not thirsty, he says.

It is a poor lie. His lips are split at both corners.

Maria catches the cup before Lysbeth empties the second.

One.

Not enough.

One first.

She has not yet learned the Dutch for waste. She does not require it. They knead the linen in the little fresh water, roll it until their wrists ache, pour the rinse over the blanket and wring both against the rail. Willem stands between them holding the dry corners out of the dirt. When the sun comes, it finds three people guarding a line of damp wool as if it were a sail keeping the ship upright.

The under-merchant finds them first.

Whose water?

Lysbeth answers before Maria understands the question. Ours.

Drinking allowance is not washing allowance.

It was issued to us.

It was issued for drinking.

Maria knows *water*, *one* and *ours*. She knows the shape of the man's displeasure from every quay she has crossed. It is the pleasure of discovering that a rule can be made expensive for somebody below it.

The assistant is sent for. He arrives with the ration slate against his chest. The under-merchant points at Maria, then at the bucket, and says Joos de Vries's name. The rest comes slowly enough for her to assemble it: stores beyond allowance; servant; guarantee; charge at Bantam.

Willem goes white beneath his sunburn.

No, Maria says.

The under-merchant glances at her as if a cup has objected.

Lysbeth touches Maria's arm. Let me.

But Maria is looking at the assistant's slate. She has watched it every morning. A row for each mess, a stroke for every measure claimed from the cask, a cross when the issue is complete. The marks say that the widow's household receives six measures each day: three at dawn, three at sunset.

The dipper says something else.

Maria takes her own slate from the pocket of Pieter's coat. She draws three mouths at the top, not faces, only circles. Beside them she puts six strokes.

*Drie*, she says, touching the circles. *Zes*, touching the strokes.

The assistant frowns. She draws the rationer's dipper, a square with a handle, and beneath it five strokes. Then she points to yesterday on his slate, and the day before, and the day before that. Each day his hand has entered six. Each day the rationer has filled five, explaining the missing measure by spillage, settling or the cask's reluctance to give up its last inch. Maria has not understood the explanations. She has counted the pours.

Five, she says in Dutch. Five. Five. Five.

She lays four rows of five on her slate. Beside them she lays four rows of six. She points at the difference, then at the wet linen.

One, she says. Ours.

The under-merchant studies the figures. His anger changes direction without diminishing.

The assistant says the loss belongs to the cask.

Does the cask drink? Maria asks in Portuguese.

Nobody understands the sentence. Willem does. A laugh escapes him, small and treacherous. He shuts his mouth with both hands.

Maria points at the cask, opens her mouth, and mimes drinking. The sailors nearest the rail understand at once. One coughs into his sleeve. Another becomes urgently interested in the horizon.

The under-merchant does not laugh. He asks the assistant whether six has been entered against the household.

Yes.

Whether five has been issued.

The assistant begins another explanation about the dipper.

Five or six?

Five.

Then the household has not exceeded its allowance.

He reaches for Maria's slate. She keeps hold of it. For one moment they stand joined by a small grey square, his hand on the frame and hers on the opposite edge. Then he releases it and tells the assistant to correct the issue.

The assistant scratches at the existing row until one stroke becomes pale dust. He does not make another page. He does not need one. The allowance has not changed; only the claim about what happened to it.

Put the washing under the servant's effects, he says. The rule still forbids it.

Lysbeth steps between him and Maria.

No.

The word stops even the sailors pretending not to listen.

The linen is my household's, Lysbeth says. The water is my household's. If you require a disobedient name, write mine.

The under-merchant looks at the blanket, then at Willem. Understanding arrives. The boy stares at the deck hard enough to split it.

Lysbeth sees the same understanding and does not retreat.

You will not turn my son's shame into her debt. You will not set it against the bond of a man ashore because that is the easiest line to reach.

The under-merchant's face reddens. He has been offered three choices: accuse the widow, name the child, or admit the ship owes what its slate says it issued. All three have witnesses.

No charge, he says.

The assistant rubs away the first mark he has begun beneath Maria's name.

The household receives the missing measure at sunset. Lysbeth divides it among three cups without ceremony. Willem drinks his too quickly and coughs half of it down his shirt. Maria waits for his horror, then holds out the wet cloth and considers it.

Laundry, she says.

His laugh comes out with the cough. Even Lysbeth smiles.

The blanket takes two days to dry. At night Willem lies on bare boards beside his mother, and Maria gives him one of the folded coats from beneath her hip. He refuses because it is hers. She tells him a loan is not a gift. He asks how she will know it is still hers while he is using it.

Maria puts the slate between them and draws one coat, one boy and an arrow that returns.

Mine, she says, then points at the arrow. Tomorrow.

Willem adds a second arrow, longer than the first, and points east.

Bantam.

She nods. He has understood more than the coat.

After that he brings her words that can be counted or kept. Half. Enough. Owed. Returned. He does not speak of the blanket, and she does not speak of the look on his face in the dark. The ship has too little room for secrets, so kindness consists mostly of deciding which truths need no witness.

Four weeks south, Willem finds Pieter's packet beneath Maria's folded coat and works one fingernail under the tarred string.

Maria catches his wrist before she knows she has moved.

The boy makes one small sound. Her grip is not Silas's grip — two fingers, no pain, released at once — but for a moment the hold is back around her: the dark, somebody deciding that what lies beside her body lies within his right, the old lesson arriving faster than the present can stop it.

Lysbeth crosses the cabin. Maria rises with the packet against her chest and waits to learn whether a servant may own the thing a child of the household wants.

Mine, she says.

It is the first Dutch word she has used wrongly on purpose. The grammar requires more. The word does not.

Lysbeth looks from her to Willem.

You heard her, she says. Ask before you touch what is hers.

The boy's face has gone red. Sorry, he says in Portuguese, one of the six words Maria has taught him. Then, in Dutch: I wanted to see the sailor's islands.

So do I, Maria says.

Lysbeth does not ask to inspect the packet. She does not remind Maria who obtained the passage, or who has the bunk and who has the floor, or what the paper in Amsterdam calls either of them. She takes Willem away by the shoulder and leaves the ownership standing exactly where Maria put it.

That evening she teaches Maria the missing grammar. *Van mij.* Belonging to me. *Eigen.* One's own. *Toestemming.* Permission.

Maria writes all three beneath *thuis*. Home. Ownership. Permission. The words look less related on the page than they are.

The next morning Willem brings the slate and asks her to write his whole name. She does. He copies it underneath with the letters leaning against one another for support. Maria makes him do it again, not because the first is unreadable, but because a name should learn early how to survive a poor hand.

By the Cape she can follow orders. By Madagascar she can follow lies.

After Madagascar the voyage stops pretending to be only a voyage. The powder entered so carefully in Amsterdam is brought up. Off Mozambique, Portuguese guns answer Dutch ones and the ship shudders through the soles of Maria's feet. She and Lysbeth wait below with Willem between them while dust shakes from the beams and men overhead shout distances into the smoke.

When Maria is allowed on deck again, a captured hull is burning to leeward. An assistant is already entering its cargo on a slate. The smoke has not cleared; the ownership has.

The fleet goes north to Goa with its guns visible, then to Calicut with gifts, letters and men dressed for a room. An agreement comes back aboard under careful hands. So do two wounded sailors, one Dutch and one Portuguese, because pain has crossed the language before Maria reaches them. Lysbeth works on the first. Maria holds water to the second man's mouth and translates his thanks, though nobody needs her to.

In the evening the same assistant who counted shot makes a fair copy of the agreement. The guns and the page are not opposites. They are the Company's two hands.

She opens Pieter's packet only when she can read the first page without asking for help.

His hand is small and crowded. Hendricks was right about the sailor's parts. Wind. Latitude. Beer. The price of oranges. A girl in the Texel roads who had one brown eye and one grey and promised nothing. Then the coast of Guinea, the Cape, Madagascar, Java. The entries shorten as the heat grows. The spelling worsens after Bantam.

Pieter never reaches the islands farther east. He knows them by what other vessels bring into Bantam: cloves drying, nutmeg split under a thumbnail, names passed west with the cargo. His own pages hold Bantam, Madura and Bali. Villages come out to trade and boats remain just beyond musket range. Captains make agreements in one anchorage and enforce different ones in the next. The book records what the voyage bought because that is what a sailor is meant to notice.

In the margins Pieter records what it took.

A hostage held until the price improves.

A storehouse burned after weights are disputed.

Two men struck from the ration list before they die, so the provision book will balance when they do.

Pieter never uses the word wolves. He does not need it. He writes the centre and the margin in the same ink, and the distance between them is the whole confession.

Near the end, fever loosens the hand but not the habit. One sentence repeats across three pages.

*Make another. Make another. Make another.*

Maria asks Lysbeth what he means.

The widow reads the pages at the stern window while the ship works under them. Not the sentence, she says. The account. He means make another copy. The under-merchant took his first one. Pieter tried to write it again from memory. That is why some pages repeat.

Why did the under-merchant take it?

Lysbeth reads farther.

Because he said everything written aboard belonged to the owners.

He wrote it.

That does not make it his.

Maria looks at the packet that crossed six years in Hendricks's locker and half a world under her coat because one frightened sailor wrote the same truth twice.

She thinks of Bell across the centre table and his copied page in the dark beneath the bar. One confession signed before the knife found him. A whole room nearly too late.

After that, she studies everything she is permitted to touch.

At first it is language work: ration headings, orders posted by the mainmast, the under-merchant's lists. She copies headings and practises their forms on waste paper, never whole documents, never yet a second account. She learns the difference between a writer's words and a clerk's hand. She learns which corrections require a line and which require a knife. She learns that a seal proves only that wax was warm near authority.

The ship carries one set of papers east.

Maria carries the knowledge that one set is only a single throat.

## IV. Bantam

Bantam arrives first as smell.

Not nutmeg alone, though nutmeg is there, warm and sweet over the water. Pepper. Woodsmoke. Fish drying. Coconut oil. Mud opened by the sun. Drains that meet the harbour without ceremony. Wet timber everywhere — hulls, jetties, warehouses, houses raised above ground that has never decided whether it is land.

Then sound: Javanese, Malay, Portuguese, Dutch, Chinese tongues, Gujarati; bells, bargaining, axes, prayer, gulls and men calling weights across water.

Then the shore itself, low and green behind a roadstead crowded with vessels from more nations than the clerk in Amsterdam had columns.

The Dutch *loge* is costly stone pretending to permanence: warehouse below, sleeping rooms above, a fenced yard, armed men at the gate and damp in everything. It is not the fortress the Company will build elsewhere when trade learns to call itself rule. The English call such a place a factory. Nothing is made there except advantage and paper.

Lysbeth's brother meets the boat late and angry. He has prepared for a widow and a boy. Maria is an extra problem until his sister shows him the Amsterdam entry.

Household servant, he reads.

The paper ends here, Maria says.

It does, Lysbeth says.

Her Dutch is careful, but it is Dutch.

He looks at her properly then.

What will you do?

Work.

At what?

What is misunderstood?

This appears to him an insolent answer. Before he can say so, a quarrel breaks in the yard.

A Portuguese-speaking broker has delivered twelve sacks of pepper and been receipted for ten. The warehouse clerk insists two were spoiled by river water and rejected. The broker insists rejected goods remain his goods. A Malay boatman insists the two sacks were carried inside with the others. The clerk has Dutch and a little market Malay. The broker and boatman share Portuguese enough for weights and anger. Nobody with authority has the two languages required; nobody without authority is believed. Everyone has armed friends.

Maria listens.

The Dutch clerk says *twelve received, two rejected*.

The Portuguese broker hears *ten received*.

The boatman says the wet sacks were opened, dried and mixed into another lot before weighing. He points to twelve tally cuts on the warehouse post. The clerk says the final two are from yesterday. The boatman shows him yesterday's cuts, dark with pepper dust; the last two expose pale new wood.

Maria looks at the cuts.

She translates each sentence without improving it. This is harder than it sounds. Men who want victory always offer the translator a better sentence than the one they said.

The factor comes down at last, coat unfastened, furious at being dragged into another quarrel in the wet yard. He orders payment for eleven sacks: ten sound, one allowed against the mixed pepper, the spoiled balance to be argued after the next tide. It is not justice. It is a sum everyone can survive until morning.

The armed friends move their hands away from their knives.

The factor asks Maria where she learned Malay.

I did not.

You spoke to the boatman.

He has Portuguese from Malacca. Enough for weights. Not enough for your clerk.

And Dutch?

On account.

The factor studies her. A Company house notices translation only when it fails. He offers her the lowest place in it: meals, a mat in an upper storeroom, no wage for the first month, and protection under the lodge's gate while she proves useful.

From whom? Maria asks.

He does not like the question.

From the town.

And inside the gate?

You will be under Company discipline.

That is not the same as protection.

It is what I can enter.

Maria looks at the broker, who is waiting for his receipt. At the boatman, who will be gone on the tide. At the clerk already gathering the disputed papers into one neat stack, where by tomorrow they will tell whatever story the top sheet requires.

One month, she says. Meals. A room with a bolt on the inside. And every translation bears my name.

Translators do not sign Company receipts.

Then the Dutch words are yours, and the Portuguese words are mine, and when they disagree you may explain it yourself.

The factor looks toward the broker.

Your name, he says.

Maria de Sousa.

He has the clerk add it beneath the settlement.

One month, the factor says. Meals, the bolt and your name beneath the work.

Maria translates the receipt aloud to the broker. Twelve delivered. Ten accepted sound. One credited from the mixed lot. One held in dispute. She makes him repeat the numbers back to her. Then she watches the clerk sand the page.

When he carries it upstairs for the factor's seal, she takes a clean sheet from the bottom of the stack.

With the original upstairs, she reproduces it from the page in her head: date, names, weights, settlement, her own new line beneath it. The clerk returns before she has finished and tells her the paper belongs to the Company.

The first does, Maria says.

She writes the final figure.

This one belongs to the man whose pepper it records.

She gives the duplicate to the broker. Not hidden. Not apologetically. In front of the clerk, the factor, the widow and the wet-timber yard.

The broker folds it into oilcloth and puts it inside his shirt.

That night Maria finishes Pieter's book by an oil lamp in a room whose bolt closes from her side. His final legible entry is not about islands. It is about paper.

*I told it once and they took the telling. I tell it again because the taking does not make it untold.*

Below it, written during the fever in a hand that barely reaches the end of the line:

*Make another.*

Maria closes the book. She does not burn it.

In the yard below, pepper is being moved before the rain. A clerk calls a number. A broker disputes it. Somewhere in the dark, a second copy waits against a man's skin.

The Company has brought Maria east as a servant because its book has nowhere else to put her.

Before her first day is over, she has begun another book.

## V. Thirty-One Days

On the thirty-first morning, the factor discovers that a month has ended.

Maria has known since midnight.

Her mat is rolled. Pieter's coat lies across it, the book inside the coat, the slate and waste-paper booklet tied together with the pencil Joos gave her. On the floor beside them is her own account of the month: twenty-seven receipts translated, nine letters, four disputes in the yard, three sets of weights made to mean the same thing in two languages. Beneath each entry is the name the factor agreed would stand below the work.

Maria de Sousa.

The factor reads the total and asks why her room is packed.

The month is finished, she says.

We made no arrangement for a second.

Exactly.

He offers the same terms. Meals. The upper storeroom. The bolt. Protection inside the gate.

Those were the terms for proving useful.

You have proved it.

Then usefulness has a price.

The factor tells her there is no place for a female clerk on the wage book. Maria asks whether Dutch changes its meaning in a woman's mouth. He says that is not the point. She asks whether Portuguese does. He says she is being difficult.

She has learned this word already. In a Company house, *difficult* means the price has begun to move in the wrong direction.

Half a junior clerk's wage, she says. Paid weekly. Paper at cost. My name remains on every translation.

Impossible.

Then your Portuguese ends today.

She lifts the mat.

The factor watches her carry the room out in one load. That is the danger of giving a person nothing: nothing is quick to pack.

Outside the gate, Iskandar — the pepper broker from the wet-yard dispute — waits beneath the warehouse eaves with the first duplicate Maria made in Bantam folded inside his shirt. The settlement held. The Dutch clerk tried two days later to call both wet sacks spoiled; Iskandar unfolded the second receipt, found another Dutch reader and collected the missing credit.

Now he has a Portuguese debt note from Malacca, a Javanese pepper grower who will accept neither his figures nor the Dutch ones, and a Chinese buyer who speaks Cantonese badly enough to be dangerous and Malay well enough to deny it.

What is your rate? he asks.

Maria looks back at the gate. The factor has not followed her. He still believes the gate is the valuable thing.

She names a price.

Iskandar argues it down by one-tenth. Maria lets him, because a broker who accepts the first number is either desperate or lying, and she needs to know which kind he is. He pays half before she opens the debt note.

The coin buys seven nights in a room above a maker of scales, from a landlady who demonstrates the bolt and waits while Maria tests it herself. Maria pays in advance and asks for a receipt. The room is narrow, hot and entirely accounted for.

A rented door is still somebody else's door. But the key is in Maria's hand, the term is written, and no gratitude is owing on either side.

It will do for seven nights.

* * *

## VI. What the Translation Buys

Seven nights become a month because the work returns before the rent is due.

Iskandar's pepper growers bring contracts in market Malay and arguments in three dialects of impatience. Portuguese merchants bring letters whose courtesies occupy half the page and whose demand sits in six words at the bottom. The English factory sends a boy with shipping notes whenever its factors suspect the Dutch version has improved Dutch interests. The Dutch send for Maria whenever the English version has done the same.

She does not learn every language in Bantam. Nobody does. She learns where each one stops.

Her Malay becomes good enough for weights, weather and refusal. Hokkien remains a fast current she can cross only by another person's bridge. Cantonese lets her deal directly with some southern merchants and tells her exactly when a Dutch clerk is calling two Chinese tongues one language for his own convenience. English comes first in scraps from the factory: headings, dates, the words *received*, *damaged*, *owing*. She acquires it in the old order — numbers before mercy.

Within the year, the factor who could find no wage-book place for her crosses the market and stands in the scale-maker's doorway.

He carries a folded Portuguese dispatch taken from a coastal boat. The seal has been opened and pressed closed badly. Maria reads the first lines without touching it.

It is a victualling account. Rice, fresh water, powder and shot. A Portuguese supply vessel is to leave Malacca on the next fair wind, pass south of Sumatra and make for the islands to the east. The name of the vessel is given. So are the master, the tonnage and the day on which a patrol waiting in the strait will know where to find her.

The factor watches Maria reach the date.

Well?

What will you do with it?

That is not translation.

No. It is the rate.

He understands. Military information costs more than pepper because pepper does not remember the translator's face.

Maria names the fee. He pays it from a purse kept for expenses nobody wants itemised in Amsterdam.

She translates the dispatch exactly. She makes two copies and has the factor mark both as received from her hand. One goes through the Dutch gate. One goes beneath Pieter's coat.

Three weeks later, a Portuguese vessel enters Bantam roadstead under Dutch guard with her mainyard damaged and dark scoring along one side. The captured casks come ashore first: rice, water, powder, shot, each one answering the line Maria copied. The crew follow in irons. One man is carried.

The factor finds her watching.

Your paper, he says, with the satisfaction of a man returning a tool to its maker. We had her before she saw us.

On the quay, one of the prisoners hears the Portuguese and turns. He looks at Maria, at the Dutch factor beside her, at the casks between them. He does not know what she translated. He knows enough.

Traitor, he says.

Maria owes Portugal nothing. Portuguese hands carried her from one sale to the next, Portuguese words recorded the prices, and no flag in that roadstead has ever claimed her except as cargo. None of this makes the man wrong.

The factor offers a second fee for examining the captured papers.

Maria takes it.

That evening Lysbeth finds her counting the payment.

You knew what the dispatch would do, she says.

Yes.

You knew they would take the ship.

Yes.

And the men aboard her.

Maria squares the coins with one finger.

Yes.

Why translate it?

Because I wanted what the money would buy.

Lysbeth waits. Maria lets her wait. There is no cleaner answer behind the first one.

That money buys the desk.

A joiner near the pepper market makes it from teak without carving: four square legs, a broad top, one shallow drawer for instruments and one deep drawer in which a page may lie flat. Maria rejects the first top because it rocks. The joiner planes one corner, sets it down again and waits while she tests it with both palms.

Iskandar watches four men carry it up the scale-maker's stairs.

That will be troublesome when you have to run, he says.

Yes.

Why buy it?

Because it will be troublesome when someone tries to take it.

The first paper Maria lays on the teak is her copy of the Portuguese dispatch. Independence bought with another man's irons is still independence. It is also still bought with the irons.

She keeps both entries.

* * *

## VII. The Westbound Account

Lysbeth builds a smaller trade with the skills nobody entered on her passenger line. Sailors come to her for fever, merchants for the complaints they do not want in a Company's surgeon's book, women from the market for everything European men have trained themselves not to see. She charges those who can pay and does not discuss the others with Maria. Willem carries water and cleans instruments.

By September, two Dutch ships are preparing to sail west.

Lysbeth's brother offers what he believes is generosity. If she stays, Willem may remain under his account and, when he is older, be found a place in the loge. A clerk's stool. A Company's training. A future already reduced to a line the boy has not asked to read.

Lysbeth books passage on the first ship.

I meant to bring one man east and buried him before we sailed, she tells Maria. I brought one boy east to keep him alive. I will not let another man call his future a place found for him.

The return entry names Lysbeth as a paying passenger and Willem as her son. It contains no advance, no service owed on landing and no claim on the boy's future wages. Maria reads it twice. Lysbeth reads it once and then reads it aloud to Willem, because a child who has crossed the world inside other people's paper is old enough to hear what the return journey says.

He signs his own name.

The letters no longer lean against one another. Maria looks for the boy's old uncertain hand and cannot find it.

I practised, Willem says.

I can see that.

It is the closest she comes to praise. He understands and stands taller.

On the morning they sail, Maria sets two oilskin packets on the teak and gives Lysbeth one. Each contains the fever pages from Pieter's book in Maria's exact copy, a letter for Hendricks and a witnessed account showing the day Maria's bonded service ended. Joos can place that account in front of the Amsterdam Chamber and recover what remains of his guarantee.

Two packets? Lysbeth asks.

One goes with you. One leaves on the next English ship.

You do not trust me.

I do not trust water.

Lysbeth smiles. She has known Maria long enough not to ask for a softer answer.

You could come west, she says. There would be room.

In whose house?

Mine.

Maria looks at Willem holding his own articles, at the roadstead beyond him, at the ships turning on their anchors toward every place she has survived.

No, she says. But I know what the offer is worth.

Lysbeth accepts this too.

Willem stops in front of Maria with his arms half-raised.

May I?

Maria considers the question as seriously as he asks it.

Once, she says.

He hugs her. Maria stands rigid. Then one hand comes up and rests between his shoulder blades, once, like a seal applied to something already agreed.

When the ship is under way, Lysbeth remains at the rail. Willem holds up the packet. Maria does not wave. She stands beside Iskandar until the hull becomes one more dark mark among dark marks and the water takes it west.

Pieter's original does not travel. It goes to a place neither Company gate nor teak drawer knows.

His truth travels twice.

* * *

## VIII. The Rate

The years after Lysbeth leaves begin to arrive as cargo.

Cotton and steel from the Coromandel coast. Lists of cloth wanted in Pattani, entrusted to Chinese merchants whose own books are older than the Company employing them. Diamonds sent through Bantam in packets small enough to vanish into a cuff and valuable enough to make six men watch the cuff. Pepper always: dry, wet, short, adulterated, promised, stolen, taxed twice and described as honest in every language available.

Each cargo brings paper. Each paper brings Maria another hand.

The city supplies the harder education. In 1608 men from the court kill a governor and seize the young ruler and his mother; offices change hands, seals change owners and agreements written the week before discover that accuracy does not make them sovereign. In 1609 the new regent orders Chinese debtors released from English custody despite the bills the English chief waves in answer. Maria reads the English debts and the Malay order. Both are true. Only one commands the gate that morning.

The English chief comes to the teak desk with three signed bills and asks Maria to find the error in them. She finds none. Names, amounts, dates, marks: the debts are properly recorded.

Then the release is unlawful, he says.

Under whose law?

The bills are valid.

Yes.

The order contradicts them.

No. The bills say money is owed. The order says the men are released. You want the first paper to prevent the second from existing.

What use is a true debt if the debtor walks out of the gate?

Maria thinks of a room in Wapping that found a murderer truly and let him walk out of its door.

It remains a true debt, she says. Paper records power. It does not replace it.

The Englishman pays for the reading and leaves with all three bills still true and none of the debtors still behind his gate. He never thanks Maria for the distinction. He brings her work again the following month.

Paper can prove what was promised. It cannot decide who must care.

By 1612, nobody in Bantam agrees what Maria is.

The Dutch call her an interpreter when entering her fee and an outside person when objecting to her copies. The English call her a broker when they need Dutch correspondence read and a Dutch agent when her reading does not favour them. Portuguese merchants call her Portuguese until she charges them, then discover objections in her history. Iskandar calls her Maria and sends the difficult work first.

The harbour's officials know her more accurately: she is the woman at the teak desk whose two versions agree.

The desk has moved out of the room above the scale-maker and into a ground-floor counting room of its own, midway between the Dutch loge and the English factory and belonging to neither. The shallow drawer holds pens, knives, sand and seal impressions that prove only what warm wax looked like on the day it was pressed. The deep drawer holds copies. The originals do not stay together and do not sleep where Maria does.

She buys the silk from a merchant newly in from Macao. Dark, almost black until the sun finds blue in it, plain enough not to advertise the price. The merchant offers it in gratitude after Maria finds a missing zero in a Dutch order. Maria pays him anyway. Gifts between friends may be given. Gifts between merchants are unfinished accounts.

The coat is cut loose enough for the heat and close enough at the sleeves not to drag through ink. Dark silk does not show the day's small stains. This is the practical reason. By the third time a client waits rather than interrupt while she writes, it has become something else.

A new Dutch senior merchant arrives in the wet season and sends a boy to summon the translator.

Maria sends back her rate.

The boy returns with the message that Company business takes precedence.

Maria sends back the rate again.

At noon the senior merchant crosses the market himself. He finds an English factor already waiting, Iskandar in the second chair and a clerk from the harbour master's office watching Maria compare two measures of pepper without improving either one.

I sent for you, the Dutchman says.

I received it.

You were employed by our loge.

For one month.

The Company brought you east.

The Company entered me east. The distinction has made you money for seven years.

He looks at the desk, the silk, the three men whose work is ahead of his.

Whom do you work for now?

The parties named on the page.

He puts a Portuguese letter on the teak. Maria does not reach for it.

Your rate is excessive.

Then the letter is inexpensive.

He sits.

Two copies, Maria says. My name beneath both. Changes after my hand are marked by the person who makes them.

Why two?

One for you. One for what you later say I wrote.

The Dutchman looks at Iskandar, who makes a sympathetic face and offers no help.

You have become very useful, the Dutchman says.

Useful is your word.

What is yours?

Broker.

What is the difference?

Maria draws the Portuguese letter toward her.

I write the rate.

Outside, the wet market carries on in several languages she can work in and a dozen she cannot. A ship out in the roads fires a salute. Nobody in the room looks up.

Maria is not safe. Safety is still a door and every door still has a keeper. But the teak desk is too heavy to run with and too heavy for one man to take.

She sits behind it in dark silk while the next man waits.
