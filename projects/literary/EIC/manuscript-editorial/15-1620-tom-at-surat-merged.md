# 1614–1622: Tom at Surat

> **1614–1620**
>
> *“A machine does not need to hate a man. It only needs him to fit.”*
>
> — Tom Fletcher, Surat, 1620

## I. The Letter Waiting

After Jack walks toward Deptford, Tom goes back to the Company.

The pay office is still open. Pritchard's petition occupies one clerk, two sailors and a corner of the table where coin should be counted. The paymaster has changed his collar. A red line remains above it where Tom's fist pressed the cloth against his throat.

Nobody mentions the wall.

The Company is skilled at continuing after an interruption. Ledgers reopen. Men receive balances. Pepper leaves the warehouse under guard.

A clerk gives Tom three things.

His balance.

An offer of another boatswain's berth.

A letter seven months old.

The letter travelled from an Essex parish to Leadenhall Street, from Leadenhall Street to a ship preparing to sail, then back to the office when Tom's vessel had already cleared the Downs. It crossed London twice and no ocean. That is enough to make it arrive after the death it carries.

His mother was ill for nine days. A parish widow sat with her. The parish buried her beside Tom's father because the older grave had room along one edge and because Tom's father, dead these six years, still held the better claim on what remained of the family.

The letter contains one question from the parish clerk: whether the son wishes the rent on the rooms to continue until he can examine the effects.

There are no effects worth the rent.

Tom knows each one. A pot with one sound handle. Two stools. His father's sea chest, emptied after the burial. A wool coverlet patched in three colours. Needles. Thread. No thimble.

The thimble lies beneath Maggie's bar.

Tom sees it on the flagstones again, bright in firelight after she drops it at his feet. He did not pick it up. Maggie did. Somewhere west along the river, the last object his mother held daily is in the keeping of the woman who told him not to come back.

The pay clerk waits for a decision about the rooms.

Close them, Tom says.

Signature here.

Tom reads the line. Disposal of effects at parish discretion. Rent discharged from the date of death. No further claim against the son.

He signs.

It is the cleanest family paper he has ever seen.

The clerk slides the berth offer closer. Surat. Better wage. Departure in six weeks. The Company needs experienced men after Swally, men who know the western coast and can train crews while factors turn victory into trade.

Tom thinks of Jack's answer at the road.

No.

The word belonged to a boy with somewhere else to go.

Tom signs the berth.

## II. What the Company Keeps

Surat remembers Tom as a scar and a whistle.

He first came there as an able seaman under men who shouted after mistakes. He returns as the man expected to prevent them. The English factory sends boats through the river mouth. Merchants send cotton inward and silver outward. Portuguese ships watch the roads from a distance made larger since Swally and smaller by every English gun added afterward.

The Company's attention is changing.

Pepper taught it hunger. Cloves and nutmeg taught it monopoly. Cotton teaches scale.

Pepper sits in sacks and announces itself to every nose in the warehouse. Cotton arrives as a hundred different things: white calico, painted chintz, coarse cloth for trade, fine cloth that merchants unfold with washed hands, lengths ordered by colours whose English names have not yet settled. One bale is affordable. Ten thousand bales become policy.

Tom does not choose what the Company buys.

He makes buying possible.

He inspects tackle at the river landing. He assigns watches to warehouse roofs during the dry heat. He trains men to pass cargo from boat to godown without dropping it into mud or opening a seam for private hands. He measures how much fresh water a crew consumes between Surat and Bantam and reduces the margin by one cask without losing a man.

The factors notice.

Fewer losses, they write.

Improved discipline.

Exact in stores and men.

The words travel to London in reports Tom never sees. Each one makes the next order easier to give him.

A boat crew refuses to cross the bar in bad weather. Tom watches the swell, agrees with them and delays the cargo. The factor complains that one tide has been lost.

The next morning another boat overturns.

Tom's men live. The cargo leaves on the afternoon water. The report credits prudent delay and careful supervision. It does not record the names of the boatmen who read the bar before Tom did.

Tom makes the clerk add them.

The clerk writes four names in a margin.

The fair copy sent to London omits the margin because the paragraph concerns management.

Tom learns this months later when the duplicate comes back attached to a commendation.

He does not ask for the names again.

This is how the Company improves him. Not by making him cruel in one decisive act. By teaching him which corrections survive the fair copy and which only cost time.

## III. The Exact Instrument

By 1617 Tom can load a ship from the paperwork before the first bale reaches the quay.

He knows which master conceals private trade beneath water casks. Which purser issues rotten cordage first so sound rope remains to be sold at the end. Which factor understates the weight of damp cotton and which broker makes dry cloth heavier by leaving it near an open jar overnight. He knows the working depth at three states of the river and the number of local hands needed to move a bale without using English sailors who will spend half the day ill from the heat.

Knowledge makes him valuable.

Value gives him authority.

Authority lets him reduce harm in small, measurable ways.

It also lets the Company move more cargo.

Tom replaces a cracked lifting beam before it kills anyone. The safer landing handles thirty additional bales a week.

He stops a purser charging men twice for slops. The crew signs for a second voyage because the first paid more cleanly than expected.

He refuses to load beyond the marked line. The factor orders a larger ship next season.

Every correction strengthens the machine that required correcting.

The paradox would interest Arthur. Tom has not seen Arthur since the hiring rail. He sees his hand everywhere: narrow sevens, open sixes, clauses built to appear finished until a creditor finds the empty part. Arthur's forms reach Surat ahead of men who signed them. Wage assignments. Equipment schedules. Recovery rights. A whole London room replicated on thinner eastern paper.

When a factor asks Tom to assess twelve newly arrived boys, he begins with teeth, shoulders and hands.

The youngest says he is seventeen. He is perhaps fourteen.

Who sent you? Tom asks.

My uncle.

Who receives the wage?

My uncle.

Do you want the sea?

The boy looks confused. They are already in Surat. He has crossed it.

Tom hears Maggie too late.

That was not my question.

The boy says he wants food.

Tom assigns him to the warehouse roof, away from the boats, and orders the cook to feed the new hands before inspection. Food before fitness. Food before any answer a hungry body gives because it sees bread behind the examiner.

The factor objects.

They were hired sound.

They were hired hungry.

Does feeding change the assessment?

Yes.

How?

It makes the answer cost less.

The factor allows one meal. Tom enters it under crew preparation.

The boys eat. Ten are taken aboard. Two remain at the factory. The youngest survives the year and signs again.

Tom does not know whether this is success.

The Company enters it as twelve retained.

## IV. The Missing Bale

The machine tests Tom with one missing bale.

It disappears between the river landing and the west warehouse in 1618. The tally at the boat says sixty-four. The warehouse receipt says sixty-three. Both carry marks from men who swear the count was correct.

The junior factor responsible for the transfer chooses the easiest explanation.

A porter stole it.

The accused man is called Yusuf. He has worked the landing for four seasons, owns no cart and lives in a room too small to conceal a bale of calico even if he could carry one through Surat without being seen. The factor has him held beside the warehouse while a clerk prepares a statement.

Tom reads the statement.

It says Yusuf was observed behaving suspiciously near the cargo.

By whom?

The factor says the general conduct was observed by several persons.

Name one.

This is not an English court.

Then why have you written an English accusation?

The factor wants the shortage resolved before the chief sees the day's figures. A named thief closes the column. An unnamed missing bale leaves it open beneath the factor's own hand.

Tom returns to the landing.

He measures the empty places beneath the tarpaulin. He checks the lifting nets, the cart sheets and the warehouse threshold for torn cloth. Nothing. Then he reads both tallies again.

The boat tally groups the cargo by merchant mark. Twenty bales under one sign. Twenty-four under another. Twenty beneath a third.

The warehouse clerk has copied the groups as nineteen, twenty-four and twenty. His total of sixty-three is correct for the numbers he wrote. The missing bale exists only because one narrow stroke failed to travel from one page to the next.

Tom puts the documents together before the factor.

The factor studies them and says the porter may be released.

No.

No?

The statement first.

It was not submitted.

It was written.

Tom makes the clerk cross out every line, write that the suspected theft arose from a copying error and put the factor's name beneath the correction. Yusuf watches from the wall. He does not thank Tom when released. Gratitude would accept that Tom had the right to decide whether the page lived.

The next morning the chief factor asks Tom how the error can be prevented.

Tom designs a double tally. One count at the boat, one at the warehouse, totals spoken aloud before either page leaves the hands that witnessed it. Discrepancies are opened at once instead of after the porters have gone.

The system prevents three mistakes in its first month.

It also moves cargo faster because clerks stop searching old books for vanished bales.

London praises the improved control.

No report names Yusuf. The man almost punished by one bad stroke disappears from the account as completely as the bale never did.

Tom remembers him.

For a year.

Then two hundred other names arrive, and Yusuf becomes the missing-bale case, and the missing-bale case becomes the double tally, and the double tally becomes Tom's procedure. A man is nearly destroyed. A system learns. The system keeps the lesson longer than the man.

That is another kind of machine.

## V. Orphan's Work

Tom's father dies repeatedly in Surat.

The first death is the true one: winter 1608, the damaged knee gone black, fever reducing a boatswain who crossed seas to a man unable to lift himself from a rented bed.

Tom does not see it. He is east, sending half a wage into an account that admits he is alive more readily than any letter can. Four winters later, his mother tells him the death in the same rooms where it happened, with the sea chest beneath the window and the table still pushed against the wall.

In her telling, the room is smaller than Tom remembers. The table has been moved so two people may pass the bed without touching the bad leg. A cup, a spoon and a folded cloth stand on the stool. The grate gives more smoke than heat. On the mantel, the old whistle lies beside a spare wrapped in sailcloth, both polished because his father has always cleaned tools he is no longer able to use.

His father has refused the parish woman's hands all morning. He refuses his wife's too.

I can sit.

The body cannot. His mother puts one arm behind the shoulders. Her husband strikes it away with what remains of the old authority, then tries alone. The effort raises him two inches from the bolster. He stays there, trembling, until she lowers him without asking again.

For a moment anger enters the face. Not at her. At the fact of being lowered.

The man spent twenty-three years making his body answer weather before weather finished giving the order. He climbed with cold hands, slept wet, took watches through fever and stood on a ruined knee until the ship reached a place where another man could be paid to notice it. Help belonged to passengers, injured boys and men who had not yet learned their work. He has no rule for receiving it himself.

His mouth is dry. His mother lifts the cup.

No.

You need water.

No.

The refusal is clear even when the eyes are not. She sets the cup down. A minute later her husband swallows against nothing and winces. The sound is small, a catch deep in the throat, but she has listened to him breathe all week and moves before permission reaches her.

This time she uses the spoon.

The first little measure runs from the corner of his mouth into the cloth. The second stays. The throat works once, painfully. She waits and offers another. He closes his lips.

Enough.

It may mean enough water. Enough lifting. Enough people leaning over a man whose work has always been done standing. His mother does not improve the sentence into anything larger, either then or when she gives it to Tom four years later.

She puts the spoon beside the cup.

The parish woman asks whether the physician should be fetched again. The man in the bed turns his face toward the wall. His wife knows the movement. He used it in harbour rooms when shore talk had gone on too long and the next watch required sleep.

Let him rest, she says.

She sits close enough to hear each breath and far enough that the bed is not another hand holding him down. Once her husband looks across the room and fixes on her apron. The gaze has the concentration of recognition but none of its arrival. She does not give her own name as a test. When she tells him this, Tom is grateful. A person ought not make the dying fail an examination merely because the living need passing.

Near dusk the breath changes. His mother takes up the spoon and holds it ready, though there is no swallow left to answer it.

Afterward the cup remains half full.

The physician's account is complete. Attendance. A cordial. Cloth for the knee. A preparation applied twice. The hand is clean and the total exact. Nothing on the page says the man refused the first visit, or that pride cost a day, or that the physician could name the corruption in the leg and do nothing except enter it correctly.

The landlord's question arrives before the grave is ready. Will the widow keep both rooms? If she will, the next week is due. If she will not, the dead man's effects must be moved before another tenant can be taken. Tom's mother sits beside the bed while a parish man asks it, still holding the damp cloth as though work once begun ought to be finished.

One room, she says.

The table, bed, two stools and patched coverlet will fit badly. The sea chest will not fit at all unless it stands beneath the window and blocks half the light. The landlord suggests selling it toward the rent.

No, his mother says.

The word comes with enough of her husband's voice that everyone looks at her.

Harcourt's steward arrives with the practical kindness of a man carrying an account already open. Burial may be advanced. The final month of the larger room may be carried. The physician's demand may be settled now rather than pressed upon the widow. Each mercy has a space beneath it for recovery from Tom's assigned wage.

She asks the burial cost.

The steward tells her.

And if we do not take the advance?

Then the parish ground, when the parish can receive him.

She looks at the bed. Waiting means keeping the larger room, buying more coal and finding linen the household does not have. Delay gives no dignity to a body. It gives the landlord another week. Harcourt has put a price on the only timely choice.

Take the burial, she says.

The steward writes.

The household effects are examined without being removed. The pot is his mother's. The stools and coverlet belong to the rooms. The sea chest predates Harcourt's service, though the steward turns it over looking for a house mark. Inside lie old cordage tools, two shirts, a Levant pilot page nobody in the room can use and the two whistles.

The old one has the thumb-worn place Tom remembers from childhood. The spare is newer but wrapped as carefully as a blade.

His mother buries the old whistle with him. Four winters later she gives Tom the spare, and with it every part of the death she had to carry because he was east.

The second death comes whenever Tom uses the spare.

One sharp note for attention. Two for an order repeated. The same worn place beneath the thumb. Men turn at the sound and for an instant Tom is both the boy who obeyed it and the man making other boys obey.

The third death begins on the steward's page before the ground closes.

His father's name remains attached to Tom's old debt years after the body is in the ground. Rent. Physician. Burial. Interest. All supplied. All recoverable. A dead man's ruined knee continues producing money for Harcourt because the book has learned to walk without him.

Tom pays it.

His mother's death is different. It produces almost nothing. One parish letter. The clean closure of two rooms. No claim.

For that reason it leaves nowhere for his anger to go.

He begins carrying the letter inside his sea chest, beneath the berth papers and above the spare shirt. It contains no sentence from her. She had nine days and did not write one, perhaps because fever took the hand, perhaps because she believed he was too far away, perhaps because working women do not become eloquent merely because death has entered the room.

Tom invents no final words for her.

He remembers ordinary ones.

Dry your feet.

That pot is hot.

Take the thimble to Maggie if you mean to lose it again.

He does not know whether she ever said the last sentence. Memory has begun putting Maggie into his mother's rooms and his mother behind Maggie's bar. The two women occupy the same narrow place in him: food, rebuke, work done without performance, a hand held out for whatever foolish keepsake he needs somebody else to protect.

One is dead.

One has told him not to return.

The Company always has another berth.

## VI. The Lone Machine

In 1620 the English factory receives an order for cloth larger than any it has yet attempted.

Calico for the islands. Chintz for England. Coarse lengths to exchange farther east. The list contains widths, colours, quantities, delivery seasons and penalties written by men who have never seen the monsoon close a road.

The chief factor says it cannot be done.

Then he gives the order to Tom.

Tom divides it.

River boats by draught. Warehouse space by week. Guards by route. Spare cordage by expected loss. He pairs factors who distrust each other so neither can hide a delay without exposing the other. He orders raised platforms before the rain, then makes the carpenters build them twice as high when local porters show him the flood mark on a wall.

The cloth begins arriving.

White. Red. Blue. Flowers, vines, grids, borders. Villages and workshops whose names never enter the English invoice send work through brokers whose names appear only where a price must be challenged. By the time the bales reach Tom they have become numbers and destinations.

He keeps them dry.

One roof fails in the night. Tom moves forty bales before the rain reaches them. One guard takes coin to open a side door. Tom discovers the missing seal, dismisses the man and recovers the cloth. One factor buys short measure. Tom makes him open every bale in the yard and measure again beneath the eyes of the sellers.

The order is filled.

London sends praise.

The local hands receive their agreed wage. The brokers receive what the contracts allow. The Company receives more than it expected. Tom receives another berth and authority over more men.

Nothing in the operation is false.

Nothing in it belongs to him except the success.

On the final evening, he walks through the warehouse after the doors are barred. Bales rise on both sides, dry, exact and ready to move. His footsteps return from the walls. No father. No mother. No Maggie. No Jack. The boys he fed are on another ship. Arthur's hand lies folded in the office. Harcourt's debt waits in London, smaller and alive.

Tom puts his hand on one bale of blue printed cotton.

For a moment he thinks of the square he carried west in 1612, oilcloth wrapped against monsoon and Portuguese shot, and laid on Maggie's bar without knowing whether it was gift, apology or proof of return. She folded it and put it beneath the counter. Hours later she put him out.

The cloth was not the wrong gift.

It was an answer to a question she had not asked.

Tom removes his hand.

Outside, the river landing continues after dark. Men call weights. Rope takes strain. A clerk asks for the next number.

Tom gives it.

The Company does not love him. It does not forgive him. It does not remember his parents or know the shape of the thimble under Maggie's bar.

It fits him exactly.

For now, he fits it back.

## VII. The Door

Tom never sits with his back to a door.

He does not know this until a clerk in Surat points it out.

They are in the factory dining room after the evening tally. Eight men, one long table, shutters open to release the heat. Tom arrives last and moves a junior merchant's cup so he can take the chair at the end nearest the entrance.

You always do that, the clerk says.

Do what?

Take the door.

It has the air.

So does the window.

The window is too narrow for a man.

The clerk laughs because he thinks Tom has made a joke.

Tom eats facing the room.

Afterward he notices the habit everywhere. In the warehouse office he stands between visitors and the threshold. On deck he takes the position from which he can see both companionway and rail. In a market coffee room he changes benches when three armed Portuguese merchants enter, not because he expects violence but because the old position leaves them behind him.

The body makes these decisions before the mind receives them.

In 1603 there was a door behind Tom and Silas Rook before it. A woman who could not speak stood on the other side. Tom placed himself between them and took a backhand across the face. Years later a boarding pike opened the same eyebrow from the other direction, as if the scar had been waiting for a second author.

Under strain, Tom touches the place.

Two fingers. One brief rub above the eye. He does it when weather closes, when a factor gives an order without enough boats, when a boy steps into a bight and does not yet know the rope has begun deciding for him.

The men read the gesture as thought.

Tom experiences it as nothing.

One night a warehouse guard brings in a thief.

The accused is a child carrying three handfuls of spilled rice inside his shirt. Twelve years old, perhaps. Bare feet. A cut at one knee. The guard pushes him through the office door and asks Tom where to hold him until morning.

Tom takes the door without thinking.

The boy sees a scarred Englishman blocking the only exit and becomes completely still.

Tom feels his fingers at the eyebrow.

For a moment the room changes. Not into the Pelican exactly. Rooms do not remember for men. Men force rooms to carry what they bring. The child is not Maria. The rice is not Bell's page. Tom is not the young man who stood against the mob.

But the door is still a door.

How much rice? Tom asks.

The guard shows him.

Charge it to spillage.

He stole it.

Then improve the sweepings.

Tom steps aside.

The boy does not move. Safety offered by the man who blocked the door still looks like another order.

Go, Tom says.

The child runs.

Tom remains in the doorway long after there is nobody left to hold.

## VIII. The Signature

The Company sends a new form in 1621.

It is called a declaration of ordinary loss. Masters, pursers and boatswains are to sign at the end of every passage, confirming that shortages in water, stores, rope and sail arose from weather, necessary consumption or the natural dishonesty of sailors rather than negligence by Company officers.

Tom reads it twice.

The clerk waits with sand ready.

Where are the amounts?

Attached after landing.

Then the declaration comes after landing.

Procedure requires signatures before cargo discharge.

I cannot declare a number I have not seen.

You are not declaring the number. Only the nature of any loss.

Any loss has not happened on this page yet.

The clerk points to the signature line. Every other officer has signed.

Tom's hand refuses to move.

It is not fear of writing. He signs wage books, orders, inventories and punishments. His name exists in Company rooms from London to Bantam. The aversion belongs to the white space above the line, the place where another man's meaning can be entered after Tom's hand has made it true.

He remembers Bell across the counting-room table, asking whether Tom would sign if he knew the debt was designed never to close.

Tom signed the next morning.

His father's ruined knee became rent, physician, burial and interest beneath that signature. Jack's father's broken body became four pounds. A hand does not need to understand a machine to start it.

Tom turns the form over and writes on the back.

*No declaration made until quantities are attached and read.*

He signs beneath that sentence.

The clerk refuses it.

Tom leaves the paper on the desk.

For three days his pay is held. On the fourth, the chief factor summons him and explains that administrative order requires trust between servants of the Company.

Trust whom? Tom asks.

The process.

Which man is that?

The chief tells him not to be insolent.

Tom touches the scar.

The ship cannot clear without a boatswain's declaration. The factor cannot replace Tom before the tide. Power is not always the larger office. Sometimes it is the man whose missing name stops the page.

The amounts are attached.

Tom finds two coils of sound rope entered as ordinary loss. The purser meant to sell them privately after discharge. Tom removes the coils from the shortage, signs the corrected declaration and watches the clerk sand his name.

The ship clears.

The next form arrives six months later with a new clause permitting estimated quantities before landing.

The machine has learned from his refusal.

## IX. Shore Leave

Tom takes shore leave once in six years.

Not market leave, when the ship remains inside every decision. Not the walk between factory and landing. A full day with no watch, no tally and no man expected to answer his whistle.

He wakes before the watch changes anyway.

The hired room is quiet. A bed, a basin, one chair, a door with a wooden bolt. Tom lies listening for rigging that is not there.

Without the ship's sounds, his body supplies them. A block knocking at the foretop. Water moving in casks. Canvas beginning to flog. He knows none is real. He listens harder.

At dawn he bolts the door, then unbolts it because sleeping behind a barrier makes the room feel smaller. He places the chair where he can see the entrance from the bed. When the keeper brings breakfast, Tom is already standing.

Food first, the keeper says.

She means before payment. The words strike another room inside him.

Mutton and mace. A rag over one shoulder. A blue square folded beneath a counter. Maggie asking whether he has used his father's whistle better.

Most days, he told her.

He has spent years proving the answer in situations she cannot see from here, and all that time knowing proof is not what she asked for.

Tom eats.

The bread is flat and fresh. The fish has been fried with spice. He tastes meat pie.

He leaves half the food and pays for all of it.

On the stair, the sole of his left boot opens at the toe.

It does not tear cleanly. The stitching has rotted under salt and lets go one hole at a time, so that the leather mouth opens and shuts with each step. Tom stops, lifts the foot and presses the sole back with his thumb.

The keeper watches from the doorway.

There is a cobbler two lanes inland, she says.

I have cord.

You have a boot that has already refused cord.

Tom looks at the broken stitches. She is right. This irritates him more than the boot.

In the street, men move without watches. A father carries a sleeping girl. Two women argue over fruit. A carpenter planes a door while the owner holds it upright. Ordinary life surrounds Tom with the intimacy of a language he once spoke and can now only understand when others use it.

He finds the cobbler by the boots hanging beneath an awning. The shop is open to the lane: a low bench, knives, lasts, pegs, a pot of blacking gone soft in the heat. The cobbler takes one look at the loose sole and holds out a hand for the other boot.

Only this one, Tom says.

Both.

The right is sound.

The cobbler bends it once. Three stitches lift along the welt.

Both, he says again.

Tom understands enough of the local trade speech to buy rope, water and a day's labour. Boots have not been necessary to that education. He points to the sun, then to the work, asking how long.

The cobbler points to noon.

Tom looks at the lane behind him. A man without boots cannot choose a direction quickly. He removes the left, then the right, and places them side by side on the bench rather than surrendering them directly. The cobbler gathers them without comment and points to a stool beneath the awning.

The stool faces the street. Tom turns it until he can also see the rear door.

The cobbler cuts the old thread. He does not hurry and does not explain. Each bad stitch comes free separately. The failure that looked sudden on the stair has been travelling around the sole for months: leather dried hard by salt, holes enlarged by strain, thread dark where water entered and pale where friction ate it.

Tom knows this kind of damage. A line parts at the final fibre and begins parting long before.

How much? he asks.

The cobbler names the price.

Tom pays half. The man counts it, puts it beneath a stone and continues. No gratitude passes in either direction. The rate has made room for silence.

For ten minutes Tom sits.

A handcart loses a pin outside the fruit stall. The owner replaces it with a nail and keeps going. A woman lowers a basket from an upper window on a cord polished by years of the same journey. Two boys carry a door between them while the carpenter walks backward, shaving one last high place without making them set it down. Somewhere behind the shops, somebody beats dust from a mat in a steady rhythm that becomes, in Tom's body, men hauling together.

He makes himself stay on the stool.

The awning line above him is wrong.

It has been taken twice around a peg and finished with a half-hitch that draws tighter whenever the cloth fills. The free end is already pinched flat. One gust will not bring the awning down. Twenty ordinary gusts will make the knot impossible to release, and then someone will cut good line because a bad turn has eaten it.

Tom looks away.

The cobbler draws waxed thread through the first sole.

Tom looks back.

The peg wants a round turn and two hitches, he says.

The cobbler follows his pointing finger, looks at the awning, then at Tom's bare feet.

Boots, the man says, and returns to them.

Tom lasts another minute.

Then he stands on the warm boards, reaches above the doorway and takes the strain of the awning across one forearm. The cobbler says something sharp. Tom holds up the flattened end, points to the place where it has begun to fail and reties the line so the load sits on the peg instead of the finish.

The awning lifts one inch and lies still.

The cobbler examines the knot. He pulls the free end. It releases. He ties it again himself, slower, and leaves it.

Tom returns to the stool.

The man works on the boots. Tom watches the street. Neither calls what happened payment.

At midday a food carrier passes with flat bread under cloth, a pot of lentils and a jar of sour milk. The cobbler buys one portion and divides it beside the blacking stone. He pushes half toward Tom.

I ate, Tom says.

The cobbler points at Tom, then at the food, dismissing the claim as irrelevant.

Tom pays for his portion. The man returns one of the coins.

Awning, he says.

Tom takes the coin back. Refusing a fair account would only make another kind of debt.

They eat without conversation. The bread is hot enough to soften the hard place at the corner of Tom's mouth. The lentils are plain. The milk is cool. Around them the lane continues its unrecorded exchange of work, food, carrying, waiting and repair. Nobody is being saved. Nothing is being proved.

At noon the cobbler gives Tom both boots. The left sole lies close. The right has been restitched before it could open. Tom puts them on and walks the length of the lane, testing the work.

He could return to the hired room. The chair still faces the door. The bed is paid through morning. A whole afternoon remains in which no man expects his judgement.

He goes to the river.

Boats require rope. Rope requires inspection. Within an hour he is helping a boatman reset an eye in a worn painter. Before the afternoon is half gone he has found a cracked block. By late light he has arranged replacement line through a broker who owes the factory nothing.

The times no longer agree because leave has no bell to make them agree. Tom notices only when the light changes. The painter leads to a frayed lashing at the thwart. The lashing leads to the cracked block. The block leads to a length of line stored in shade and sold without Company measure. Each repair names the next. By doing them, Tom turns an afternoon without orders into a watch only he knows he has taken.

At sunset he returns to the ship relieved.

The relief shames him less than the room did. A ship does not ask whether a man can sit still inside his own life. It gives him a line under strain and accepts the answer of his hands.

The sea carried home in him: door, food, thimble, blue cloth, Maggie's exact refusals.

Home carried back to sea: his habit of repair, his place at the threshold, the rule that a hungry boy eats before he answers.

Neither place releases the other.

* * *

## X. The Letter Not Sent

Tom writes to Maggie seven times.

The first attempt begins in the Surat factory after his mother's parish letter arrives. He writes Maggie's name, then nothing beneath it. News of the death feels like a claim: feed me, forgive me, tell me I still belong somewhere. He folds the blank page around the name and puts it in his chest.

The second begins with Jack.

*The boy came home alive.*

This is true. It also arranges Jack's survival as evidence for Tom's defence. He hears himself entering the nineteen dead in smaller writing below it. He tears the page.

The third says the debt was corrected.

The fourth explains the paymaster's collar.

The fifth contains only the words *You were right*, which appear humble until Tom reads the demand hidden inside them: now tell me I am no longer wrong.

He burns that one.

The sixth is a proper letter. Weather. Surat. Cotton. His mother's death. Jack's work in Deptford, learned from a sailor newly arrived from England who saw the boy outside a rigging shed. Tom writes two pages without once saying why he has written them.

He signs late.

The signature makes the letter into something that may travel. Until then it was only thought arranged on paper.

Tom carries it to the packet clerk.

Which ship? the clerk asks.

Tom names the westbound vessel.

Fee?

The clerk names it.

Tom pays, then takes the letter back before the clerk can enter it.

Not ready?

No.

The clerk has handled merchants' promises, dying men's instructions and confessions addressed to wives who will receive them after the sender has found courage elsewhere. He does not ask.

The seventh letter begins in 1622 after the boy at the companionway reties the coil and goes to eat.

Tom writes:

*I remembered before I knew what remembering required.*

He sits with the sentence until dawn.

Then he adds:

*I do not know whether that matters to you. It matters to the orders I give.*

For once the page does not offer survival as acquittal. It asks for nothing. That should make it sendable.

Tom folds it.

Does not sign.

The unsigned letter remains beneath his mother's parish notice. Two messages in the same chest: one that travelled too late and one kept from travelling at all.

Silence is not the absence of an entry.

It is an entry renewed every time the ship sails west without the page.

* * *

## XI. The Thimble

The thimble appears when Tom is most tired.

Not as a vision. As an error of the hand.

He reaches into a pocket for his knife and expects brass beneath his fingers. Opens his sea chest and looks toward the corner where his mother kept thread. Sees a glint beside a deck bolt and bends before recognising a washer.

The real thimble is in Wapping.

Unless Maggie sold it.

She would not.

Unless the Pelican burned.

He would have heard.

Unless she threw it into the Thames after he left.

She picked it off the flagstones.

Tom does not know this last fact. He remembers the sound of brass striking stone and the door closing behind him. Everything after belongs to hope pretending it is inference.

At night the scene replays with changes.

Sometimes he picks it up. Maggie tells him the wager is closed and turns away.

Sometimes Jack picks it up and carries it aboard. The thimble rolls loose during the Madagascar squall and disappears through a scupper.

Sometimes Maria stands behind the bar in dark silk, though Tom has never seen her in dark silk and has not seen her at all since the night she vanished. She puts the thimble beside Bell's page and bolts the door from within.

Tom wakes touching the scar.

He begins carrying a brass sailmaker's palm in his coat. It is larger than a thimble, flat and practical, its dimpled metal set in leather. He tells himself the familiar brass stops the hand searching.

It does not.

Men think the palm belonged to his father. Tom never corrects them. A false explanation can become privacy if nobody profits from it.

* * *

## XII. The Echo

In 1622 a young sailor freezes in a doorway.

The ship is taking water at anchor after a cargo boat strikes the quarter. Not enough to sink her. Enough to make every order urgent. Men run pumps, shift stores and rig a fothering sail over the damage.

The boy stands at the companionway holding a coil, unable to decide whether the order sends him below or aft.

Tom sees the hesitation.

Move!

The boy does not.

Tom crosses the deck and stops before striking distance.

Years ago he would have used the hand. Jack on the yard, his weight on the pale buntline. Fear broken by force before the rope broke itself. That blow saved Jack and remains a blow.

Tom points with the whistle.

Look at me. Which line?

The boy names it.

Where does it lead?

Aft.

Then go aft.

The boy moves.

Tom hears his own voice and his father's inside it. Beneath both, Maggie: do not call the taking a rescue. Beneath Maggie, Bell asking one quiet question across an unsigned page. Beneath Bell, a woman behind a door who has no English word available and a room deciding what her silence means.

None of these people is present.

All of them alter the order.

By midnight the leak is controlled. The boy brings back the coil properly flaked and waits for praise.

Tom examines it.

Tie it again.

The boy's face falls.

Tom touches the first turn.

This one crosses. Under load it binds. Again, and then eat.

The boy reties it. The line runs clean.

Good, Tom says.

The word still feels expensive. He spends it.

The boy goes to the galley.

Tom takes the doorway and watches the ship settle around the repair. He is forty-one. The scar aches before weather. His name stands beneath more Company orders than he can remember. Somewhere west, a woman he loves may still hate what he became. Somewhere east, other ledgers are closing around names he has not yet heard.

The sea repeats nothing exactly.

It carries the sound forward changed.
