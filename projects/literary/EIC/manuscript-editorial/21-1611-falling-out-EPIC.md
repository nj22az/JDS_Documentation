## XV. With or Without Me

Carter comes in while Jack is practising a rolling hitch on the leg of the table. The chandler has grown prosperous without growing quiet. His coat is good broadcloth now. Tar no longer lives beneath his nails; three other men carry it for him. He sees Jack, then Tom, then Maggie's face, and wishes with visible force that he had chosen another door.

The boy is not mine, Carter says before anybody accuses him.

No, Maggie says. Only the debt.

His father spoiled twelve lengths of Riga pine.

His father was under them when they fell.

And was paid through the quarter while he healed. Carter removes his gloves one finger at a time. I have a yard to keep. If I forgive every loss because the man beneath it was hurt, I will have no yard and none of them will have wages.

There it is, Maggie says.

There what is?

The room inside you where grief becomes use.

Carter does not know how many forms that room can take. He only knows she has found something in him and dislikes being found.

Tom explains the arrangement the way he has learned to explain things: plainly, in order, leaving no place for a question to catch. Jack signs in the morning for the Company's next fleet. He goes on Tom's crew, under Tom's eye. His wage runs half to himself and half against the yard account until the four pounds are clean.

Maggie sets down the pitcher. Half a boy's wage.

Half of mine went the same road nine years, Tom says. It did not kill me.

It did something to you.

He does not hear it. He has come to the good part, and he wants her to see the good part, because the good part is his. There is an instruction allowance, he tells her — a thing a boatswain may enter against a rated man, two shillings the month, credited where the boatswain directs. His own goes against Carter's account until the four pounds close, and then it turns to Jack, and Jack keeps it. It is lawful. It is clean. He has read the article twice and the purser will not blink.

He lays it out unhurried and a little proud, a man showing good work to the one person whose good opinion he has carried across nine years of ocean. For the length of it he is easy inside the Company's own tools. For the length of it he is very like a small neat clerk who once sat in a warm room above a rope warehouse and made three true accounts of one cargo.

Maggie does not look at the arrangement. She looks at his mouth, at the ease in it, at the pride sitting behind the ease.

She has been arguing with him. She stops. You do not argue with a thing that is finished, and something in the room has just finished.

That is very neat, Tom, she says.

It keeps him fed and it closes the debt. Both.

Yes, she says. It is exactly what they would do. It is what they did to your father, and to Bell, and to every name I have cut into that wood — kept them fed, closed the debt, and never once had to think themselves cruel, because the arithmetic was so very neat.

I am not Harcourt, Tom says.

No, she says, and that is the worst of it. Harcourt has to be told what he is. You have learned to say it to yourself and call it kindness.

He has an answer ready. He has had it ready for nine years, worn smooth on the watches: that the boy signs with or without him, that on another deck Jack gets a man who sells his rations and beats him for not knowing work nobody taught him, that Tom can keep him alive and no one else in that hiring room will try. Every word of it is true. He has said it to himself until it stopped sounding like a defence and started sounding like a fact.

He opens his mouth to say it, and looks at her, and cannot. Because she is not saying the boy will die. She is saying Tom has learned to price a boy and feel kind about it, and that is true as well, and the two truths do not cancel. They sit side by side, both entered, both correct, the way they always do in the books he has learned to keep.

He shuts his mouth. It is the first argument he has ever lost by agreeing with it.

Maggie comes around the counter. She does not take up the mallet. She stands in front of the scarred man in the boatswain's coat and looks at him the way she has looked, across thirty years, at men laid on the centre table — all the way, without hurry, taking the inventory that is owed.

There was a boy, she says. Her voice does not shake, which is worse than if it shook. He stood against that door with a man twice his size in front of him and would not move. I told him he would die for a clove. He stayed. I cut his mark in that wood before his ship had cleared the stairs — the one mark I ever cut before the sea had answered, because I could not make myself believe the sea would dare take him.

She is not looking at the door where the boy once stood. She is looking at Tom, and grieving, openly, a man who is standing in front of her and breathing.

I do not know where he went, she says. But you have come back wearing his face, and you talk like the men he would not move for, and you are proud of it. So I will not pretend. I have buried better men than the one you have made of yourself. I said their names over them. I am not going to stand here and say yours while you are still warm.

She reaches under the bar, into the dark beside the pale notches, and brings out the brass thimble. She does not throw it down. She sets it on the flagstones at his feet, carefully, the way a thing is set down beside a grave.

Take it or leave it, she says. It was a wager that you would come back. You did not. Somebody in your coat did.

Tom has stood in the Portuguese guns at Swally and made no sound the sea could keep. He has taken the fever that emptied a deck around him. He took his father's death in a letter seven months cold, and Pell going over the side of the *Ascension*, and the long arithmetic of a debt built never to close, and none of it, not once, reached past the hard face to the thing behind it.

This reaches it. Not loudly. The face goes the way a hull goes when the sea has finally found the seam it was always going to find — quietly, and all at once, and not to be made whole by any hand aboard.

He does not argue. He has nothing to argue with: she is right, and he loves her, and those two together leave a man no ground to stand on. He does not tell her the one thing that might turn her, because there is no shape he is allowed to say it in, and because it would change nothing — it would not make the true thing she has just said any less true.

I haven't got a boy, he says. It is not a defence. It is only the truth, arriving small and too late.

No, Maggie says. The sea made certain of that too.

He looks at the thimble on the stones. He does not pick it up. He has not earned it back, and he knows to the penny what he has and has not earned; it is the one account he keeps honestly.

The door is behind you, Maggie says, and turns her back, because she cannot watch the rest and stay standing. Don't come back until you remember what a human life is worth.

Tom crosses to the door he once could not be moved from, and opens it, and the cold comes up off the river. He does not look back. A man who looks back is asking to be called, and he will not put that on her — whatever she is grieving, he will not make her refuse him to his face as well.

He goes out into it.

The cold takes him. Wapping Wall runs east into the dark, toward the stairs and the ships and the whole turning world, and Tom walks it the way a man walks who has just learned that the one door he wanted at his back is the door now shut to him — competently, going nowhere in particular, already a little homeless in a way that has nothing to do with the sea, though the sea is where he will carry it.

Behind him the door closes. The cold settles into the room and takes its time leaving.

Carter collects his gloves.

What happens to the yard account? Maggie asks. Her voice has come back to level. It is the only thing about her that has.

The chandler looks at Jack, and for a moment the man who once drove a belaying pin beneath Silas Rook's boot stands inside the merchant's expensive coat.

If the boy signs, half wages until it is discharged.

And if he does not?

Carter's answer takes too long.

The steward will proceed against the rooms.

Maggie nods. Not approval. Entry.

When the room has emptied and the fire is low, she crosses to where the thimble lies on the flagstones and picks it up. She does not set it back on the bar. She returns it to the dark fault beside the notches, behind the folded page, where the house keeps what it is owed and what it has failed to keep. A wager is not cancelled by the keeper. Only by the sea, or by the man himself, coming back through the door as the one who left by it.

She wipes the counter where nothing has spilled. Under her hand is the mark she cut for him nine years ago, deep and pale gone dark with use. She does not cut a new one beside it. He is not dead. She only grieves as though he were, because grief was the one honest entry left to make, and Maggie has never in her life made a false one.

Not once. Not even tonight — when one false entry, one small kindness against the truth, one word that was only *stay*, would have kept in the room the last thing the sea had left her.

## Editorial note (proposal only — not deployed)

Full rework of **§XV only**; the rest of *The Return* is unchanged. Present
tense throughout; **no narrator prolepsis** (the "fourteen years" is left to the
next chapter's dateline, as everywhere else in the book); **no line of
confession** on either side. Built directly from the interview:

- **Trigger = who he's become.** The instruction-allowance beat is now the hinge:
  Tom is *proud* of the clean mechanism, "very like a small neat clerk… above a
  rope warehouse" — Bell's counting-room, in his own mouth. Maggie "looks at his
  mouth, at the pride sitting behind the ease."
- **She mourns him alive.** She *stops arguing* ("you do not argue with a thing
  that is finished"), then eulogises a breathing man: "I am not going to stand
  here and say yours while you are still warm." The thimble is *set down* "the way
  a thing is set down beside a grave," not thrown in disgust.
- **Because she's right.** "It is the first argument he has ever lost by agreeing
  with it." He has the true defence ready and finds it cannot stand, because the
  two truths "do not cancel." No raging against a just verdict.
- **Permanent, unconditional love.** He does not argue, does not resent, will not
  even look back "because a man who looks back is asking to be called, and he will
  not put that on her." The love is everywhere and named nowhere.
- **Only Maggie can break him.** The guns/fever/father/Pell list — none of it
  "reached past the hard face" — and then "this reaches it." Establishes the peak
  above jail (Bantam, when deployed, folds into this list) and the deaths.
- **Fritiof born.** The closing threshold: "already a little homeless in a way
  that has nothing to do with the sea, though the sea is where he will carry it."
  The wanderer begins here, in present tense, without prolepsis.
- **Maggie's heart — the one feasible crack.** The final line ("one word that was
  only *stay*… the last thing the sea had left her") makes her love *feasible*
  and confirms nothing — the single place in the book where it is even possible,
  exactly as designed.

**Two calls for you:** (1) the closing line leans hardest of anything in the book
toward Maggie's side — it keeps it a *maybe*, but if you want her heart even more
sealed, I can end on "Maggie has never in her life made a false one. Not once."
and stop there. (2) "you talk like the men he would not move for" — I can make the
Harcourt/Rook naming explicit again (as the deployed version has it) if you want
the echo louder.
