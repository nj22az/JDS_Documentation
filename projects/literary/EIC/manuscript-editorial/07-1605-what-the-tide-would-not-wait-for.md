# What the Tide Would Not Wait For (1605)

> **1605**
>
> *"He heard the bell and he heard the girl, and he chose to stay."*
>
> — a song learned aboard the *Hector*, 1605

***

## I. Anders's Song

Bantam heat does not cool at night; it only changes what it smells of. By
day the roadstead carries pepper and mud and drying fish; after dark it is
woodsmoke, sweat, and the particular sourness of European men three months
without proper linen. The *Hector* rides at anchor with her sister ships,
waiting on cargo the factors have not yet agreed a price for, and idle men
make their own weather belowdecks.

Anders makes his out of songs. He is Company-Baltic, which is to say
English enough on the muster roll and nothing of the kind in his mouth —
a Gothenburg man who has hauled a Company's rope since before Tom was
born, and who has, in every port from the Sound to the Sunda Strait, made
exactly the same discovery about himself: that a woman ashore always
seems finer than a woman on paper, and that he has never once been the man
his own good sense promised he would be.

Mind the bell and mind the tide, he sings, some evenings, mind the wage
that's in your hand — for the sea will drown you honestly, but the shore
will drown you and lie that it's land.

Tom is twenty-four, and has spent nine months learning that a boatswain's
whistle answers no question he actually wants asked. He has not yet
drowned, honestly or otherwise, and privately believes the song describes
an older kind of foolishness than his own — a man fifty-one years deep in
his own weaknesses, not a man who has only just begun counting his.

Whose song is it? he asks.

Every port's got its own verse bolted on, Anders says. Bantam's is
Bantam's. You'll hear it here and nowhere else, and if you're fortunate
you'll never learn why firsthand.

Tom drinks. He does not consider this advice so much as colour — a story
a man collects the way he'll collect a scar or a whistle, evidence of a
life worth telling afterward. He has always wanted salt on his coat and
something to say that no one else in Wapping could say back. Anders's
song, to him, is simply proof the sea keeps its promises: it hands a man
exactly the sort of tale he came looking for.

He does not yet understand that a tale is not free merely because it is
offered.

***

## II. Sri

Three days later the factors have their price, and the *Hector* is set to
load and sail on the evening tide. Tom is sent ashore at midday with two
other hands to see a broken block replaced at the Dutch loge's cooperage
— an errand of an hour at most, and the boatswain's exact words, *an hour,
Fletcher, no more*, follow him down the gangplank and are, within that
hour, entirely forgotten.

The cooperage stands two lanes back from the roadstead. Between it and
the water is a house that keeps no sign, and in the doorway of it a woman
named Sri, who trades information the way Tom's own father once traded
rope — professionally, for a fair price, to whoever needs it — and who has
decided, watching him hesitate at her threshold with a repaired block
still under one arm, that an Englishman new enough to blush is worth an
hour of her time regardless of what he can pay.

She has three languages and uses all of them on him within a quarter
hour, mostly to laugh at how few he has in return. She tells him which
factor cheats his weights and which does not. She tells him a story about
a Company ship two seasons gone that he will spend the rest of his life
unable to verify or disprove. She asks whether he has ever seen the water
at Ternate, green enough, she says, to make an Englishman weep for shame
at his own grey river, and Tom — who has never seen anything but the
Thames and a millpond he once mistook for the ocean — understands, with
the particular vertigo of a young man discovering the world is larger
than the story he came to find in it, that this is precisely the
afternoon he signed his name to have.

He does not leave when the hour is spent. He tells himself ten minutes
more, the way a man always tells himself ten minutes more, and Sri, who
has watched a hundred sailors do this particular arithmetic and lose it
every time, does nothing to correct him. It is not unkindness. It is
simply not her ship.

***

## III. The Bell

The *Hector*'s bell reaches two lanes inland easily enough in the evening
quiet — once, to call the boats; twice, a quarter hour on, to call them
harder — and Tom hears both, and knows exactly what each one means,
because he has stood watch beside that same bell and rung it in earnest
for other men's negligence without once imagining himself on the wrong
side of it.

He thinks of Anders's song. He thinks, distinctly and with the total
confidence of a young man who has not yet been wrong about himself in any
way that mattered, that the song is a warning built for fools, and that a
fool is a category he does not belong to — merely a young man enjoying,
for one evening, the exact freedom the sea had promised him and the
Company had spent two years quietly taking back a shilling at a time. He
does not hear the bell as an order. He hears it as a story arriving at its
proper complication, the kind he will tell afterward, in some other room,
years from now, in a voice steadied by having survived it.

The third bell does not come. The *Hector* does not ring a courtesy she
has already extended twice to a man now a quarter hour overdue on an
errand of one hour entire; she puts up her boats, takes the land breeze on
her quarter, and goes.

Tom is on his feet and running before he has finished understanding why.
He reaches the strand in time to watch the ship become, in the space of
perhaps two hundred yards, no longer his ship — a shape on the evening
water attending to its own business, exactly as indifferent to him as the
tide that carries it, which has never once in the history of the world
paused to hear a young man's reasons.

Sri finds him still standing at the tideline some while later, and does
not laugh, which surprises him more than the leaving did.

You'll want a place to sleep, she says. And a plan by morning that isn't
this one.

He has, for the first time since Wapping, nothing at all to show her in
return.
