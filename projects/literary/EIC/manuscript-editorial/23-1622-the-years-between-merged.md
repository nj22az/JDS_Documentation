# 1614–1623: The Years Between

> **1614–1623**
>
> *“I ask whether he is alive. I do not ask whether he remembers me.”*
>
> — Maggie, keeper of the Pelican

## I. Paid Off

The sea never brings a man home alone.

It brings the dead in his mouth, the missing in his pauses and every officer he hated still giving orders inside the story. A crew pays off at Blackwall and reaches the Pelican by degrees: first the men who kept enough advance for ale, then the men whose wives have counted the wage, then the one who spent an hour outside the pay room arguing over a charge and needs to tell somebody who knows the price of an empty number.

By 1614 the order is settled: Maggie waits until they have eaten.

Hunger edits toward the next plate. Ale edits toward courage. She gives a returning man stew, bread and one pint, then asks the same questions in the same order.

Which ship?

Who did not come off it?

Names.

After the names, she asks about the living.

The questions after food are smaller than the questions before it.

Not what great weather they survived. What they ate when the salt beef stopped surrendering to the pot. Who slept beneath the leaking seam, mended another man's shirt, cheated at cards or still picked onions from the common bowl. The Company asks what labour came home. Maggie asks how the men lived while they were still ordinary enough not to know which of them would.

One November evening three paid-off hands arrive together and stop just inside the room, not from fear but because each expects one of the others to choose the table. A year of answering the same orders has made choice feel like work. Maggie points them to the long bench, sets down one pot and three bowls, and cuts the loaf before any man can say he has coin.

The oldest pushes his bowl away when he sees peas.

I cannot look at them, he says.

Then look at the bread.

He does. He eats half the loaf while the others empty the pot. Maggie brings him broth from the smaller pan and asks no question about waste. A man home from the sea may refuse one thing without being called ungrateful for the rest.

The youngest still holds his spoon as though another hand will claim it between mouthfuls. He finishes first and keeps scraping after the bowl is clean. The third man puts aside every piece of carrot, arranging them along the rim by size. His mates watch him do it and begin laughing before Maggie understands.

Every mess, the oldest says. Fair weather, foul weather, fever through half the lower deck. He sorts the carrots.

They are not all the same, the man says.

They are all carrots.

That is why God did not make you a purser.

The laughter is not loud. It does not need to be. The joke has crossed two oceans in a wooden bowl and reached Wapping without an officer improving it. Maggie lets it run until the youngest asks whether there is more. She fills his bowl. Only then does she ask the ship.

They give it. She asks who did not come off.

This time the names arrive without speeches. One man lost to the flux before the Cape. One taken from the foretop by a line that carried away clean. Two buried on an island while the ship watered. A boy who seemed to mend, asked for biscuit, and died before the cook had soaked it soft enough for him. The oldest gives each surname if he knows it. The carrot-sorter supplies a parish for one and the name of a sister for another. The youngest knows the boy only by the place his hammock hung.

What was he like awake? Maggie asks.

The youngest looks startled.

The boy.

Sang badly.

What song?

All of them.

The oldest says the boy tied his shoes together round a stanchion every night after a rolling sea stole one. The third remembers that he saved the burnt skin from the porridge pot and claimed it tasted of Sunday bread. None of these facts assists a widow's claim. Maggie keeps them in the room anyway.

She learns the voyage by asking about habits no manifest keeps. Who wakes before every watch? Who can mend a cuff without looking? Who sings the fourth line wrongly until the whole mess adopts it? Which man gives away his onions and steals every carrot? Three sailors correct one another until a person appears where a rating stood.

The ordinary work matters because kindness hides there without acquiring a witness. A topman goes aloft twice for a hand shaking with fever. A cook saves the bottom of the barley pot for the boys and calls it burnt. Another hand silently takes a sick man's turn at the pump, then denies he did more than wanted doing.

Maggie asks who did those things. Often nobody knows. A ship can remember an order by the man who shouted it and forget the hand that carried it out.

When the three men have finished, the youngest gathers the bowls and carries them to the bar without being asked. The oldest folds the unused end of the loaf in the cloth it came in. The carrot-sorter slips his row back into the pot when he thinks Maggie is not looking, unwilling to eat them and equally unwilling to leave food visible on a table.

They have been ashore six hours. Their bodies are still keeping a mess.

Maggie gives them one pint each. Now ale may edit what remains, but hunger has already told its honest part.

Tom's name reaches her in the summer of 1614 from a topman who served the homeward passage. Fletcher, the sailor calls him, because shipboard rank makes surnames useful. Boatswain. Scar through the brow. Brought the ship round the Cape under a jury mast and took a paymaster by the collar when the books swallowed a boy's wage.

Alive? Maggie asks.

Alive when I left him.

Where?

At the Company's door.

Going in or coming out?

The sailor considers. In.

Maggie puts another piece of bread on his plate. She does not ask whether Tom spoke of the Pelican. She does not ask whether he touched the scar while deciding. Those questions would make the sailor invent kindness to pay for his meal.

News is expensive enough without buying the false part.

* * *

## II. Jack

Jack comes from Deptford three months later.

The boy's thin coat has been replaced by a rigger's short jacket, shiny at both elbows and honest about hard use. He is broader. Two fingers on the right hand close crookedly around his cap. The sixteen-year-old who left the Pelican knew three knots. This man smells of tar, warmed hemp and the low shed where he is learning the rest.

Maggie puts mutton broth before him.

I have money, he says.

Then you may pay after.

He eats before she asks anything. Jack understands the order better now.

Are you going again?

No.

Not even for better wages?

Especially not for better wages.

She looks at the hand. Jack turns it palm down. Maggie lets him. A person may refuse a question before it is spoken.

The account? she asks.

Closed.

Properly?

It took some opening first.

Tom?

Some.

Jack says no more. He has not come to make one man stand trial in front of another man's absence. He tells her he has a month's place with a Deptford rigger and a fixed tool charge. At the end the tools become his. He made the master put that on the page.

Maggie gives one small nod.

Did Tom send you?

No.

Did he give you a message?

No.

The second no costs Jack more. Maggie sees him prepare to add something merciful and stops him by carrying away the bowl.

He knows the road, she says.

Jack comes back at intervals after that. Sometimes Carter has sent him to inspect a hoist. Sometimes a Deptford boatman has given him a word for somebody in Wapping. Once he comes because the Pelican's sign rope is chafed and he has noticed from the road.

He takes the old line down, splices a new eye and charges Maggie the proper rate after she refuses friendship's price.

The sign hangs true.

The crooked fingers hold.

Neither of them calls that an account closed.

* * *

## III. A Man in Pieces

Other crews bring Tom back in pieces.

One sailor says he is the hardest boatswain east of the Cape. Another says he delays a cargo boat because local hands read danger on the Surat bar, and the boat sent out against their judgement overturns before noon. A third swears Tom can see private trade through a sealed provision chest. A fourth calls him Company to the marrow and means it as praise.

Did he sell the men's ration? Maggie asks.

No.

Take their advance?

No.

Strike them?

When they were slow.

Slow from fear or slow from hunger?

The sailor has not divided men that way. Maggie makes him try.

In 1619, a warehouse hand newly paid off remembers twelve new boys arriving at Surat two years earlier, thin from the passage. The factor begins examining them for work. Tom stops the examination and orders food.

Before he asked what they could do? Maggie says.

Yes.

Before he put them on a ship?

No. Ten went aboard after.

Then keep both parts.

The man looks offended. I only said he fed them.

And now you have said what the feeding was for.

Maggie is not collecting evidence for Tom's acquittal. That is what paid-off men think at first. They see her stop wiping when his name comes, see the blue cotton folded on a high shelf beneath the counter, too fine for the room and too carefully kept to be forgotten, and decide they can purchase favour with a good report.

She makes them give the bad part too.

The hostile men make the opposite mistake. They think one hard order will buy them a free pint if they describe the boatswain as a tyrant. Maggie makes them name the weather, the rope and the men under it. Sometimes the order remains cruel after the facts. Sometimes it becomes the only one that brought a deckhand home.

She keeps both.

The Company sends fair copies. The sea sends contradictions.

Maggie trusts the contradictions more.

* * *

## IV. The Name Missing

In 1620 a guard newly home tells the room about a stolen bale of calico that was never stolen.

He remembers the English factor who wanted a porter punished, the two tallies that disagreed and Tom laying the pages together until one missing stroke returned the whole bale to the warehouse. He remembers the new double count afterward because it shortened his watch.

What was the porter's name? Maggie asks.

The guard does not know.

You watched them hold him.

I watched the gate.

What did they call him?

Porter.

And the man who found the error?

Fletcher.

There is the Company's arithmetic even inside a sailor's memory: the English officer keeps his name; the man almost punished becomes his labour.

Maggie gives the guard no second pint until he remembers more. A red headcloth. A room near the west warehouse. Four seasons at the landing. A first sound, perhaps Yus or Yusuf, offered without confidence.

She does not cut the name into the bar. A doubtful word does not become true because oak is harder than air. She says Yusuf once, gives it to the room for the night and lets uncertainty stand beside it.

Jack is there, delivering a sling for Carter's cask hoist. He listens from the short end of the counter.

Tom saved him, he says when the guard has gone.

For how long?

Jack looks at her.

That is an unfair question.

Most exact ones are.

The new tally prevents another false charge. It also moves more cloth. The correction and the use arrive in one story, as they so often do now when Tom's name travels west.

Jack turns his ale between both hands.

He was like that aboard, he says. Fix the rope and the ship goes faster into the same weather.

Maggie could ask then. Did he say my name? Does he hate me? Why did he choose the Company again when the road west was open?

She asks whether Jack has enough work for winter.

He does.

She feeds him anyway.

* * *

## V. No Letter

By 1620 Tom is easier to locate and harder to know.

His procedures travel on ships that do not carry him. Double tallies. Raised cotton platforms. Water allowances copied in a boatswain's hard hand. Men who have never served under him complain about his rules in the Pelican. Men who owe their full wage to one of those rules defend him with equal ignorance.

Maggie asks whether he is alive.

The answer keeps being yes.

She does not ask when he is coming home.

The Company packet office is less than an hour's walk. For a fee and the right ship she could send a page to Surat. Jack offers once to find a clerk who will write it plainly. Maggie refuses before he finishes.

What would it say? he asks.

The door remains where it was.

That sounds like asking him back.

It is not.

What, then?

The truth.

Jack waits.

Maggie wipes the counter. A truth sent for the purpose of producing an answer is already becoming a bargain. She put Tom out because he had learned to call the taking of a boy inevitable and his own place in it kindness. If he returns because loneliness has made the sentence cheaper, neither of them will know what has been repaired.

So she sends nothing.

No page comes from him.

Absence therefore remains equal on both sides, which is not the same as equal cost.

Packet days announce themselves in the room. Sailors carry folded pages for wives, mothers, creditors and women whose names they will not say aloud. Maggie keeps letters behind the bottles until their owners come. She never asks whether one arrived without a name for her. A clerk who had such a page would know the house. Every packet that empties without it is answer enough, and not an answer at all.

* * *

## VI. The Next Handhold

Early in 1623 a paid-off sailor freezes just inside the Pelican door.

The room is warm. Nothing threatens him. His body has mistaken the dark entrance for a companionway on a leaking ship, and for several breaths he cannot decide whether to come in or run.

Maggie does not touch him.

The hearth is six steps, she says. Can you walk that far?

He looks at her.

Yes.

Then that is all you are doing.

He reaches the fire. She puts bread and stew into his hands and waits until the shaking leaves enough of them to hold the spoon.

The sentence was Tom's. The sailor heard it from him in another doorway after an urgent order became too large to obey. Tom made him name the line, name where it led and take only the next part. Later he made the boy tie the coil again.

Then eat, the sailor says. That was his next order after the handhold. Eat.

Did he strike you?

No.

Did he praise you?

The sailor smiles into the bowl. One word. Cost him more than the food.

Maggie turns away to draw ale. The counter does not need wiping, but she wipes it.

Jack comes in before the sailor leaves. He has a wife now, mentioned not as news but as the person who says he must stop carrying wet rope against his good coat. He hears the end of the story and looks at Maggie.

Anything to send east? he asks.

No.

Anything if he comes west?

Maggie looks toward the door. Tom stood against it once, repaired its hinge once and last passed through it with his mother's thimble on the stones behind him. The wood has held every version.

Food first, she says.

Jack nods. It is not a message. He does not carry it.

By year's end the sea has sent Maggie a hundred sentences about Tom. Not one is from Tom. She lodges none beside Bell's page. Hearsay is not a true copy, however badly the heart wants to keep it.

The blue cotton remains folded beneath the counter. The thimble remains inside the fault. His old notch darkens with the bar.

The silence is intact.

* * *

The same nine years run a second account, on the other side of the ocean, in a hand Maggie has never seen.

## VII. The Second Desk

The second teak desk is smaller than the first.

Maria orders it in 1614 from the same joiner who built the heavy one in her counting room. He remembers the woman who rejected a rocking corner and tests this top before she arrives. Four square legs. One drawer. No carving.

Where will it stand? he asks.

Amboyna.

He looks toward the roadstead as though the island might be visible beyond the ships. It is not. Amboyna is a name moving west in bales and east in orders: cloves, soldiers, rice, powder, debt. A place may be absent from the horizon and still occupy half a counting house.

The desk goes aboard a Javanese trader with two packets of paper, a chest of plain cotton and a letter to a woman Maria has never met. The woman's name is Aminah. Her brother carries cloves between Hitu and the market at Ambon; her husband died owing money to three merchants, each of whom owns a different account of the sum. Aminah speaks Malay and the island tongues Maria does not. She reads the figures the Portuguese leave behind and distrusts every seal she has not watched being pressed.

Iskandar found her by sending the same disputed receipt east by two ships and asking who returned it corrected.

Both copies came back in Aminah's hand.

Maria's letter offers a monthly rate, paper at cost and half the fee on work Aminah originates. It requires two copies of every page. One travels by the quickest safe ship. The other stays behind the false bottom of the new desk.

Aminah sends no answer in words. Four months later the first packet returns: clove weights from three villages, Dutch advances against the next harvest, Portuguese prices whispered at Banda and the names of two shipmasters taking private cargo beneath Company stores. Every figure is copied once in Malay and once in the merchant's own language. Corrections are crossed through, not scraped away.

At the bottom Aminah has written her rate.

It is higher than Maria offered.

Maria pays it.

The next desk goes to Makassar. That one belongs to a Chinese bookkeeper in Makassar who writes a narrow merchant's Malay and five kinds of number. The third is not a desk but an oilskin box under the floor of a Gujarati broker's godown at Masulipatam. A Tamil ship-chandler keeps another packet in a rice jar nobody in his household would mistake for rice. In Patani, a widow who supplies sailcloth remembers reports instead of storing them until two different ships have carried the words away.

Maria does not own these people. That is the first rule.

She buys their work. She writes the term. She pays when the page arrives, including when the news has become useless during the voyage. Information that loses a race with the monsoon is still labour. Companies forget this whenever delay gives them an excuse to keep the coin.

The pages begin to cross on her teak.

A Portuguese captain buys rice at one port after denying a shortage at another. A Dutch merchant orders more chains in the same month he promises a village its growers will remain free. An English factor complains that cloves cannot be had and quietly asks what a private parcel would cost. No single page proves the shape. Laid together, they show it.

Iskandar comes in one morning and finds string stretched across the room.

One end is pinned beside a copy from Amboyna. Another reaches a freight list from Makassar. A third joins an English order to a Dutch insurance account. The string does not mark sea routes. It marks agreements men believe are separate because they signed them in separate rooms.

You have made a fisherman's net, he says.

A net closes around the thing inside it.

Does this not?

Maria looks at the strings. No. It shows where the thing may pass.

What thing?

Power.

Iskandar considers the answer and points to the cord running from Amboyna.

And who holds that end?

For now, Maria says, Aminah.

* * *

## VIII. The Price of One Tree

The Dutch do not want cloves. They want the sentence *all the cloves* to become true.

The difference arrives gradually in Maria's papers and all at once in the villages. Contracts become quotas. Quotas become inspections. Trees outside the approved ground become unlawful trees, as though a root can trespass against a charter signed on another continent. Where growers sell elsewhere, soldiers come with axes. Where island leaders object, the next agreement contains fewer island names.

Aminah records what the official reports call pacification.

Twenty-seven trees cut on one holding. A storehouse opened under guard. Two brothers taken for questioning and one returned. Rice withheld until a village produces the man accused of selling ten katties of cloves to Makassar. The reports give no speeches. Aminah has learned that adjectives are where a hostile reader begins his attack. She gives dates, weights, names and who was standing close enough to see.

Maria sells some of the information to English factors.

She knows what they want. Not the growers' freedom. Entry. A share. Proof the Dutch monopoly can be broken wide enough for an English ship to pass through. English outrage grows sharpest where English cargo has been excluded.

She raises the rate.

An English chief protests that the information concerns Dutch abuses.

Does that make your silver less heavy?

It makes us the natural buyer.

There is no natural buyer. Only the buyer named on the page.

He pays. Maria sends half east to Aminah and divides the remainder among the hands whose reports made the pattern visible. The chief assumes he has purchased a source. What he has purchased is one reading.

The same week, Maria refuses a Dutch request for the names behind it.

We pay you, the Dutch merchant says.

For stated work.

This is Company security.

Then the Company should have written secrecy into the first agreement.

The merchant puts both palms on the teak. You cannot trade between us forever.

Maria looks at his hands until he removes them from her desk.

No, she says. Nothing is forever. That is why the term has an end date.

In 1621 the packets from Banda stop.

The first missed ship means weather. The second means fear. The third brings a Dutch list of confiscated property and an English letter full of words such as outrage, massacre and opportunity. None names the woman who remembered reports in Patani or the men who carried Banda figures through Makassar. Maria lays the pages side by side and finds six familiar hands absent.

A Company report gives totals without people. Villages reduced. Leaders removed. Islands secured.

Maria takes out the earlier copies and writes the missing names on a separate sheet. She cannot prove every death. She will not turn absence into certainty because certainty would make a stronger sentence. Beside each name she writes the last date on which that hand was known alive.

The page has no buyer.

She pays for it herself.

* * *

## IX. What Distance Costs

The network acquires a reputation before it acquires a name.

Men come asking for Maria's correspondents as though they are ships she may charter out. A Dutch factor wants a hand inside an English warehouse. An English chief wants the price paid to a harbour clerk at Makassar. A Portuguese captain wants to know which Javanese master carries Dutch letters without entering them on his freight list.

Maria offers information. They ask for people.

The distinction irritates them.

If the report is sound, the Dutch factor says, why conceal who made it?

Because the report must arrive again next month.

Then you expect us to trust an unnamed source.

You trust the weights because they agree with the cargo. Trust the page the same way.

He calls this evasive. Maria calls it the rate and waits until he chooses which word costs him less.

Distance protects the hands, but it also eats them.

A boat overturns in the Sunda Strait with two months of the Makassar bookkeeper's figures sealed inside a bamboo tube. The tube comes ashore. The boy carrying it does not. Maria knows his name only because the bookkeeper enters courier fees precisely: Rafi, seventeen, half before sailing and half owing to his mother.

Iskandar takes the coin to the kampung himself.

The mother asks whether her son died for the Dutch or the English.

Neither, Iskandar says.

Then who?

He tells Maria later that he could not answer. The boy died for weather, for wages, for a packet belonging to merchants who will never know what his face looked like. Every answer makes the death smaller or makes their work larger than it is.

Maria enters the remaining half under Rafi's full name.

Not courier, she says.

The account needs a purpose.

The account has one. Payment owed.

Iskandar watches her write. You say paper does not replace power.

It does not.

But you keep writing the names.

Yes.

Why?

Maria sands the line and returns the excess to the pot.

Because power should have to step over them.

The network changes after Rafi. No packet travels alone when two routes are available. No courier carries both a report and the key needed to read it. Fees due to families are paid before the next commission, not after. The rules make every page slower and more expensive.

Clients complain.

Maria raises the rate.

She also learns the other cost of distance: a person far away may use her name for something she would refuse nearby.

A Portuguese merchant in Makassar produces a letter guaranteeing his honesty under Maria's seal. The seal is a good copy. The promise is not. He has bought pepper on credit from three growers and put the same expected cargo against all three debts.

Maria could deny him by letter. By the time denial arrives, the pepper will be loaded and the growers will hold three papers against one ship.

She goes herself.

The passage takes twelve days outward and nine back. She spends two weeks putting the three accounts on one table, makes the merchant sell a parcel of cloth to cover the shortage and leaves each grower with the same witnessed schedule. The settlement costs Maria more than the false guarantee earned him.

You might have let them discover the forgery, Iskandar says when she returns.

They did discover it.

They might have discovered it was not yours.

That is a different discovery.

She has a new seal cut. Not more elaborate. More inconvenient. The mark now includes one tiny broken line no copyist would preserve unless told it matters. The Makassar bookkeeper knows the break. Aminah knows it. Four other hands know it. Nobody is told all the places where it must appear.

Trust, Maria understands, is not one rope drawn tight across the sea. One cut would finish it. Trust is an intersecting web: each strand proves where another has held, and no single hand owns the whole.

* * *

## X. The English Hand

Daniel Vale first crosses Maria's desk as a correction.

The year is 1622. An English account from Amboyna charges a parcel of rice twice, once to the factory and once to a private mess. Aminah's clerk has marked the duplication in Malay. The English copy carries a cramped note along the margin:

*Not received into our store. Ask Mr Vale, who witnessed the unloading.*

Beneath it stands a small D and a surname written hard enough for the nib to catch.

Maria has seen thousands of names. Most pass under her hand once and return to being other people's lives. She copies this one because the dispute requires it.

Daniel Vale.

The answer comes two ships later. Vale confirms that six sacks went directly to the sickhouse after rain entered the lighter. He gives the names of the two porters, the condition of the seals and the hour the cargo came ashore. He does not improve the English factory's case. He does not improve the Dutch warehouse's. He states where the rice went.

Maria circles the name.

Why him? Iskandar asks.

He names the porters.

Honest men name witnesses?

Men who expect a page to be challenged do.

She sends the corrected account onward. With it she sends a question: whether Mr Vale will copy future disputed landings before surrendering the originals to either factory.

His reply is cautious. He has Company duties. He cannot undertake private employment prejudicial to his masters. The sentence is the English Company's sentence. Below it, in smaller writing, he lists the cost of paper at Amboyna and asks whether duplicates may be entered as an office expense.

Maria smiles without showing Iskandar.

Tell him no, she says. If his masters pay for the copy, his masters own the copy.

What do I tell him instead?

The rate.

Daniel's hand appears irregularly among Aminah's packets through the following year. Warehouse landings. Japanese soldiers' rice allowance. Repairs charged to the English factory for a roof the Dutch also claim to have repaired. His pages are not secret intelligence. They are ordinary accounts made dangerous by being exact.

Once he sends only a question.

*If two true reports name different owners of the same store, which report do you keep?*

Maria writes beneath it:

*Both. Ownership is the dispute. Do not edit it out.*

She makes a duplicate before the answer goes east.

* * *

## XI. Closed Water

Early in 1623, the Amboyna packets change.

Ships are searched twice. Letters arrive with seals that have been warmed and pressed back crooked. Aminah begins writing market figures across harmless family messages, then sends the key by another vessel. Dutch orders speak of vigilance. English letters speak of insult. Japanese mercenaries at the fort appear in both sets of paper: wages in one, suspicion in the other.

Maria stretches new string across the counting room.

An order for extra lamp oil. A request for a Japanese interpreter. A prohibition on boats leaving after dark. A list of English stores copied by a Dutch hand. Alone, each page is administration. Together, they are a door being bolted.

She writes to Aminah: leave the second desk. Take the copies that name people. Send nothing through the fort.

The warning leaves on a Makassar prahu whose master has carried her paper for nine years. A contrary wind holds him east of Java. By the time his vessel reaches Amboyna, the desk is empty.

For six weeks, nothing comes back.

Then three accounts arrive on the same afternoon.

The English version says its factors have been seized on a fabricated conspiracy with Japanese soldiers. The Dutch version says a plot against the castle has been discovered and confessed. A harbour list records English prisoners transferred from the fort to other custody. The names are abbreviated, the condition of the men omitted.

Maria reads all three before she touches the food Iskandar has brought.

Eat, he says.

After.

Before. A dead broker reads nothing.

It is an old rule between them: food before argument, food before bad news, food before any decision a frightened body will pretend is reason. Maria eats three spoonfuls of rice while the papers wait. Then she draws the harbour list close.

Towerson is there. So are men whose hands she knows from English freight accounts. The Japanese names have been reduced to marks beside numbers. Daniel's name is not among the condemned.

It is not among the released.

Maria checks the second Dutch sheet. At the bottom, below powder issued and a boat ordered provisioned, stands a line entered by a junior clerk who expects nobody outside the castle to care:

*One English factor retained for examination and onward account. D. Vale. To Batavia by first secure passage.*

The line has no reason attached. No judgement. No mercy. Daniel has become movement between two columns.

Iskandar reads it over her shoulder.

You know him?

His hand.

That is not the same.

No.

Maria takes out Daniel's question about the two true reports. Beneath her answer, his cramped acknowledgement is still visible: *Both retained.*

She lays it beside the transfer line.

Around them, the counting room holds eleven years of crossings. Amboyna to Makassar. Makassar to Bantam. English store to Dutch fort. Rice to sickhouse. A witness into a ledger. Every strand is ordinary until somebody needs to know where it leads.

Maria removes the string pinned beside Amboyna and fixes its loose end beneath one word on the wall.

Batavia.

Then she begins another page.
