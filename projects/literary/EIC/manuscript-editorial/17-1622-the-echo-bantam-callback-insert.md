# Proposed insert — 17-1622-the-echo, Section III "Shore Leave"

## Why here

The deployed chapter already establishes, without explaining, that Tom
cannot simply rest ashore: he wakes before the watch changes out of habit,
bolts and then unbolts his door because a barrier makes the room feel
smaller, and — the specific line this insert targets — arranges his seat
so he can watch two directions at once. The chapter's own closing lines
list "door" as one of the things "the sea carried home in him," without
ever saying why a door specifically. If the Bantam interlude is approved,
this is the one sentence in Book One that most directly wants a root
under it.

## The deployed text, exactly as it stands (excerpt)

> He finds the cobbler by the boots hanging beneath an awning. The shop is
> open to the lane: a low bench, knives, lasts, pegs, a pot of blacking
> gone soft in the heat. The cobbler takes one look at the loose sole and
> holds out a hand for the other boot.
>
> Only this one, Tom says.
>
> Both.
>
> The right is sound.
>
> The cobbler bends it once. Three stitches lift along the welt.
>
> Both, he says again.
>
> Tom understands enough of the local trade speech to buy rope, water and
> a day's labour. Boots have not been necessary to that education. He
> points to the sun, then to the work, asking how long.
>
> The cobbler points to noon.
>
> Tom looks at the lane behind him. A man without boots cannot choose a
> direction quickly. He removes the left, then the right, and places them
> side by side on the bench rather than surrendering them directly. The
> cobbler gathers them without comment and points to a stool beneath the
> awning.
>
> **The stool faces the street. Tom turns it until he can also see the
> rear door.**
>
> The cobbler cuts the old thread. He does not hurry and does not explain.

## Proposed insertion (new paragraph, placed immediately after "the rear
door." and before "The cobbler cuts the old thread.")

> A door he cannot see past is not a fear he has ever given a name to. It
> is simply an old arithmetic, learned once in a cell with a slit too high
> to reach and too narrow to matter, where a man who could watch the door
> at least knew which direction trouble would arrive from. Seventeen years
> have not undone the lesson. They have only taught him to make it look,
> from the outside, like a man simply choosing where to sit.

## Notes

- Deliberately does not name Bantam, the qadi, or the killing. A reader
  who has read the interlude will recognize the cell instantly; a reader
  who hasn't will read it as one more piece of Tom's established
  guardedness — consistent with the book's rule that these connections
  are seasoning, not required knowledge.
- No other line in the existing chapter needs to change. This is a single
  paragraph, one insertion point, fully reversible.
- Contingent on the Bantam interlude itself being approved — this insert
  has no independent reason to exist otherwise.
