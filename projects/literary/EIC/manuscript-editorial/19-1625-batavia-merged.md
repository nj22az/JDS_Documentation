# 1623–1626: Batavia

> **1623**
>
> *“The word arrived before the dead did. The dead never arrived.”*
>
> — Tom Fletcher, Surat, 1623

## I. The Word

Amboyna reaches Surat inside a sealed packet.

The ship carrying it has been expected for pepper prices, sailing orders and private letters. Men wait at the landing asking after brothers, debts and promotions. The packet clerk brings the official bundle ashore first, protected beneath waxed cloth while sailors' bags remain in the boat under rain.

The chief factor opens the seal.

He reads standing. Halfway through, he sits.

Nobody in the office asks what has happened. Rank teaches men to wait until bad news has been given a usable shape.

The chief reads the first page again, then the second. His lips move over one word.

Amboyna.

The word passes through the room without information attached. Tom knows the island: cloves, Dutch fort, small English factory, Japanese soldiers in Dutch pay, boats moving between places European maps draw larger than they are. He knows Towerson's name from cargo instructions. He has never met him.

How many? Tom asks.

The chief factor looks at him as though the question has crossed a boundary before permission.

Ten English.

Dead?

Executed.

For what?

Conspiracy against the Dutch castle.

Did they conspire?

The chief looks down at the packet.

They confessed.

That was not the question.

No, the chief says. It was not.

Outside the office, a clerk tells another clerk. A porter hears. By noon the market knows the English say Dutchmen killed English merchants on a clove island. By evening the story has acquired cannon, a siege and fifty dead. None of these travelled in the packet. Horror dislikes an empty column.

Tom does not repeat the number until he sees the list.

* * *

## II. Three Accounts

The packet contains three versions.

The Dutch account is cleanest.

English factors and Japanese mercenaries planned to seize the castle. Suspicion led to lawful examination. Confessions agreed. Sentence followed under local authority. The writing uses order as proof: discovery, questioning, confession, judgement. Each step stands behind the next so the reader cannot remove one without appearing to attack the whole structure.

The English account is a letter copied in haste before the factory closed. No plot. Arrests at night. Men held apart. Questions whose answers were decided in advance. Water.

The word appears once.

*They used water until the men would own any design put to them.*

Tom touches the scar above his eyebrow.

He reads the sentence again.

The third account is commercial.

It lists English property sealed by Dutch officers, debts likely unrecoverable, stores requiring reassignment and the effect upon clove supply. It was written after the first reports and before the bodies were cold enough for certainty. The final paragraph recommends strengthening English positions where defence is practical and reducing exposure where the Dutch monopoly makes small factories vulnerable.

Surat. Masulipatam. Cotton.

The chief factor finds Tom reading that page.

This is what London will want first, he says.

Before the names?

London will have the names.

First?

The chief is not a cruel man. Cruelty would make the exchange simpler.

Ships move before grief does, he says. If we do not alter the orders now, more men may be stranded.

Tom knows the logic. Weather does not pause because a sailor died. A damaged stay must be replaced before mourning because the next death is already loading it. He has used necessity to move men away from bodies.

The chief is right.

The page is still obscene.

Both facts stand.

Tom lays the commercial account beneath the other two rather than above them.

Paper order is not justice. It is the only correction available in the room.

* * *

## III. What the Company Does

The Company copies Amboyna.

One copy for London. One for the western factories. One for the ship commanders. Extracts for agents whose work may be affected. A private caution for English merchants dealing under Dutch guns. A shorter version for men not trusted with the names.

Every copy changes the event by choosing what the recipient needs.

Shipmasters receive the warning about Dutch searches and armed forts.

Factors receive the property losses.

Investors will receive the injury to English trade.

Sailors receive a rumour.

Families receive nothing yet because confirmation must precede alarm.

The dead are confirmed enough to redirect cargo and not confirmed enough to tell their wives.

Tom is ordered to inventory every English hand available for reassignment from the eastern stations. Boatswains, gunners, carpenters, clerks able to keep accounts in Portuguese, men who know the island passages. The chief wants readiness if London orders retaliation.

Or withdrawal.

Nobody uses that word.

Tom opens the personnel ledger.

Men become capacities. Able at guns. Experienced in surf. Malay enough for stores. Unfit after fever. Reliable. Difficult. The same categories he has used for years now stand beside Amboyna and reveal their full appetite.

He stops at one young factor newly arrived from England.

The man asks whether the executed Englishmen fought.

The account says there was resistance during arrest.

Then the Dutch had cause?

For arrest?

For concern.

Tom looks at him. Concern is a word power uses before it has chosen the act.

The factor wants to know whether he should accept a posting east.

Do you want it?

I want advancement.

That was not the question.

The echo belongs to Maggie. Tom gives it without explanation.

The man declines the posting.

The chief factor fills it the same afternoon.

One choice left in a man's hands does not stop the machine. It only changes which hand enters the slot.

At dusk the copying clerk brings Tom the extracts for inspection. He has made five versions from
the three accounts, each addressed to a different appetite.

The shipmasters' paper begins with the danger to English persons. The merchants' paper begins with
the seized goods. The paper for London begins with the affront to the nation and ends with a table
of losses so exact it makes rage look audited. A fourth, meant for the Company's servants in India,
asks for vigilance and restraint in the same sentence. The fifth is no more than six lines and will
be read aloud to the crews.

Tom reads it.

It says Englishmen were cruelly put to death after false accusation. It does not say how. It does
not mention the Japanese prisoners. It does not say that the men confessed, because a spoken
confession will travel farther than the means used to obtain it. It ends by assuring the sailors
that the Company will defend its faithful servants.

Will it? Tom asks.

The clerk looks toward the chief factor's closed door.

That is the form.

I asked whether it will.

I know what you asked.

The clerk is older than Tom had noticed. There is ink beneath all ten nails and a tremor in the
finger holding the page. His nephew serves at Masulipatam. The Company has not told him whether the
boy will be brought west or sent farther east to replace one of the dead.

Tom returns the extract.

Read the names before the assurance.

Those are not in the approved form.

Then approve them.

I cannot.

Tom could carry the paper through the door and make the chief answer. He can already feel the old
part of himself choosing the angle: shoulder first, voice low, no room left for retreat. Instead he
pulls a stool to the clerk's side.

Make two copies, he says. One as ordered. One with the names.

Which do we send?

Both.

That will be remarked upon.

Good.

They work by one lamp. The clerk writes while Tom checks the letters against the packet. When the
crew copy is finished, the ten English names stand between the accusation and the promise. The
Japanese remain a number because no other truth has reached the room.

Tom makes the clerk add: *and ten Japanese men whose names are not given in the account.*

It is not much. It is more than the form permits.

* * *

## IV. The Names

The list arrives two weeks after the first packet.

Towerson stands at the top.

Below him are nine English names with positions attached: factor, assistant, steward, servant. The positions shrink the men even while distinguishing them. Tom copies each name onto a separate page without the office.

The Japanese dead are counted, not named.

Ten Japanese mercenaries, the report says.

Tom asks the messenger whether another list exists.

Not in English hands.

The Dutch must have paid them.

Presumably.

Then there are names in a wage book.

Not in this packet.

Tom leaves ten blank lines below the English names.

The clerk objects that blank space invites later alteration.

That is what it is for.

Tom writes *names not yet recovered* beside the lines and signs beneath the note. Signs late. Signs after reading every word. The habit has become principle so gradually he cannot say where fear ended and judgement began.

A factor passing the desk sees the list.

What is that for?

The dead.

The official copy has them.

It has positions and a number.

What will yours do?

Nothing.

The answer surprises them both.

The page will not punish the Dutch, restore Towerson or give ten Japanese families a body. It may never leave Surat. Tom has learned from Maggie without seeing her: keeping a name is not justice, and calling it justice only steals from the larger debt.

He keeps the page anyway.

* * *

## V. Grief and Use

Within a month, Amboyna becomes an argument for cotton.

The chief factor does not say the massacre is useful. He says dependence on the spice islands is dangerous. He says the Dutch forts make English investment vulnerable. He says Indian cloth serves markets no single castle can close.

Every statement is true.

Orders expand. Brokers are summoned. Warehouse space is recalculated. Tom's double-tally procedure spreads to two new landings because more bales will pass through them. Men who curse the Dutch in the morning work harder by afternoon on the trade Amboyna has made urgent.

Tom hears Carter in a room eleven thousand miles away, though Carter has not yet heard Daniel speak: grief and use entering together, neither false, the second already consuming the first.

At the Surat table, the men drink to Towerson.

The chief pours Portuguese wine because nobody wants Dutch spirits. One merchant proposes never buying from a Hollander again. Another points out that half the ships carrying their letters east are Dutch or sail under Dutch passes. The vow contracts before the cup is empty.

Tom drinks water.

The young factor who declined the eastern posting asks what revenge would look like.

War, somebody says.

English forts.

Dutch blood.

A monopoly of our own.

The last answer is offered as a joke. Nobody laughs quickly enough.

Tom thinks of the rice thief at the warehouse door, Yusuf beside the false tally, Jack's half-wages disappearing between correct books. A system does not become just because the flag over it changes.

He touches the scar.

The factor asks what he would do.

Find out what happened.

We know.

We have three accounts.

They agree men died.

That is not the disputed part.

Later, in the warehouse, Tom watches the first consequence arrive in cloth.

Ratan's men bring blue cotton under tarred covers, twice the quantity ordered the month before.
The bales are set on the dry boards, opened, sampled and entered. One square carries a red climbing
flower close enough to the cloth beneath Maggie's bar that Tom's hand stops above it.

Ratan sees.

You know the pattern.

I know one like it.

English buyers ask for more now, Ratan says. They say the islands are closed.

Men are dead.

That also.

Ratan does not apologize. He has dyers to pay, printers waiting on carved blocks, carriers who must
eat whether English grief is pure or profitable. Tom has known him too long to mistake commerce for
indifference. Ratan touches the sampled edge.

If I refuse the order, will they live?

No.

If you refuse the cloth, will they live?

No.

Then we should make the account honest.

Together they reject three damp bales, correct two false weights and add the printers' rush fee as
its own line instead of burying it in the broker's charge. The dead remain dead. The living are
paid a little more plainly because of them.

Tom signs only after Ratan reads the total back.

On the quay, another factor tells him that Amboyna may be the making of Surat.

Tom turns so slowly the man hears his own sentence a second time.

I mean the trade here.

I know what you mean.

The factor retreats into talk of tonnage. Tom lets him. Striking him would cleanse one sentence and
leave the appetite that produced it untouched.

That night Tom writes no letter to Maggie. He places a clean sheet beside the Amboyna copies and
imagines telling her that ten men have died, ten others have died without names, and the warehouse
is busier. He cannot find an order for those truths that does not turn one into an explanation of
another.

He folds the blank sheet once and keeps it.

* * *

## VI. One Retained

The fourth account comes from Bantam in a packet without Company seal.

It is a copy of a harbour issue: provisions for prisoners transferred after the Amboyna examinations. Rice, water, guards, boat hire. The page has travelled through private merchant hands because official routes have begun swallowing anything that complicates the clean versions.

Tom reads the quantities first.

Enough rice for more men than the Dutch account says were held after sentence.

At the bottom is a note:

*One English factor retained for further examination and sent under guard to Batavia.*

No name.

No condition.

No explanation of why one man enters a boat while the others enter graves.

The chief factor says the note may be an error.

Then we correct it.

How?

Ask Batavia.

Through whom? The Dutch authorities accused our factory of conspiracy and executed the men responsible for answering letters.

Private ships still go.

Private ships carry private risk.

Tom looks at the page. Somewhere east, one man may be alive because a clerk chose *retained* instead of *condemned*. Or dead because the next page has not reached them. The difference is an entry moving between offices.

He thinks of the unsigned letter beneath his mother's death notice. Paper kept from travelling can imprison the living as completely as paper sent.

The chief tells him London will inquire.

How long?

Months.

Tom puts two fingers above his eyebrow.

The chief recognizes the gesture now.

What are you proposing?

Nothing yet.

This is true. It is also the answer Tom gives when action has begun before he can name it.

He makes a copy of the transfer note.

On his private list, beneath Towerson and the nine English dead, beneath the ten blank Japanese lines, he adds one more:

*English factor. Name unknown. Batavia.*

The word has travelled from Amboyna to Surat.

Now Tom begins travelling toward the word.

* * *

## VII. The Unclosed Entry

The desk that was too heavy for one man to take crosses the water in a Company hull, freight paid at Maria's own rate.

Six men carry it up from the Batavia quay. The joiner who built it is twenty years and a sea away; the top still does not rock. She sets it in a ground-floor room within sight of the castle, near enough that the castle's clerks can reach her without deciding the walk means anything, and stretches the old string across the new walls. One cord she pins exactly as it travelled: its far end beneath a copy of a harbour list, its near end beneath a clerk's transfer line.

*One English factor retained for examination and onward account. D. Vale.*

The entry is two years old now. Maria has watched it renew itself quarter by quarter in the castle's provision books — rice, water, guard wages, a surgeon's visit charged and initialled — and in all that time no examination has been attached to it. Nobody examines Daniel Vale. Nobody releases him. The entry has outlived its reason the way debts outlived their men in Harcourt's books, and for the same cause: closing it would require somebody to write down why it was opened.

She goes to the cells once, early, before she spends anything.

Names can be forged. She has forged enough to know. She stands in the visiting room with a castle clerk beside her and asks the thin Englishman to write his name, and watches the letters come cramped and hard into the page, the nib catching on the downstroke of the V exactly as it caught on a rice account four years ago. The hand is the one she circled. The man attached to it watches the pen the whole time it is in her fingers, and does not once look at the door.

You asked me a question once, she says in English. Which report to keep, when two true reports disagree.

The Englishman's eyes come up from the pen very slowly.

Both, he says. The voice is a ruin. You wrote: ownership is the dispute.

Do not edit it out, Maria says.

It is all the introduction either of them makes. It is enough. He knows her the way she knows him — by the writing, which is the only part of a person paper ever honestly carries.

After that she goes to work on the entry.

She does not ask the fiscal for mercy. Mercy is a favour, favours are debts, and a debt held by a powerful man is a leash. Instead she brings him arithmetic. The commissioners in Europe are pricing Amboyna between the two Companies, claim against counter-claim, and every quarter the castle feeds an Englishman it has neither tried nor released, its own provision book adds a line for England's lawyers to read aloud. A prisoner without a charge is not a prisoner. He is an unclosed entry, renewing itself against the Company's account.

Close it while closing is cheap, she says.

The fiscal reads the assembled pages twice. Released to whom? he asks. A body must be received. I will not have him drown in the roads and become a second claim.

An English signature and an English deck, Maria says.

Then find them, the fiscal says, and believes he has ended the matter.

She reads the roads. English hulls are rare at Batavia now; the English council keeps its house and its protests and sends its ships to Bantam. But one has come in at last, carrying letters too angry to trust to a Dutch bottom, and when Maria asks the harbour clerk who keeps her deck, the clerk shrugs and gives her the only name a deck requires.

The boatswain.

* * *

## VIII. The Claim

It has taken Tom fourteen months to travel toward one line of harbour ink.

He volunteers for the runs other men avoid, Surat to Bantam through a sea gone official with other people's guns, and at Bantam he volunteers again, and by the time the council's letters want carrying to Batavia there is no cheaper way to move them than the boatswain who keeps asking. Nobody wonders at it. The Company has learned that Tom goes where the work is worst, and has long since stopped looking for the reason, which suits the reason.

The English house at Batavia is a claim with rooms attached.

Clerks compute it daily: ten men, their wages, their goods, their seized stores, the insult to the nation entered under separate cover. The dead have become exact. Tom stands at a rail of ink and asks the question he has carried from Surat.

The retained factor. The one sent here under guard.

The council man does not look up. That matter is not helpful.

He may be alive.

That is what is not helpful about him. The man signed a Dutch confession. Produce him, and the Dutch produce the signature, and half the claim begins to argue with itself. Ten dead men make a massacre. Nine dead men and a witness under water make a proceeding.

He says it without cruelty, which is the worst of it. Harcourt's voice needed no cruelty either. Tom looks at the clean columns and understands that he has crossed half the world to stand in the same counting room he left, and that his own flag is flying over it.

He has corrected the machine for twenty years, one line at a time. He has never before heard his own side price a living man below a dead one and call the price patriotism.

Where is he held? Tom asks.

The council man finally looks up. Leave it, boatswain. The matter is being managed.

By whom?

That evening a boy stops him in the lane behind the godowns and puts a folded paper into his hand. No seal. No name. An hour, a street, a door — which in Batavia is a name, and the council's clerks have already taught him whose. There is a broker the castle listens to, they say, when they think he is not listening. A woman. She sets her own rate and the fiscal pays it.

Tom reads the paper twice, the habit of a man who signs late.

Then he goes.

* * *

## IX. The Counting Room

The room is long and low, strung with cords like rigging scaled for a chart table, and smells of ink, sandalwood and the harbour. Tom checks it from the threshold the way he checks every room, takes the doorway out of twenty-two years' habit — and stops, because behind a heavy teak desk, in dark silk that holds blue where the lamp finds it, sits a woman he has seen once in a dream he never told anyone, standing behind a bar she never stood behind, bolting a door from within.

She looks at him. He looks at her.

The scar through his eyebrow is new to her. The way he has put himself in the door is not. She has seen a man stand exactly so, half a head shorter than what was coming, bleeding and staying — and he has seen this stillness before, this way of watching a room decide, from behind a counter, beside a mallet, a lifetime ago, in the breath before one word.

One long look. Not love. Recognition.

Neither names it. He knows her name — it has lain under a bar in Wapping for twenty-two years in a dead man's hand — and he does not spend it. She never had his. A deck's name is enough, and she has been priced too often herself to price a man past what the work requires.

You are the boatswain, she says.

You are the broker.

Sit down, she says, and he does, and the night they are not speaking of sits down with them.

She lays two papers on the teak, squared to the edge. The first is Dutch: an order of release, sealed, wanting only the receiving signature. The second is her own — a passage account, terms exact — and where the passenger's name stands, Tom stops reading.

He takes his private list from inside his coat. Below Towerson and the nine, below ten blank lines, stands his last entry: *English factor. Name unknown. Batavia.* He borrows her pen without asking, crosses out three words and writes two.

Daniel Vale.

Maria watches the correction the way Maggie once watched a hinge repaired: as the nearest some people come to saying what they mean.

Your council would not give you the name, she says.

My council is adding him up.

I know. I read their sums. She turns the release a half-inch straighter. That is why the paper wants a deck and not a house. He is released to a signature, not to the claim. Once the claim has him, he becomes evidence, and evidence is kept, and I have seen what keeping does to that man.

What is your rate? Tom asks.

There is no rate. Some work I pay for myself.

He looks at her then, properly, and the question he cannot ask — *why* — stands in the room with all the other things standing there. Maria answers the askable part.

There is a room in London that reads accounts truly, she says. I have carried that fact twenty-two years. It is the most valuable thing I own, and I am spending it once. A tavern on Wapping Wall. The Pelican. When England lets him ashore, take him through that door and give him to the keeper.

I know the house, Tom says.

Yes, Maria says. I thought the deck might.

Nothing else is said about it. Nothing else needs surviving.

She rises, and from a shelf behind the desk takes a squat stone bottle, and uncorks it, and pours one measured pull into a cup, and sets it in front of him. Tom drinks. Juniper, fire, pine resin — his eyes water, and the woman in dark silk does not laugh, and for a moment the room is full of everyone who has ever handed this particular fire across this particular distance.

For the passage, she says, corking the bottle with her thumb. Courage enough to reach the river. After that he will need his own.

At the door Tom pauses, hand on the latch, and does not look back, and goes. Maria stands a while at the desk. Then she writes the evening's last entry in the book no clerk will ever audit, where the room in Wapping has stood as an asset for twenty-two years, and marks it, for the first time, partly drawn against.

One more thing crosses the water before the pens. Tom's list has kept ten blank lines above the entry he corrected — *names not yet recovered*, a boatswain's hand — and Maria read them for what they were, and said nothing. When the release comes down to the gate, a folded slip rides among the papers: ten names in a clerk's exact Dutch, no note attached, no fee entered. Tom writes them one by one into the spaces that were kept for them. He does not ask. She does not offer. The lines the account left open are people now, and neither of them will ever say why she could do it.

* * *

## X. The Man Who Reads

The release is an afternoon of pens.

A clerk reads the entry aloud, all eleven words, twice — once for the castle's book and once for the receiving signature — and Daniel stands between two guards in clothes somebody has found for him, cut for a bigger man, and watches the pen the way he watches all pens now, and waits to learn which column he is being moved to.

The receiving signature belongs to an English boatswain: a scarred, weathered man of middle years who fills the doorway of the office without seeming to notice he is doing it. The clerk pushes the paper at him, sand ready, in the manner of clerks everywhere — sign here, receive the person — and the boatswain does a thing Daniel has not seen done in three years, in two Companies, in any language.

He reads it.

All of it, to the end, at his own pace, while the sand waits and the guards shift and the afternoon creaks. Then the page above it. Then, only then, and slowly, he signs.

Daniel has watched many men sign. He has never once, since the coral room, watched a man read first, and something in him that has been braced since dawn — braced, if he is honest, since a schoolmaster's voice first said *session* — lets down by a fraction of an inch. Whoever this man belongs to, whatever ledger is moving him, he is not the pen's servant. The pen waits for him.

At the gate the boatswain steps into the doorway — Daniel's chest closes; a man in a doorway is a fact his body has opinions about — and then, catching himself on some instruction Daniel cannot see, steps aside, and leaves the opening empty, and waits.

Daniel does not move. An open door held open by a stranger is still a door and still a stranger.

The boatswain does not explain, or reassure, or use the word free, which would have finished it. He says: The boat is at the third stair. Can you walk that far?

Yes, Daniel says.

Then that is all we are doing today.

It is the exact size of a sentence a man can obey. Daniel walks it. On the deck a sailor swings the water dipper toward him and his hands begin before he can govern them — both hands, flat, shaking, the old animal shriek rising silent through his arms — and the scarred man takes the dipper away without hurry, as though it were a fouled line, and puts his own small beer in Daniel's grip instead, and sends the sailor forward. After that, no one swings the water dipper toward Daniel again, and no one says why not, and no one looks at him when he drinks.

The place given him aboard is not quite a cabin. A partition makes three walls beneath the quarterdeck; a light hinged screen supplies the fourth and is lifted aside whenever men carry stores through. There is a narrow berth, a chest whose owner has gone forward for the passage, and a chair that faces the closed screen.

Daniel stops at the opening.

The boatswain goes in first. He does not test the bed or make a show of finding danger absent. He takes the chair by its back, turns it until a seated man can see the opening and the strip of deck beyond, and wedges one rear leg beneath the chest batten so the roll will not turn it back. Then he leaves.

Daniel sits.

The first food comes before dusk. Rice, fish broth and a piece of shore bread lie on a board set within reach of the chair. The cup has a wooden cover fitted over it. No hand carries the meal into the enclosure. A sailor puts the board down outside, pushes it across with one foot and continues aft with a second bowl, as though Daniel's supper is one delivery among several and therefore contains no question.

Daniel waits until the deck has changed voices before he eats.

The broth is warm. He takes three spoonfuls, then bread, then the rest of the broth. The rice defeats him. It remains on the board when the sailor returns. The man looks at the bowl, looks toward the galley and takes it away without asking whether Daniel disliked it, whether he is ill, whether the Dutch fed him rice, whether a man released from three years of rooms ought to show more gratitude for food.

At morning the portion is smaller.

That is how the deck learns him: by subtraction without remark. Half a bowl. One piece of bread. Broth cloudy enough not to shine back at him. When the shore bread is gone, ship's biscuit is laid in the broth until the edge yields to the spoon. The covered cup appears on the left of the board each time, always in the same place, so his hand can find it before his eyes must.

At first the cup holds small beer. Later, when the beer sours and is not improved by being called beer, there is thin barley broth, water clouded with rice, or fresh water carried under the cover and left until he is alone. At Bantam the casks are filled again. The drink changes. The cup does not.

No order establishes this. Daniel listens for one. He listens for his name in the boatswain's voice, for the explanation that will turn the arrangement into a favour and the favour into a debt. He hears orders for braces, casks, watches, a boat warped closer under the quarter. He hears a sailor corrected for leaving a coil where a running foot can find it. He never hears himself discussed.

The men announce themselves through work. A hand comes for the borrowed chest and knocks once on the partition before entering. The sailmaker needs the deck space and tells Daniel which way the rolled canvas will pass. A boy sweeping under the berth pushes dust into a pile, sees Daniel's feet and says only lift, when you can. Nothing in the words asks him to be well.

On the third night the lantern outside the partition begins striking its bracket whenever the ship rolls. Iron against iron. A small, exact blow, pause, return. Daniel counts twenty-seven before he understands that he is counting. At thirty-four the boatswain arrives, takes down the lantern and carries it away.

Dark is not better.

Daniel sits with both hands on the chair and hears the deck continue without the light. Nobody comes to explain the removal. Some time later the boatswain returns with the catch newly pinned and a strip of leather between bracket and frame. He hangs the lantern, tests the swing once and goes. The light remains. The striking does not.

During foul weather the opening must be closed against spray. The first time a sailor reaches for the partition, Daniel is out of the chair before either of them knows he has moved. The sailor stops with the hook in his hand.

Rain coming aft, he says.

Daniel can see it beyond him, grey and hard.

Leave a hand's width, the boatswain says from somewhere on deck.

The sailor secures the screen with a gap at the hinge. Rain works through and darkens the floor. Daniel watches the narrow line of the deck through it until the squall passes. When the screen is opened again, no one congratulates him for having remained.

The passage makes no steady improvement. One day he eats at every issue and stands for an hour in the open. The next he cannot cross the partition and the food stays until flies find it. A week after that he walks to the rail, sees a sailor rinsing a bucket and returns so quickly he strikes his shoulder on the chair. The boatswain moves the bucket. He does not move the sea.

Daniel begins to understand that this is the limit and the mercy of the arrangement. The deck cannot remove water, doors, hands, orders or men standing over other men. It can put a cover on a cup. Turn a chair. Leave food within reach. Repair one striking lamp. Make the next necessary thing small enough to take without promising what comes after it.

A deck, it turns out, has better manners than a court.

He never asks who paid for him. He has assembled it, entry by entry, the only way he knows: the woman who verified his hand; the boatswain who reads before signing; two exact people, strung together across water by paper he cannot see. Somewhere in the world there is an account with his name in it, kept by hands that name their porters.

It is not freedom. It is the next thing to it: being carried by people who keep true books.

* * *

That night, in a ground-floor room within sight of the castle, Maria unpins one cord from her wall and rolls the string small. The wall keeps the pinhole. The account it belonged to is not closed — she has stopped believing accounts close — but it has moved, at last, out of the columns that were killing it, onto water, going west.

The man is the message. She has sent the room back one of its debts.

* * *

## XI. The Desk

The Company receives him at Surat the way it receives everything. It finds him a column.

Before the column, the ship must give him back.

At the outer roadstead the room beneath the quarterdeck is dismantled around him. The borrowed chest returns to its owner. The chair is pulled free of the batten and carried aft. A sailor lifts the screen from its hooks so cargo can pass, and in one movement Daniel's fourth wall becomes two boards in a man's arms. The covered cup goes to the galley. The strip of leather stays beneath the lantern catch, indistinguishable from every repair aboard.

Nothing that helped him has been entered as his property. Every part must remain with the ship.

Daniel watches the familiar arrangement become equipment again. He understood it was temporary. Understanding does not prevent his body from reading the cleared space as eviction.

Tom brings no reassurance. He gives Daniel the next action.

The ladder first. Then the boat. Nothing beyond it until we are aboard.

Daniel can see the ladder. That makes the order possible.

Men go down ahead with mail, the surgeon's box and two water casks needed ashore before the tide turns. Nobody waits beneath Daniel with arms raised. Tom descends first and sits where Daniel can see him, one hand on the gunwale, looking toward the river instead of upward. Daniel takes each rung at the speed his feet permit. The men loading the casks work around that speed as though the delay belongs to tide and tackle, not to him.

In the lighter, a jar of water stands beneath a cloth. The boatman reaches for its cup, sees Tom's hand move once across his knee and pours for himself instead. Daniel's covered cup has come down separately in a small provision basket. No order is spoken. The small accommodation crosses from one crew to another because a man notices, a passage more fragile than any paper Maria has paid to send.

The river narrows. Heat gathers beneath the coat Daniel was given in Batavia, but he cannot yet make himself remove it. Villages, palms and low muddy landings move past at the speed of oars. The open banks should feel like release after stone. Instead there is nowhere for the eye to stop. He watches the provision basket between his shoes until the factory stairs rise ahead.

A receiving clerk waits beneath an awning with a list. He asks the ship, the date, Daniel's rating and what effects accompany him. The questions are ordinary. Daniel reaches the last and cannot answer. He had a sea chest at Amboyna. Shirts. Letters from Nell. A deal box of copied pages. The Dutch made each object evidence, property or ash. None has crossed with him.

No effects, Tom says.

The clerk looks at the bigger man's coat and asks whether it is issued clothing.

Loaned for the passage.

To be returned?

When he has another.

The clerk leaves the recovery line blank. Daniel watches the space remain open and waits for somebody to put a price into it. Nobody does.

Tom does not carry him up the stairs. He walks one step below, near enough to stop a fall and far enough that Daniel never feels a hand deciding his direction. At the top, a Company servant points toward the factory and begins to explain the room, allowance and duties prepared for the returned factor.

Tom lets the explanation pass. The chair, cup and patched lantern could not come ashore as Daniel's possessions. Their lesson can. A useful arrangement is not the object. It is the person who notices what the object must do.

Released is not a station, so a desk is. A ledger, an allowance set against his back wages, a room that holds the heat the way the cells held the damp. Around him, a factory of men who have been advised — in the wordless way such advice travels — that the word Amboyna is not helpful, and who therefore say it never, which means Daniel lives inside the word entire, since it is the shape of every silence aimed at him.

On the first morning the desk faces the wall and the chair leaves the door behind Daniel's right shoulder. He stands beside it without removing his coat. Clerks pass, glance and find reasons to pass again. The scarred boatswain arrives carrying a coil for the warehouse, sets it down, turns the desk a quarter round and tests whether the chair can see the entrance without blocking the clerks' path. Then he carries the coil onward. By the time the chief factor comes to inspect his new hand, the room appears to have arranged itself.

At noon the shipboard cup appears beside the ledger, covered and set on his left, with bread and broth but no charge. Daniel understands that Tom has carried one practice ashore and left the rest for the room to learn. Food is never used to summon an answer.

He keeps the ledger exactly. His figures become the cleanest in the house, and the factors praise them without looking at him, and none of them understands what they are watching. The columns are not diligence. The columns are the wall of the last room he owns. A man can hold on to a column when he cannot hold on to anything else, and Daniel holds on for nearly a year: entry by entry, quarter by quarter, hand steady from eight in the morning until the light goes, and shaking the rest of the time, in the privacy the Company's incuriosity mercifully provides.

In the evenings, sometimes, a scarred boatswain sits within sight of him and says nothing.

Daniel never asks him why. The watching appears in no book — he has checked, being what he is; there is no order, no allowance, no entry anywhere attaching that man to this desk — and paper's silence, which once meant menace, has begun in this one case to mean the opposite. Whatever the boatswain is doing, nobody is paying him to do it. In Daniel's ruined arithmetic, that makes it the only account in Surat he can trust.

Twice he puts paper by the lamp to write to Nell.

The first time he writes her name and sits until the candle is half gone and writes nothing under it, because everything true is an injury — *I signed a lie; I watched a pen decide the world; I am not the man who promised you a story* — and everything gentle is a lie, and he of all men cannot make his hand do it. The second time he does not manage the name. How do you tell someone you are not the man they remember? The question folds itself around the pen and takes it, the way the riding turn took the boy's fingers at the capstan, and Daniel puts the paper away and adds, in the privacy of his own head, the cruellest entry in his private book: that the coral room reaches even Rochester, and Nell is under the water too, and does not know it.

The end, when it comes, is one word long, which is fitting, since his whole life has been decided in single words — Amboyna, Batavia, retained, both.

A form arrives from the chief factor's office in the spring of 1626, a routine certification for the season's stores, wanting only his signature under the standing phrase.

*Examined and found sufficient.*

Daniel reads the word examined.

He sets down the pen — places it, exactly, parallel to the page, the way he places everything — and says, to nobody in the room, in a voice the clerks will repeat to each other for a week:

I cannot.

And the scarred boatswain, who is there, who is always somehow there, stands up without a question in him, and goes out, and comes back before evening having done something at the shipping lists, and that is how Daniel learns — not being asked, being told, the only kindness left that his body can accept — that he is going home.

* * *

## XII. The Eighth Letter

Tom has written to Maggie seven times and sent nothing. The seventh letter lies unsigned in his sea chest, under a parish notice, and on the passage west he does not take it out. He understands at last what the eighth letter is. It is not paper. It walks the deck at his side in a bigger man's coat, flinching at the water the ship is made to cross.

The passage west takes five months, and Daniel stands at the rail for most of them.

The sea he crosses is the sea that was crossed to kill his friends: the same water, folded and refolded eleven thousand miles, indifferent as ink. He learns, slowly, in fair weather, to stand the sight of it. The boatswain's deck carries him the way it carried him from Batavia — a place kept clear, meals left without comment, no questions, work happening around his stillness with the tact of men who have all, in their different tonnages, been broken somewhere and shipped home once themselves.

England comes up out of the north Atlantic grey as the day he left it, as though the five years were weather that happened to other people. The Channel. The estuary. The river, narrowing, crowding, bristling with masts — more than he remembers, riding deeper than he remembers, the trade he once wanted to be a story in, going on at full flood without him.

On the last morning, coming up with the tide, the boatswain stands beside him at the rail and looks west up the water toward whatever it is that scarred men look toward, and says the only sentence of the whole passage that is not about work.

There is a house on the Wall. We will go there first.
