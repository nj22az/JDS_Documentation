# 1613: The Boy in the Rigging

> **1613**
>
> *“He kept me alive once with his hand and every day with his voice.”*
>
> — Jack Mercer, Deptford, 1626

## I. The River Lets Go

Jack's first work aboard is to carry his own debt down a ladder.

The sea chest costs nine shillings under the limit Arthur wrote into the articles. Inside are two shirts, one pair of stockings, a wooden bowl, a spoon, a blanket that smells of another man's sleep and a knife valued as new though its handle has been cut with somebody else's initials. The purser points to each item while a clerk enters it. Jack keeps Maggie's questions folded inside his coat and asks the price twice.

The purser looks at Tom.

Answer him, Tom says.

The second price is eightpence lower than the first.

Jack does not feel victorious. He feels the ship noticing him.

Men notice a boy who arrives beside the boatswain. They notice more when the boatswain makes the purser account for a spoon. By the time Jack has dragged his chest below, he is Tom's pet, Tom's clerk and Tom's little wife, according to three men who have not asked his name. One steals the blanket before dusk. Jack finds it beneath a gunner's mate and pulls until the man opens one eye.

Go ask your father for another.

Tom is six feet away, inspecting a frayed stay. He hears. Jack knows he hears because nothing said on deck escapes him. Tom does not turn.

Jack pulls again. The mate sits up and gives him the blanket with a smile that promises the matter is not finished.

Later, when the watch changes, Tom finds Jack trying to tie his chest shut with the bowline he learned in Wapping.

Wrong knot.

It holds.

So does a locked door. Can you open it under load?

Jack cannot.

Tom shows him a slipped hitch and makes him tie it until his fingers find the turn in darkness. He does not mention the blanket.

You heard him, Jack says.

Yes.

You could have made him give it back.

Yes.

Why didn't you?

Because tomorrow I may be on the other end of the ship.

Jack pulls the hitch free. He understands the lesson and hates the man for making it true.

The vessel drops downriver with the morning tide. Wapping passes as wet roofs, smoke and a confusion of stairs. Jack stands at the waist searching for one sign among a hundred. He cannot see the Pelican. He sees Carter's yard, or thinks he does: timber stacked behind the seasoning sheds, cranes black against the low sky, the place where his father lies in two rooms with a hand that can lift a spoon.

Four pounds. Two shillings from Tom every month. Half Jack's wage until the account is clean.

Numbers behave better ashore. On the moving deck they refuse to stand still.

At Gravesend a boat brings the last letters. None is for Jack. Tom receives a folded page bearing the Company's seal, reads it once and puts it inside his coat.

From Maggie? Jack asks.

No.

Did you tell her we sailed?

She knows what ships do.

The river widens. England lowers behind them. Jack waits for the moment the sea begins and cannot find it. Brown water becomes grey, grey becomes green, and the land is simply smaller each time he looks.

* * *

## II. Above the Deck

The first time Tom sends Jack aloft, the ship is barely clear of the Downs.

The order is to loose the foretopsail. Jack knows every word separately. Together they mean climbing into a wind that has already taken his cap.

Up, Tom says.

Jack puts one foot on the shrouds.

Not the ratline with your hands. The shroud. Ratline parts, you fall. Shroud parts, we all have larger trouble.

Jack changes his grip.

Up.

The word follows him. It is not encouragement. Tom has a voice made to cross weather, low in ordinary speech and enormous when lifted. It finds Jack through canvas cracking, blocks knocking and forty men pretending not to watch the boatswain's boy discover height.

At the futtock shrouds the climb turns outward. The deck is no longer a place but a shape below him, narrow and unreasonable. Water moves on both sides. Jack locks both arms and stops.

Keep moving, Tom calls.

I can't.

Nobody on deck laughs. Tom's silence prevents it.

Jack, look at the mast.

He presses his face to tarred rope.

Not down. Not out. One hand. One foot. Then the others.

Jack moves one hand.

Again.

One foot.

Again.

The voice builds a ladder where the ratlines have failed. Jack reaches the top, crawls over and lies against the platform with his eyes shut.

No sleeping, Tom calls. Loose the sail.

A man beside Jack shows him where the gasket lies. Jack's fingers are too stiff to free it. The man reaches across, then stops when Tom shouts from below.

Let him find it.

Jack finds it.

The sail opens with a violence no cloth should possess. It bellies, strikes him with its edge and nearly tears the line from his hands. He swears. The men laugh now, because the danger has become ordinary enough to permit it.

Coming down is worse. Gravity knows which country it belongs to.

At the deck Tom examines Jack's hands. Tar in the palms. One split nail. No rope burn deep enough to cripple him.

Again at the afternoon watch, Tom says.

Jack thinks this is cruelty until the second climb frightens him less. By the fifth, his feet begin to know where the ship will be before the roll arrives. By the tenth, another new boy watches him for the route.

Tom does not praise him. Praise makes a sailor listen for praise when he should be listening for wind.

He corrects everything.

Your thumb outside the coil.

Never stand inside the bight.

Feel the line before you look at it.

Tie it again.

Again.

Again.

Jack hears Tom in his sleep. He hears him while eating, while pissing over the lee rail, while hauling until the skin opens beneath his fingers. On the seventh day he turns at another man's shout because all voices have begun to sound like orders.

On the ninth, he makes his first dangerous mistake.

A buntline jams while the foretopsail is being taken in. Jack climbs to clear it, folds himself across the yard and puts his weight on the jammed line where it has worn pale against the block. Tom sees the colour from the deck.

Off that line! he roars.

Jack freezes.

Move!

The urgency fixes him harder. Tom is up the shrouds before Jack understands he has begun climbing. He comes over the top, works himself astride the yard and strikes Jack once across the shoulder with an open hand hard enough to break the grip fear has made.

Jack folds across the spar. Tom catches his coat, drags him inward and wraps Jack's arms round the yard.

The pale buntline parts on the next roll.

One end whips away into empty air.

Tom's face is close to Jack's. Not angry. Worse: afraid, and already turning the fear into instruction.

When I say move, you move.

You hit me.

Yes.

You could have said the rope was bad.

I did not have time to improve the order.

Below them the sea passes, green-black and without opinion.

Tom makes Jack climb down first. On deck he puts the severed rope into the bosun's store and enters it against ordinary wear. He does not charge it to the boy who stood on it.

* * *

## III. The Voice

There are kinder men aboard than Tom.

The cook gives Jack the bottom of the porridge pot, where the oats have caught and taste almost roasted. The old quartermaster teaches him to judge speed from the bubbles passing the hull. The sailmaker lets him sleep in the loft one wet afternoon while pretending not to see him.

None of them keeps him alive as often.

Tom's voice stops Jack before a block drops where his head is going. It sends him across the deck before green water comes over the waist. It tells him when to flatten, when to climb, when the familiar line in his hand has become the wrong line because darkness and panic have altered everything around it.

The voice is not kept for Jack. It works the entire ship.

Men hate it, obey it and repeat it in smaller voices down the watches. Tom sees the flaw in a knot from twenty feet. He smells loose wet hemp before it parts. He can wake from the deck beneath a coat and know by the changed pitch of canvas that a sheet is running.

He can also make a grown man feel six years old for coiling against the lay.

When a sailor named Pritchard drops a marline spike from aloft and misses Jack by the width of one hand, Tom has Pritchard at the mast before the spike stops rolling. He does not strike him. He makes him stand the boy's next two watches and carry Jack's work as well as his own.

Pritchard takes the punishment out on Jack in words.

Boatswain's little debt, he calls him. Two shillings buys a loud father.

Jack has not told anyone about the allowance.

He finds the purser's book open that evening while stores are issued. Beside Thomas Fletcher is the monthly deduction, entered exactly as Arthur wrote it. Beside Jack's name is the assigned half wage. The whole ship knows because books have no privacy from the men who keep them.

Jack waits until the night watch.

I told you not to pay it, he says.

Tom is splicing a length of line beneath the stern lantern.

I heard you.

They call me bought.

Men aboard call the moon a whore when it gives poor light. The moon keeps its account.

I'm not the moon.

No.

Then stop it.

Tom draws the splice tight. I can stop one mouth. The others will learn to whisper. Which do you prefer?

Jack has no answer that does not sound like surrender.

Tom gives him the line.

Find the join.

Jack runs both hands along it and cannot.

That is what good work looks like, Tom says. Not the man who paid for the rope.

It is almost comfort. Tom ruins it by making Jack splice the next length three times.

Most of the voyage is not weather or punishment. It is one watch followed by another, each made from small discomforts that nobody will remember if the ship arrives.

Jack wakes for the forenoon watch already wet.

The damp has come through the deck above, gathered on a beam and fallen steadily onto his chest. His shirt holds a dark patch from collar to belly. The hammock sags beneath him, one shoulder pressed against the ship's side and one knee against the sleeping man beside him. On the other side a pair of bare feet rests level with Jack's face. Their owner has wedged himself between two guns and snores whenever the hull rolls to larboard.

Jack tries to sit. The hammock folds him in half.

Tom strikes the sole of his shoe.

Watch.

I'm awake.

Then begin resembling it.

There is nowhere to dress except where he has slept. Jack works damp arms into his coat while the next hammock brushes his hair. Somebody has taken his left stocking from the line and left a strip of oakum in its place. He finds the stocking under Pritchard's head. Pritchard does not wake while Jack pulls it free, though his smile suggests a complicated kind of sleep.

The way forward is blocked by men leaving the morning watch and men rising for the day, all of them with the same needs and none with room to meet them. At the beakhead Jack waits behind six sailors while the sea appears and vanishes through the rails. Privacy consists of facing outward. The man ahead finishes, ties his breeches and warns Jack to hold the stay when the bow drops.

Jack thinks this advice insulting until the bow drops.

He returns with both shoes wet and one hand smelling of tar. A sailor at the pump tips a bucket of seawater across the deck and catches Jack to the ankles.

Now the rest matches, the sailor says.

Jack scoops a double handful from the scupper and throws it back. The ship rolls. The water misses the sailor and strikes Pritchard in the thigh.

Pritchard looks down at the spreading mark.

That boy's aim will make a gunner.

It is the first joke of the watch, and because Jack has helped make it, he is allowed to laugh.

The ration issue begins beside the main hatch. Each mess brings its wooden tally and whatever cups it has not lost. Biscuit is tipped from a cask onto sailcloth. Some pieces are whole, some split, some reduced at the edges to meal and droppings. The purser's assistant weighs the share for each mess, calls the number of mouths and makes a mark in the book.

When Jack first comes aboard, he thinks a ration belongs to the man who eats it. After a month he understands that it belongs first to the page. A man may be standing at the cask with an empty hand, but if the page says sick, absent, punished or dead, the biscuit can pass through him without stopping.

Tom gives Jack their mess tally.

How many?

Jack counts the men. Pritchard; the two at the pump; the lookout not yet relieved; Tom; himself; five more waiting with cups or still working.

Eleven.

Again.

Jack counts again. Eleven.

The assistant calls ten.

Eleven, Jack says.

The assistant does not look up. Ten on the mess.

Tom takes the tally, turns it over and puts one black thumbnail against a cut near the end.

That is eleven.

That is the boy.

I have observed him.

Half wage, the assistant says. It is entered.

Jack feels every man in the line hear it. Pritchard's name for him returns before Pritchard can speak: boatswain's little debt. Two shillings buys a loud father.

Tom's voice remains level.

The wage book pays Carter. The victual book feeds Jack. Unless the Company has found a way for half a boy to do a whole watch, weigh eleven.

The assistant looks toward the purser, but the purser is occupied arguing over a cheese that has become liquid inside its rind. He adds the biscuit.

Jack carries the share away.

Does half wage mean half everything? he asks when they are beyond the line.

It means half your wage.

And the two shillings?

Mine.

The food?

The ship's expense. It must keep alive what it intends to use.

Jack turns this over. It sounds less like kindness than arithmetic, which makes it easier to accept.

Their beer comes next. The first casks were small and good enough to make men sing after the evening issue. This cask gives a brown, sour drink with threads that cling to the cup. The assistant calls it beer because that is what the book calls it. Pritchard calls it rope with ambitions.

One of the pump men lifts a thread on his finger.

Splice it, Tom.

Too weak, Tom says.

The men laugh. Jack laughs late because he is surprised Tom has made the joke.

Water is worse in a quieter way. The cask smells of wet cellar when the bung comes free. The first dipper carries black specks. The second carries a pale shaving of wood and something soft that nobody examines. Men complain, but they hold out their cups. The good water is already behind them in the river mouths of England. This water has crossed every mile they have.

The cook takes his share to the galley and sets barley in it before the taste can become an argument. He salts the pot lightly and keeps the broth thin enough to pour. Jack crumbles his biscuit into a cup and waits while the pieces surrender. Hard biscuit breaks teeth; softened biscuit reveals inhabitants. Three small beetles rise to the surface.

Pritchard leans over his shoulder.

You have more meat than the rest of us.

Jack lifts one beetle with the spoon and drops it into Pritchard's cup.

Half wages, he says. Full meat.

The two pump men laugh first. Then the lookout, climbing down hungry from the mast. Pritchard looks at the beetle, looks at Jack, and drinks without removing it.

Waste nothing, he says.

That is the next joke of the watch. This time Jack knows in time that he belongs inside it.

Tom makes him count the water measures before he eats. Ten are drawn. The eleventh comes up short because the cask gives only a wet knock when the dipper reaches the bottom.

Put the shortage in the book, Tom tells the assistant.

The cask is empty.

That is the shortage.

It issued full when opened.

It does not issue full now.

The assistant says evaporation, leakage and bad cooperage as though three explanations add up to one measure of water. Tom waits. Waiting is the quiet form of his voice. At last the assistant writes beside the issue.

Jack watches the pen.

What happens if he doesn't write it?

At the next reckoning, the book has more water than the hold. The book wins until men become too thirsty to agree.

And if he writes it?

Then the cask loses the argument.

Tom tips half his cup into Jack's.

I have mine.

No, you have counted yours. Drink.

Jack drinks. The water tastes of the cask, the dipper and Tom's refusal to discuss what he has done.

The watch works forward from the ration line. First the pumps. Jack takes one handle with Pritchard opposite him. The pump wheezes, catches and brings bilge water up in brown surges that smell worse than the cask water and are easier to trust. Nobody has entered this water as drink. Nobody asks it to be anything but foul.

They pump until the carpenter calls the well low. Then buckets come over the side and the deck is flooded. Men scatter sand. Others push flat stones back and forth on their knees, scouring grease, blood, spilled broth and the black tracks of bare feet from the planks. Jack works a stone no larger than his palm. The sun climbs. His wet shirt dries stiff with salt while his knees become wet instead.

Pritchard crawls past and brushes Jack's back. Men begin smiling at nothing.

Jack keeps scrubbing. A twist of oakum swings behind him from the band of his breeches, tarred at the end like an animal's tail. He sees its shadow each time the sun clears the sail. He lets it remain.

The old quartermaster asks whether London boys grow that way or only shipboard ones.

Only the handsome ones, Jack says.

The tail survives another length of deck. Then Jack crawls beneath a wet line, catches it in one hand and, while Pritchard reaches for the bucket, tucks it into the back of Pritchard's belt. The exchange passes without anyone naming it. Two minutes later the quartermaster asks Pritchard the same question.

Pritchard looks behind himself and finds the oakum.

The deck waits.

He bows.

Improved by sea air, he says.

Even Tom's mouth alters at one corner.

The laughter does not excuse the dropped marline spike. It does not erase the names or the debt. It only gives Jack a place among men who have too little room, too much work and four hours in which to become tired of one another. Belonging aboard is not being spared. It is being answered.

When the deck is clean, Tom sends Jack to the weather shrouds with a serving mallet, spun yarn and a pot of tar. A length above the deadeyes has begun to chafe where rope works against rope. The damage is no longer than Jack's thumb. Left alone, Tom says, it will become the length of his arm and then the height of the mast.

So replace it.

With what?

New rope.

Where does new rope come from?

The store.

And when the store is empty?

Jack looks east, where the sea has no warehouse in it.

Tom puts the spun yarn in his hand.

Preserve first. Replace when preservation fails.

Jack lays the yarn against the shroud. Tom makes him begin again because the turn is loose, again because he has trapped water beneath it, and once more because the serving runs against the rope's lay. On the fourth attempt the mallet draws each turn close and hard. Tar coats Jack's fingers and works under his nails. The ship rises beneath him in a rhythm his body now anticipates.

Below, the ordinary life of the watch continues. A bucket travels aft. A sail is patched on deck. The cook insults the purser's cheese. Somebody sings three lines of a song and cannot remember the fourth, so the entire waist supplies a different one. The versions disagree about whether the woman in Plymouth owns a tavern, a husband or a knife.

Jack looks down at the serving.

The turns sit together so closely that he cannot see where the last begins.

Find the join, Tom says.

Jack runs one tarred finger along the work. This time he can.

There.

Better.

It is the smallest word Tom owns that can mean praise.

Near noon the heat thickens. The deck seams soften underfoot. Men who complained of cold rain in the Channel now work bare to the waist and curse the sun. Jack's mouth dries before he finishes one coil. He glances toward the water cask, though he knows by the bung lying beside it that there is nothing left until another is brought up.

Tom sees the glance.

Finish the coil. Then shade.

Jack coils with the lay, each circle flat inside the next. Pritchard passes and does not disturb it. This restraint is almost an apology.

The bell ends the watch. The relieving men rise from the dim places below, marked by hammock rope and sleep. They blink at the white deck. Jack looks toward the hatch and thinks of his own hammock, the wet patch perhaps shifted from chest to face, the feet waiting beside it. The space now seems less like punishment than permission to become unconscious.

Food comes first. The cook ladles the last thin barley broth into mess cups. Jack drops in the final piece of his biscuit and presses it down with a spoon. Around him men eat quickly before the next order can claim their hands.

One sailor does not.

The man sits in the full noon sun with both arms crossed over his chest. His biscuit remains untouched beside the cup. Broth soaks halfway through it, leaving the upper edge dry.

Jack points at the biscuit.

If you don't want that, Pritchard will eat it and the beetles.

The sailor looks at him without understanding. His face is grey beneath the heat.

Cold, he says. God help me, boy. I am cold.

Jack looks at the sun. Tom looks at the untouched biscuit.

## IV. Nineteen

By evening the sailor is shaking beneath two blankets. By morning four more men cannot stand. The surgeon calls it the flux and orders the sick separated, an instruction for which the vessel has no space. Men sleep between guns, beneath boats, against bulkheads wet with their own breath. Separation is a word belonging to houses with spare rooms.

Tom clears the sail room and slings hammocks close enough that the sick turn together when the ship rolls. He orders vinegar splashed on the boards. He burns foul bedding until the captain objects to Company property going over the side as smoke.

Dead men draw no wage, Tom says. Choose your account.

The captain allows the fire.

Jack carries water. At first this seems easier than going aloft. Then the men begin asking him to stay.

One is nineteen and has a sister in Limehouse. One has a wife who believes he is on a coastwise run to Bordeaux. One took the Company's advance, lost it at dice before sailing and now laughs whenever the fever climbs because he remembers the expression on the clerk's face. Their names are Ellis, Brown, Cooper, Venn. Jack repeats them until sleep rubs the edges off.

The surgeon moves from hammock to hammock with a lancet and a basin. Tom watches him bleed the first three and stops him at the fourth.

It is the accepted treatment, the surgeon says.

They have lost water from both ends for two days.

The fever must be relieved.

Relieve one more into that basin and I will make you drink it.

The surgeon appeals to the captain. The captain looks at the men able to work, counts how many answer to Tom's voice and tells the surgeon to try water first.

Tom has no medicine. He has observation and the memory of every voyage that killed men in the same order. He has the cook keep the thinnest barley broth on the fire, salted lightly and carried warm from hammock to hammock in cups. Some men keep it down. Some do not. Nobody calls this knowledge because no gentleman wrote it in a book.

On the fifth day Jack wakes cold beneath the tropical sun.

Tom sees before Jack speaks. He takes the bucket from Jack's hand and presses two fingers beneath his jaw.

How many stools since dawn?

Jack is too tired to be embarrassed. Three.

Tom puts him in the sail room.

I can work.

No.

You said men obey the first order.

That was the first order.

Fever strips the ship to sound. Timbers complain. Water moves in casks. Men breathe as though hauling something uphill. Jack lies among them and understands for the first time that a vessel can continue while the people inside it stop.

Tom brings the water himself.

Jack tries to sit. Tom puts one hand against his chest and holds him down. The hand that struck him on the yard is careful now, which makes Jack angrier than the blow did.

Don't, Jack says.

Drink.

I don't want your two shillings.

Drink.

I don't want—

Tom tips the cup against his mouth. Jack drinks or drowns in a very small quantity of water. Most of it stays down.

Why me? Jack asks when he can speak.

You were nearest the bucket.

The answer is a lie and a kindness and Tom's refusal to name either one.

Men die around them.

The first is Ellis. The surgeon's mate sews him into his hammock with a round shot at the feet. Tom makes the clerk enter the full name before the body goes over. The clerk says the muster number is sufficient. Tom stands beside the book until the letters are written.

Ellis.

Brown.

Cooper.

Venn.

The list reaches nineteen before the fever releases the ship.

Jack is not on it.

On the first morning he can stand, he climbs to the foretop without being ordered. Halfway up, his legs begin to shake. Tom's voice rises from the deck.

One hand. One foot. Then the others.

Jack does not look down.

## V. The Next Weather

The ship changes after nineteen burials.

Hammocks hang farther apart because there are fewer men to fill them. Watches lengthen. Boys do work that belonged to able seamen at the Canaries. Nobody calls Jack Tom's pet now. The word requires an innocence the voyage has used up.

Pritchard gives Jack a strip of leather for his palms and claims it was spare. The cook stops pretending the burnt oats are an accident. The quartermaster lets Jack cast the log and accepts his count without checking it twice.

Tom gives him nothing except more work.

At dusk he takes Jack to the foremast and makes him lay one hand against the wood. The vibration changes as the ship rises. Rigging speaks through the mast: every stay carrying its own degree of strain, every sail pulling at a different height.

What do you hear? Tom asks.

Nothing.

Feel, then.

Jack closes his eyes. Beneath the ordinary shudder is a small repeated knock.

Block at the foretop?

Tom waits.

No. Lower. The brace.

Which brace?

Jack follows the tremor through the mast and points.

Lee fore brace.

Tom nods once.

They renew the worn strop before darkness.

Weeks later the African coast lies somewhere beyond the western horizon. The master speaks of passing Madagascar before the monsoon turns. Heat sits on the deck after sunset. Flying fish strike the hull in the night. Clouds build where the sky has been empty all day.

Jack has learned enough to watch Tom watching them.

Bad weather? he asks.

Weather, Tom says.

That is not an answer.

It is the only one the sea has given.

Tom checks the braces himself before the watch goes below. Jack follows without being told.

The boy who left Wapping could tie three knots and could not swim. The boy in the rigging can feel a worn line through the mast and knows that neither fact makes him safe.

Above them, the first high cloud draws a white hook across the dark.

* * *

## VI. The White Hook

The white hook in the western sky is gone by morning.

In its place stands a wall.

It is too far away to seem large. A low darkness below, a hard white edge above, the whole shape sitting where sea and sky have spent weeks refusing to admit they are separate. The master studies it through a brass tube and says the weather will pass astern.

Tom looks at the sails.

Jack looks at Tom.

This has become another instrument aboard: the angle of the boatswain's head, the amount of time he spends answering a simple question, whether he inspects a line with his eyes or puts his hand on it. This morning Tom touches everything.

He checks the foremast wedges. He sounds the standing rigging with the handle of his knife. He makes Pritchard climb to the foretop and examine the topmast cap, then goes up after him and examines it again. He orders the fore braces cleared from the pins and recoiled where they can run without catching a man's ankle.

The master watches from the quarterdeck.

You expect trouble, Fletcher?

I expect rope.

We have rope.

Then I expect it to work.

The master does not enjoy the answer. He enjoys less that Tom has already made the men shorten the loose ends of their clothing and move spare spars off the forward deck. Preparedness can look like insubordination to a man who has just predicted fair passage.

By noon the wind falls.

Canvas hangs against the masts. The sea becomes long, dark glass lifting under the hull without breaking. Heat gathers beneath the courses and stays there. Men speak quietly because the absence of wind has taken away the sound that usually hides them from one another.

Jack is sent aloft to stop a block knocking against the foretopmast. He carries a short lashing between his teeth and climbs through air thick enough to drink.

From the foretop he can see Madagascar as a low bruise to the west. It is the first land in weeks, if land is what it is. The coast shows itself only when the ship rises: a dark line, then nothing, then a dark line again.

Below him the vessel is stripped of illusion. Seams, patched canvas, pale scars where fittings have moved under strain. Nineteen fewer men. A ship does not become whole because land is near.

Jack secures the block. Before descending, he looks toward the weather.

The wall has moved.

No longer sitting at the edge of the world, it has eaten half of it. The white upper edge is gone. Beneath the black cloud, the sea has turned the colour of lead freshly cut.

Jack starts down.

Tom calls from the deck. Stay there. What do you see?

Rain.

Where?

Everywhere.

That is not a bearing.

Jack braces one arm round the mast and forces himself to look again.

West by south. Moving north.

The master lowers his tube. Passing astern, he says.

Tom does not answer him. Watch the sail, he calls to Jack.

There is hardly enough wind to shape one.

That makes the small movement easier to see.

Jack watches the foretopsail. The lower edge hangs slack. One corner lifts, falls, then lifts again though the deck beneath it has rolled the other way.

The clew is moving, Jack calls.

Which?

Weather clew.

The master turns toward the binnacle.

The wind has not reached the deck. It has reached the upper canvas.

Tom's voice crosses the ship.

All hands. Shorten sail.

The master says, I have not given that order.

Tom looks at the black wall, then at nineteen empty places where men should be running.

Give it now.

* * *

## VII. Before the Wind

The order comes late and everybody obeys it quickly.

Men spill out of the waist and up the shrouds. The courses are clewed. Topsail sheets are eased while clewlines and buntlines come in. Canvas that hung dead a moment before begins breathing in short, irregular pulls.

Jack remains at the foretop because Tom told him to watch. Pritchard climbs past and throws him a gasket.

Move, boy.

Tom told me—

Tom wants the sail in before he wants your opinion.

Jack crawls onto the yard. Six men lie across the spar, its timber taking their weight while their arms gather canvas in hot, heavy folds.

The first wind arrives from the wrong direction.

It touches the back of Jack's neck. The ship is still moving on the old air, such as it was, while the new air comes across the quarter. The topsail shivers between the two.

Jack looks down.

Tom stands beside the foremast, one hand on the stay. His face is turned upward.

The sail fills backward.

Not fully. One deep inward breath, enough to put every seam in relief and make the yard kick beneath Jack's chest. The men swear and flatten themselves.

Then it empties.

Jack sees the mast move.

All masts move. Tom taught him this. A rigid mast is already broken. But the foretopmast does not return along the same path. It bends away and comes back with a small second motion at the cap, like a loose tooth tested by a tongue.

Jack shouts.

The wind takes the word.

He shouts again. Foretopmast! At the cap!

Tom has seen it.

Down from the yard! he roars. Lay down! Move!

The men come off the spar. Jack reaches the top and stops because Pritchard is below him on the futtock shrouds.

Tom points.

Backstay. Slide it.

Jack throws one leg over the aft lead of the rigging and descends more by controlled falling than climbing. Tar burns through the leather on his palms. His feet strike the deck.

Tom catches his shoulder, turns him and puts a line into both hands.

Lee fore brace. Take it aft. Do not belay until I tell you.

The line is already under strain.

Why me?

Because you felt it before. Go.

Jack runs aft with the brace, threading between men hauling the fore course up. He reaches the station beyond the mainmast where the old quartermaster and two fever-thinned sailors wait for hands enough to control the yard.

Ten seconds is not a measure anybody makes during danger.

Later Jack will build it from actions.

He takes the brace.

One.

The quartermaster tells him to keep his thumb out of the coil.

Two.

Tom orders the weather brace eased.

Three.

The first rain hits, drops large enough to burst in the dust between the deck planks.

Four.

The foretopsail fills backward again.

Five.

The yard tries to swing. Jack and the quartermaster haul the lee brace to bring it round.

Six.

Somebody forward shouts that the cap is opening.

Seven.

Tom looks up.

Eight.

He takes one step away from the mast.

Nine.

The wind arrives.

Ten.

The foretopmast goes.

* * *

## VIII. The Going

It does not snap like a stick.

It comes apart as a system.

The cap tears loose first. Iron leaves timber with a scream. The topmast bows forward under the backed sail, carrying yard, canvas, blocks and a confusion of rigging that for one suspended instant still resembles a mast.

Then the whole weight falls toward the bow.

Men run without choosing directions. The sail catches wind on the way down and drags the broken spar sideways. A stay rips through the foretop rail. Blocks strike the deck hard enough to bury their corners. The yard lands across the forward boats and breaks one clean through.

Jack is aft of it because Tom put him on the lee brace.

The brace jumps in his hands.

The quartermaster lets go. One of the other men does not. The line strips skin from his palms and pulls him face-first across the deck. Jack sees the running coil open beside his ankle.

Thumb outside, Tom's voice says inside him.

He steps clear as the line tears past.

Forward, Tom is not clear.

A parted backstay lashes round his left arm and tightens as the fallen topmast goes over the side. Tom is dragged to one knee. The next pull will take him against the rail; the pull after that will take the rail with him.

Jack has Tom's knife.

He does not remember taking it. The handle is in his hand because Tom gave him the lee brace and the knife hangs beside every order Tom gives. Jack runs forward while other men run aft.

The deck changes angle beneath a wall of rain. He falls, keeps the knife and loses a piece of skin from one knee. The backstay tightens. Tom's shoulder strikes the rail.

Cut it! Tom shouts.

The line is tarred hemp thick as Jack's wrist.

Jack cuts.

The blade makes almost no mark.

Not across. With the lay!

Jack turns the knife and drives its point between the strands. The rope opens enough to show pale fibres within. He saws through one bundle, then another. Every movement of the wreck in the water closes the rope against the blade.

Tom's arm has gone dark below the turn.

Jack puts one foot against the rail, holds the knife in both hands and pulls upward through the last strand.

The stay parts.

The severed end goes over the side with such force it cuts a smoking line across the wet rail. Tom falls backward onto Jack. For a moment they are both on the deck beneath water that is rain and sea together.

Then Tom is moving.

He does not thank Jack. There is no time in which gratitude would not be another obstruction.

Clear the wreckage! Axes forward! Hands off every line until you know where it leads!

His left arm hangs badly. His voice does not.

The fallen mast pounds the hull. Each strike shakes the deck. If it is allowed to remain, the broken spar will drive through the planking or drag the ship broadside to the wind.

Men take axes to the surviving rigging. Every cut releases a new violence. One block flies upward when its load goes and catches Pritchard beneath the jaw. He disappears without a sound under the wrecked sail.

Jack sees his boot.

He starts toward it.

Tom catches the back of his coat with the good hand.

No.

He's under there.

The mast is over the side.

He's alive.

You do not know that.

I know his boot.

The ship rolls. The broken spar lifts in the sea and slams back against the hull. Beneath the canvas, the boot moves.

Jack tears free.

This time Tom follows.

They crawl beneath the edge of the sail while the quartermaster and three others haul it clear by inches. Pritchard lies between the fallen yard and the splintered boat, blood running from his mouth. Jack gets both hands beneath his shoulders. Tom grips the coat at the waist with his good hand.

Together they drag him out before the mast strikes again.

The axes finish the last stay. The topmast, yard and sail fall away into grey water and vanish behind the ship, still joined, still briefly looking like something built by men.

The foremast remains standing below the top.

Nothing above it does.

* * *

## IX. Under Bare Poles

The squall passes in less than an hour.

That is the insult of it.

The black wall goes east. Rain thins. A broken sunlight opens across the water and makes every wet surface shine: the severed rigging, the split boat, the blood at Pritchard's collar, the empty place above the foretop where half the mast used to be.

The sea looks refreshed.

The ship looks robbed.

Pritchard lives. His jaw is broken and three teeth lie somewhere beneath the forward gun. The surgeon binds his head and says he may never chew on that side. Pritchard asks whether the lost teeth are to be charged as Company stores. The words are mud in his mouth. Jack laughs before he can stop himself.

Pritchard does too, then regrets it.

Tom's shoulder is not broken. The backstay has cut a dark groove round the upper arm and pulled the joint until every movement makes his face empty. The surgeon offers a sling. Tom uses it for one watch, then takes it off because a boatswain pointing with his right hand sends men toward the wrong rope.

The master holds an inquiry before the deck is dry.

He asks why sail was not shortened sooner. He asks whether the foretopmast was unsound. He asks who last inspected the cap. Each question travels carefully around the fact that he said the weather would pass astern.

The clerk sits at the cabin table with his pen waiting.

Tom gives him no shelter.

The upper wind changed before the wind at deck level. The foretopsail backed twice. The topmast moved out of true at the cap. I ordered men down and put Mercer on the lee brace. The mast failed before the sail could be taken in.

Mercer saw the movement? the master asks.

Yes.

A boy?

A lookout aloft.

Was the cap defective?

It is in the sea.

Then we cannot know.

We know it failed.

The master turns to Jack. How long between your reporting the movement and the loss of the mast?

Jack looks at Tom.

Answer the question, Tom says.

Ten seconds.

You counted?

No.

Then how can you state ten?

Jack gives the actions. The line in his hands. The quartermaster's warning. The first rain. The orders to ease and haul. The shout from forward. Tom stepping clear. The wind.

The clerk stops writing midway through because actions do not fit the narrow space left for the answer.

Approximately ten seconds, he writes.

The master asks whether Jack would have remained aloft without Tom's order.

Yes.

And been lost with the mast.

Yes.

The question waits for gratitude. Jack has gratitude. He also has Tom's blood beneath his fingernails from opening the backstay.

Tom would have gone over if I hadn't cut him loose, he says.

The clerk looks up.

The master does not want this second truth. It complicates the shape of command. A boatswain saves a boy; a boy does not save a boatswain. One is discipline. The other looks like dependence.

Tom puts his injured arm on the table.

Enter it.

The clerk enters both.

* * *

## X. A Shorter Mast

For nine days the ship moves under reduced sail while the crew builds what the sea allows.

They have spare timber, but no spare foretopmast equal to the one lost. Tom chooses a shorter spar and has the carpenters shape it. The riggers fit new stays. The quartermaster serves the lines. Jack works wherever a narrow body can reach.

This is rigging without metaphor. Measure, splice, parcel, serve. A rope too short does not become long because the ship needs it. A weak eye does not become sound because the captain signs an order. Every piece tells the truth when weight arrives.

Jack likes the work.

It is the first thing aboard he likes without also fearing it.

Tom shows him how to bury the ends of a splice so the join carries strain along its length. Jack's first is bulky. His second is neat and weak. Tom cuts both open.

The third holds.

Good, Tom says.

It is the first praise Jack has received from him.

He tries not to show what it does.

At sunset they stand beneath the shortened mast. The vessel looks wrong, one shoulder lower than the other, but the new rig draws.

Why the lee brace? Jack asks.

Tom flexes the fingers below his injured arm.

It needed another hand.

There were men on deck.

Not men who saw the mast move.

You believed me.

You were aloft. That was your work.

The answer refuses heroism. It gives Jack something better: his observation belonged in the decision.

You knew it would go?

I knew it might.

Ten seconds.

Nine would have done.

Jack looks up at the shorter spar, the new stays and the place in the sky where the old mast failed.

And eight?

Tom considers.

Do not build a life around finding out.

Weeks later the water changes colour.

It is not yet the impossible green Tom promised in Wapping. But birds begin appearing at dawn, flying with purpose instead of resting lost on the yards. Weed passes the hull. One evening the air carries smoke from land nobody can see.

Jack stands at the foremast with one hand against the wood. The new rig speaks in a higher voice than the old one. He can feel his own splice carrying its share.

Behind him, somewhere off Madagascar, a foretopmast lies descending through dark water with ten seconds still inside it.
