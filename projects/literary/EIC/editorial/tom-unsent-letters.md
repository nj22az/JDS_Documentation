# Tom's Seven Unsent Letters (1615–1625)

**What these are.** The private record of the exile that begins when Maggie
casts Tom out in 1612. He writes to the one person he loves and can never
address; he sends none of them; the love is in every line and named in none —
he cannot write the word even to a page he will burn. Together they are a
portrait of the whole exile: Surat, the heat, the work, the reforms, Amboyna,
Daniel, the manoeuvre to Batavia — the making of the wanderer, addressed to the
home that closed to him.

**Design rules.** No confession (the love saturates and is never stated).
First-person, so a letter may hold Tom's *own* stated intentions ("I am going to
get him") — that is not narrator prolepsis. Each is dated and grounded in
exactly where Tom is in life. These could later be woven into the reader (the
1620–22 "Echo" material already references the fifth and seventh); for now they
are a planning artifact.

---

## I. Surat, the dry season, 1615

*Three years out. The 1613–14 voyage with Jack is over; Tom has signed the
Surat berth. The Company packet has just brought him, seven months late, word
that his mother died the spring before.*

Maggie —

The packet found me in March with a letter my sister wrote in the summer before last. My mother has been the better part of a year in the ground at Barking, and I have been walking a deck the whole time not knowing it — giving orders, eating my ration, a man with a mother, and she gone out from under me the whole while. That is the sea's particular cruelty. It lets you keep a thing long after it is taken, so you have to lose it twice: once in the world, and once again when the paper catches up.

I am telling you because there is no one else I would tell it to plainly. The men here would be sorry the way men are sorry, and I would have to manage their sorrow, and I have not the strength for it. You would only put a plate in front of me and let me be quiet. I did not know, until I sat down to write this, that I have been hungry for that plate for three years.

Surat is hot past describing and the work has no bottom to it and I am good at the work, which you would not count in my favour. I have not forgotten what you said. I turn it over on the middle watch when the ship is quiet. I am trying to find the place in the machine where a man can stand and still be a man, and I have not found it, but I am looking now, which I was not doing the night I stood in your room so pleased with myself.

The thimble is under your bar and my mother is at Barking and I am here, and not one of the three of us is where we ought to be.

*He does not send it. There is no honest way to send grief to a house he was told not to come back to. He puts it in the bottom of his chest, beneath his father's spare whistle, and does not take it out again for two years.*

## II. Surat, the wet season, 1617

*Five years out. Settled into the factory work; beginning the small reforms —
the double tally, feeding the passage-boys before they are assessed.*

Maggie —

I have done a thing I think you would not spit at.

Twelve boys came off the spring passage thin as old rope, and the factor set to examining them for work before they had eaten — teeth, hands, the whole reckoning of a man against his price. I stopped it and had them fed first. It cost the Company a morning and cost me an argument I won by being the largest man in the yard, which is not how you would have won it, but the boys ate.

I have made them keep two tallies on every landing now, counted by different hands, so no clerk's slip can put a bale on a porter's back and a rope round his neck by accident. It is a small thing and I know its size. It only means the machine cannot lie by mistake in this one yard while I am standing in it, and that by the next monsoon after I am gone it will go back to one tally. I do it anyway.

I have written this whole letter as if to be marked, and there is the old fault, plain — wanting you to see the work and say it will do. I have not the right to your good word. I gave that up at your door and I will not come round the back of the world to steal it. I only wanted to set down somewhere that I heard you. Late. But I heard you.

*He reads it over, sees the whole of it is a boy holding up a copybook, and cannot decide whether that shames him. He does not burn this one. He folds it small and lays it face down in the chest, because it is true even where it is vain.*

## III. Surat, 1619

*Seven years out. The missing-bale case: a warehouse error nearly hangs the
porter Yusuf; Tom lays the two tallies together, clears him, and has the charge
struck aloud.*

Maggie —

A man was near hanged this month over a missing bale, and the bale was never missing.

A clerk's stroke went wide and the count came up short, and the English factor wanted a back for it, and the back was a porter named Yusuf — a red headcloth, four seasons at that landing, a wife, I am told, though a man's wife is never in the Company's account of him. I laid the warehouse page against the lighter's page the way you lay a man's story against the room's, and the missing stroke was there, and the bale was there, and it had never been anywhere but where it was set down. I made them strike the charge, and I made them read it struck, aloud, so the yard heard the man's name said as a name and not as a loss.

I knew to do it because you are in my head, Maggie, every hour, standing at that bar asking whose name it is. You put yourself there. I did not invite you and I cannot get you out, and it is the one part of what happened at your door that I am glad of. A man carried you east inside him without your leave, and it kept a rope off another man's neck.

*He keeps this one, though he lays it face down with the second, because he sees that a letter which uses a saved man to plead its own cause is not altogether clean, and he will not send her a thing that is even a little unclean, not even in his own defence.*

## IV. Surat, 1621

*Nine years out. The cloth order filled perfectly; the machine that fits him so
well he has begun to fear it. His single day of shore leave, which he cannot
rest through and fills with repair.*

Maggie —

They gave me a day ashore this month — a whole day, the first in years — and I want to set down for you that I did not know how to spend it.

I mended a cobbler's awning-rope that was not mine to mend. I sat where I could see two doors at once, which is a habit now and not a choice. I ate lentils at a fair price and was pleased at the fairness like a fool. And all day I was waiting for the thing a day ashore is meant to give a man, and it did not come, because I have mislaid the place it comes from.

They say I am the best boatswain east of the Cape, and it is true, and it is worth exactly nothing to me. The Company found a shape and I fit it, and I have fit it so long that I have started to think the shape is me. On the good days that frightens me. On the bad days it does not, and those are the worse ones.

There is a house on a wall in Wapping where a day ashore used to mean something, and I am not let into it, and I earned the bolt on that door with my own two hands. I am writing only to tell you that I know to the penny what I traded and what I got, and that the getting does not cover the trade and never will, and that I keep the books too honestly to pretend it does.

*He does not send it. He folds it into the packet with the others — there are four now — and thinks that a man who keeps a bundle of letters he will never send is either faithful or mad, and cannot tell which, and suspects at this distance they are the same thing.*

## V. Surat, 1622 — burned

*Ten years out. The one where he finally writes the three words — and sees, in
writing them, that saying them is asking to come back. He burns it. (Canon: "the
fifth, 'You were right', burned for the demand hidden in it.")*

Maggie —

You were right.

That is the whole of the letter, and I have been ten years getting to it. You were right about the boy, and about the paper, and about the man I had made and called kind. You were right, and I have spent a decade proving you right in warehouse after warehouse, saving one man at a time out of a machine I keep running with my own hands, until I can no longer tell whether that makes me your student or only a better-mannered part of the machine.

I wanted you to have the three words. And I have sat here with them written in front of me and seen at last what they are. A man does not say *you were right* to the one who put him out unless he means, underneath it, *so let me come back*. It is a demand in an apology's coat. It is the neatest sum I ever set down, and I built it against you, to turn my being right about my being wrong into a key for your door. You taught me to see that trick. You would see it in a heartbeat.

So you cannot have the three words. I am sorry. They are true, and that is exactly why I may not send them: the sending would be the one dishonest thing in them.

*He holds it to the lamp and lets it burn down to his fingers. It is the nearest thing to relief he has felt in ten years, and he understands that this, too, is why he cannot be allowed to keep it.*

## VI. Surat, 1623

*Eleven years out. Amboyna. The three accounts reach Surat; Tom keeps the ten
blank lines for the Japanese names; a retained English factor is entered on a
Dutch list, moved to Batavia for no reason any paper gives. The letter turns
from penance to purpose.*

Maggie —

There has been a bad thing at Amboyna, and it has reached me as three papers that all tell the truth, which is the surest sign I know that the whole of it is a lie.

They put ten of our men and some of their own and a company of Japanese soldiers to the water until the men owned a plot the water had invented, and then killed them for owning it, and now the Company copies the killing into whatever shape each reader has the appetite for. In Surat they have already begun to say Amboyna may be the making of us. A man said it within my reach and kept his teeth by a nearer margin than he will ever know.

I have done the one thing I know how to do. I have kept the names. Ten English names set down entire, and under them ten empty lines for ten Japanese men whose names the account does not trouble to give, and I will not close those lines with anything but the names, when I can get them. You would say a blank is still an entry. It is the truest thing you ever taught me, and I have made a whole ledger of it out here in the dark.

And there is a man, Maggie. An English factor, taken alive when the rest were killed, written on a Dutch list as *retained* and shifted to Batavia like a cask moved to another shelf, for no cause any paper gives. A living man turned into a line between two columns. I know that line. I have been that line. I am going to go and get him.

I do not yet know how. But for the first time since your door I have a thing to do that is not penance, and I find I can breathe with it. Whatever I have become, it seems it can be aimed.

*He does not fold this one away with the rest. He puts it inside his coat, against the paper with the ten blank lines, and it stays there. It is the first of the letters that is about what is coming and not about what is gone.*

## VII. Batavia, 1625 — folded, unsigned

*Thirteen years out. He has spent fourteen months manoeuvring onto a Batavia
deck to be the English signature that can take Daniel Vale off the Dutch books;
he has met Maria ("Not love. Recognition."). The last written letter. The eighth
is Daniel himself. (Canon: "the seventh — 'I remembered before I knew what
remembering required' — folded unsigned.")*

Maggie —

I have him. His name is Daniel Vale, and he is the retained man from the Dutch list, and I have spent fourteen months and every favour I own working my way onto a Batavia deck to be the English signature that could lift him off their books. There is a woman here who reads an account the way you read a room. She asked no fee. She said there is a house in London that reads accounts truly, and to take him there and give him to the keeper. She did not know she was handing me back my own door as the price of another man's.

I have learned the thing your whole life has been saying, that I was too pleased with myself at twenty-two to hear and too broken at thirty-one to do. You cannot *say* you have changed. You can only walk a changed thing through a door and set it down and let the room see for itself.

So I am not going to send you words. I am going to bring you a living man the machine had already entered as lost, and set him in front of you, and if there is anything left in your face when you see him, that will be the only letter worth the sending — and you will have written it, not me.

I have remembered what a human life is worth. I remembered it before I knew what the remembering would cost, or ask, or take fourteen years to spend. I am bringing you the proof and not the promise.

*He does not sign it. A signature would say this comes from Tom, and the Tom who left has no standing to write to her; only the man who comes through the door with Daniel Vale beside him may speak, and he will let the door do the speaking. He folds the letter once. He does not send it and he does not burn it. He carries it home in his coat, against the ten names, and it is still there, unsigned, on the evening he brings Daniel Vale through the Pelican's door.*

## Notes for weaving into the reader (later, if approved)

- The seven are the concrete body of the canon line "writes Maggie seven
  letters, sends none; the eighth is Daniel himself." The **Echo** material
  (1620–22) already names the fifth and seventh; those two must stay verbatim in
  spirit if any are shown.
- Candidate homes if shown on the page: brief excerpts inside **Tom at Surat**
  (letters I–IV), the burned fifth glimpsed in the **Echo** beat, the sixth
  against the ten-names material, the seventh carried unsigned into **Batavia /
  The Man Who Came Back Wrong**. Never all seven at once — the power is that we
  mostly *know they exist* and rarely see them.
- **No confession**, even here: the word "love" appears in none of the seven.
  That absence is the point — the man cannot write it even to a page he will
  burn. Hold that guard if any are deployed.
