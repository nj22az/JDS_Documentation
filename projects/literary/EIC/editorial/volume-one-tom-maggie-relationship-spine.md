# Volume One — Tom and Maggie Relationship Spine

Status: governing canon decision for *The Venture*.

## Emotional premise

Tom and Maggie are the first sustained relationship in the cycle. The reader
meets Tom at twenty-two and leaves him in his fifties, after the woman he kept
returning to is gone. The Pelican is where they repeatedly find one another,
each time older and less able to pretend that the other is merely a keeper or a
customer.

They never call one another mother and son. Maggie never says she is proud of
him. Her love appears as food, scolding, accurate disappointment, the keeping of
his mother's thimble and her refusal to let either his courage or his shame
become theatrical. Tom returns even when he knows she will see through the man
the Company has made.

The relationship contains real moral friction. Maggie is not an endlessly
approving sanctuary. Tom is not kept innocent by the author's affection for
him. Company service makes him competent, useful and morally smaller before it
teaches him what competence is for. Maggie's disappointment matters because
her pride has mattered.

Matthew Bell's murder gives the relationship its first unresolved account. Tom
saves Mara, but the room lets Silas Rook escape while Bell dies. Maggie and Tom
spend the book refusing the comforting fiction that a correct verdict was the
same thing as justice. Bell's folded account begins the counter-ledger beneath
the bar; Daniel Vale's true copy joins it in 1626.

## Six-chapter architecture

| Chapter | Year | Tom | Maggie | Irreversible movement |
|---|---:|---|---|---|
| I — The Boy Who Signed | 1603 | Twenty-two, coastwise seaman, inherited debt, brave before he understands responsibility | Strong, guarded, sees her absent son in him without making Tom a replacement | Tom stops Mara's lynching; the room identifies Bell's murderer but lets him leave; Bell's account exposes Tom's debt |
| II — Dutch Courage | 1603 | Signs despite knowing Harcourt's machinery and leaves his mother's thimble | Prices the voyage honestly and keeps both Bell's account and Tom's wager | The Company replaces Bell before noon; Tom's paper begins taking half his wage |
| III — The Return | 1612 | Comes home a boatswain and brings sixteen-year-old Jack to sign, calling recruitment protection | Has cut a notch for every local boy the sea did not return | Maggie hears the old room's excuse in Tom's “with or without me” and throws him out |
| IV — The Man Who Came Back Wrong | 1626 | Carries Daniel Vale into the bar and stands against the Company's attempt to purchase silence | Feeds Tom before forgiving him; gives Daniel access to the hidden gap | Daniel's true copy is placed beside Bell's account; Maggie slides Tom a pint and the fourteen-year rupture ends without absolution speeches |
| V — The South Land | 1629–1630 | Hears what became of Rook and tries to make the room responsible for all his later murders | Refuses false nobility: Rook's murders are his; letting him leave belongs to the room | Maggie distinguishes Mara's sentence from justice: justice would have been Bell walking home |
| VI — Last Orders | 1635 | Returns in his fifties, debt-free and permanently altered by the Company | Dead, present through Joan, the pie, her standing order and the house she kept | Tom eats before he is told, touches the thimble once, leaves it in the Cache and says goodnight |

## Name rule

The room knows Tom as Tom. Maggie never speaks his surname while alive. In the
narrative chapters, **Fletcher** is withheld until Maggie's posthumous standing
order: “If a scarred man named Fletcher ever comes through that door — feed him
first. Then tell him.” Reference material and the character bible may use his
full archival name.

## Guardrails

- Do not have either character use “mother,” “son,” “proud of you” or a deathbed
  declaration.
- Keep every decisive encounter in the Pelican or at its immediate river edge.
- Distant Company history enters through bodies, pay papers, testimony, cargo,
  rumour and consequence—not detached historical display.
- Tom may become skilled and respected, but not an officer-gentleman. He remains
  a working seaman and boatswain.
- Maggie's missing Swedish son remains his own person. Tom does not replace him.
- Saving Mara is a real good. Letting Rook escape is a real failure. Never use
  either truth to erase the other.
- Bell remains morally compromised: his late courage does not cancel the people
  and debts he previously converted into clean entries.
- Mara warns the VOC about Rook before the *Batavia* sails. The Company reads
  her warning as proof of usefulness; she does not knowingly place him among
  civilians.
- Preserve the documented *Batavia* outcome: seven island executions; Wouter
  Loos and Jan Pelgrom as the two men put ashore; other offenders returned to
  Batavia. Rook is fictionalised among the returned offenders, not substituted
  for either marooned man.
- Tom touches the thimble once in 1635 and leaves it in the gap. The Cache
  remains intact.
