# Volume One — Tom and Maggie Relationship Spine

Status: governing development decision for *The Venture*.

## Emotional premise

Tom and Maggie are the first sustained relationship in the cycle. The reader
meets Tom at twenty-two and leaves him in old age. The Prospect is where Maggie
and Tom repeatedly find one another between voyages, each time older and less
able to pretend that the other is merely a keeper or customer.

They never call one another mother and son. Maggie never says she is proud of
him. Her love appears as food, scolding, accurate disappointment, the keeping of
his mother's thimble and her refusal to let either his courage or his shame
become theatrical. Tom repairs what is broken in her house, brings her things she
did not ask for, notices when lifting has begun to hurt her, and returns even
when he knows she will see through the man he has become.

The relationship must contain real moral friction. Maggie is not an endlessly
approving sanctuary. Tom is not kept innocent by the author's affection for him.
Company service makes him competent, useful and at one point morally smaller.
Maggie's disappointment matters because her pride has mattered.

## Six-part architecture

| Part | Year | Tom | Maggie | Irreversible movement |
|---|---:|---|---|---|
| I — The Boy Who Signed | 1603 | Twenty-two, coastwise seaman, inherited debt, brave before he understands cost | Strong, guarded, sees her absent son in him and refuses to sentimentalise it | Tom signs and leaves his mother's thimble “till I'm back” |
| II — Between Ships | 1606 | Returns from Middleton's second voyage alive and signs again | Recognises him before allowing herself to show relief | She refuses to return the thimble because being ashore is not the same as being home |
| III — An Able Man | 1610 | Boatswain's mate after the third voyage; father's debt finally cleared | Proud of his competence and frightened by how naturally he now leaves | Tom repairs the tavern and chooses another voyage freely, no longer under debt |
| IV — The Company Man | 1614 | Hardened by command and able to describe abandonment as necessity | Refuses to let survival excuse the habit of converting people into entries | Their first real rupture: Maggie tells him the Company has taught him to look past a man |
| V — The True Copy | 1626 | Middle-aged, no longer performing hardness, helps Daniel resist the Company's purchase of silence | Old, slower, still morally exact | Tom chooses a broken man over advancement; Maggie calls him an idiot and means she is proud |
| VI — Till You're Back | 1657 | Seventy-six, permanently ashore | Dead, present through the keeper she trained and the care she taught | Tom holds the thimble, returns it to the Cache and feeds the next boy with a paper |

The 1657 coda deliberately follows the Company's transition to permanent joint
stock. The institution learns to continue beyond individual voyages at the same
moment the novel shows a human relationship continuing beyond one of its lives.
The two continuities are moral opposites: one preserves capital; the other
preserves care.

## Guardrails

- Do not have either character use “mother,” “son,” “proud of you” or a deathbed
  declaration.
- Keep every decisive encounter in the Prospect or at its immediate river edge.
- Distant Company history enters through Tom's body, pay papers, shipmates,
  testimony and choices—not detached historical excursions.
- Tom may become skilled and respected, but not an officer-gentleman. His arc is
  a foremast hand becoming boatswain's mate and later boatswain.
- Maggie's missing Swedish son remains his own person. Tom does not replace him.
- Tom reclaims the thimble only long enough to hold it in 1657, then returns it
  to the gap. The Cache remains intact.
- The fixed visual cast remains unchanged. Age Tom and Maggie through posture,
  hair, hands, clothes and accumulated wear without redesigning their faces.
