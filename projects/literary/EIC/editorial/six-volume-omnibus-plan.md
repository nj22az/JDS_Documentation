# Six-Volume Omnibus Plan

Decision locked: 12 July 2026.

*The Front-Row Seat* will become a cycle of six self-contained historical novels.
Each volume must give a first-time reader a complete story without requiring the
anthology, another volume or knowledge of the shared chronology. Once all six
books are complete, they may also be issued in chronological order as a single
omnibus.

The deployed anthology remains the canon source and compressed map. It is not to
be overwritten while the novels are drafted.

## Working volume map

| Volume | Working title | Seed period | Governing material |
|---|---|---|---|
| One | *The Venture* | 1603–1657 | Maggie, Tom's life between voyages, Daniel's return and the first keeper succession |
| Two | *The Gallows Years* | 1696–1701 | Esther Finch, Every's wake, Kidd and Fenner's testimony |
| Three | *Kings of Bengal* | 1757–1790 | Plassey, famine, the Company bailout, Boston and the Bounty |
| Four | *The Poppy* | 1839–1880 | Mei, the opium trade, the Company's formal death and the *Cutty Sark* |
| Five | ***The Watchman's Daughter*** | 1888–1892 | Su Zhang's complete story |
| Six | *A Warden's Watch* | 1940–2019 | Gwen, Hannah, the Blitz, the Cache and the modern reckoning |

Only *The Watchman's Daughter* is a fixed novel title at this stage. The other
titles and exact boundaries remain working labels until each volume receives its
own full outline.

### Volume One relationship decision

Tom does not disappear after signing. Volume One follows six returns to the
Prospect from his youth to old age, making Tom and Maggie the cycle's first
sustained relationship. Their bond is mother-and-son in action but never in
label: food, repair, scolding, pride withheld from speech, real disappointment
and the thimble kept “till I'm back.” Maggie's last living scene with Tom occurs
in 1626. A 1657 coda returns the old Tom to the keeper line after Maggie's death
and leaves the thimble in the Cache. See
`editorial/volume-one-tom-maggie-relationship-spine.md` and
`manuscript-editorial/volume-one-the-venture-integrated.md`.

## Binding spine and drift rule

The six books do not have equal freedom of location or historical emphasis.

| Volumes | Dramatic centre | East India Company status | Geographic freedom |
|---|---|---|---|
| One–Four | The Prospect of Whitby and its immediate Wapping river world | Primary historical engine and first organising subject | Restricted: distant events arrive through witnesses, letters, cargo, records and remembered experience |
| Five — *The Watchman's Daughter* | Su Zhang's lived East End: household, laundry, chandlery, Prospect, streets and alley | Legacy only; the Company no longer acts after dissolution | Deliberately free to leave the Prospect and remain with Su |
| Six | The Prospect remains the home and return point, but London and the docklands may become full stages | Legacy and modern recurrence only | Free to drift when Gwen, the Cache, Blitz London or the modern city requires it |

### Volumes One–Four: Prospect and Company first

Every chapter must satisfy both tests:

1. **Why the Prospect?** The chapter's decisive testimony, conflict, discovery or
   choice must occur at the tavern, within its household or at its immediate
   river edge.
2. **Why the Company?** The East India Company must be the active cause, employer,
   beneficiary, adversary, institution or commercial system driving the chapter.

The quay, warehouses, foreshore and nearby yards may operate as the Prospect's
wings. Bengal, the Atlantic, China and the Pacific enter through people and
evidence. A chapter may briefly inhabit an owned memory, but it may not leave
Wapping merely to display researched history.

Volume Four contains the formal 1858 dissolution and therefore carries the
transition. Its later *Cutty Sark* material remains Prospect-first and shows the
Company's routes, methods and commercial world continuing under other names. It
does not treat the dissolved Company as an active legal body.

### Volumes Five–Six: the permitted drift

Su's book is the cycle's deliberate formal break. The camera may follow her
through Whitechapel and Wapping because her life—not the tavern's ability to
receive testimony—now governs the story. The Prospect remains part of her home
world, but it is no longer a compulsory container for every decisive action.

Volume Six inherits this freedom. It may move through wartime streets, docks,
shelters, the Cache's history and modern Canary Wharf. The Prospect must remain
the place to which the cycle returns and against which the changed city is read,
not a device that forces every scene behind the same bar.

## The Company after 1858

After dissolution, the East India Company never acts in the narrative present.
It survives through legacy:

- **people:** pensions, careers, fortunes, training, habits and silences;
- **institutions:** Crown administration, racial categories, policing assumptions
  and professional authority inherited from imperial practice;
- **material London:** docks, warehouses, shipping routes, family businesses and
  wealth built through Company trade;
- **records:** categories that preserve official actions while losing their human
  cost;
- **behaviour:** extraction, distance, classification and the belief that correct
  procedure removes personal responsibility.

Dr Reginald Cray is one such legacy. He is a former Company surgeon whose pension,
authority, habits of observation and emotional detachment survived the
institution that rewarded them. He is not the Company made flesh, and Company
service is not a diagnosis or proof that he committed the Whitechapel murders.
Su deals with the man attacking a woman in front of her; the older institution
explains part of the power he carries, not the moral justification for her act.

## Reader contract for every volume

Each book must contain:

- its own protagonist or governing ensemble, dramatic question and irreversible
  choice;
- enough historical and social context to stand without an external timeline;
- a beginning that establishes every relationship needed for its climax;
- an ending that resolves the book's emotional and moral movement;
- no cliffhanger whose answer exists only in another volume;
- no shared object, ancestor or tavern tradition that must be recognised from an
  earlier book to understand the action.

The Prospect, the Thames, the Cache, the keeper line, the fuchsia and implicit
ancestry may cross between books. They are rewards for an omnibus reader, never
missing instructions for a standalone reader.

For clarity in project documents, **volume** or **book** means one of the six
standalone novels. **Part** means an internal division inside one novel.

## Volume Five: The Watchman's Daughter

Su's novel answers one complete question: can she inherit the watchman's burden
without turning restraint into passivity or grief into vengeance?

The book begins with Su already living a full life inside the Zhang household.
It establishes the practice, the four rules, Lee, Liz, Kate, the laundry, the
chandlery, the Prospect and Cray before any of them are required by the climax.
The alley ends the external threat. The chapters after it resolve Su's bodily
shock, her parents' knowledge and her changed understanding of the rules. The
1892 epilogue shows her choosing an ordinary future rather than receiving one as
a reward.

The physician's case remains unopened and Cray's possible connection to the
Whitechapel murders remains unanswered. This is deliberate moral closure, not a
missing solution: Su acts on the assault she witnesses and refuses to invent a
justification after the fact.

The Company's role in this volume is entirely posthumous. Historical explanation
is admitted only when it changes a living relationship or present danger: Cray's
pension and professional authority, the Zhang family's dockside economy, the
racial order Su must calculate and the records that would believe an English
gentleman before her. There are no free-standing Company-history chapters.

## Volume Six: A Warden's Watch

The final book may drift after Su's formal break, but it must distinguish legacy
from repetition. Gwen's wartime London, the inherited Cache and Jerry's modern
corporate thinking may rhyme with Company history without claiming they are the
same institution.

The Prospect functions as home, archive and final point of comparison. The book
may leave it; the omnibus must return to it.

Jerry Vale is the final book's active modern antagonist. His fictional fund buys
distressed debt attached to the Prospect and offers to preserve the building by
separating its property, operation and historical rights. He understands Company
history and admires its structural survival rather than its violence. The final
conflict is therefore not history against ignorance, but public memory against a
man who wants to own and curate the warning.
