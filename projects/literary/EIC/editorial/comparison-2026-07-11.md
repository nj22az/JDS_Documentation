## Material from the live canon worth retaining

1. **The Wager.** Tom now makes a consequential choice before signing, Mara acts
   for herself, and Maggie makes the room choose what sort of community it is.
   This gives the opening immediate drama and makes the Company's paper/debt
   theme physical rather than merely discussed.
2. **The Deposition and The True Copy.** These are among the strongest additions.
   Daniel watches a truthful document become a lie, then puts his own account
   into the Cache. The addition strengthens character, theme and the 2019 payoff
   simultaneously.
3. **The expanded human detail in 1888.** The opening walk, Kate's unfinished
   song, the shirt and the ageing of Wei's hands make Su's world tangible. The
   revised accidental encounter also keeps “never first” morally intact.
4. **The clearer part divisions.** They make the chronological design easier to
   understand and give readers places to breathe.
5. **The live Foreword.** The author's engineering and seafaring vantage point is
   commercially useful and gives the book an honest reason for existing.

## Legacy discipline worth restoring

1. **Maggie's ancestry was previously implicit.** The live opening directly tells
   the reader that her Swedish line will return as Johansson. The earlier version
   trusted the shared Gothenburg origin and left the connection unstated. Restore
   that restraint; it is more moving and matches the author's original intention.
2. **The Boston Man is dramatically overextended.** James Holman has good details,
   but the scene explains the politics, completes several motif connections and
   supplies polished modern interpretation for almost 2,000 words. Retain the
   eyewitness link between Wapping and Boston, the whale-line image and Naylor's
   personal question; cut the additional Canton genealogy and roughly half the
   speech.
3. **The 1888 centrepiece now outweighs the book around it.** At 13,236 words it
   is more than six times the length of *The Hell Ship* and more than one fifth of
   the narrative. Retain the story but remove repeated interpretation in “The
   Practice” and “The Alley.” A target of 10,000–11,000 words would preserve its
   status as the centrepiece without turning the preceding book into a preface.
4. **The Ghost of Bengal makes a whisper into a mechanism.** The implied survival
   of Cray and Dr Heron's parentage are intriguing, but the connection makes the
   multi-century design feel engineered and partially reopens an ending whose
   power comes from uncertainty. Test this as optional back matter before keeping
   it in the main narrative.
5. **The Character Map explains every hidden thread.** It is useful editorially
   but removes the reader's pleasure of discovery. Keep it as an internal control
   document or clearly marked spoiler appendix, not ordinary narrative back matter.

## Language audit

The prose is clear, literary and accessible. Its present difficulty is density,
not vocabulary. Across the 62,390-word live narrative there are:

- 717 em dashes;
- 202 uses of “the way”;
- 100 uses of “the river”;
- 166 uses of “nothing”;
- 428 negative constructions such as “does not,” “did not” and “is not.”

None is objectionable by itself. Together they create a recognisable cadence that
becomes predictable. The line edit should therefore cut repetition selectively,
not flatten the voice. The governing rule is: when the scene has already conveyed
the meaning, remove the sentence that explains it again.

## Historical-control flags for the fact-check pass

- The 1858 chapter promises Harding a Distinguished Service Order, but the DSO
  was instituted in 1886. Replace it with a period-appropriate honour or promotion.
- The repeated claim that only 43 of approximately 500 men returned from
  Lancaster's 1601 Company voyage is not supported by the source reviewed. Royal
  Museums Greenwich records more than 100 deaths from 480 men before the fleet had
  rounded the Cape. The editorial opening now uses only that supported formulation.
- “Dutch courage” should not be spoken as an established expression in 1603. The
  Oxford English Dictionary dates the noun much later; its related “pot-valour” is
  first evidenced in 1623. The title can function as retrospective wordplay, but
  the phrase and invented origin story have been removed from Hendricks's dialogue.
- The tavern site existed, but *Prospect of Whitby* is a later name. In the 1603
  presentation, identify it as a Wapping tavern rather than by its modern name.
- Fictional epigraphs must be visibly distinguished from historical quotations,
  or replaced with sourced period material.

[Royal Museums Greenwich — dangers of Company service](https://www.rmg.co.uk/stories/maritime-history/library-archive/shipwreck-sickness-pirates-dangers-working-east-india)

These are preliminary flags, not a substitute for the full historical audit.

## Recommended editorial target

Retain the live canon's plot, characters and added dramatic material. Aim for a
finished narrative of approximately 56,000–58,000 words by removing explanation,
duplicated thematic conclusions and over-completed connections. Do not simplify
the vocabulary. Preserve the present-tense, tavern-centred voice, but let each
character's social position and century affect the rhythm of speech.

The first practical comparison will use the 1603 opening. It will retain *The
Wager*, restore the subtle ancestry treatment, remove unsupported history, and
show what a commercially accessible line edit can achieve without turning the
book into ordinary modern prose.
