# Jerry Vale — Character Brief

## Function

Gerald “Jerry” Vale is the final active antagonist of *The Front-Row Seat* cycle.
He is not descended from a Company director and does not require a secret family
connection. His inheritance is institutional: he understands the East India
Company's methods and deliberately values the parts that survived scandal,
famine, insolvency and state intervention.

Tom begins the cycle by signing because he cannot see the machine beyond the
paper. Jerry ends it having studied the whole machine and choosing it because he
can see how well it protects capital from consequence. The Tom-and-Jerry echo is
present in their first names and final structural opposition; characters never
announce it as a joke.

## Public identity

- Early forties in 2019.
- English hedge-fund manager at fictional Albion Reach Capital.
- Polished, healthy, attentive and socially effortless.
- Uses “Jerry” because informality removes friction; legal documents say Gerald.
- Reads history seriously. He cannot be defeated by supplying a fact he omitted.
- Treats moral language as less precise than financial language, then uses
  financial precision to keep moral responsibility unassigned.

## Active scheme

Albion Reach buys a portfolio of distressed pub-company debt. The Prospect's
portion is isolated inside Wapping Heritage Holdings Number Two. Jerry proposes:

1. publicly supported conservation work;
2. transfer of the freehold as security;
3. sale-and-leaseback to the existing operator;
4. scheduled rent increases that the ordinary pub cannot sustain;
5. private dining and members' space upstairs;
6. a separate brand company controlling the Prospect name and heritage rights;
7. acquisition of Cache photographs, transcripts, adaptations and commercial
   use as proprietary historical content.

The pub remains open. That is how Jerry can truthfully claim to have saved it
while extracting the people, affordability and uncontrolled memory that made it
valuable.

## Corruption

Jerry's corruption is demonstrated, not announced:

- Albion Reach acquires neighbouring leases before a public conservation
  programme is announced.
- He refers to unnamed “constructive conversations” with stakeholders.
- Public grants repair the asset while his fund retains the appreciation.
- Ownership is divided across entities so responsibility is always elsewhere.
- He offers Hannah money and a ceremonial custodian role in exchange for rights.
- When she refuses, he reminds her that his fund controls the operator that
  employs her without phrasing the statement as a threat.

No real fund, pub company, official or grant programme is implicated. All
transactional entities and corrupt relationships in the draft are fictional.

## Personal kindness and structural harm

Jerry gives Abdi Warsame twenty pounds for a wasted delivery. Three months
earlier he approved a cost memorandum that removed paid waiting time and shifted
vehicle expenses onto drivers in Abdi's delivery group.

The kindness is genuine. It changes nothing. This connects Jerry to Aldridge:
both use personal decency as evidence that they cannot be responsible for
institutional harm.

## Relationship to Company history

Jerry knows Plassey, the famine, the bailout, the Tea Act and the opium trade. He
does not admire the violence. He admires the structure's ability to survive the
violence, preserve capital and make itself indispensable to the state.

His governing formulation is:

> “The Company conquered countries. We allocate capital.”

He believes the distinction is morally complete. The novel shows it is legally
important and morally insufficient.

## Outcome

Jerry wins materially. The transaction proceeds, rents rise and the Prospect's
history becomes branded heritage. Hannah prevents a complete victory by
documenting and dividing the Cache so no single owner can suppress or monetise
the whole record.

The ending avoids the fantasy that exposing hypocrisy defeats concentrated
capital. Jerry's failure is narrower: he acquires the property but not exclusive
ownership of its memory.

## Voice guardrails

- Never make him shout, sneer at workers or confess that he is evil.
- Never make him ignorant merely so another character can lecture him.
- He prefers distinctions, structures, incentives and precise verbs.
- He answers the narrow question accurately and leaves its human implication
  outside the category.
- His humour is real, his manners are good and his threat is usually grammatical.
