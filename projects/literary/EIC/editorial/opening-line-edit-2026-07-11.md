# Opening Line-Edit — 1603

## Status

This is a demonstration edit of the two 1603 narrative units. The protected live
canon remains unchanged. The revised prose is in `manuscript-editorial/` and is
intended for approval before the same method is applied to the rest of the book.

| Unit | Live canon | Editorial copy | Change |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| *The Boy Who Signed* | 3,623 words | 2,498 words | −1,125 (−31.1%) |
| *Dutch Courage* | 3,109 words | 2,103 words | −1,006 (−32.4%) |
| **1603 opening** | **6,732 words** | **4,601 words** | **−2,131 (−31.7%)** |

The count includes headings, captions and source comments, so it is a comparison
measure rather than a typeset-book count.

## What the edit keeps

- Maggie's authority and Swedish origin;
- Tom's hunger for the sea and his decision to sign;
- the full dramatic function of the Wager, including Mara's own resistance;
- the returned Dragon sailors, Hendricks and the competing charters;
- the thimble, the marks beneath the counter and the fight-versus-Company line;
- the present-tense, oral quality of the narration.

## What the edit changes

- Removes the explicit Maggie-to-Johansson genealogy. The connection can now be
  discovered rather than announced.
- Cuts repeated explanations after images and actions have already made the point.
- Reduces Maggie's history to the facts that affect the scene, avoiding a second
  miniature novel before Tom's conflict begins.
- Compresses Hendricks's warning while preserving his strongest dialogue.
- Resolves an unclear pronoun at the tavern door that accidentally gave Tom
  Hendricks's memory of Maggie's arrival.
- Removes the spoken origin story of “Dutch courage”. The title may remain as a
  retrospective authorial phrase, but a character should not use it in 1603.
- Replaces the modern tavern name in the epigraph with “a Wapping tavern”.

## Historical controls applied

The live text's repeated claim that only 43 of roughly 500 men returned from the
first Company fleet has been removed. Royal Museums Greenwich states that more
than 100 of 480 men were dead by the time Lancaster's fleet rounded the Cape; it
does not support the 43-survivor figure. The edited opening uses only the supported
formulation. See [Royal Museums Greenwich: dangers of Company service](https://www.rmg.co.uk/stories/maritime-history/library-archive/shipwreck-sickness-pirates-dangers-working-east-india).

The Oxford English Dictionary dates the noun “Dutch courage” much later than the
scene; even the related “pot-valour” is first evidenced in 1623. Hendricks therefore
offers Tom genever without naming the later expression. See the
[OED entry for *Dutch*](https://www.oed.com/dictionary/dutch_adj).

The present name *Prospect of Whitby* belongs to the tavern's later history. The
site may still be used, but the 1603 characters should not call it by that name.

## Editorial judgement

The revised opening is faster and clearer without becoming simpler in thought or
vocabulary. The Wager now arrives roughly a thousand words earlier, and the two
chapters no longer state the same mortality argument several times. A final pass
should decide one book-wide matter: either identify fictional epigraphs globally
in the front matter, or replace them with sourced period material.

If this level of compression feels right, use it most strongly in the five expanded
areas identified in the comparison report. Chapters already close to the legacy
length need a lighter pass.
