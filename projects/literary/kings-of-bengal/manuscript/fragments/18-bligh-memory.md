# Chapter Eighteen Fragment

## The Man Tregowan Knew

### Wapping, 1790

“You knew Bligh.”

Bess said it from behind the bar, loudly enough to stop three other
conversations and start five.

Elias Tregowan kept his eyes on the launch dimensions Samuel had copied from the
paper. Twenty-three feet from stem to stern, if the report was right. Nineteen
men when the boat left the ship. Water, bread, a quadrant, a compass and the
clothes they had been permitted to keep. Every man in the Prospect had already
crossed the Pacific in her twice and drowned once, all without leaving his
stool.

“I worked on two vessels he sailed,” Elias said.

“That is knowing him,” said the cooper by the fire.

“It is knowing him at work.”

“Was he a devil?”

“Not at a pump.”

The room disliked this answer. It had been promised a monster or a vindication
and had received a qualification.

Bess put the newspaper beside Elias's hand. “Which vessels?”

“*Resolution*. Then *Bounty*.”

The second name quietened the room properly.

In 1776 Elias had been called to Deptford for a water-handling job small enough
that no account of Cook's voyage would ever contain it. *Resolution* had come
back from the southern ocean with the habits of an old ship and was being asked
to become a new one again. Men covered her decks, each improving a different
part of her and cursing every other improvement for occupying the same space.

Bligh was twenty-two and already carried himself like a man late for a tide no
one else had noticed. Sailing master, Elias had been told. Young for it. Too
young, someone added, which was how Elias knew the appointment had been earned.

He found Bligh beside the main pump with three measurements written on a scrap.

“Which one is yours?” Bligh asked.

“None yet.”

“Good. They disagree.”

Elias measured the worn piece, then the replacement, then the place where the
replacement was meant to work. The old part had lost material. The new part
matched the list. The ship matched neither.

“Which do you intend to alter?” Bligh asked.

“The cheapest one that is wrong.”

Bligh's mouth moved very slightly. Elias did not know for another ten years that
this had been amusement.

The young master remained while Elias worked. He asked what changed under load,
how the handle would tell a man below that the leather was beginning to fail and
which spare could be made aboard without a yard. He interrupted vague answers.
He remembered exact ones. When an officer assured him the pump would be adequate
for ordinary service, Bligh asked the man to describe an ordinary ocean.

Elias liked him for that.

He liked him less when Bligh corrected a measurement Elias had given from
memory—and was right.

Eleven years later they met again aboard a small purchased ship at Deptford.
Bligh looked older, though not gentler. The great cabin no longer belonged to
the captain. It was becoming a house for plants: frames, racks and places for
water to go without rotting the ship that carried it. Breadfruit, they said.
More than a thousand pots if the plan was fulfilled.

Elias worked where living water met dead timber. He considered drainage, weight,
access and what a man could repair at sea. Bligh moved through the conversion
with the same exhausting attention he had brought to *Resolution*. He remembered
Elias before Elias was certain he remembered him.

“Tregowan. The cheapest thing that is wrong.”

“Lieutenant. The man who measures other men's answers.”

Bligh showed his teeth. This time Elias recognised it.

They argued over an access opening. Elias wanted room for a man's shoulders and
tools. The plant frames wanted the same inches. Bligh demanded both. Elias told
him timber did not answer to command. Bligh replied that neither did the Pacific
and he was expected to cross it.

The opening was made. The racks lost space for several pots. Bligh accepted the
loss after making Elias prove every inch of it.

At no point did Elias ask why the breadfruit was wanted in the West Indies.

He knew the pumps. He knew the drainage. He knew how water would be carried to
the plants and taken away before it found the vessel's seams. The destination
was written on another man's paper. The purpose belonged to gentlemen. Elias
had work enough without borrowing responsibility from an empire.

That had been his belief in 1787.

In the Prospect three years later, Bess waited with the pencil Samuel could no
longer hold for long.

“Well?” the cooper demanded. “Devil or no?”

Elias looked at the dimensions of the launch.

“He could navigate,” he said. “He could remember. He could work longer than the
men beside him and then be angry that they required sleep. He would hear a loose
word as another man hears a loose fastening.”

“That sounds a devil to me.”

“It sounds a sailing master.”

“And the mutiny?” Bess asked.

“I was in Deptford.”

“You knew him.”

“Yes.” Elias moved one stiff finger along the pencilled length of the launch.
“That is why I know the difference between a man and an explanation.”

He asked Bess to draw the boat to scale. She did. Then they began placing the
water, bread and bodies inside it, not to prove that Bligh was good, nor that his
men were wicked, but to understand what nineteen people had survived.

When the arithmetic was finished, Elias returned to the *Bounty*.

“There is another measurement,” he said.

Bess looked up.

“The plants had the great cabin. The men did not. We measured every drain and
every opening. I never measured what the purpose required of them.”

“Would Bligh have told you?”

“He would have told me exactly.”

“Then why did you not ask?”

Elias looked at his hands.

“Because the work passed.”
