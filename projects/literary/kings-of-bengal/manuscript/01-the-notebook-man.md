# Chapter One

## The Notebook Man

### Wapping, 1757

The pump worked perfectly while the ship was tied to the quay.

This was the first fact, and the least useful.

Four men turned the cranks. The chain came up through the trunk with a wet,
regular clatter, each leather drawing its portion of Thames water into the
trough. The water ran black for a moment, then brown, then clear enough for the
foreman to pronounce the matter finished. He stood with one boot on a coil of
new rope and watched the stream as if he had produced it himself.

“There,” he said. “You see?”

Elias Tregowan did see. He had seen the new leathers fitted, the chain shortened by
one link, the upper wheel brought square and the trunk sounded for obstruction.
He had measured what could be measured. The handles no longer struck at the top
of their turn. The chain did not climb the teeth. No water returned where it
ought not. If the ship had intended to remain forever against Wapping stairs,
the repair would have been beyond criticism.

The man beside him shook his head.

He was young, perhaps Elias's age or younger, though the passage had pared him
down until age was difficult to place. His name on the ship's articles was Rahim
Ali. The mate called him Ram because the mate preferred one-syllable errors.
Rahim had corrected him twice, then stopped spending the effort.

“It does not make that sound,” Rahim said.

The foreman looked at the water. “It makes precisely that sound.”

“Here.”

“Where else would you like us to try it?”

Rahim pointed downriver, where the anchored ships sat to the tide with their
yards made thin by the morning mist. “There.”

The foreman laughed. Two of the men at the cranks laughed with him. The third
kept turning and watched Rahim.

Elias put a hand on the trunk. The oak carried every knock into his palm. He
could distinguish the upper wheel from the chain, the chain from the leathers,
and the leathers from the small complaint of timber that had spent six months
crossing oceans and did not enjoy being told it was home. All was regular.

“What sound?” he asked.

Rahim looked at him before answering. Elias recognised the calculation. It was
the look of a man deciding whether a question had been asked in order to receive
an answer.

“Three strokes even,” Rahim said. “Then one knock below. When she rolls to
larboard. Worse after the pepper came aft.”

“The pepper has been discharged.”

“Yes.”

“And she is not rolling.”

“Yes.”

The foreman spread his hands. “Then we have cured it two ways.”

This produced more laughter. Rahim did not look at him. He watched Elias instead.

Elias disliked being watched when a mechanism had already given him its answer.
He disliked still more that Rahim had not said the repair was bad. He had said
the test was incomplete.

He held up one hand. The crank men stopped. Water continued down the trough and
then ceased, leaving the stink of the bilge sharper in the cold air.

“Bring the old leathers,” Elias said.

The foreman sighed. “Tregowan, we have a lighter waiting for the tea. The
examiner has signed the discharge. This is paid work you are unfinishing.”

“Then it will cost nothing to look.”

That was not true, and everyone knew it. Looking was rarely free. Looking found
causes, and causes had owners.

One of the yard boys brought the discarded leathers in a shallow box. They were
dark with water and grease, eight broad discs cut to travel within the pump
trunk. Seven were worn around their edges in the ordinary way. The eighth had
been shaved almost flat on one side. Elias set it against the new pattern. The
yard replacement was round and handsome. The old one looked careless.

“Sea work,” the foreman said. “Cut with a galley knife, by the look of it.”

Rahim crouched. “By my knife.”

“There you are.”

Elias turned the leather over. Two holes had been tried before the chain pin
found its place. The final hole was not central. Bad work in a yard. Aboard a
rolling ship with water rising in the well, it might have been the only work
possible.

“Why off-centre?” Elias asked.

Rahim reached for the piece, then stopped short of touching it. “The chain pulled
to this side below. If round, it caught. If cut here, it passed.”

“Because the trunk is out.”

“Because the ship is out,” Rahim said. “With the after hold full.”

The foreman made a noise through his nose. “The ship is built to carry cargo.”

“Then perhaps,” Elias said, “we should test the pump as if she carried it.”

The foreman stared at him. Elias felt no triumph. He had been wrong thirty
seconds earlier and was now only wrong more accurately.

He lowered himself through the hatch with a lantern, past the empty spaces where
pepper, saltpetre and silk had made a second dark architecture inside the hull.
Rahim followed without being invited. In the pump well the air was close and
sour. Elias set the lantern behind the trunk, then along it. The upper length was
true. Lower down, near the step, an old repair had drawn one timber inward. Not
enough to stop a free chain in a light ship. Enough, perhaps, when loaded trim
changed the line and a larboard roll brought the chain against it.

Rahim tapped the place with one fingernail: three light taps, then a heavier one.

“That,” he said.

Elias put his ear to the wood. Above them, boots crossed the deck and carts
grumbled along the quay. The hull turned every sound into a possible symptom.

“How long?”

“After Madras. Not before.”

“What changed at Madras?”

“Cargo aft. One link taken from the chain. Mate said it was slack.”

The shortened chain had not made the fault. The drawn timber had not made it
alone. The loaded trim had not made it alone. Each explanation could be entered
truthfully, and each would conceal the failure if it were allowed to stand by
itself.

Elias held the lantern while Rahim showed him how the handle kicked when the
chain caught. It was a small motion, transmitted through decks and distance. A
man who turned the crank for ten minutes at a quay would call it nothing. A man
who turned it for two hours while water climbed past his ankles would know its
name.

“We put back your shape,” Elias said.

“No.” Rahim pointed to the inward timber. “You make that true. Then round is
good.”

Elias looked at him.

Rahim's face did not change. “At sea we had no timber.”

It was not modesty. It was the clean separation of a temporary repair from a
cause. Elias felt, with some irritation, respect arrive.

Above them, the foreman shouted that the Company's time was being stolen. Elias
shouted back that the Company might come below and recover it.

* * *

Samuel Naylor heard this exchange from the quay and wrote down none of it.

He had been given a ruled sheet, a tally stick and an instruction simple enough
to survive contact with the morning: verify the marks on twelve bales of raw
silk before they passed into the warehouse. He was nineteen, clean because his
mother had brushed his coat in the doorway, and present because Mr Aldridge had
needed a clerk who could add without moving his lips.

Samuel could add very quickly while moving nothing at all.

The eighth bale bore a Calcutta mark, a private merchant's device and two
weights. One, painted near the rope, read one hundred and eighty-six pounds. The
other, entered on the Company's receiving ticket, read one hundred and
seventy-nine.

He weighed it twice. The beam settled at one hundred and eighty-six.

“Seven pounds over,” he told the warehouse porter.

“Seven pounds wrapping,” the porter said.

“The wrapping is on the scale.”

“Which is why it is taken off in the book.”

Samuel looked at the bale. The matting, rope and protective outer cloth might
indeed weigh seven pounds. The merchant had marked what arrived. The receiver
had entered what could be charged, stored or sold. Both numbers described the
same object. Neither number was false.

“Which do I certify?”

The porter laughed without pleasure. “Whichever man pays you.”

Samuel did not find this useful. He entered both weights in the margin and drew
a line between them. The line comforted him. It said that the difference had not
escaped notice.

Mr Edmund Aldridge arrived as Elias and Rahim came up from the hold. Aldridge was
not yet rich enough to dress badly. His coat was sober blue, his wig plain, his
shoes constructed for floors rather than mud. He carried no papers. Other men
carried papers toward him.

“Naylor,” he said. “Why has the eighth bale stopped?”

Samuel showed him the two figures.

Aldridge read them once. “The gross mark belongs to the voyage. The receiving
weight belongs to us.”

“Which is its true weight, sir?”

“Both, properly named.”

He said it kindly. That made the answer harder to resist.

“And if the wrapping weighs six pounds?”

“Then someone has gained a pound of silk or lost a pound of confidence. You may
open the bale and discover which, but the merchant will claim you damaged it,
the warehouse will miss its lighter, and twelve men will wait while you defend
the purity of arithmetic.” Aldridge glanced toward the ship. “Or you may note
the variance, as you have done, and allow the work to continue.”

Samuel looked at his line. It seemed smaller now.

“You noticed,” Aldridge said. “That is the beginning of usefulness. A clerk who
notices everything and completes nothing is only another obstruction.”

Behind him the foreman was objecting to a repair he had approved half an hour
before. Elias had the old leather in one hand. Rahim stood beside him in the thin
cotton jacket he had worn off the ship. No one appeared to have remembered that
London in November required different cloth.

“Will the pump delay unloading?” Aldridge asked.

“No,” Elias said.

The foreman said, at the same moment, “Yes.”

Aldridge considered them. “Mr Tregowan?”

Elias noticed the name. “The pump passes here. It did not pass at sea. We know
why. I will make the lower trunk true after discharge.”

“Can she move safely before then?”

“In the river, light, yes.”

“Can you sign that?”

Elias's eyes went to Rahim, then to the ship. “I can sign what I said.”

Aldridge smiled slightly. “A valuable distinction. Write precisely that and no
more.”

Samuel expected him to be angered by the delay. Instead Aldridge listened to
Rahim's account, asked the mate when the chain had been shortened and ordered the
discarded leather kept with the repair note. He did all of this without raising
his voice or calling any man a liar. Samuel saw at once why people trusted him.

Then Aldridge told the foreman to keep the lighter waiting without charge to the
Company, and Samuel saw why people feared him.

“Naylor,” he said, “Mr Hollis requires a note-taker in Wapping this afternoon.
You have a clear hand.”

Samuel tried not to show pleasure. Nathaniel Hollis was a member of Parliament.
Samuel's mother had once stitched new cuffs onto a coat for a man who had seen
him enter a carriage.

“What am I to record?”

“A soldier's recollection of Bengal. Sergeant Coates. Mr Hollis is considering a
question for the House concerning presents received by Company servants.”

At the word Bengal, Rahim turned.

It was not a large movement. Samuel noticed because Aldridge noticed.

“You were there?” Aldridge asked him.

Rahim looked toward the mate, who had begun supervising something at the other
end of the quay with great attention.

“On the river,” Rahim said.

“At Calcutta?”

“At Hugli. At Kasimbazar. Many places.”

“At Plassey?” Samuel asked.

Rahim's eyes moved to him. “Near.”

Samuel had his pencil ready before he understood that he had reached for it.

“What did you see?”

“Today?” Rahim said. “A bad test.”

Elias looked down so that the beginning of a smile belonged to nobody else.

Aldridge did not smile. “Mr Hollis asked for the sergeant. His interest is the
conduct of British officers.”

There was no insult in the sentence. It merely placed Rahim's knowledge in a
category for which the requested paper had no column.

Samuel put the pencil away.

* * *

The Prospect of Whitby stood close enough to the river to appear, at high water,
like another vessel that had decided not to sail.

Samuel had passed it many times and entered twice. His mother did not object to
ale but objected to men who allowed ale to become biography. The Prospect had a
long memory for such men. Its beams were older than anyone who drank beneath
them. Its floor leaned toward the Thames as if listening.

The landlord, William Finch, was broad enough to make the space behind the bar
look temporary. His sister stood beside him with a chalk stub and a slate.
Eleanor Finch was known as Nell to everyone except men attempting charm. To
those men she was generally Miss Finch until they left.

She looked first at Samuel's shoes, then at the satchel.

“You have come to inspect the beer or improve the customers?”

“I am here for Sergeant Coates.”

“Then the customers are safe.”

William jerked his chin toward the third stool. Coates sat with both hands around
a tankard. His coat retained enough red to announce what the rest of him might
have preferred to forget.

Samuel chose the stool beside him and placed the notebook on the bar.

Nell set down a small cloth first.

“For the ink,” she said.

“I have not spilled any.”

“That is why the cloth is still clean.”

He moved the notebook onto it. She watched him align the edge with the bar.

“You work for Mr Hollis?” Coates asked.

“Today.”

“And after today?”

“Where I am required.”

Coates drank. “That is a soldier's answer.”

Samuel opened to the first page. “Mr Hollis wishes to understand the events at
Plassey, particularly any payments promised or made to Company servants.”

“Does he.”

“He may raise the matter in Parliament.”

“Will Parliament give me back my sleep?”

Samuel's pencil hovered. He had not been asked to record questions.

Nell reached between them, took Coates's empty tankard and replaced it with a
full one. “Write that down,” she said.

Samuel looked at her. “It is not evidence.”

“It was an answer.”

“To a question I was not instructed to ask.”

“Then your page has better manners than you do. It only hears important people.”

William told her to leave the gentleman to his business.

“He is not a gentleman,” Nell said. “Look at the cuffs.”

Samuel looked involuntarily at his mother's work. Nell saw that she had struck
something and, to her credit, did not press it.

Coates watched them both. “Your book clean?” he asked.

“Yes.”

“Then keep it that way.”

He stood. Samuel felt the interview departing before it had begun. He thought of
Mr Hollis, of Aldridge's phrase—a clerk who completes nothing—and of his mother
brushing the coat.

“I saw a bale this morning with two true weights,” he said.

Coates remained standing.

Nell stopped writing on her slate.

Samuel continued because stopping would make the sentence foolish. “One included
what carried the silk. One excluded it. Mr Aldridge said both were true, properly
named.”

“Is this how Parliament speaks now?” William asked.

Coates lowered himself onto the stool again. “What did you write?”

“Both.”

“Then write both now.”

Samuel set the pencil to the page.

Coates told him about the rain first.

Not Clive, not Mir Jafar, not the treasury. Rain on the twenty-third of June,
hard enough to close the distance between earth and sky. Company soldiers
dragging tarpaulins over powder. The Nawab's guns falling silent because their
charges were wet. A cavalry commander coming on when the men who had promised
to support him remained still. Shot passing through a man three files from
Coates, who looked down at himself and asked what had happened.

Samuel wrote until the first pencil lost its point. Nell placed a small knife on
the cloth without comment.

Coates told him of the mango grove and the mass of men across the paddy. He told
him how the great body under Mir Jafar had not advanced. From a distance it had
looked disciplined. Only later did stillness acquire its price.

“You understand,” Coates said, “the fighting was real.”

“Yes.”

“Write it. A bought field still kills the men standing on it.”

Samuel wrote it.

Coates named sums. He named promises. He named men where he knew their names and
said he did not know where he did not. The Company's victory, as it appeared in
Samuel's notebook, did not shrink. It changed shape. Three thousand had indeed
stood against many more. Twenty-two Company men had indeed died. Courage had not
been invented. It had been entered as the cause of an outcome purchased
elsewhere.

The room quietened by degrees. Not everyone listened, but enough did that the
ordinary noises began to sound deliberate: a chair dragged, a dog dreaming under
a table, fourpence disputed beside the fire.

“Mr Hollis wants the presents,” Coates said. “That is the word, is it?”

“Yes.”

“Presents. As if Bengal tied a ribbon around itself.”

William put down the glass he was drying.

Coates described the treasury. Silver carried out under guard. Fortunes large
enough to make wages absurd. A throne promised to the man whose troops remained
still. He did not claim to have stood in the room where the agreement was made.
He had stood in the rain where it became fact.

Samuel's hand began to ache. He changed his grip.

“And what did you receive?” Nell asked.

Coates looked at her.

“Nell,” William warned.

“He said write both.”

Coates's face tightened, then loosened. “My pay. Prize money. Pension if I keep
my account clean enough.”

Samuel's pencil stopped.

“Will this endanger it?” he asked.

“Now he asks,” Nell said softly.

Coates looked at the open book. “It may.”

Samuel thought of closing it. He thought of tearing out the last page and giving
Coates everything before it. He thought of Mr Hollis receiving a safe account,
accurate in each detail and empty of the fact that made the details matter.

“Why tell me?”

Coates turned the tankard by its handle. “Because the papers call it a glorious
victory. Because Clive is buying England with Bengal's money and England thinks
it has received a gift. Because men died believing the army before them was the
danger.”

He looked past Samuel toward the dark window. The tide had risen. Reflected
lanterns broke and joined on the water as boats passed.

“Plassey was not a battle,” he said. “Not in the way they mean it. It was a
transaction, with a musket, in the rain.”

Samuel wrote the sentence.

Coates watched it appear. “Now write the cost.”

“Your pension?”

“That is my cost.” Coates put one finger on the page, below the fresh line. “Do
not mistake it for the price.”

Samuel waited.

Outside, somewhere beyond the glass, a repaired pump would be quiet until men
needed it in the dark. A bale would enter a warehouse at two different weights.
A man who had been near Plassey would leave the quay because nobody had asked
for his account.

Samuel turned the page.
