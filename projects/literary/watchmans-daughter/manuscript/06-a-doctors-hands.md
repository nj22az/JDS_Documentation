# Chapter Six: A Doctor's Hands

*Thursday, 30 August 1888*

Dr Cray's hands are the first part of him the room trusts.

They rest on either side of his brandy at the Prospect, long-fingered and pale,
the nails cut square. Age has raised the veins but not disturbed their steadiness.
The rest of the doctor permits several opinions. Some find him courtly. Some find
him cold. Men who served east of the Cape recognise the habits of a Company man
before he mentions his pension and adjust their manners accordingly. Nobody
disputes the hands.

Su notices them because her father's hands have taught her to.

She is delivering the bar cloths and twelve shirts left for collection by men who
will remember them only after the third drink. Flo checks the docket behind the
counter. At the window, late sunlight lies across the Thames in a broad dirty
strip. The room has not yet learned its autumn fear. Men leave alone without
discussing the route. A woman may open the door without every head turning.

Cray sits on his usual stool with his leather physician's case at his feet and
his hat beside it. The case is good saddle leather, oiled against rain. It has
accompanied him for as long as Su remembers and interests her no more than a
carpenter's tool chest.

He is finishing the first of his two brandies when the docker comes in.

The man's name is Ned Pike. Su knows him by washing: three shirts, one red neckerchief,
collars never, payment reliable. Two mates support him through the door. His face
has gone the colour of candle grease and his right arm hangs lower than the left,
held away from his body as though it belongs to another man and has been attached
carelessly.

A sling load caught him at the dock gate. He avoided the timber and met the wall.

Flo sees the arm and puts one hand over her mouth.

Cray turns on his stool.

Did the load strike your head?

Pike says something that may be no.

Did you lose consciousness?

No.

Can you feel these?

Cray has crossed the room without hurry. He touches Pike's fingers one at a time
and asks him to move them. The fingers obey. He feels at the wrist, then places
his palm lightly against the shoulder without trying to move it.

Dislocation, he says. Anterior, I think. Sit him down.

There is a chair in the corner with one short leg, levelled by a folded playbill
so old nobody knows the performance. Flo brings a sound chair instead. Pike's
mates lower him. Sweat runs from his temple into his beard.

Cray returns to the bar and finishes his brandy.

The room dislikes this for three seconds. Su feels the dislike pass through it.
Then Cray sets down the empty glass, removes his coat and folds it over the back
of his stool. His cuffs are white. His waistcoat remains buttoned.

Pain makes the muscles defend the injury, he tells Pike. Fear makes them defend
it better. We shall persuade both that nothing interesting is about to happen.

Nothing interesting, Pike repeats, looking at his arm.

Not to me.

The answer earns a small sound from the room, not quite laughter. Pike hears it
and manages enough of a grin to join them.

Cray asks Flo for two towels and one of the long bar cloths Su has just delivered.
He folds the cloth into a broad band and passes it around Pike's chest. One mate
holds the ends behind the chair, providing counterweight. The other is sent to
the bar because concern is making him pull in the wrong direction.

Cray sits facing Pike and takes the injured arm above the wrist.

Look at me, he says.

Pike looks.

You may shout. Most men do. It will alter nothing and may improve your opinion of
the treatment.

He begins with almost no force. A steady draw, the elbow supported, the arm moved
by degrees. He talks throughout, not comfort exactly, but information: what he is
doing, what the shoulder is doing in return, when the worst part will arrive. His
thumb reads the joint through Pike's shirt.

Su watches from beside the laundry basket.

The work is familiar. Not the method, but the attention. Her father would call it
listening: pressure answered by structure, the hand learning before the eye. Cray
has the same absence of wasted movement. When Pike's muscles seize, the doctor
does not contest them. He waits, changes the angle by a fraction and begins again.

There is a small, heavy sound, like a drawer pushed home.

Pike shouts after it is finished.

His whole body changes. The grey leaves his face. The arm is his again, painful
but located, and he stares at it with the astonishment of a man receiving lost
property.

Cray releases him and reaches for the towel Flo is already holding out.

Keep it supported. No lifting. If the hand becomes cold or numb, send for me.

What do I owe you? Pike asks.

You owe your friends a drink. They carried you badly but with conviction.

And you?

Cray glances at the empty brandy glass.

When you can lift one without assistance.

The room approves. Pike's mates approve loudest. Flo pours Cray's second brandy
without being asked.

It is kindness. Su does not revise it into something else merely because she
knows another story.

Eight winters ago, the same hands came to Lee in daylight. They examined him
carefully. The questions were correct; the medicine was measured; the fee was
usual. Nothing about this shoulder makes the night visit happen. Nothing about
Lee makes the shoulder less skilfully restored.

Both facts remain.

Cray puts his coat back on. As Flo helps Pike settle near the fire, he notices Su
beside the counter.

Miss Zhang.

Doctor.

Your mother's hands trouble her in the cold, I believe.

He remembers. Whether he remembers Lee as well, his face does not say.

They do.

Cray takes a pencil from his waistcoat and writes on the clean edge of an old
newspaper: camphorated oil, turpentine in a smaller measure, warmth before sleep.
His script is narrow and perfectly level.

Rub it into the joints. Not the fingers alone—the wrist as well. The pain often
announces itself where it is heard, not where it begins.

Su takes the paper.

Thank you.

He gives the slight smile people call courtly. There is no mockery in it that she
can prove, and no apology for the fee counted eight winters ago. He has placed the
families of the world in separate drawers and does not experience the arrangement
as contradiction.

You have your father's quiet, he says.

Su looks at him.

It is intended as praise. He waits the smallest moment for her to receive it as
such.

Behind him, Pike attempts to raise his restored arm and is sworn at by both
friends. Flo takes away the doctor's empty glass. The river light has left the
window.

My father listens when people speak, Su says.

Cray's smile remains, but it has become a decision rather than an expression.

An admirable habit, he says.

He picks up the leather case, inclines his head to the room and leaves.

Sau-Ling makes the liniment that evening. It smells strong enough to clear the
shop of customers and works well enough that she uses it all winter.

Both facts remain too.
