# Chapter Four: The White Bowl

The white bowl comes down from its shelf only when Wei tells the story, and Wei
tells the story only when somebody has offered to pay him for a kindness.

On this occasion the somebody is a young fireman off a P&O steamer, newly ashore
and short one sea-bag. The bag has gone to Antwerp while its owner has come to
London, which both men agree is the sort of division shipping companies make
when left unsupervised. Wei advances him a shirt, two collars and enough money for
a bed at the Strangers' Home. The fireman promises repayment on Thursday. Wei
says nothing. The fireman promises again, more formally, adding the name of his
ship, its master and three relations in Kowloon who may be held responsible for
his character.

Wei tells him to return the shirt clean if he can.

After the young man leaves, Su says, You could have put it on his account.

I have.

She looks at the ledger. No new line has appeared.

Where?

Wei taps one finger against the side of his head.

That account does not survive an audit.

It has survived every audit so far.

At supper, Sau-Ling asks why a good shirt has left the premises on the shoulders
of a stranger. Wei says it was not a good shirt. Su says it was the one with only
two patches. Sau-Ling says that among their shirts this qualifies as ceremonial
dress.

Then she looks at her husband for a moment, sees something Su cannot, and reaches
for the teapot.

Tell it properly this time.

Wei frowns. Tell what?

The reason our daughter will never be rich.

That could be any of several stories.

The bowl.

Su has heard the bowl perhaps twenty times. Lee used to demand it whenever rain
kept them inside, and then interrupt at the same places until Wei threatened to
give the story to another family. Since Lee died it has been told less often.
Some stories acquire an empty chair and become difficult to seat correctly.

Wei finishes his tea. Without rising, he looks towards the high shelf above the
shop counter.

Su brings the bowl down.

It is wrapped in a square of blue cloth gone grey along the folds. The porcelain
beneath is plain, thick and uneven at the foot, with one faint line in the glaze.
Nothing about it explains twenty-seven years on the highest shelf in the house.
It would hold rice for one hungry person or soup for two polite ones. Sau-Ling has
better bowls in the kitchen and chips in all of them.

Su sets it before her father.

It begins, Wei says, with your grandfather dying at an inconvenient time.

Sau-Ling pours tea. Death is often criticised for its scheduling.

The funeral took what money we had. The landlord took the yard. Between them
they divided the estate with great efficiency.

You had no estate.

That made it easier.

He was twenty-nine, perhaps thirty; the age changes with the telling. His father
had kept a small school and set bones in a lane behind the Hong Kong waterfront.
The school had already moved once, from Canton after the war, and survived on the
respect of men who possessed more respect than cash. When the old man died,
respect did not pay the landlord.

For six weeks Wei ate at a tea stall two doors from the lost yard. The woman who
kept it had known his father and therefore considered herself entitled to monitor
his decline. She increased the rice when his money decreased, added salt fish
without entering it on the slate and spoke of neither practice.

The night before his ship sailed, she filled her mother's white bowl with rice,
pickle and an egg.

Half an egg, Sau-Ling says.

An egg.

You have promoted it every year.

It had the moral authority of an egg.

The woman told him to bring the bowl back himself before he went out to the
anchorage. He promised. In the morning the harbour office discovered a fault in
his papers. Then it discovered that the clerk empowered to correct the fault was
at breakfast. By the time the necessary stamps had been applied, the tide had
turned and the last lighter was already pulling away.

Here Lee used to ask why Wei had not arrived earlier. Wei always replied that he
had arrived early and the British Empire had arrived first.

He stood on the wharf with his passage paid, everything he owned aboard the
steamer and no means of reaching it. The harbourmaster answered his Cantonese in
English directed slightly above his head. Wei did not yet have enough English to
understand the words, but he understood the direction perfectly.

Then the boy found him.

Thirteen or fourteen, bare feet, a ledger under one arm. Some serang's runner or
comprador's boy; Wei never learned whose. The boy looked once at the pass in
Wei's hand, once at the departing lighter, and said: Run.

They ran the waterfront.

At this point the story always changes speed. Wei's voice loses twenty-seven
years. He takes them between porters and fish barrows, beneath wet washing and
mooring lines, across a customs rail with an officer shouting behind. The boy
knows which alleys continue and which end at walls. He makes spaces where none
exist. Wei follows with his canvas bundle striking his back and the whole future
pulling away across the harbour.

And in the middle of it, he says, I was happy.

Sau-Ling has stopped correcting him. She always stops here.

Everything was lost, or nearly lost. My father was in the ground. My school was
gone. My ship was leaving. It felt good to run.

At the last pier a sampan waited where no sampan had reason to wait. The boy
settled a price with the oarsman and handed Wei down. Wei took out his remaining
coins.

He would not take them, Su says. She has taken Lee's old interruption without
meaning to.

No.

Why?

I did not ask.

You did ask.

I asked too late. He was already gone.

The sampan caught the steamer at the anchorage. Wei climbed aboard wet, breathless
and still carrying the money he had tried to spend. Below the waterline, a cook
served rice with vinegar, oil and chilli from a copper pot. A cabin boy tripped
over a lashing and emptied a can of water down Wei's back. The child dropped to
his knees with his arms around his head, waiting for the blow experience had
promised.

Wei placed one hand on the boy's shoulder and waited.

When the boy looked up, an old sailor on the next crate began laughing. Then the
boy laughed. Then Wei did. Three strangers sat in steerage laughing at a wet
shirt while Hong Kong went out behind them.

On the second day he found the white bowl in his bundle, packed there the night
before so it would be safe until morning.

Su turns it between her hands. The line in the glaze catches lamplight and loses
it.

There were ships back every week, she says.

There still are.

You could have sent it.

She asked me to bring it.

Not to send it, Su says.

Not to send it.

And the runner boy?

Had not offered a forwarding address for his payment.

Wei wraps the bowl again. It returns to the high shelf, where the shop's dust
cannot reach it because he never allows dust the time.

Never for payment, Su says.

Her father picks up his tea.

That is not what the rule means.

It is part of what it means.

It is a story about missing a boat.

Sau-Ling says, Your father dislikes discovering that his stories have meanings.

Meanings encourage people to repeat them inaccurately.

You repeat it inaccurately.

I own it.

Su looks at the bowl above them and at the empty place by the table where Lee
used to supply the interruptions. The tea-stall woman cannot be repaid. The boy
cannot be found. The cabin boy's fear cannot be struck from the world. None of
this prevented Wei from eating the rice, taking the sampan or keeping his hand
gentle on a frightened shoulder.

Some debts, Su understands, are not instructions to find the creditor. They are
instructions about the next person who arrives wet, late and carrying everything
he owns.

On Thursday the young fireman returns. His sea-bag has found Rotterdam. He brings
Wei the price of the bed and the borrowed clothes, counted exactly, and a paper
packet of tea besides.

Wei accepts the money. He refuses the tea.

The fireman leaves it on the counter and walks out before the refusal can be
repeated.

Wei looks at the packet. Su looks at Wei.

Not a word, he says.

I have said nothing.

You are saying it very loudly.

Sau-Ling uses the tea on Sunday. Nobody enters it in any account.
