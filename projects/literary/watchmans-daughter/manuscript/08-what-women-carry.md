# Chapter Eight: What Women Carry

*Wednesday, 5 September 1888*

Before anything enters the copper, Su empties the pockets.

A laundry that neglects this rule acquires boiled pennies, rust marks from nails,
paper reduced to grey pulp and buttons whose owners will swear they were attached
when the garment left home. Sau-Ling taught Su to search every fold twice. Men's
coat pockets are visible and numerous. Women's are concealed beneath openings in
skirts, tied at the waist, sewn into aprons or improvised where the dressmaker
thought no pocket was required.

Most contain the small equipment of getting through a day: keys, thread, a crust
wrapped in paper, tram fares, pins, a thimble, somebody else's address. Su has
found a child's tooth, three dominoes, a glass eye and a love letter returned so
often that the folds had worn through the promises. She does not read what can be
recognised as private. She places each person's belongings in a saucer, writes the
name on a scrap and returns them with the clean work.

Since Buck's Row, the saucers have changed.

Mrs Rance's apron yields a table knife with a rounded end. The Kaplan girl's skirt
contains two door keys tied together, a long steel pin and a paper screw of ground
pepper. From Flo's second-best bodice Su removes a small scent bottle filled with
water. It is square, thick-bottomed and heavy enough to bruise a washboard.

Sau-Ling regards the row of objects on the sill.

London is defending itself from the laundry.

The knife has already cut a line in Mrs Rance's apron pocket. Pepper escapes its
paper and dusts the Kaplan girl's hem. The scent bottle has no stopper secure
enough for boiling water.

Su says, It would be worse if we found them after the copper.

For the clothes or for us?

Both.

Sau-Ling fetches three more saucers.

At nine Mrs Rance arrives for the apron. She sees the knife lying beside it and
looks first embarrassed, then defiant.

My husband said I should carry something.

Does your husband carry a table knife? Sau-Ling asks.

He carries a clasp knife.

Then he has kept the clasp and given you the table.

Mrs Rance picks up the blade. She holds it inside her fist with the rounded point
emerging below her little finger. The torn pocket has stained a narrow rust line
across the clean apron.

Su looks at her hand.

If someone seizes your wrist, that will cut you.

It has no edge.

Then it will not cut him either.

Mrs Rance puts it down.

What am I meant to take?

The question is asked with enough anger that another person might hear accusation.
Su hears fear forced to stand upright in a public shop.

She folds the apron over the knife and considers. There is no object she can hand
across the counter that will make a dark street fair. The practice took twelve
years to enter her feet and is still corrected before breakfast. A blade cannot
become those years because a frightened husband puts it in a pocket.

What do you already carry? Su asks.

My work keys. Money when I've any. This.

She lifts a covered tin from her basket. Dinner for the rooms she cleans.

Which road do you take?

Whitechapel Road, then Baker's Row.

Stay where the shops are open. If you change the route, tell the woman waiting at
the other end. Carry what leaves your hands free.

That isn't a weapon.

No.

Mrs Rance looks disappointed by the honesty of the answer.

Sau-Ling takes a piece of stout sailcloth from beneath the counter and wraps the
knife before returning it.

If you carry foolishness, she says, keep it from tearing the apron.

Mrs Rance laughs despite herself. The sound does not remove her fear. It gives the
fear something else to stand beside.

By eleven two of the three saucers have been claimed.

The Kaplan girl pours the loose pepper back into its paper and ties it with
thread. She says her aunt heard that pepper thrown in a man's eyes will blind him
long enough to run.

Have you practised throwing it? Su asks.

Of course not.

Then the wind knows more about the plan than you do.

The girl puts away the pepper and threads one of her keys between two fingers.
At least this cannot blow back.

Close your hand, Su says.

The key's wards press into her palm. When Su asks her to unlock the shop door, she
must open the fist and rearrange everything before the key will enter.

The girl frowns at her keys. What would you carry?

Laundry.

I'm serious.

So am I.

The answer irritates her, but it is true. Su's basket is large enough to keep a
stranger at a distance and ordinary enough not to turn every encounter into a
contest. More importantly, it belongs in her hands. Fear has persuaded half the
women in Limehouse to carry objects they have never used and cannot afford to
lose.

At noon Wei returns with a box of lamp wicks and finds pepper on the sill, two
empty saucers and Flo's scent bottle in the third.

We have become an armoury? he asks.

Your daughter has disappointed the customers, Sau-Ling says. She refuses to sell
courage by the ounce.

We do not have the shelf space.

Sau-Ling points her needle at Su. Tell her what she should carry.

Wei puts the lamp wicks beneath the counter.

What has she asked me?

Nothing.

Then I will not improve her answer.

This is one of his more irritating forms of trust.

Su says, The fan?

His eyes move to the inside pocket of his coat, where the folded wood rests.

The fan belongs to the work.

The street may require the work.

Then you will know when it does. A newspaper cannot know for you.

He goes into the chandlery to count lamp glass. Sau-Ling watches him leave.

He practises these answers before coming home, she says.

He would make them shorter if he did.

Flo claims the scent bottle after dinner.

Water? Su asks.

Brandy would tempt the wrong sort of rescue.

What will you do with it?

Flo closes her fist around the square glass.

Feel better until I don't.

She slips it into her bodice. Neither woman calls that useless.

Flo has brought a message from the Prospect. The men there have organised a
walking company for women leaving after dark. Ned Pike keeps the list because his
shoulder prevents him from proving helpful with both arms. Two men will leave the
Prospect every half-hour, carrying a lantern and a stick, and escort any woman who
has written her name and destination on the board.

Did the women ask for it? Sau-Ling says.

Some did. Then the men improved it.

How?

There are rules now.

Sau-Ling looks at Su.

The bar cloths are ready.

They both know the cloths were ready yesterday.

At three Su carries them to the Prospect.

The walking-company board stands beside the door. It began as a brewery slate and
still has PORTER 2D faintly visible beneath the new columns. Ned sits at the end
of the bar with his arm in its sling and chalk in his left hand. Pike's shoulder
has become public property. Every man who enters asks whether he can lift it yet,
then tries to answer by lifting his own.

Six women's names are on the board. Beside each is a time, destination and the
names of two men assigned to walk her. One line gives a lodging-house number. A
second says rear entrance. A third records that the woman finishes alone at ten.

Su reads the list once.

Who sees this?

Everyone, Ned says. That's the point.

She looks around the public room.

Everyone is not the point you think it is.

Ned follows her eyes to the open door, the windows and the men at the counter.

It's so nobody's forgotten.

It says where six women will be, when they will leave and which door they use.

Flo, stacking the clean cloths, says, I mentioned that.

Ned's ears redden.

We can put it behind the bar.

You can ask the women whether they want their addresses written at all.

A sailmaker named Booth turns from his pint. Hard to protect people if nobody
will say where they're going.

Hard to protect them if you tell the room, Su says.

We're trying to help.

Then the work should survive a correction.

Booth dislikes this. Men often offer help as a finished object and consider any
alteration a refusal of the whole gift.

Ned lifts the board one-handed and nearly loses it. Su catches the lower edge.
Together they move it behind the bar. Flo takes the chalk, copies the times onto a
small card and tears the destinations from the public sheet.

The first woman due is Mrs Dyer, who closes the tobacco shop at six and has asked
for company as far as Ratcliff Cross. The second is a music-hall dresser who has
not asked but whose employer entered her name. Flo draws a line through it.

Booth says, She'll thank us when nothing happens.

Nothing happens most nights, Flo says. You cannot claim all of them.

Su leaves before the argument finds a larger audience.

At the laundry, a docket waits on the counter from a lodging house off Fashion
Street, eight beds used by women who pay nightly and sometimes not at all. The
keeper has asked whether the usual sheets will still be collected and says it
must happen before dusk.

We can refuse it, Su says.

Sau-Ling is already checking the hems.

Why?

It adds another journey. They owe for the last two.

They owed before the murder.

Now the route is worse.

The route is the same. We are worse at looking at it.

Su hears the answer as criticism and resists it.

Clean sheets will not keep them safe.

No. Must a thing do everything before it is permitted to do one thing?

Sau-Ling opens the ledger to the lodging house account. Two weekly charges remain
uncrossed.

Fear may change the hour, she says. It does not choose our customers.

She enters the new collection time in the ledger and leaves the old debt open.

At half past six Su returns from Fashion Street with eight sheets in the basket
on her hip.
The shops along Commercial Road are closing. Each shutter removes a rectangle of
light. She knows the sequence and can measure the remaining journey by it.

Booth and another man emerge from a doorway behind her.

Miss Zhang, Booth calls. We'll see you home.

No, thank you.

We're going that way.

They are not. The Prospect lies behind them and Booth lives towards Shadwell.

Su keeps walking. She can make them leave by embarrassing them, angering them or
accepting enough of the performance that they may depart feeling thanked. Each
choice requires her to manage the feelings of men who have appeared unasked in
the name of her safety.

She resents the calculation. She resents that refusing all calculation would be
another kind of foolishness.

Mrs Dyer is waiting beneath the lamp outside the closed tobacco shop, looking
towards the Prospect for the escort she requested.

Su stops.

She asked for you, Su tells Booth.

We're assigned to you now.

I did not assign you.

Mrs Dyer has seen them. Relief changes her shoulders.

Booth follows Su's gaze. For one useful moment, being wanted matters more to him
than having been refused.

Right, he says. We'll take her first.

The two men cross the road. Mrs Dyer receives them gladly. Their lantern joins
the line of shop lights towards Ratcliff Cross.

Su continues alone.

She does not mistake winning the argument for making the road safe. Pride is not
one of the rules. She notes the open chandler's door at the next corner, crosses
before the cooper's yard narrows the pavement and listens until Booth's boots have
turned west with Mrs Dyer. She is glad the older woman has the lantern. She is
glad it is no longer following her. The two gladnesses do not cancel each other.

At the Causeway she takes the shop key from her pocket. For three streets it has
been pressed between her fingers as the Kaplan girl demonstrated, its wards
biting the base of her thumb and keeping her hand half closed.

She opens her fist.

The key lies flat across her palm. Her fingers move again. She puts it into the
lock, turns it and enters the lighted shop.
