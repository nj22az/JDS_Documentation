# Chapter Five: Lee's Winter

Lee's peg remains behind the shop door.

It is lower than the others because Wei fixed it when his son was five and said
he would move it up when the boy grew. For eight years it has held no coat. In
summer Sau-Ling loops spare ticket string over it. In winter it is bare. Nobody
has discussed removing it, and nobody hangs anything heavy enough to hide it.

Lee himself objected to the peg.

Too low, he said when it was new.

It is level with your shoulder, Wei told him.

My shoulder is too low.

Grow, then.

I am.

Not at a useful speed.

This was the shape of most conversations between father and son: a complaint
submitted, inspected and returned unrepaired.

In the winter of 1880, Lee is seven and Su is eleven, and the yard has only just
been admitted into his mornings. He has spent the preceding year watching from
the kitchen step, copying whatever he can see and inventing whatever he cannot.
Now Wei lets him stand beside Su in the blue hour before dawn.

Lee's stance is terrible.

His feet turn out. His knees collapse inward. Every instruction first passes
through an argument. He believes the purpose of moving slowly is to prepare for
moving very fast and therefore attempts to arrive at the fast portion by private
arrangement.

Lower, Wei says.

I am low.

Su, beside him, says, A sparrow could walk beneath you without bending.

There are no sparrows awake.

The folded fan taps the outside of his knee.

Lower.

Lee lowers himself, glaring at Su as though she invented gravity. Ten breaths
later his legs begin to shake. At twenty he shifts backwards until his coat
touches the wall.

The fan touches his stomach.

The wall is not part of the form, Wei says.

It was there already.

Leave it where it is.

Su looks ahead because looking at Lee will make her laugh and laughing will make
the yard last longer. His indignation has always delighted her. It is difficult
to be solemn around someone who treats the physical universe as a badly managed
shop.

When Wei turns to correct the position of her wrist, Lee rises half an inch.

Su says, He moved.

Traitor, Lee says.

Lower, Wei says without turning.

Afterwards, in the kitchen, Lee steals the sugared corner of Su's bread. He does
this while maintaining eye contact, a method so open it becomes briefly
confusing.

That was mine.

You were not eating it.

I was saving it.

I have saved it from becoming stale.

Sau-Ling slides the remaining sugar jar out of his reach. Your brother has a
calling.

Thief?

Barrister.

Lee eats the evidence.

He spends the morning at the shop counter making boats from spoiled docket paper.
Each receives a cargo entered in pencil: pepper, tea, lamp oil, twelve tigers, the
Queen. He launches them in the gutter after the rain begins. The Queen overturns
before reaching the corner. Lee blames the pilot.

At noon he ties six knots in a length of waste cord and asks Su which will hold.
None will. Three are not knots at all, but arrangements the rope has agreed to
temporarily.

That one, he says, pointing.

It will slip.

It looks good.

The river does not care how a knot looks.

Lee pulls it. The cord comes apart in his hands.

He studies the ends as though betrayed by a friend. Then show me.

Su shows him. Around, through, back on itself. Lee copies her and pulls until the
cord bites his palms.

This one holds.

It does.

He puts it in his pocket.

By three he is asleep beneath the counter.

Lee has not slept in daylight since he was small enough to fit inside a laundry
basket. Su finds him curled against a coil of new rope, cheek pressed into his
sleeve. When she touches his forehead, the heat frightens her hand away.

Sau-Ling carries him upstairs. Wei closes the shop before dark.

The first night they call it a chill because a chill is a thing blankets may
answer. Sau-Ling warms bricks and wraps them in flannel for the foot of the bed.
Su brings water. Wei sits beside the mattress counting Lee's pulse with his
thumb. The rain turns to sleet against the window.

Lee wakes angry at the blankets.

Too many.

You're cold, Su says.

I'm hot.

His teeth begin chattering before the sentence ends.

At ten Wei puts on his coat.

Dr Cray keeps rooms two streets away. He has been home from Bengal for several
years and is known at the Prospect as a man who can set a shoulder before the
patient has finished objecting. His brass plate is kept bright. His housekeeper
answers the bell.

Wei stands beneath the stone hood of the doorway with sleet melting on his hat
and explains that his son is seven, feverish and worsening.

The housekeeper listens without opening the door wider. The doctor is at supper.

Wei says he will wait.

The doctor will call in the morning.

The boy may be worse by morning.

Then the doctor will find him worse.

Wei has enough money in his pocket for the usual fee. He does not have enough to
make a different man important. He looks past the housekeeper into a hall lit by
two lamps. Somewhere beyond it, silver touches china. A man laughs once and is
answered by another.

Wei removes his hat.

Please tell him Zhang, the chandler on the Causeway. Tell him I came myself.

I shall tell him.

The door closes carefully, without anger.

When Wei returns, Su knows the answer from the snow remaining on his shoulders.
If he had entered the warm house it would have melted.

Morning, he says.

Sau-Ling wrings out the cloth and puts it back across Lee's forehead.

The second night begins before the first has properly ended. Lee becomes briefly
better towards dawn. He asks for rice, eats two spoonfuls and complains that the
practice will go on without him.

You hate the practice, Su says.

I hate being bad at it.

Same thing for you.

He tells her the room is moving. She tells him it is not. He insists that the
white bowl has fallen from the shelf and asks her to put it back. The bowl is
downstairs where it has always been.

Su puts an empty cup on the chest beside him and says, There. Safe.

Lee looks at the cup, then at her. That isn't it.

It is dark.

It is blue.

Go to sleep.

You are a poor liar.

So sleep and spare us both.

He smiles with only one side of his mouth and closes his eyes.

Dr Cray comes at nine.

He is dressed for the street, clean cuffs beneath dark sleeves, leather case in
one hand. He takes off his gloves finger by finger and gives them to Su without
looking at her. His hands are pale, narrow and beautifully kept.

At the bedside he is exact. He feels Lee's pulse, lifts an eyelid, looks at his
tongue and listens to his chest through a wooden stethoscope. He asks when the
fever began and whether the boy has taken water. His questions are good questions.
His attention, now that it has arrived, is complete.

Sau-Ling stands at the foot of the bed. Wei stands by the window. Su holds the
doctor's gloves.

Cray says fever.

They know this. The word's authority does not enlarge its usefulness.

He leaves instructions: water in small amounts, cloths changed, the room quiet.
He prepares something bitter in a spoon and waits until Lee swallows. Nothing in
his manner promises recovery, and nothing admits danger beyond what the room
already knows.

Will he mend? Sau-Ling asks.

Children are resilient.

It is not an answer, but it has the shape of one and comes from a doctor. For a
few minutes they try to live inside it.

At the counter downstairs, Cray names his fee. Sau-Ling takes the household money
from its tin. There are enough coins because there must be. She places them in
his hand.

He counts them.

Not greedily. Not apologetically. He moves each coin with his thumb, confirms the
sum and closes his purse. It is ordinary professional conduct. This is the part
Sau-Ling will remember after the sound of his voice and the medicine's smell are
gone: the small clicks of her son's illness becoming correct in another man's
account.

Cray puts on his gloves.

Send word if there is a marked change.

There had been a marked change the night before. Wei had sent himself.

The doctor leaves.

Lee dies on the third night.

There is no final sentence worth keeping. Towards evening he asks for water. Su
lifts his head and gives it to him. Later he says her name, but when she leans
close he has forgotten why. Near midnight his breathing changes. Sau-Ling knows
first. She puts one palm against his chest as though quieting him, although by
then the room is quiet enough.

Su waits for something to happen after. A sound from the street, perhaps, or the
house itself to register an alteration of this size. A cart passes. Water ticks
in the tap. The high window rattles in the east wind.

Wei takes the folded fan from his coat and sets it on the chest. It has splinted
and corrected the bodies of strangers for two generations. There is nothing in
its knowledge for this.

After the burial, the shop opens.

Lee's paper boats disappear from the gutter one by one. His bad knots remain in
a drawer until Sau-Ling needs the cord. The empty cup returns to the kitchen. Su
expects grief to preserve objects and discovers that work consumes them without
malice.

Only the peg remains unused.

Eight winters later, Su stands behind the shop door with a coil of ticket string
in her hand. She could hang it on Lee's peg. Her mother has done so all summer.

Instead she reaches above it to her own.

The lower peg stays bare.
