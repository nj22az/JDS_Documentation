# Chapter Seven: Buck's Row

*Friday, 31 August 1888*

The woman reaches Limehouse before her name does.

She arrives at half past eight in the mouth of a carman collecting two shirts.
He has come from Broad Street with flour on his boots and the pleasure of being
first with bad news. Sau-Ling is sorting collars at the counter. Su is in the yard
turning the last wash beneath the mangle. Wei has gone for lamp oil.

Woman found in Buck's Row, the carman says. Throat cut. Near took her head off.

Sau-Ling lays down the collar in her hand.

What woman?

Don't know yet. One of the street women, they're saying.

What was her name?

I said, they don't know.

He knows what the knife has done. He knows where the body lay, what the constable
first mistook it for and which sort of man the police believe might have held the
blade. He knows the hour within fifteen minutes. The woman's name is the only
portion of the story that has failed to travel.

Sau-Ling listens until he begins describing the wounds again.

You know what was done to her and not what she was called.

Nobody knows what she was called.

Then know less loudly.

The carman looks offended. He has brought news without charging for it and feels
the service has been poorly received.

My shirts ready?

Su brings them from the shelf. He pays, tucks the parcel under his arm and leaves
to improve another shop's morning.

The bell settles.

Sau-Ling picks up the collar. She turns it once, finds the frayed point and puts
it in the mending pile.

Where is Buck's Row? she asks.

Whitechapel. Behind the road, Su says.

Do we deliver there?

No.

Who does?

Mrs Rance passes the row on her way to scrub rooms near Baker's Row. The Kaplan
girl may, when she goes to her aunt.

Sau-Ling looks towards the delivery slate. Nothing written there has changed. Six
names, six parcels, three amounts still owing. Yet Su feels the routes behind the
names alter. A street they do not use has entered the shop and touched two women
who do.

At nine Mrs Rance arrives before her washing is folded.

She usually comes after six with her wrists red from other people's water. Today
she has left work between floors. Her hair is still covered by a cotton square;
stove blacking sits in the creases beside her nails.

I'll take it now.

It is wet, Sau-Ling says.

I'll dry it at home.

It will dry badly.

Let it.

Mrs Rance puts down the full price although two petticoats remain on the line. She
does not mention Buck's Row. Sau-Ling does not ask. Su wraps the damp things in
old cloth instead of paper and enters the early collection on the slate.

At ten a cooper's wife sends her eldest boy to move Monday's collection from
seven in the evening to five. At eleven the Kaplan girl comes herself and says her
aunt has forbidden the Whitechapel route. She will go round by Mile End Road,
adding half an hour and two crowded crossings.

Is it safer? Su asks.

There are more people.

That is not the same thing.

It is what my aunt has.

Su changes the address note.

By noon the murder has become alterations in other women's handwriting.

The first newspaper comes beneath Ned Pike's good arm.

His injured one is bound across his chest in a clean sling. He should be at home,
but the shoulder Dr Cray restored yesterday has granted him a story of his own
and he has spent the morning carrying it between public houses. At each telling,
the timber grows larger and his escape more deliberate. Pain has not improved his
judgement, though it has made sitting down easier to recommend.

Flo sent me for her aprons, he says.

Sau-Ling looks at the sling. Flo sent you because she knew you would come out
anyway.

She has confidence in me.

She has information.

The folded *Echo* slips from beneath his arm. Su catches it before it reaches the
wet floor.

The headline occupies more space than the woman ever did.

HORRIBLE MURDER IN WHITECHAPEL.

WOMAN SHOCKINGLY MUTILATED.

HEAD NEARLY CUT OFF.

The letters are made to be read from across a street. Beneath them, in smaller
print, the woman is perhaps thirty-five, dark-haired, of medium height. Her
clothing is called scanty. Her neighbourhood is called low. Her body has been
searched and her pockets have produced a comb, a piece of soap and no name.

Ned holds out his good hand.

They say butcher's knife.

They say several things, Su says.

Madman, most likely. No ordinary man does that.

Sau-Ling gathers Flo's aprons into a parcel. Ordinary men are quick to place
wickedness outside their profession.

Ned considers this.

I only meant—

Your parcel.

Two customers have entered behind him. A sailmaker and a clerk from the
bonded warehouse lean towards the paper. Neither knew Buck's Row before breakfast.
Both now possess a theory about it.

Gang, says the sailmaker. There's gangs levy money on women. Everyone knows it.

The clerk says, Left-handed fellow. Doctor says so.

Butcher, Ned repeats.

A foreign sailor, someone offers from the doorway.

The four men look briefly at one another. Each has selected a murderer from a
class to which he does not belong.

Su folds the paper and puts it face down on the counter.

Ned notices the movement.

We were reading that.

You were adding to it.

The clerk's mouth tightens. He is not accustomed to correction from a laundress,
particularly one who has not raised her voice enough to make the correction
impolite.

Only saying a woman should take care, he says. Out at that hour—

Sau-Ling turns towards him.

Finish.

He does not.

The sailmaker discovers an urgent interest in his cuffs. Ned tucks Flo's aprons
beneath his sound arm. The newspaper remains on the counter when they leave, its
headline against the wood.

For the rest of the afternoon, people arrive to read it.

Nobody admits this purpose. They need a button, a length of twine, a shirt that
was not promised until Tuesday. Their hands find the counter. Their eyes find the
folded square. Before long the paper is soft at the creases and each account has
acquired a new certainty.

The woman was drunk.

She was sober.

She was carried there dead.

She was killed where she lay.

She knew the man.

No woman would go with a stranger.

No respectable woman would be there.

That last opinion comes from a man whose washing is delivered to a woman who is
not his wife.

Su lets him finish because facts do not always require assistance.

At four she carries two parcels to Shadwell. The journey is ordinary except for
the people explaining why it is not. A fishwife tells three other women to keep
to the middle of the road. A constable moves them aside for blocking a cart. Two
girls leave a rope walk together, linked at the elbows, laughing too loudly at
nothing Su can hear. Outside a lodging house, a man announces that women will be
safe if they behave sensibly. The women beside him ask what sensible behaviour
would have purchased the dead woman another morning. He answers a different
question.

Su has walked these streets since she was old enough to carry a parcel without
dragging its corner. She knows which paving stone rocks in rain, which warehouse
dog only performs anger and which alleys shorten a journey honestly. The city has
always contained danger. Until today, danger belonged to places and people: a
drunken group outside a music hall, an unlit stair, a customer who watches the
door before putting his hand on the counter.

Now it is being spoken of as weather. Everywhere and nobody's fault.

She returns by the same route.

This is partly stubbornness and partly experiment. The streets do not look
different. That irritates her more than if they had.

At closing, Wei reads the abandoned newspaper standing at the counter. He does
not turn it over to see whether the account continues on another page.

Bad reporting, he says.

You know the street? Su asks.

I know sentences that sell themselves.

He folds the paper once more, concealing the headline, and sets it beside the
stove for lighting tomorrow's fire.

Sau-Ling removes it.

Not that page.

It is paper.

It has been enough things today.

She puts it on the high shelf beneath the household almanac.

That night, the Zhangs bar the door at the usual hour. Wei checks the rear bolt
only once. Sau-Ling leaves the lamp in the front room burning while they eat, then
complains about the oil. Nobody names the reason.

*Saturday, 1 September 1888*

By morning the woman has two names and three ages.

Mary Ann Nicholls, one paper says. Polly to the women at the lodging house.
Thirty-six in one account, nearer forty in another. She was turned out for want of
fourpence and believed the night might still provide it. Her remaining property
is now reported as a handkerchief, a comb and a piece of looking glass.

The facts have improved without becoming sufficient.

Kate comes for her bodice shortly after nine.

She holds it against herself while Su checks the repaired sleeve. Three new hooks
sit in a straight line where the old ones failed. Kate pulls each as though
testing mooring gear.

You'll tear them out again, Su says.

Then we'll know the thread was honest.

Kate sees the paper on the high shelf.

They named her?

Polly Nichols.

Kate repeats it once.

Did you know her? Su asks.

No. Might have seen her. Whitechapel is full of people you know well enough to
nod to and not well enough to bury.

She puts on the bodice behind the hanging cloth in the corner, emerges and turns
to inspect the back in the small shop mirror. There is not enough mirror for the
whole garment, so Su holds a second piece at an angle.

Kate studies the reflection.

Fourpence, she says.

What?

For the bed. I've spent fourpence badly enough. Never knew it could be the border
between indoors and Buck's Row.

Sau-Ling, writing at the ledger, says, Any coin becomes a border when it is the
last one.

Kate smooths the repaired sleeve. The bodice fits. She puts the remaining penny
on the counter. Su pushes it back.

After Kent.

You mistrust my credit?

I trust the agreement.

Kate pockets the penny and asks Su to enter no more work until after Kent.

We leave next week, she says. John has heard of a farm still taking hands.

Bring back the other half of the song.

If the wages are poor, I'll steal you two.

She wraps the bodice in her old paper and leaves wearing her usual coat. The bell
gives its hopeless shake. Another customer enters before the sound has finished.

By Saturday evening every delivery on Sau-Ling's slate has an earlier time beside
it. Women collect wet washing, pay boys to carry bundles or add half a mile to
remain under shop lamps. Men offer escorts with the satisfaction of having
invented company. Some are kind. Some enjoy the permission to ask where a woman
is going. From a distance the two kinds look alike.

Su copies the altered rounds onto a clean page.

Buck's Row is a street she has never walked.

By nightfall it has entered every route she knows.
