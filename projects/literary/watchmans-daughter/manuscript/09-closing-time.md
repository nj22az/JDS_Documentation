# Chapter Nine: Closing Time

*Thursday, 6 September 1888*

Closing time is an agreement.

The lamps remain lit. The door still opens. Nothing physical prevents a customer
from asking one last question or remembering a purchase after the shutters begin
to move. Yet the sign turns, the ledger closes and everybody agrees that the room
has changed from a shop into somebody else's evening.

Most people understand this.

Mr Prowse, the cooper, is at the counter when Su turns the sign. He has spent ten
minutes choosing between two identical lengths of lamp wick and now discovers
that his penny has entered a hole in his coat lining. Sau-Ling offers to put the
wick aside. Prowse refuses the credit, finds the coin near his elbow and apologises
for keeping them.

Evening, Miss Zhang. Mrs Zhang. Wei.

He leaves. The bell moves once above his hat. Su closes the door behind him.

There is no gratitude required when a man accepts the end of a transaction. The
absence of trouble is not a kindness. Still, after the altered routes, the pocket
knives and Booth's unrequested escort, Prowse's ordinary departure touches a
place in Su she would prefer not to have acquired.

Wei lowers the first shutter. The iron foot scrapes across the same groove it has
worn in the floor. The sound returns Su to another closing, two years earlier,
when one man decided the agreement did not include him.

Mr Vale had been a customer for five months.

He kept tallies at a warehouse near Ratcliff and bought dubbin, twine and lamp oil
in quantities small enough to suggest the warehouse supplied none of them. He was
perhaps thirty. He wore his fair moustache longer than his position required and
spoke to Wei with exaggerated clarity, as though a louder price became more
English. With Su he was familiar in the way some men are familiar with girls they
have watched become women: claiming a history the girl did not know they shared.

He was not unpleasant on every visit. Once he helped Wei roll a cask clear of the
rain without being asked. He returned a penny when Sau-Ling gave too much change
and brought the empty oil bottle back instead of leaving it in the gutter. He
could be useful, honest and tedious in the same afternoon. Nothing about him
announced that one day he would place his hand on Su. This was not because she had
failed to notice an announcement. There had not been one.

On the November evening Su was seventeen, he entered as she reached for the sign.

Closed, she said.

Need oil.

Tomorrow.

Need it tonight.

Then you needed it before closing.

Vale smiled. He enjoyed her answers when other people could hear them. Alone,
they appeared to offend him.

Wei and Sau-Ling were in the yard lifting wet sheets from the final rinse. Water
struck the stones. The copper lid clattered. They were twenty feet away and, for
the moment, in another country.

Vale put a penny on the counter.

Su could have taken it and measured the oil. The sale would have required two
minutes. Refusing would require an argument of unknown length. She knew this and
resented that he knew it too.

Tomorrow, she said again.

Nobody about?

We are closed.

He looked towards the yard door, then back at her.

You needn't be unfriendly.

I need you to leave.

Su opened the street door. Vale did not move towards it. He came around the end
of the counter instead.

Only having a word.

Have it outside.

He laughed, softly enough to make the sound private, and placed one hand at her
waist. His thumb went beneath the apron tie.

Remove your hand.

Don't be a child.

His fingers closed.

That was the beginning.

Su covered his hand with hers before he understood she had moved. She did not try
to pull away. The grip at her waist supplied the connection. She stepped outside
his forward foot, turned his thumb towards the counter and let his straightening
arm carry his shoulder down.

Vale struck the wood chest first. His cheek followed. The penny jumped once and
rolled beneath the flour bin.

The whole exchange occupied less time than his laugh.

His free hand spread against the counter. Surprise left him without speech. Su
held his wrist between her body and the wood, his elbow raised only far enough
that struggling made the choice worse.

Let go.

Say what you did.

What?

Say it.

I touched you. Christ. It was a joke.

Not that.

She increased the turn.

Vale made a sound through his teeth. His free hand was no longer reaching for
her. Both feet were planted. His body had stopped trying to rise. Nothing in him
was advancing except his anger.

The danger had ended.

Su did not.

She wanted him to admit that he had understood the word *leave*. She wanted the
pain to separate his choice from the harmless accident he was already making of
it. She wanted him to carry one true sentence out of the shop.

She turned another fraction.

Su.

Wei stood in the yard doorway with his sleeves wet to the elbow. Sau-Ling was
behind him holding one corner of a sheet.

Su released Vale and stepped back.

Vale rose, cradling the wrist. Nothing was broken. His moustache had folded
against his cheek and now pointed in two directions.

Your girl attacked me.

Wei looked at the open street door, the position of the counter and the apron tie
hanging loose at Su's side.

Did you touch my daughter after she told you to leave?

Only tried to give her a bit of—

Did you?

Vale looked at Su. The admission he had refused under pressure arrived easily
when another man requested it.

I may have put a hand on her.

Wei retrieved the penny from beneath the flour bin, crossed to the ledger and
placed the coin on Vale's page.

Your account is closed.

I'll take my custom elsewhere.

Yes.

Sau-Ling came in from the yard. She set the wet sheet over a chair, found Vale's
line and put one finger beneath the last amount.

Sevenpence first.

Vale stared at her.

You have received the dubbin, she said. Outrage does not settle an account.

He placed a shilling on the counter. Sau-Ling counted fivepence change into his
good hand. The ordinary precision of it diminished him more effectively than Su's
hold had done.

At the door he said, People will hear about this.

Sau-Ling lifted the loose end of Su's apron tie.

Tell them this part.

Vale left.

Wei shut the door. Su waited for him to say she had done well. She had ended the
touch without striking him, without breaking him and without calling for help.
Seventeen still believed that success should be recognised quickly or it might
spoil.

Wei closed Vale's page.

Rules, he said.

Su gave them in Cantonese.

English.

Never first. Never for show. Never for payment. Never in anger.

Which?

None.

Wei looked at her.

He began it. Nobody was watching. He owed me nothing. I was angry, but that is not
why I moved.

When did he stop?

What?

When did he stop being dangerous?

Su knew the answer and disliked him for requiring it.

At the counter.

When did you stop?

When you came in.

Wei waited.

Sau-Ling retied the apron at Su's waist. Her hands were gentle and her mouth was
not.

The man deserved worse, she said.

Wei nodded. Deserving is not instruction.

He would have denied it, Su said.

He did deny it.

Then I should have made him say it plainly.

The practice is not a machine for making truth.

What is it for?

Stopping what must be stopped.

And after?

You step back.

Su looked towards the yard. Anger remained in her, clean and hot. Vale had left
with his wrist whole, his account settled and his version of the evening already
being prepared. The rules offered no arrangement by which this became fair.

Never in anger does not mean never angry, Wei said.

She looked at him.

It means anger does not choose the movement. It does not choose how long you
continue.

That night Sau-Ling mended the apron tie at the kitchen table.

The cloth itself was undamaged. Only three stitches had pulled loose where Vale's
thumb entered. Sau-Ling removed all six and began the seam again. Su watched the
needle pass through the same holes with smaller, more even bites.

He will tell people I attacked him, Su said.

He intended to tell himself that before you moved.

Then holding him longer made no difference.

It made no difference to his lie.

Sau-Ling drew the thread flat.

It made a difference to you.

Su looked towards the stairs. Wei had gone up without finishing his tea.

He thinks I was wrong.

Your father thinks Mr Vale was wrong. He also thinks you held the wrist too long.
He is capable of keeping two accounts, though marriage has taught him to conceal
the talent.

Was I wrong?

You were right to stop him. After he stopped, you wanted pain to make the world
agree with you. The world is a poor listener.

Sau-Ling tied off the thread and put the apron into Su's hands.

His hand belongs in his account, she said. Your extra breath belongs in yours. Do
not let either man put both entries under your name.

May I still hate him?

It would be inconvenient if permission were required.

At dawn he made her show him.

The yard was white at the edges with frost. Su had slept badly. Wei wore the same
shirt as the night before and held the folded fan too tightly.

Again, he said.

He gave her his right wrist. She covered, stepped outside his foot and brought
his shoulder towards the wall. At the point where Vale had stopped, Wei tapped
her elbow with the fan.

End.

She released and remained where she was.

The fan touched her breastbone.

Step back.

There is no attack.

Then stepping back costs you nothing.

She stepped.

Again.

They repeated the exchange. Cover, turn, settle, release, step. Each time Wei
found something to correct. Her heel rose. Her shoulder tightened. Her release
was late by a breath. After the tenth repetition Su understood that the lesson
was no longer in the hold. It was in the pace of empty stone she placed between
herself and the person she had stopped.

He changed hands. She worked from the other side. Without anger to lend speed,
the movement felt plain and slightly ugly: two bodies, one bad decision, the
shortest route back to separation. That was how Wei wanted it. Nothing in the
practice was meant to improve a person's opinion of herself.

Again.

By the twentieth, her anger had attached itself to her father.

You care more that I hurt him than that he touched me.

Wei's face changed.

Only a fraction. At seventeen she saw disapproval. At nineteen, remembering, she
can name it: fear struck through with pride, neither one permitted to speak in
front of the other.

I care that the world will give you reasons, he said. Reasons are plentiful.

That is not an answer.

It is the one I have.

Again.

In the present shop, the first shutter settles into its groove.

Wei reaches for the second. Su takes the lower edge without making a gift of the
help. Together they set it across the window.

At seventeen she thought his corrections meant he did not trust her. Now she
knows how completely he did. He trusted her with the part after necessity, the
part no attacker could choose for her. His frightened pride had no language of
its own, so it borrowed the word *again*.

Su slides the door bolt home.

Then she steps back.
