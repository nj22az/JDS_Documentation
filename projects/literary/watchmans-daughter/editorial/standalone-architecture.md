# Standalone Architecture

## Recommended form

Build a Su-centred novel, not a miniature edition of the anthology. The 1888
chapter supplies the decisive autumn; the new book should create the life around
it.

### Part One — The Rules

Su's ordinary world before the murders: the Causeway shop, laundry work, dawn
training, Wei's crossing, Sau-Ling's contrary idea of debt, Lee's childhood and
death, and the practical geography of Chinese and maritime Wapping.

The purpose is not origin-story exposition. It is to make the shop, yard and
family worth returning to before danger enters them.

### Part Two — The Autumn

Build Liz and Kate as recurring customers and friends before 30 September. Let
Cray remain a useful, respectable fixture whose conduct can be read two ways.
The watch committees, racial suspicion and newspaper hunger tighten around Su
without turning the book into a police procedural.

### Part Three — Never First

The shirt, Su's watching, the failed attempt to tell anyone, the unnamed woman in
the alley, and the confrontation. Preserve the governing moral fact: Su acts
because she sees a murder already in progress, not because she decides to hunt
the Ripper.

The unopened case remains essential. It keeps the story about Su's choice rather
than offering a fictional solution as history.

### Part Four — What the River Kept

The clean sleeve, her parents' wordless knowledge, the uncollected bodice, the
fuchsia, and the arrival of Erik in 1892. Give the aftermath enough room to show
how Su lives with an unanswered question.

The strongest main ending is Su and Erik on the river: companionship without an
inquisition. The 1940 material may then appear as an optional epilogue, but only
if Dr Heron's resemblance does not turn uncertainty into a hidden solution.

## Existing material versus new material

| Area | Existing strength | Expansion needed |
|---|---|---|
| Su and Wei | Rules, crossing story, ageing hands | More ordinary training, affection and disagreement |
| Su and Sau-Ling | Laundry, mercy ledger, clean sleeve | Daily work, faith, language and conflict before the crisis |
| Su and Lee | Powerful death memory | Living scenes before his illness so he is more than a grievance |
| Su, Liz and Kate | Immediate warmth and memorable details | Repeated encounters that earn the later grief |
| Dr Cray | Excellent ambiguity and physical presence | Earlier useful acts; less explanatory retrospective analysis |
| Erik | Strong emotional solution | A real courtship and early marriage, independent of Maggie |
| 1940/2019 | Evocative afterimages | Optional only; neither is required to complete Su's arc |

## Scale

A 45,000–60,000-word illustrated historical novel is the natural first target.
The current Su-centred prose provides roughly 15,000–18,000 directly usable words.
The additional length should come principally from relationships and daily life,
not more clues, more victims or a more explicit Ripper theory.

## Material to keep out of the main narrative

- a complete 1774 or 1839 Company chapter;
- an explicit martial-arts genealogy traced through every century;
- Maggie as Erik's announced ancestor;
- confirmation that Cray was Jack the Ripper;
- a Character Map explaining the answer after the novel has refused to give one.

Those elements can remain in research notes or bonus material without controlling
the reader's experience.
