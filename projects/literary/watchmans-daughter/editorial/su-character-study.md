# Su Zhang — Character Study

## The governing idea

Su is controlled, not cold. Her stillness is work.

She has been taught since seven to notice force before it becomes action, and she
has grown up in a laundry where stains, seams, debts and lies all leave physical
evidence. Observation is therefore her craft, her protection and her principal
flaw: she trusts what she can read and dislikes needing what cannot be verified.

The standalone book should not merely prove that she is capable. It should show
what capability costs her, what delights her when no danger is present, and what
kind of life she would choose if the autumn allowed her to choose freely.

## Personality already present in the canon

The new scene *Three Streets* extends this evidence backwards. At six, Su already
believes that a problem correctly reasoned through is a problem solved. She leaves
school without telling anyone because telling would make her wish a request. The
journey is affectionate, funny and nearly fatal. Her fear arrives only after the
danger, establishing that adult composure is partly temperament and partly a habit
formed very early.

### Exacting

Su notices bad knots, altered routines, a loose clasp, ageing hands and the weight
of a basket. She thinks in ledgers because work has taught her that vague things
become manageable when given shape.

This should appear in ordinary life as well as detection: she takes real pleasure
in a seam lying flat, a parcel balanced correctly and a task finished without
fuss. Precision is not merely grimness; it is one of the ways she enjoys being
good at something.

### Proud

She does not want rescuing, explaining or translating by people who have already
decided what she is. Her politeness to the watch committee is strategic, but it
also contains pride. At nineteen she is old enough to know her competence and
young enough occasionally to overestimate the completeness of it.

Pride should sometimes make her impatient with Wei's corrections, reluctant to
ask Sau-Ling for help, and too quick to keep a burden private.

### Rule-bound but not obedient

Su keeps the four rules “resentfully, completely”. That distinction is her moral
centre. She does not obey because her father is always right; she tests the rules
against anger and keeps them because she understands what she could become without
them.

Her eventual maturity is not rebellion against Wei. It is the creation of a fifth
understanding that belongs to her: stopping harm and caring for what remains are
parts of the same act. Wei teaches the ending of violence; Sau-Ling teaches the
work after it.

### Bilingual and privately divided

Su keeps her languages in separate rooms. Some feelings have weight in Cantonese
and sound hollow in English; other parts of her life exist only in the language
of docks, tickets and customers. She is not suspended between two complete worlds.
She has made a third, private one out of both.

Dialogue should occasionally show her choosing a language because of what it lets
her avoid. She may answer Wei in English when she wants distance and Sau-Ling in
Cantonese when an English sentence would make a feeling too public.

### Tender through attention

Su does not announce affection. She remembers Kate's unfinished song, understands
the load-bearing purpose of Liz's false story, feels the change in Wei's hands and
keeps the uncollected bodice. Her love takes the form of accurate memory.

This makes her well suited to laundry work: she knows people by the places their
clothes wear thin. The book should allow her to notice comic, vain and affectionate
details too, not only evidence of suffering.

### Angry

The present chapter describes a banked heat. It should not become her only inner
weather, but it is essential. Su is angry at racial condescension, at Cray's moral
arithmetic, at poverty's demand for politeness, at Wei's ageing and at the fact
that rules do not resurrect Lee.

Her anger is not frightening because it is exotic or martial. It is frightening
because she is disciplined enough to make it useful.

### Capable of humour and pleasure

This is the least developed side and the most important addition. A whole novel
cannot leave her permanently unreadable without turning strength into a pose.

Her humour should be dry, exact and generally delivered with a straight face. Lee
can make her laugh against her will. Kate can make her enjoy foolish songs. She
can tease Wei by obeying the letter of an instruction too perfectly. With Erik,
her humour can become gentler because she is no longer using it as armour.

Her pleasures should remain small and concrete: a difficult stain lifted, cold
air after the copper room, a tune remembered correctly, print read in peace, the
brief sensation of moving quickly with nothing pursuing her, and later a quiet
hour in Erik's boat.

## Flaws that give her room to grow

- She mistakes self-containment for strength and can make secrecy do work that
  trust ought to do.
- She is severe in judgement, especially when another person's motives cannot be
  read cleanly.
- She keeps score even while insisting she will not collect the debt.
- She can be impatient with weakness because she has had little permission for
  her own.
- She is better at ending a crisis than living through the untidy period after it.
- She assumes that if she has observed enough, she is entitled to certainty. The
  unopened case forces her to surrender that entitlement.
- She acts privately to avoid being refused. At six this takes her out of school
  and into the docks; at nineteen it helps leave her alone with a question nobody
  else knows she is carrying.

None of these makes her foolish. They make the alley a genuine character test
rather than a demonstration of superior technique.

## Recommended explicit desire

Before the murders take over the city, Su should want something ordinary and
forward-looking: to be recognised as the person capable of inheriting and joining
both halves of the family business—the chandlery accounts and the laundry—not
merely as Wei's trained daughter or Sau-Ling's help.

This desire fits the existing material. She already understands both trades,
notices Wei's decline and possesses the literacy and discipline to run the shop.
It gives the autumn something concrete to interrupt and makes Erik's later arrival
part of a life continuing, not a reward delivered after trauma.

## Relationship arcs

### Wei

They begin with love expressed as correction. Su wants acknowledgement; Wei is
afraid that praise will loosen the restraint on a gifted child. The book should
let them disagree. By the end, his refusal to ask about the alley becomes an act
of trust rather than emotional absence.

### Sau-Ling

Su initially reads her mother's mercy as softness and Wei's rules as strength.
The clean sleeve reveals that Sau-Ling can make the morally harder decision. Su's
adult self should become a synthesis of both parents, not her father's successor
alone.

### Lee

Lee must live on the page before he dies. Su should be irritated by him, amused by
him, protective of him and once unfair to him. Otherwise he remains a grievance
rather than a brother, and her later refusal to collect his debt costs too little.

### Liz and Kate

They draw different parts of Su into daylight. Liz understands the divided life
of an immigrant. Kate makes solemnity difficult and gives Su permission to enjoy
something unfinished. Their scenes should contain errands, teasing and ordinary
disagreement, not a continuous premonition of death.

### Cray

Cray is the person Su cannot finish reading. His genuine competence and selective
kindness offend her belief that a ledger ought eventually to balance. The case is
not merely evidence about him; it is a final temptation to make the world legible.

### Erik

Erik is not the man who heals Su. He is the first person with whom she does not
have to perform being unhurt. His quiet gives her room rather than demanding that
she fill it, and her dry humour should surface most freely with him.

## Voice

Su speaks less than many characters, but her dialogue should not consist entirely
of grave, polished statements. Prefer concrete replies, literal observations and
the occasional line whose humour arrives a beat late. When she is frightened she
becomes more precise, not more eloquent. When she is safe, her sentences may
lengthen.

## Scene test

Every new Su scene should reveal at least two of the following:

1. a preference unrelated to danger;
2. a relationship in which she is not the strongest person present;
3. a mistake or uncharitable judgement;
4. humour, pleasure, embarrassment or vanity;
5. a choice between Wei's closed ledger and Sau-Ling's open one;
6. an ambition for the life beyond 1888.

If a scene shows only that Su is observant, calm and formidable, the existing
chapter has already done its work.
