# Scene Outline

Target: approximately 60,000 words. The chapter allocations are guides, not
quotas. Parts One to Three make Su fully known before the alley occupies the final
quarter of the novel.

This is Volume Five of a planned six-book cycle, but it must read as a complete
novel in isolation. Its four parts are internal movements, not instalments. Every
person, rule, object and shared-series motif needed at the climax must first earn
meaning inside this book. The alley resolves the physical conflict; the final
chapters and 1892 epilogue resolve Su's moral and emotional movement.

## Part One — The Yard

### 1. The Copper — 2,400 words

Late August 1888, before the first canonical murder. A seam in the wash-copper
begins leaking before dawn. Su and Sau-Ling keep the morning's work moving while
Wei repairs what he can. Establish the household's humour, competence, money and
division of labour. Su notices that Wei now needs her strength without admitting
it. End with the shop opening onto an ordinary dockside day.

### 2. Two Languages — 2,200 words

Dawn training. Wei corrects Su with the folded fan and requires the four rules in
Cantonese and English. Dramatise the first serious use of the practice at fourteen
against three dock boys. Wei's response is correction rather than praise. In the
present, Su realises his grip closes a fraction late in the cold.

### 3. Monday Customers — 2,500 words

Liz and Kate arrive separately and overlap at the counter. Liz and Su compare
words that retain different weight after migration. Kate calls Su “duchess”,
brings a bodice for mending and sings only the first half of the quay song. The
chapter is warm, untidy and funny; nobody behaves like a future victim.

### 4. The White Bowl — 2,300 words

Wei tells the family story of missing the lighter in Hong Kong, the runner boy who
refused payment and the white bowl he failed to return. Su knows the performance
by heart and Sau-Ling corrects his deliberate errors. Rule three becomes personal:
some aid cannot be turned into a wage or settled for comfort.

### 5. Lee's Winter — 2,400 words

Move eight years back. Give Lee a full day alive: stubborn at training, hiding a
sweet, irritating Su, becoming ill. Cray declines the night call until morning and
counts Sau-Ling's fee. Lee dies on the third night. Return to 1888 through an
ordinary object of his still used in the shop.

### 6. A Doctor's Hands — 2,200 words

At the Prospect, Cray resets a docker's shoulder with extraordinary skill. Su,
watching in 1888, refuses to let the steadiness cancel what the same hands withheld
from Lee. Cray asks after Sau-Ling's rheumatism and recommends a liniment that
works. Both facts remain true.

## Part Two — The Autumn

### 7. Buck's Row — 2,000 words

News of Mary Ann Nichols reaches the laundry through customers and dock talk. Keep
the murder offstage. The significant change is in women's routes, pockets and
walking pace. Su's work continues while the city begins rewriting every ordinary
man as a possibility.

### 8. What Women Carry — 2,200 words

Laundry deliveries show what women now carry: pins, small knives, bottles, keys
between fingers. Men form opinions and an informal escort rota; the organised
Whitechapel committee comes later. Sau-Ling refuses to let fear decide who
deserves clean linen. Su resents being escorted and resents needing to calculate
the refusal.

### 9. Closing Time — 2,300 words

Dramatise Su's second serious use of the practice at seventeen, when a customer
refuses to leave and puts his hand where it has no right. The scene is brief; the
longer consequence is Wei making her recite the rules and correcting her stance.
In the present, Su understands that this was his frightened pride.

### 10. Kate Goes Hopping — 2,200 words

Kate prepares for Kent with more hope than money. She asks Su to add a concealed
wages pocket to the already repaired bodice and sings another incomplete version
of the song. Kate promises the missing half. Their farewell is casual because
neither believes it needs to carry final meaning.

### 11. Words with Floors — 2,200 words

Liz works, drinks tea at the laundry and speaks of Swedish grief. Su recognises
the false Princess Alice history and protects it without announcing that she has
done so. Liz sees more of Su's loneliness than Su enjoys having seen.

### 12. The Last Saturday — 2,300 words

29 September. Follow Su's full ordinary day and, separately but within documented
limits, the last fragments Liz and Kate are known to have lived. Do not stage their
murders or place Cray near them. The chapter ends before midnight.

### 13. The Double Event — 2,600 words

The names arrive through a newspaper read aloud at the Prospect. Su does not fall
because she is holding a basket and there is nowhere safe to put it. Sau-Ling cries
over Kate's paid, uncollected bodice. Wei cannot close the high shutter bolt; Su
does it for him and understands that she is now the stronger one.

## Part Three — The Open Column

### 14. Kate's Bodice — 2,400 words

Grief enters through unfinished work. Sau-Ling gives her creed: mercy is the
column kept open at a loss. Su resists both comfort and explanation. Let the family
disagree about whether work heals anything while continuing to work together.

### 15. Rules That Do Not Change — 2,300 words

Su takes her anger to the yard. Wei tells her the practice stops things; it does
not avenge them. A person who goes looking for a debt will find one. Su learns that
restraint can be active labour rather than passivity.

### 16. The Watch Committees — 2,200 words

Two committee men follow Su on Glamis Road, then congratulate themselves for
escorting her. Their cudgels are both protection and threat. Su's dry interior
judgement and outward courtesy reveal the full person Cray will later fail to see.

### 17. A City Looking Through — 2,300 words

Show the small Limehouse community, the Strangers' Home, sailors between berths,
mission charity and racial suspicion without creating an exotic “Chinatown”. Su
sees how institutions can look directly at a person and still refuse to recognise
them. This is social knowledge, not preparation for an investigation.

### 18. Two Brandies — 2,400 words

An evening at the Prospect. Cray is courteous, useful and quietly entitled. He
speaks over a barmaid, decides whose pain is serious and expects gratitude for an
ordinary kindness. Su dislikes him because she knows the cost of his selectivity,
not because she suspects him of the murders. His physician's case remains closed
and unremarked.

### 19. The Ninth of November — 2,200 words

Mary Jane Kelly's death makes the city's fear uncontainable. Keep the body and
room offstage. Su, Sau-Ling, Wei, Flo and the customers react as people whose daily
lives are being consumed by other people's appetite for horror. Su carries the
folded fan thereafter as a piece of the dawn yard, not as a weapon in a hunt.

## Part Four — Never First

### 20. The Last Monday Account — 2,200 words

Late November. A boarding house at the far end of Wapping pays only on the last
Monday. Su takes its clean linen herself. The chapter restores ordinary noise,
food, annoyance and work immediately before the confrontation.

### 21. The Shortcut — 2,200 words

Returning late, Su leaves the busy Highway and takes the familiar route behind
the sufferance wharf. Fog reduces the city to one step at a time. She hears cloth
drag on brick, rounds the buttress and finds Cray attacking an unnamed woman.

### 22. Three Beginnings — 3,500 words

The basket drops and the woman escapes. Cray instantly puts his gentleman's face
back on. He orders Su home and invokes the vulnerability of “her people”. Su gives
him no fear, argument or acknowledgement. She looks through him towards the way
the woman fled. His privilege cannot tolerate being treated as ordinary.

He begins three times. Su ends each in the smallest available way and steps back.
Wrist, lancet, elbow; then his last full lunge. She pivots. His momentum carries
him over the wharf edge. No victory line, gore or confirmation follows.

### 23. The Clasp — 2,300 words

Su shakes only after the danger has ended. The case lies in the mud. Opening it
would turn what she did on witnessed fact into retrospective payment. She removes
her hand from the clasp and gives the unanswered object to the Thames, weighted
with chain.

### 24. What Her Parents Know — 2,200 words

Sau-Ling receives the ruined clothes and boils them past testimony without asking.
At dawn Wei reads the change in Su's crossed wrists. He corrects her weight and
does not demand a story. Neither parent's silence is ignorance.

### 25. No Answer — 1,800 words

Cray disappears. The canonical series ends after Kelly; the wider Whitechapel file
does not. Su does not match dates, reconstruct movements or seek proof. She knows
only what occurred in her lane. Life resumes without becoming innocent.

## Epilogue — The Swede — 2,500 words

1892. Erik Johansson brings salt-stiff shirts into the shop. Su reads the burden
in his shoulders and chooses not to demand its name. Their relationship begins in
ordinary work, four years after the alley, and opens a life rather than closing a
plot. Maggie's family connection remains unstated.
