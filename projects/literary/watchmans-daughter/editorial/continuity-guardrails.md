# Continuity Guardrails

## Fixed character facts

- Su Zhang is nineteen and London-born in autumn 1888.
- At approximately six, around 1875, Su attends a small mission school off
  Commercial Road. The school itself is fictional; elementary attendance at that
  age is consistent with the London School Board regime established after 1870.
- She lives and works in her family's chandlery and laundry near the Causeway,
  within the Wapping–Shadwell–Limehouse river world.
- Wei Zhang grew up in Hong Kong after his father left Canton during the Opium
  War. He carried the chandlery trade, the practice, the folded fan and the white
  bowl to London.
- Sau-Ling was later sent for from Hong Kong. She runs the laundry, was baptised
  at a dockside mission and understands mercy as an account deliberately kept at
  a loss.
- Wei began teaching Su at seven. Her four rules are: never first; never in anger;
  never for payment; never more than is needed.
- Dr Reginald Cray is fictional. In 1858 he is a Company surgeon of roughly thirty;
  in 1888 he is about sixty-one and has been a familiar local figure for fifteen
  years.
- Su knows only that Cray attacked one woman in the alley. Neither she nor the
  narration proves that he committed the five Whitechapel murders.
- Erik Johansson arrives in 1892. His relationship with Su must work without any
  knowledge of Maggie or the 1603 ancestry thread.
- In 1940 Su would be about seventy-one, consistent with the unnamed Chinese
  laundrywoman described as seventy-odd.

## Resolved Lee chronology

Lee dies in winter 1881, aged seven. Su is twelve. The event is therefore seven
winters before the autumn of 1888.

This resolution follows the earlier project history and preserves the explicit
age of twelve in Su's memory. The protected `source-canon/` and verbatim seed keep
the original “eight winters” wording as evidence of the discrepancy. The
standalone draft uses “seven winters” and “seven years on”. A later anthology
backport should make the same two-word correction without otherwise rewriting the
1888 chapter.

## Historical-fiction boundaries

- Elizabeth Stride and Catherine Eddowes were real people. Their friendships with
  Su and all private dialogue are inventions.
- The five Whitechapel murders remain unsolved. The standalone Author's Note must
  state this directly.
- The Zhang practice and its family lineage are fictional. Do not attach them to
  a named historical martial tradition without evidence.
- Period details about London's Chinese population, the Causeway, missions,
  laundries, chandlers, watch committees and police treatment require a dedicated
  source pass before expansion.
- Any use of “Chinese boxing”, racial descriptions or period newspaper language
  must be historically controlled and narratively necessary.

## Character guardrails

- Su is observant and trained, not invulnerable. The alley costs her physically
  and morally.
- Wei's rules restrain power; they are not mystical aphorisms.
- Sau-Ling is not merely the softer parent. Her moral accounting is as rigorous as
  Wei's and reaches the decision he cannot make for Su.
- Liz and Kate must exist as women before they become names in a murder history.
- Cray's kindness and cruelty must both remain legible. If either is obviously a
  disguise, the ambiguity collapses.
- Erik's refusal to interrogate Su is seamanship and respect, not passivity.
- Su's composure must not erase her dry humour, pride, impatience, ambition or
  capacity for pleasure. Stillness is something she does, not all that she is.

## Ending guardrail

The case goes into the Thames unopened. Any later Cray/Heron resemblance may
create unease, but it must not retroactively open the case for the reader.
