# Canon and Guardrails

## The governing story

Su Zhang is a nineteen-year-old Chinese-English laundress and chandler's daughter
in Limehouse. She is not waiting to become heroic. She works, jokes, translates,
grieves, resents, loves and trains long before the alley tests anything her father
has taught her.

The novel's central movement is from **daughter** to **watchman**. Su gradually
understands that Wei is ageing and that the moral burden of the practice will
become hers. Sau-Ling supplies the counterweight: some accounts cannot be closed;
mercy means carrying them at a loss.

## Binding character canon

### Su Zhang

- Nineteen in 1888; born in London to Cantonese parents.
- Works beside Sau-Ling in the laundry and Wei in the chandlery.
- Reads force through shoulders, wrists, weight and balance; reads ordinary life
  through knots, cloth, stains, dockets and hands.
- Calm and restrained in public, but not without emotion. The reader must know
  her amusement, impatience, affection, grief and fear. Cray mistakes control for
  emptiness because he cannot imagine her interior life.
- Has trained since seven. Before Cray, she has used the practice in earnest only
  twice: against three dock boys at fourteen and against a man refusing to leave
  the shop at seventeen.
- Never becomes an investigator, vigilante, chosen one or modern action heroine.

### Wei Zhang

- In his sixties; a ship chandler, practitioner and bonesetter's son.
- Came from Hong Kong after his father's death, carrying the practice, a folded
  wooden fan and an unreturned white bowl.
- Teaches four rules in Cantonese and English: **Never first. Never for show.
  Never for payment. Never in anger.**
- His hands are ageing. He does not announce this; Su learns it through work.
- His affection is practical, dry and exact. Correction is often the language in
  which he expresses fear and pride.

### Sau-Ling Zhang

- Su's mother; sent for from Hong Kong once Wei's shop could support two.
- Runs the laundry with Su. Baptised at the mission church by the docks without
  surrendering her own judgement to its bookkeeping.
- Believes mercy is a column kept open at a loss, deliberately and forever.
- Warm, formidable and funny in private. She must not exist merely to wash away
  evidence after the confrontation.

### Lee Zhang

- Su's younger brother, born when she was four and dead of fever at seven.
- Was beginning his own dawn training; clever, stubborn and all elbows.
- Dr Cray deferred the night call until morning, then counted Sau-Ling's fee. His
  death is not proof of murder, but it is the family's clearest experience of how
  privilege assigns value to a life.
- Lee is a remembered child, not a symbolic grievance. Give him humour, habits
  and a relationship with Su before depicting his death.

### Elizabeth “Long Liz” Stride and Catherine “Kate” Eddowes

- Real women whose friendship with Su is invented openly and respectfully.
- They are customers and people before they become names in a case.
- Liz and Su understand what language loses during migration. Liz's Princess
  Alice story is treated as load-bearing, never exposed by Su.
- Kate calls Su “duchess”, sings unfinished songs and promises to bring the lost
  half of one back from the hop fields.
- Their chapters must contain appetite, humour, work, annoyance and plans. They
  must not be written as doomed foreshadowing.

### Dr Reginald Cray

- A retired Company surgeon in his sixties; immaculate, controlled and protected
  by English class, race, sex, profession and pension.
- Can perform genuine kindness. He has healed people without payment and gives
  useful medical advice. This does not erase his decision that Lee was not worth
  leaving supper for.
- Reeks of entitlement rather than theatrical evil. He expects deference as a
  natural property of the world.
- Su does not suspect, follow, question or investigate him before the alley.
- In the alley he is most provoked by her refusal to recognise his importance.
  She watches his shoulders, looks through him and reserves her attention for the
  woman and the open way home.

### Erik Johansson

- Appears in the 1892 epilogue, not as a reward for surviving 1888.
- A Swedish sailor from the timber trade. His father is Swedish; his mother was
  born on a Trinidad estate to a family transported from Calcutta under indenture.
- His connection to Maggie's Swedish family remains implicit.
- He and Su build intimacy through patience and the refusal to force open another
  person's hidden cargo.

## The alley — immutable sequence

1. Su is returning from an ordinary late delivery through the narrow riverside
   alley behind the sufferance wharf. She is not looking for Cray.
2. She hears a scuffle and finds him pinning an unnamed woman against the wall.
3. Su drops the laundry basket. The woman escapes and is never identified.
4. Cray restores his gentleman's manner immediately. He orders Su home and offers
   not to mention what happened, reminding her that “her people” cannot afford a
   dispute with him.
5. Su says nothing. She looks through him rather than at him. His rank, education
   and maleness fail to produce recognition in her, and this becomes intolerable.
6. There are three beginnings, all Cray's. Su steps back after each ending.
7. First: he grabs her. She uses the fan as a bonesetter's fulcrum and breaks his
   wrist. Second: he draws a lancet left-handed. She strikes the tendons and breaks
   the elbow. These are the fan's only two functional beats.
8. He could leave or call the watch. He does neither. Her stillness has made being
   witnessed by her more intolerable than pain.
9. Third: he throws himself at her. She pivots away. His own momentum carries him
   over the wharf edge into the Thames.
10. The confrontation is tense and cinematic, never graphic or triumphant.

## The unanswered case

- The physician's case is ordinary until it is left in the alley.
- It may contain evidence of the five murders. It may contain a retired surgeon's
  instruments. No narration may privilege either answer.
- Su does not open it, because what she did must stand on what she witnessed: one
  woman under his hands and three attacks he chose to begin.
- The case enters the Thames unopened and weighted with chandlery chain.
- No later chapter, descendant, document, recovered body or authorial wink may
  confirm or deny that Cray was the Ripper.

## Prose and ethical controls

- Close third person, present tense, principally through Su.
- Let domestic action carry character. Do not explain an image after it has
  already conveyed the meaning.
- Keep Cantonese use limited to moments where language itself matters; do not
  exoticise the household.
- Avoid invented “ancient Chinese” mysticism. The practice is work, inheritance,
  anatomy, repetition and manners.
- Avoid the fictional-Limehouse opium-den mythology. The community is small,
  maritime, working and ordinary.
- Historical victims retain their names and documented boundaries. The novel
  claims no solution to their murders.
- No gore. Violence is rendered through balance, breath, sound and consequence.
