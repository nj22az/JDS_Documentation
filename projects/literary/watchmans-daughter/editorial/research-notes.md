# Research Notes

These are controls for drafting, not prose to be imported wholesale. Where the
historical record is uncertain, the novel should preserve that uncertainty.

## Limehouse and the Zhang household

- The Strangers' Home for Asiatics, Africans and South Sea Islanders stood on
  West India Dock Road and opened in 1857. It provided lodging, employment and
  repatriation support for Asian and African sailors and also served as a mission
  centre. More than 5,000 people reportedly passed through it between 1857 and
  1877. A small settled community grew around the maritime population. Source:
  [Open University/summary and references](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strangers%27_Home_for_Asiatics%2C_Africans_and_South_Sea_Islanders).
- The 1881 material indicates a very small and internationally mixed local
  population, not the enormous, exotic Chinatown later fiction imagined. Added
  scenes should emphasise sailors, boarding houses, chandlers, eating houses,
  missions and ordinary families. Avoid the opium-den mythology.
- Limehouse Causeway, Pennyfields and West India Dock Road are suitable anchors
  for the Zhang business. Exact walking routes to Wapping must be checked against
  an 1888 map before the final geography pass.

## Elizabeth Stride

- Born Elisabeth Gustafsdotter near Torslanda in 1843; moved to London in 1866.
  She spoke English fluently, worked at times in sewing and cleaning, and had an
  intermittent relationship with dock labourer Michael Kidney.
- She was known to tell the false story that her husband and children died in the
  1878 *Princess Alice* disaster. The novel may treat the story as emotionally
  load-bearing but must not invent a definitive reason for it.
- She was killed during the night of 29–30 September 1888 and found in Dutfield's
  Yard. Source overview with record references:
  [Elizabeth Stride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elizabeth_Stride).

## Catherine Eddowes

- Eddowes and John Kelly took annual hop-picking work in Kent in September 1888.
  They returned to London on foot after poor results. This supports Kate's hope
  that the hop fields will improve her finances, but the novel should not make the
  trip more successful than the record allows.
- She and Stride were killed on the same night, within roughly an hour. Source
  overview with record references:
  [Catherine Eddowes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catherine_Eddowes).

## The murders and public fear

- Mary Ann Nichols was found on 31 August; Annie Chapman on 8 September; Stride
  and Eddowes on 30 September; Mary Jane Kelly on 9 November.
- “Canonical five” is a later grouping. The broader Whitechapel murder file
  continued beyond Kelly. The novel must not state that violence against women
  stopped because Cray disappeared. It may state only that the canonical sequence
  ends after Kelly and that Su never learns what, if anything, this means.
- Anti-Jewish and anti-immigrant suspicion was materially inflamed by press
  coverage. This can shape Su's public world without sending her into a police
  station or turning the novel into an investigation.

## Medical plausibility

- A wooden monaural stethoscope is appropriate for Cray's 1880 visit. The form
  predates the scene by decades and remained in medical use through the later
  nineteenth century. Do not describe a modern binaural instrument.
- A doctor's night call to a poor dockside household was possible but could not
  be assumed. Cray's refusal to leave supper is therefore a choice within the
  social power of his profession, not a claim that no doctor would have gone.
- Closed reduction of a dislocated shoulder by controlled traction and
  counter-traction long predates 1888. Chapter Six deliberately describes the
  physical principles without assigning Cray a named modern technique.
- No exact fee is stated for Lee's visit. The family can experience the charge as
  substantial without fixing a sum until ordinary East End medical fees have
  been checked against a primary source.
- Camphor and turpentine both appeared in period external liniments. Cray's exact
  recommendation still needs a pharmacy-history and toxicology check before
  publication; it is character detail, not health advice.
