# Three Streets

## I. The Distance

The first thing Su learns at school is that three streets can be a foreign
country.

She is six years old and has been attending the mission room off Commercial Road
for four mornings. It occupies the upper floor of a converted cooper's shop and
smells of damp wool, chalk and the vinegar the cooper uses below. There are
twenty-eight children when everyone comes, which nobody ever does, sitting at
benches made for twenty. The youngest learn letters from a board. The older ones
copy scripture and columns of figures onto slates. A painted eye hangs above the
teacher's desk to remind them that God sees everything.

Su does not like the eye. It is badly drawn.

Its left lid is lower than its right, and the pupil points towards the stovepipe
rather than the room. If God sees everything, she reasons, He ought at least to be
looking in the correct direction.

She has not said this aloud. She has already understood that school contains a
large number of facts nobody wishes to have improved.

On the fourth morning the teacher writes *MOTHER* on the board.

The other children repeat it together. Twenty-seven voices make the word flat and
public. Su looks at the M, then at the window, where rain is trembling in the
corners of the glass.

Her mother will be at the copper now, sleeves rolled past the elbow, lifting the
first sheets with the wooden dolly. Steam will have taken the pins out of the hair
at her temples. There will be a cup of tea on the ledge going cold because Sau-Ling
always pours it before the work and remembers it after. The shop is three streets
away. Su knows this because her father counted them on the first morning.

Three streets is not far. She can count to three in two languages.

The teacher turns to help a boy whose slate pencil has broken. Below, the cooper's
men roll a cask through the yard, and the noise takes the whole room's attention
for the length of one breath.

Su puts down her chalk.

She does not tell anyone she is leaving. Telling would make it a question, and a
question may be answered no.

Her coat hangs on the last peg. She takes it, closes the door gently behind her,
and goes home to see her mother.

## II. The Route

For the first street, everything behaves.

There is the baker's red awning, wet enough to be brown. There is the shop with
three brass lamps in the window and one always crooked. There is the alley where
a black dog sleeps under a handcart and opens one eye as she passes. Su knows the
dog. The dog knows nothing is required of it. They understand one another.

At the corner she turns left.

The second street is occupied by a brewer's dray.

Two horses stand across the road, enormous at the level of a six-year-old, their
flanks steaming in the rain. Barrels are coming off the cart. Men shout numbers
and roll them down a board, and every barrel makes the stones jump. The pavement
is blocked by baskets, a broken crate and a woman arguing about onions.

Su cannot see the crooked lamp behind her. She cannot see the next corner ahead.

This is inconvenient, not frightening.

She goes around.

Around means through a passage beside the public house, across a yard where a boy
is plucking a chicken, and out by a different arch onto a street she has never
seen. It contains a wall with no windows, high as a church, running in both
directions. Beyond it, cranes move slowly through the grey sky.

Su stops.

The river is behind the wall. Home is near the river. Therefore the wall must lead
home.

The reasoning is excellent. The wall is not.

It belongs to the docks and goes on for what appears to be the rest of London.
Every gate looks like the last gate. Every opening contains men, ropes, carts and
another wall beyond. The rain thickens. Her school shoes begin admitting water.

She walks east because the river runs that way on the map her father once drew in
flour on the table. Or perhaps it ran west. The flour had been needed for supper
before she had finished studying it.

At the next gate, a wagon loaded with tea chests grinds past. Su catches the smell
of wet rope, tar and river mud—the smell of her father's shop—and follows it
inside.

Nobody stops her. Adults do not look down when they are carrying something heavy.

The gate admits her into a country made entirely for giants. Warehouses rise on
both sides, their doors open black above her. Chains descend from beams. A crane
turns with a complaint that seems to come from the sky. Men move cargo under the
orders of voices she cannot locate. Between the sheds, water appears: a dock basin
flat and dark under the rain, ships sitting in it higher than houses.

Su's confidence changes shape.

She has not yet become afraid. She has begun to understand why fear might be
useful.

She turns to leave. A second wagon is coming through the gate, and behind it the
gatekeeper is already pushing one iron leaf closed against the weather. She calls
out. The wagon drowns her voice in wheels and hooves. The iron leaf meets its mate
with a sound that enters through her ribs.

The way back is gone.

## III. The Water

Su decides not to cry.

This is partly because crying would be useless and partly because a docker has
noticed her. He is carrying a hook over one shoulder and has a beard the colour
of the rope piled behind him.

Here, he says. Whose are you?

Mine, Su says.

The answer is true but unsuccessful.

The man looks around for a more informative adult. There is none. What's your
name?

Su Zhang.

Susan what?

Su. Zhang.

John?

Zhang.

He tries it once more and produces something that is neither syllable. Su folds
her arms. He seems to understand that the interview has deteriorated.

Where d'you live, then?

The Causeway. At the chandlery with the blue door.

There are three blue doors on the Causeway and more chandlers than churches. The
docker scratches his beard. Did you come off one of the ships?

I came from school.

Which school?

Su knows exactly which school. It is the school three streets from home, above the
cooper, beneath the badly painted eye of God. She has never heard it called
anything else.

School, she says.

The docker closes his eyes briefly. Stay there.

He points to a bollard beside the shed and goes towards the gatehouse.

Su stays for almost a minute.

Then a bell rings out on the water. Men answer it. A lock gate begins to move at
the end of the basin, and the surface, flat a moment before, pulls towards the
opening. A small empty lighter comes along the wall on a line, its bow swinging
in. On its deck is a pile of laundry baskets.

They are not her mother's baskets. From the distance they are close enough.

Su steps off the bollard.

The lighter touches the quay below with a hollow knock. A narrow plank lies from
the stones to its deck. A man on board has his back to her, hauling the line. If
she crosses the plank, she can ask whether the boat is going to the Causeway. If
it is, she will be home before the docker returns, and the entire morning will
once again make sense.

She puts one foot on the plank.

The lighter moves.

Not far. Six inches, perhaps. Enough. The plank grinds sideways. Su's wet sole
goes out from under her and the dock opens black between the boat and the wall.

Somebody catches the back of her coat.

For one second she is hanging by the collar, feet finding nothing, looking down
at water with oil turning colours on it. Then she is on the stones, hard enough
to lose her breath.

The docker stands over her with both fists closed in her coat. His face has gone
white around the beard.

Stay, he says.

This time the word is not an instruction. It is what remains after a man has
nearly watched a child go between a hull and a wall.

Su stays.

The fear arrives late. It begins in her legs and works upward, a shaking so deep
she is offended by it. She locks her knees. This makes the shaking worse.

The docker sits on the wet stones beside her. He does not say *I told you*. This
is the first good decision any adult has made all morning.

He takes off his neckerchief and puts it around her shoulders, though it is wet
and smells of tobacco. Su holds both ends under her chin.

I was going home, she says.

So I gathered.

I know the way.

Course you do.

I took a different part of it.

That'll be where the trouble got in.

She considers this and gives one unwilling sob. The docker looks towards the
water as though he has not heard it.

## IV. Found

Sau-Ling arrives without her shawl.

That is what frightens Su most.

Her mother does not go into the street without a shawl. She does not leave the
copper with its fire drawn or the shop with washing on the counter. She does not
run. Yet she comes through the reopened dock gate bareheaded, apron dark with
rain, one sleeve still rolled and the other fallen to her wrist, running beside
the schoolmistress and ahead of Wei.

Su stands so quickly the docker's neckerchief drops.

For a moment Sau-Ling stops. She looks at her daughter, at the water, at the plank
now drawn safely aboard. Her face does several things and permits none of them to
finish.

Then she crosses the yard.

Su has prepared an account. The dray blocked the proper street. The wall led the
wrong way. The gate closed. The lighter appeared to be useful. Each item is true
and together they explain nothing.

I missed you, she says.

Sau-Ling goes down on her knees and takes Su's face in both hands. Her palms are
hot and rough from the copper. She looks over every inch as though checking linen
returned damaged.

You may miss me from school, she says.

It was too far.

It is three streets.

That is far when you are six.

Something happens to Sau-Ling's mouth. It is almost a laugh and almost the other
thing. She pulls Su against the wet front of her apron, and Su discovers she has
been waiting to cry until there was somewhere familiar to put it.

She cries without dignity. Into the apron, loudly. Sau-Ling holds her through all
of it.

Wei thanks the docker. The docker returns the thanks awkwardly, as though being
thanked for catching a child is an accusation. The schoolmistress has a speech
prepared about doors, obedience and the regulations of the London School Board.
She gets as far as doors. Sau-Ling looks at her. The remainder of the speech
decides to wait for another family.

They walk home together, Su between her parents. It is, as promised, three
streets. She does not recognise any of them.

At the shop, Sau-Ling makes tea and sits with her daughter while the copper goes
cold. This has never happened before. Su puts both hands around the cup.

Next time you miss me, her mother says, you tell the teacher.

She will say no.

Then you miss me until the bell.

What if you do not come?

Sau-Ling waits until Su looks up.

I will come every time.

It is a promise made to a six-year-old and therefore larger than any adult can
sensibly promise. Sau-Ling makes it anyway.

That evening Wei spreads flour on the table and asks Su to draw the way she took.
She gives him the baker, the dog, the dray, the endless wall and the gate. The
route bends back on itself twice and ends at the black mark she makes for the
water.

You saw the river and thought it was home, he says.

It is home.

It is also a road. Roads go away as well as back.

Su studies the white map. Then she corrects the turn beside the public house and
draws the route again.

The next morning she returns to school holding Sau-Ling's hand. At the door, her
mother lets go. Su goes upstairs, hangs her coat on the last peg and sits beneath
the badly painted eye.

When the teacher writes *MOTHER* on the board, Su does not leave.

She checks the window once.

Then she copies the word exactly.
