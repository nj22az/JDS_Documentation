# Seven Winters

## I. All Elbows

At seven, Lee Zhang is nearly all elbows.

This is Su's considered opinion, formed over a winter of being made to stand
opposite him in the yard before dawn while he forgets where his feet are. He has
two of those as well, allegedly. They appear at unexpected moments and in places
no sensible foot would choose.

Again, their father says.

The yard is blue with the hour before sunrise. Frost has found the cracks between
the stones, and the wash-copper behind them is beginning its first low mutter of
the day. Coal smoke and lye sit in the air. Beyond the wall, the river works in
the dark, chains knocking somewhere against a mast.

Lee settles into his stance. One knee falls in. His right elbow escapes towards
Shadwell.

Su looks at her father.

I have not said anything, Wei says.

You looked it.

Then correct what I looked.

She puts two fingers against Lee's knee and presses it out. He lets the whole leg
go loose so that she has to catch him.

You are doing that deliberately.

I am relaxing.

You are collapsing.

Father says softness is better than stiffness.

Water is soft, Su says. Mud is also soft. One carries ships. The other eats boots.

Lee considers this, then looks past her to Wei. Which am I?

At present, Wei says, you are late for breakfast.

Lee grins. Su does not. This is because she is twelve and dignity has lately
become important to her, though nobody else in the family has agreed to recognise
the change.

Again, Wei says.

Lee comes at her with the serious expression he believes fighters wear. Su waits
for the shoulder to confess, steps aside and turns his wrist. He passes her in a
small astonished circle. She could put him on the stones. Instead she catches the
back of his jacket and sets him upright one pace short of the copper.

Three movements, Wei says.

Two.

The catch was a movement.

The catch was kindness.

Kindness still counts.

Lee, who has recovered quickly from being saved, says, I nearly had you.

You nearly had breakfast in the wash-copper.

Her father's fan touches the back of her knee. Not a strike. A fact. Su lowers by
the width of two fingers.

I was balanced, she says.

You were arranged to look balanced. That is not the same thing.

There is no answer to this that will not sound twelve years old, so she gives him
none. Lee sees the silence and knows precisely what it costs her. He bites the
inside of his cheek to hold back a laugh. This is a mistake. Su can see the laugh
moving in his shoulders.

Again, she says.

This time he lasts longer.

* * *

At the breakfast table Lee's sleeve finds the treacle. Su watches it happen and
says nothing until he lifts his arm and draws a brown line across the wood.

Sau-Ling closes her eyes.

It was the sleeve, Lee says.

The sleeve has confessed, Su says. We are waiting for its accomplice.

He looks down at his own hand as though surprised to find it attached.

Their mother points towards the copper without opening her eyes. Lee takes the
cloth and goes. Su smiles into her tea where nobody can charge her for it.

After breakfast Wei sets the chandlery invoices beside his bowl. Su reaches for
them. He puts one finger on the top sheet.

Laundry tickets first.

I finished yesterday's.

Then today's will be frightened of you.

She has been asking since Michaelmas to take the morning accounts. She can add a
column faster than the apprentice at the ropewalk, and unlike him she does not use
her finger or move her lips. Her father knows this. His refusal to admit knowing
it has become a contest between them, conducted without witnesses and with all
the patience of a siege.

I found the missing sixpence in Carter's order, she says.

You found where I put it.

You put it there?

He drinks his tea.

That is dishonest.

It was never sent to Carter.

Dishonesty in rehearsal remains dishonesty.

Then you should not rehearse looking at papers that are not yours.

Lee returns with the cloth, hears only the end and understands none of it. Are
you two fighting?

No, Wei says.

Yes, Su says.

Sau-Ling takes the invoices from under her husband's finger and gives them to her
daughter.

You may fight after the column is added, she says. I want to know which of you is
right before the rice goes cold.

Su does not look at her father. Looking would make victory cheap. She bends over
the first page and finds the second error before he has finished his tea.

## II. The Quiet

Three days later Lee stops being troublesome.

That is how Su knows first.

He comes in from carrying lamp-oil to the river stairs and sits beside the copper
without being told. His hair is wet with sleet. He does not complain that his
boots leak, though his boots have leaked since October and he generally considers
this a fresh injustice each time water enters them.

Su puts the back of her fingers to his forehead.

He flinches. Your hands are cold.

Your head is hot.

Then we improve each other.

It is a good answer. She would usually tell him so by finding something wrong
with it. Instead she takes off his wet jacket.

By evening the heat has gone through him. Sau-Ling makes up the bed nearest the
stove and sends for what the neighbours send for: willow bark, broth, a woman
from two doors down who has raised eight children and buried three and therefore
knows both more and less than any doctor. Lee sleeps, wakes angry, sleeps again.

On the second waking he asks whether dawn practice happened without him.

Nothing happens without you, Su says. It merely becomes quieter.

Did Father teach the long form?

To whom?

You could do it alone.

I could also play chess alone. I would win, but the company would be poor.

He gives a small laugh that turns into coughing. Su holds the bowl while he is
sick into it and looks away, because at seven dignity is not yet important until
the instant it is needed.

At ten that night Sau-Ling says the doctor's name.

Nobody answers at first.

Cray has rooms two streets over and a brass plate by the door. He charges the
usual fee, which is another way of saying too much. He has set shoulders in the
bar and brought the potboy through a fever in the spring. The potboy's mother says
he is a saint. The docker whose shoulder he set says he is an artist. Wei says
nothing about men who are called either.

Then he puts on his coat.

I am coming, Su says.

You are staying.

I can carry the lantern.

You can carry the bowl when he wakes.

She wants to argue. Lee moves under the blankets, and the argument becomes a
luxury belonging to another room. Wei goes into the sleet alone.

He returns with it silvering his shoulders. Sau-Ling reads the answer before he
has shut the door.

Morning, he says. The doctor will call in the morning.

Did you tell him Lee is seven?

Wei takes off his coat.

Did you tell him?

I told him my son is ill.

That was not what she asked, and they both know it. Su takes the coat from him
harder than a coat requires and hangs it by the stove. Meltwater runs from the hem
and makes a dark account on the floorboards.

## III. The Fee

Dr Cray calls after breakfast.

He is not hurried. This is not yet a crime in Su's mind, though it has begun to
look like one.

His coat is dry and brushed. His case is dark leather, kept oiled, the buckles
quiet. He smells faintly of soap and something medicinal beneath it. When he takes
Lee's wrist, his fingers settle exactly over the pulse without searching.

Lee opens his eyes.

You are the doctor.

So they tell me.

Can you make me well before morning?

Cray glances at Su, perhaps expecting help with the question. She gives him none.

I shall do what can be done, he says.

It is a careful sentence. Su dislikes it immediately.

He listens to Lee's chest and looks into his throat. He asks Sau-Ling about the
night, the broth, the coughing, the colour in the bowl. His questions are good.
His hands are good. Nothing in his face is unkind. Nothing in it is afraid.

When he closes the case, Su understands that he has finished before anyone has
said so.

Fever, he tells her parents. Keep him cool. Small water, often. I will come again
if there is a change.

There is already a change, Su says.

Cray looks at her fully for the first time. His eyes are pale and attentive.

Your sister is observant, he says to Lee.

I am his sister, Su says to the doctor.

Quite.

Sau-Ling pays him. The coins have been waiting in a saucer. Cray counts them with
his thumb, not greedily and not apologetically. One, two, three. The ordinary
movement of a man making certain an account is correct.

Su watches his thumb.

He closes his purse, recommends a preparation for Sau-Ling's hands, and leaves.
The advice will prove useful. This will make remembering him worse.

Lee dies on the third night.

Before the last sleep he asks Su whether his stance had improved.

No, she says.

His eyes open, offended even through the fever.

Your right elbow was still trying to emigrate.

It was not.

It had papers for Gravesend.

He smiles. A little. Enough that she knows the joke reached him.

She tells herself for years that this was kindness. Some years she believes it.

## IV. What Calm Looked Like

After the burial, dawn comes as though the house has not earned a morning.

The yard is hard with frost. The copper is cold. Sau-Ling has not lit it, which is
the only refusal anyone has seen her make all week.

Wei stands with the folded fan in his hand.

Su takes her place opposite him.

For a while neither moves. There should be another body in the yard, badly placed.
The empty part is exactly Lee's size.

Begin, Wei says.

She begins. Her first turn is too fast. The second has anger in it. On the third,
the fan touches her wrist and sends her hand down.

Again.

He counted the money, Su says.

Wei's face does not change.

He stood there and counted it.

I know.

Three words would be more useful. Anger would be more useful. If her father broke
the fan across the wall, if he walked back to the brass plate and made the street
hear Lee's name, there would be somewhere to put what is inside her.

Instead he waits.

The practice stops things, he says. It does not answer them.

Then what is it for?

The question leaves her louder than she intended. A gull lifts off the roof.

Wei looks towards the empty place and back to her.

For what can still be stopped.

It is not enough. It is also the only answer he has.

Again, he says.

Su sets her feet. Her right knee falls in by the width of a finger. She knows it
has. She leaves it there, a small deliberate wrong, needing him to see something
he can correct.

The fan touches the outside of the knee.

There, he says.

She moves it out.

At twelve, Su thinks calm is the name for a place pain cannot enter. She watches
her father through the whole winter and mistakes his stillness for proof.

It will take seven years, and a dark lane, to understand that calm may also be the
shape pain makes when there is nowhere safe to set it down.
